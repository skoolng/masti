A sequence is formed by the digits of π: 3, 1, 4, 1, 5, 9, 2, 6, … Is this a mathematical sequence? Explain. Hard
Show Answer

Find the value of k if k+2, 2k−1, 3k+1 are three consecutive terms of a sequence with constant second differences equal to 2. Hard
Show Answer

First differences: (2k−1)−(k+2) = k−3 and (3k+1)−(2k−1) = k+2. Second difference = (k+2)−(k−3) = 5. But second difference = 2 → 5 = 2 has no solution. If AP (second diff=0): k−3 = k+2 → no solution either. No valid k exists under these constraints for constant-difference 2.

The pth term of an AP is q and qth term is p. Find the (p+q)th term. Hard
Show Answer

a+(p−1)d=q …(1); a+(q−1)d=p …(2). (1)−(2): (p−q)d=q−p → d=−1. From (1): a=q−(p−1)(−1)=q+p−1. a_(p+q)=a+(p+q−1)d=(p+q−1)+p+q−1−(p+q−1)=(p+q−1)−(p+q−1)=0.

The mth term of an AP is n and nth term is m (m ≠ n). Find the (m+n)th term. Hard
Show Answer

Same setup as above (general): a_(m+n) = 0. Proof: a+(m−1)d=n, a+(n−1)d=m. Subtract: (m−n)d=n−m → d=−1. Then a=n+m−1. a_(m+n)=(n+m−1)+(m+n−1)(−1)=0.
