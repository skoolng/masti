Set A Easy – Wave Types Recall

What is a transverse wave? Give two examples.Easy
Show Answer

A transverse wave is one where particles vibrate perpendicular (at 90°) to the direction of wave travel. Examples: light waves (electromagnetic), water waves, waves on a guitar string.

What is a longitudinal wave? Give two examples.Easy
Show Answer

A longitudinal wave has particles vibrating parallel to (along) the direction of wave travel. Examples: sound waves, ultrasound waves, seismic P-waves.

What are compressions and rarefactions in a longitudinal wave?Easy
Show Answer

Compressions: regions where particles are pushed together (high pressure). Rarefactions: regions where particles are spread apart (low pressure). Sound alternates between compressions and rarefactions as it travels.

Can sound travel through a vacuum? Why or why not?Easy
Show Answer

No. Sound is a mechanical wave requiring a medium (particles) to transmit compressions and rarefactions. A vacuum has no particles — confirmed by the bell-jar experiment: as air is pumped out, the ringing bell becomes inaudible.

In which direction do particles vibrate in a sound wave?Easy
Show Answer

Back and forth parallel to the direction the sound wave travels (longitudinal). Air particles oscillate to and fro along the direction of sound propagation.

What causes sound? How does a vibrating tuning fork produce sound?Easy
Show Answer

Sound is caused by vibration. Tuning fork prongs vibrate back and forth — each forward vibration compresses adjacent air, each backward vibration creates a rarefaction. These pressure variations travel outward as a sound wave.

What type of wave is light: transverse or longitudinal?Easy
Show Answer

Transverse. Light (and all electromagnetic waves) has oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to the direction of propagation. It does not require a medium and can travel through vacuum.

Give one difference and one similarity between sound waves and light waves.Easy
Show Answer

Difference: Sound = longitudinal, needs a medium. Light = transverse, travels in vacuum. Similarity: Both transfer energy without transferring matter; both can be reflected and refracted; both have frequency, wavelength, and speed.

Set B Medium – Wave Behaviour Analysis

Explain why sound travels faster through water than through air, using the particle model.Medium
Show Answer

Sound travels by particle-to-particle collisions. Water molecules are ~800× closer together than air molecules. Closer particles transmit compressions more quickly — each particle has less distance to "push" the next one. Higher intermolecular forces in water → faster transmission.

Compare particle movement in: (a) a transverse wave on a rope, (b) a longitudinal sound wave in air.Medium
Show Answer

(a) Rope: each particle moves up and down perpendicular to the wave direction. The wave moves horizontally while particles only move vertically. (b) Sound: each air particle moves back and forth parallel to wave direction. Compressions and rarefactions travel outward while individual air molecules oscillate in place on average.

How would you demonstrate that sound is a longitudinal wave using a slinky spring?Medium
Show Answer

Stretch a slinky on a table. Push one end forward and pull back repeatedly. Compressions (coils pushed together) and rarefactions (coils spread apart) travel along the slinky in the same direction as the push — demonstrating longitudinal propagation. Compare by shaking end up-and-down to show a transverse wave on the same slinky.

Why does an astronaut on the Moon need a radio to communicate but not to see their partner?Medium
Show Answer

Sound waves (mechanical, longitudinal) cannot travel through vacuum on the Moon's surface. Light (electromagnetic, transverse) needs no medium and travels through vacuum. Radio waves are also electromagnetic — they carry voice signals as modulated EM waves across the vacuum between astronauts.

Seismic S-waves (transverse) and P-waves (longitudinal): which arrives at a seismograph first? Why?Medium
Show Answer

P-waves arrive first (hence "primary"). They are longitudinal and travel faster through Earth's layers (~6–8 km/s in crust) than S-waves (~3–4.5 km/s). The time gap between P and S arrivals is used to locate earthquake epicentres.

Explain diffraction of sound waves. Why can you hear someone talking around a corner?Medium
Show Answer

Diffraction: waves spread around corners/obstacles when wavelength is similar to the gap/obstacle size. Sound has wavelengths of 0.017–17 m (audible range) — similar to doorway and corridor dimensions. Sound diffracts significantly around corners, filling the space beyond. Light wavelengths (~400–700 nm) are far too short to diffract noticeably around corners.

Describe how a speaker converts electrical signals into sound waves.Medium
Show Answer

Electrical signals drive a coil in a magnetic field (electromagnet). Alternating current creates alternating magnetic force → coil (attached to cone/diaphragm) vibrates back and forth → cone compresses and rarefies adjacent air → longitudinal sound waves transmitted at same frequency as electrical signal.

How do noise-cancelling headphones use wave interference to reduce sound?Medium
Show Answer

A microphone detects ambient noise. A DSP chip generates an anti-noise signal with same amplitude, same frequency, but exactly 180° out of phase. This anti-noise superimposes on ambient noise — destructive interference — compressions and rarefactions cancel → silence at the ear. Most effective for low-frequency constant sounds (engine hum, air conditioning).

Set C Hard – Extended Wave Analysis

Evaluate: "Transverse waves always travel faster than longitudinal waves." Is this true?Hard
Show Answer

False. Speed depends on the medium, not wave type. Light (transverse) travels at 3×10⁸ m/s — far faster than sound. But seismic P-waves (longitudinal) travel faster than S-waves (transverse) through Earth. Speed = √(elasticity/density) for mechanical waves — no universal rule favouring transverse or longitudinal.

Explain how the cochlea in the human ear detects different frequencies and converts them to nerve signals.Hard
Show Answer

Sound → eardrum → ossicles (hammer, anvil, stirrup) → oval window → fluid waves in cochlea. The basilar membrane varies in stiffness/width along its length — high-frequency sounds resonate near the base (stiff, narrow); low-frequency near apex (flexible, wide). Hair cells at each position detect specific resonant frequencies → convert mechanical vibration to electrical nerve impulses → auditory nerve → brain. Frequency analysis is done mechanically by the cochlea.

How do P-waves and S-waves together prove Earth has a liquid outer core?Hard
Show Answer

P-waves (longitudinal) can travel through both solid and liquid. S-waves (transverse) cannot travel through liquids (no shear stiffness). Seismographs show: P-waves detected globally (some travel through core). S-waves create a "shadow zone" on the opposite side of Earth — S-waves cannot penetrate the outer core. This absence of S-waves beyond ~104° proves the outer core is liquid.

Explain constructive and destructive interference of two sound waves with the same frequency, defining nodes and antinodes.Hard
Show Answer

Constructive interference: crests/compressions overlap → amplitude doubles → louder sound (antinode). Destructive interference: compression meets rarefaction → cancel → silence (node). Standing wave: nodes (zero displacement) alternate with antinodes (maximum displacement). This principle is used in room acoustics design, noise-cancelling headphones, and musical instrument resonance chambers.

Describe quantitatively how an oscilloscope compares frequency and amplitude of two different sound sources.Hard
Show Answer

Connect microphone to oscilloscope. Sound A: measure time for one complete cycle (T₁), f₁ = 1/T₁; measure peak voltage = amplitude₁ (proportional to loudness). Sound B: same → T₂, f₂, amplitude₂. If T₁ < T₂ → f₁ > f₂ (Sound A has higher pitch). If amplitude₁ > amplitude₂ → Sound A is louder. x-axis = time, y-axis = pressure/voltage.

Noise: trains produce 90 dB, conversation 60 dB. The dB scale: 10 dB increase = 10× intensity. How many times more intense is a train than conversation?Hard
Show Answer

Difference = 30 dB = 3 × 10 dB. Each 10 dB = 10× intensity. Three steps of 10× = 10³ = 1000× more intense. A train is 1000 times more intense than normal conversation, explaining why prolonged exposure causes hearing damage.

Explain how bats use ultrasound rather than audible sound for echolocation, and what determines the minimum object size they can detect.Hard
Show Answer

Resolution limit: waves can only detect objects larger than ~λ. For 40 kHz: λ = 343/40000 = 8.6 mm. For 20 kHz (upper human limit): λ = 17 mm. Ultrasound → shorter wavelength → can detect smaller objects (insects ~5 mm). Lower-frequency sound would diffract around small prey, giving no echo. Bats adjust calls up to 100 kHz to detect 3 mm objects. Same principle applies to medical ultrasound resolution.

Explain why water waves are sometimes described as neither purely transverse nor purely longitudinal.Hard
Show Answer

Surface water waves: particles move in circles (both vertical up-down AND horizontal back-forth components). Deep water: circular/elliptical motion combines transverse and longitudinal motion. Only at the surface do they appear primarily transverse (up-down). They are sometimes classified as "surface waves" — a distinct type. In shallow water near shores, the circular motion becomes more elliptical, eventually breaking.

Set A Easy – Wave Parameter Definitions

Define amplitude and state what it represents for sound.Easy
Show Answer

Amplitude (A) is the maximum displacement of a particle from its equilibrium position. For sound: amplitude = loudness. Greater amplitude = louder sound.

Define frequency (f) and state its unit.Easy
Show Answer

Frequency is the number of complete oscillations (cycles) per second. Unit: Hertz (Hz). For sound: frequency = pitch. Higher frequency = higher pitch.

Define wavelength (λ) and state its unit.Easy
Show Answer

Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive points in phase (e.g., crest to crest, compression to compression). Unit: metres (m).

What is the period (T) of a wave and how is it related to frequency?Easy
Show Answer

Period T = time for one complete oscillation. T = 1/f. A wave of 500 Hz has T = 1/500 = 0.002 s = 2 ms.

Write the wave speed equation and define each term.Easy
Show Answer

v = f × λ. v = wave speed (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m).

A sound wave has frequency 440 Hz and wavelength 0.78 m. Calculate its speed.Easy
Show Answer

v = fλ = 440 × 0.78 = 343.2 m/s (approximately 343 m/s in air at 20°C — concert A note).

A wave has frequency 200 Hz. What is its period?Easy
Show Answer

T = 1/f = 1/200 = 0.005 s = 5 ms.

If the speed of sound is 340 m/s and frequency is 170 Hz, find the wavelength.Easy
Show Answer

λ = v/f = 340/170 = 2 m.

Set B Medium – Wave Calculations

A sound wave travels at 340 m/s with period 0.01 s. Find: (a) frequency, (b) wavelength.Medium
Show Answer

(a) f = 1/T = 1/0.01 = 100 Hz. (b) λ = v/f = 340/100 = 3.4 m.

A water wave has wavelength 3 m and period 2 s. Find its speed.Medium
Show Answer

f = 1/T = 0.5 Hz. v = fλ = 0.5 × 3 = 1.5 m/s.

Radio waves travel at 3×10⁸ m/s. An FM station broadcasts at 100 MHz. Find the wavelength.Medium
Show Answer

λ = v/f = 3×10⁸ / 10⁸ = 3 m.

How does doubling the frequency affect the wavelength (speed constant)?Medium
Show Answer

λ = v/f. If f doubles, λ halves (inversely proportional). Wavelength and frequency are inversely proportional at constant wave speed.

A tuning fork vibrates at 512 Hz. Sound speed = 340 m/s. How many wavelengths fit in 10 m?Medium
Show Answer

λ = 340/512 = 0.664 m. Number = 10/0.664 ≈ 15 wavelengths.

Explain why a flute (higher pitch) has a shorter wavelength than a tuba (lower pitch) at the same speed of sound.Medium
Show Answer

λ = v/f. Flute produces higher frequency → smaller λ. Tuba produces lower frequency → longer λ. Wavelength is inversely proportional to frequency at constant speed. This is why wind instrument tube length determines pitch — longer tube = longer wavelength = lower frequency.

An oscilloscope shows a wave with 2.5 complete cycles in 5 ms. Find frequency and period.Medium
Show Answer

Period = 5 ms / 2.5 = 2 ms = 0.002 s. Frequency = 1/0.002 = 500 Hz.

Compare wavelengths of 20 Hz (lowest human hearing) and 20,000 Hz in air (v=340 m/s).Medium
Show Answer

λ₂₀ = 340/20 = 17 m. λ₂₀₀₀₀ = 340/20000 = 0.017 m = 1.7 cm. The audible range spans 1.7 cm to 17 m — a 1000-fold range, explaining diffraction around different room sizes.

Set C Hard – Extended Wave Calculations

Light (λ=500 nm, v=3×10⁸ m/s) enters water (v=2.25×10⁸ m/s). Find frequency in air and wavelength in water.Hard
Show Answer

f = v/λ = 3×10⁸ / 500×10⁻⁹ = 6×10¹⁴ Hz (frequency unchanged on entering water). λ_water = v_water/f = 2.25×10⁸ / 6×10¹⁴ = 375 nm. Wavelength decreases in water; frequency stays constant.

A 1500 Hz sound in steel (v=5000 m/s) vs air (v=340 m/s). Find wavelength in each.Hard
Show Answer

λ_steel = 5000/1500 = 3.33 m. λ_air = 340/1500 = 0.227 m. Same frequency, different speeds → different wavelengths. Frequency is conserved when crossing media; wavelength and speed change together.

An oscilloscope screen is 10 cm wide representing 4 ms. A wave shows 2 complete cycles. Find f, T, and if v=340 m/s, find λ.Hard
Show Answer

T = 4 ms/2 = 2 ms = 0.002 s. f = 500 Hz. λ = v/f = 340/500 = 0.68 m.

Bats emit 80 kHz ultrasound. Find wavelength in air and minimum detectable object size.Hard
Show Answer

λ = 343/80000 = 0.00429 m ≈ 4.3 mm. Minimum object size ≈ λ ≈ 4.3 mm. Objects smaller than this won't reflect the wave effectively — below the bat's resolution limit.

Two waves: A (f=400 Hz, A=2 cm) and B (f=800 Hz, A=1 cm). Compare (a) wavelengths (v=320 m/s), (b) periods, (c) which is louder?Hard
Show Answer

(a) λ_A = 320/400 = 0.8 m; λ_B = 320/800 = 0.4 m. (b) T_A = 2.5 ms; T_B = 1.25 ms. (c) A is louder (amplitude 2 cm > 1 cm). A has lower pitch (lower f).

Room temperature drops from 20°C to 0°C (v: 343→331 m/s). Speaker emits 1000 Hz. How does wavelength change?Hard
Show Answer

λ_20 = 343/1000 = 0.343 m. λ_0 = 331/1000 = 0.331 m. Wavelength decreases by 1.2 cm (3.5%). Frequency fixed by speaker; speed decreases in colder air; wavelength decreases proportionally. Orchestras tune after warm-up — temperature affects tuning.

UV (λ=200 nm) vs IR (λ=10 μm). Both at 3×10⁸ m/s. Calculate frequencies and compare energy. (E=hf; h=6.63×10⁻³⁴ J·s)Hard
Show Answer

f_UV = 3×10⁸/200×10⁻⁹ = 1.5×10¹⁵ Hz. f_IR = 3×10⁸/10⁻⁵ = 3×10¹³ Hz. E_UV = 9.9×10⁻¹⁹ J. E_IR = 2×10⁻²⁰ J. UV energy is ~50× greater → explains why UV damages DNA/causes sunburn but IR only warms you.

A musician plays 261 Hz (middle C) 343 m away. How long for sound to arrive, and how many complete cycles are in the air between them?Hard
Show Answer

t = 343/343 = 1 s. λ = 343/261 = 1.315 m. Cycles in air = 343/1.315 = 261 cycles. Exactly equal to the frequency — 1 second of waves at 261 Hz = 261 complete cycles in transit.

Set A Easy – Speed of Sound Recall

What is the speed of sound in air at 20°C?Easy
Show Answer

343 m/s (approximately 340 m/s). About 1235 km/h or 1 km every 3 seconds.

What is the speed of sound in water and in steel?Easy
Show Answer

Water: ~1480 m/s. Steel: ~5000 m/s. Both faster than air due to particles being closer together and more strongly bonded.

In which state of matter does sound travel fastest? Why?Easy
Show Answer

Solids. Particles are closest together and most tightly bonded → compressions transmitted faster. Speed order: solids > liquids > gases.

Can sound travel through a vacuum?Easy
Show Answer

No. Sound is a mechanical wave requiring matter. A vacuum has no particles — no compressions can form. Speed of sound in vacuum = 0.

How does temperature affect the speed of sound in air?Easy
Show Answer

Speed increases with temperature. Warmer air → faster-moving molecules → compressions transmitted more quickly. Formula: v ≈ 331 + 0.6T m/s (T in °C). At 0°C: 331 m/s; at 20°C: 343 m/s.

A thunderclap is heard 3 s after a lightning flash. How far is the storm? (v=340 m/s)Easy
Show Answer

d = v × t = 340 × 3 = 1020 m ≈ 1 km away. (Light travels almost instantly; sound takes 3 s.)

Why do you see lightning before hearing thunder?Easy
Show Answer

Light travels at 3×10⁸ m/s — reaches you almost instantaneously. Sound travels at only 340 m/s — much slower. The gap between seeing and hearing is proportional to the storm's distance.

Predict the speed of sound at 35°C. (v ≈ 331 + 0.6T)Easy
Show Answer

v = 331 + 0.6 × 35 = 331 + 21 = 352 m/s. Common in India's hot summers — slightly faster than at 20°C.

Set B Medium – Speed Calculations

A gun is fired 850 m away. Sound arrives in 2.5 s. Calculate the speed of sound.Medium
Show Answer

v = d/t = 850/2.5 = 340 m/s.

A SONAR pulse returns in 2 s. Speed in water = 1500 m/s. Find depth.Medium
Show Answer

d = v×t/2 = 1500×2/2 = 1500 m. (Divide by 2 — pulse travels down AND back.)

Explain why whales can communicate over thousands of kilometres underwater.Medium
Show Answer

Sound travels ~1480 m/s in water — 4× faster than air. In the SOFAR channel (deep ocean ~1000 m), sound is refracted and trapped by temperature/pressure gradients, channelling energy horizontally with minimal spreading. Low-frequency whale songs (10–40 Hz) have very long wavelengths → little energy absorption → propagate 10,000+ km.

An echo is heard 0.4 s after a shout. How far is the reflecting wall? (v=340 m/s)Medium
Show Answer

d = v×t/2 = 340×0.4/2 = 68 m.

Sound travels at 343 m/s in air and 5120 m/s in steel rail. A person hears two thuds (rail first, then air) with a gap of 0.9 s. Find the distance.Medium
Show Answer

Δt = d/343 − d/5120 = d(0.00292 − 0.000195) = d × 0.002724 = 0.9. d = 0.9/0.002724 ≈ 330 m.

Concorde flew at Mach 2. What was its speed in m/s and km/h?Medium
Show Answer

Mach 2 = 2 × 340 = 680 m/s = 680 × 3600/1000 = 2448 km/h. At this speed the aircraft outruns its own sound, creating a continuous sonic boom heard on the ground.

A dolphin uses echolocation in water (v=1500 m/s). A click echo returns in 0.04 s. Find the prey distance and the wavelength if f=500 Hz.Medium
Show Answer

d = 1500×0.04/2 = 30 m. λ = v/f = 1500/500 = 3 m.

Design a school experiment to measure the speed of sound using an echo from a wall. Include measurement method, calculation, and two sources of error.Medium
Show Answer

Stand d=50–100 m from a wall. Clap boards together repeatedly. Adjust rate until each echo coincides with next clap. Time 10 clap intervals (t). v = 2d × 10/t. Errors: (1) human reaction time (~0.2 s) — use physics app/Audacity for precise timing; (2) wind affecting sound speed — experiment in still air; (3) imprecise distance — use laser measuring tape. Average multiple trials.

Set C Hard – Advanced Speed Problems

Seismic P-wave (v=6000 m/s) and S-wave (v=3500 m/s) are detected. S arrives 25 s after P. Find earthquake distance.Hard
Show Answer

Δt = d/3500 − d/6000 = d(1/3500 − 1/6000) = d × 1.19×10⁻⁴ = 25. d = 25/1.19×10⁻⁴ ≈ 210,000 m = 210 km.

Explain the sonic boom produced by a supersonic aircraft.Hard
Show Answer

As an aircraft exceeds sound speed, it outruns all the sound waves it emits. Sound waves pile up into a conical shock wave (Mach cone). Every point on the ground along the flight path receives this shock wave as the cone sweeps by — a continuous "boom" (pressure spike + expansion wave). Not a single explosion — sustained as long as aircraft remains supersonic.

A bat emits a 50 kHz pulse. Echo returns in 6 ms. Find: (a) distance to prey, (b) wavelength, (c) minimum detectable object size. (v=340 m/s)Hard
Show Answer

(a) d = 340×0.006/2 = 1.02 m. (b) λ = 340/50000 = 6.8 mm. (c) Minimum detectable size ≈ λ = 6.8 mm.

An ultrasound scan uses 5 MHz waves in tissue (v=1540 m/s). Find wavelength and minimum resolvable feature. Compare to clinical requirements.Hard
Show Answer

λ = 1540/5×10⁶ = 3.08×10⁻⁴ m = 0.308 mm. Minimum feature ≈ 0.3 mm. Clinical requirements: detect tumours ≥ 1 mm, arterial plaques ≥ 0.5 mm, fetal features ≥ 2 mm. 5 MHz is well suited for abdominal imaging. 10–15 MHz used for superficial structures (0.1 mm resolution but less penetration).

Two students measure the speed of sound in air: A gets 342 m/s at 20°C; B gets 330 m/s at 0°C. Are both results consistent with the formula v ≈ 331 + 0.6T? Evaluate.Hard
Show Answer

At 20°C: expected = 331+12 = 343 m/s. A got 342 — within 0.3%, excellent. At 0°C: expected = 331 m/s. B got 330 — within 0.3%, also excellent. Both results are fully consistent with the formula. Small discrepancies are within experimental error. The formula is validated.

Calculate the Mach number of spacecraft re-entry at 8000 m/s at 80 km altitude (v_sound ≈ 280 m/s at that height).Hard
Show Answer

Mach = 8000/280 ≈ 28.6. This is hypersonic re-entry — extreme air compression generates plasma (ionised air) from aerodynamic heating. Temperatures reach ~1650°C on heat shield. Radio blackout occurs as plasma blocks EM signals. Heat shield materials (carbon composite) must withstand this.

A seismic geologist finds a reflection from a rock layer at 0.3 s in sand (v=600 m/s). If limestone (v=3000 m/s) lies beneath, explain what further echoes might indicate.Hard
Show Answer

Sand layer depth = 600×0.3/2 = 90 m. At the sand-limestone boundary: acoustic impedance mismatch → partial reflection (arrives at 0.3 s) and partial transmission. Transmitted wave enters limestone, reflects from deeper boundary, returns. A second echo (at t > 0.3 s) would indicate another layer below limestone. Travel time difference reveals depth of that deeper layer. Each echo = one boundary. Complex multi-layer geology can be mapped by analysing all echo timings and relative intensities.

Explain how the SOFAR channel in the ocean works as a natural "waveguide" for sound, allowing communication across ocean basins.Hard
Show Answer

Sound speed in ocean depends on temperature (decreases with depth) and pressure (increases with depth). At ~1000 m depth these two effects balance — minimum sound speed. Snell's law: sound bends toward lower speed regions. Sound refracted downward from above and upward from below at the SOFAR axis — trapped in a horizontal "channel." Energy doesn't spread spherically (3D) but only horizontally (2D) → much slower energy loss with distance. Low-frequency sounds (10–100 Hz): λ = 10–100 m, very little absorption → propagate 20,000 km. Used by navies (SOSUS network), whales, and for tsunami monitoring.

Set A Easy – Echo and SONAR Recall

What is an echo?Easy
Show Answer

An echo is a reflected sound wave that reaches the listener at least 0.1 s after the original sound, so it is heard as a distinct repetition rather than merging with the original.

What is the minimum distance from a surface to hear a distinct echo? (v=340 m/s)Easy
Show Answer

Echo must arrive at least 0.1 s after original. d = v×t/2 = 340×0.1/2 = 17 m minimum distance.

What is SONAR and what does it stand for?Easy
Show Answer

SONAR = Sound Navigation And Ranging. Uses sound pulses reflected off underwater objects to detect and measure distance to submarines, fish, ocean floor, etc.

Write the formula used in SONAR to calculate depth.Easy
Show Answer

depth = (speed of sound in water × time for echo) / 2. d = v×t/2. Division by 2 because sound travels to object AND back.

What is reverberation?Easy
Show Answer

Reverberation is persistence of sound due to multiple reflections from walls, ceiling, and floor. The original sound overlaps with reflections — sounds blur together when echo delay is less than 0.1 s.

Why do concert halls use acoustic panels and soft furnishings?Easy
Show Answer

Acoustic panels and soft furnishings absorb sound waves, reducing reverberation. Prevents sound from blurring. Hard walls reflect too much → excessive reverberation → muddy sound. Optimum reverberation time: ~2 s for orchestral music, ~0.5 s for speech.

A SONAR pulse returns in 1.6 s (v_water = 1500 m/s). Find the depth of the sea floor.Easy
Show Answer

d = v×t/2 = 1500×1.6/2 = 1200 m.

Why does sound reflect better off hard smooth surfaces than soft rough ones?Easy
Show Answer

Hard smooth surfaces (walls, cliffs) reflect most incident sound as organised wave fronts. Soft rough surfaces (carpets, curtains, foam) absorb energy and scatter remaining energy in many directions — very little coherent reflection returned.

Set B Medium – Echo Calculations and Applications

A ship hears an echo from a submarine in 0.3 s (v=1530 m/s). Find submarine's depth.Medium
Show Answer

d = 1530×0.3/2 = 229.5 m.

A cliff face is 510 m away. How long after shouting will the echo return? (v=340 m/s)Medium
Show Answer

t = 2d/v = 2×510/340 = 3 s.

Explain how echocardiography uses ultrasound reflection to image the heart.Medium
Show Answer

Ultrasound pulses travel into the chest. At boundaries between different tissues (heart muscle, blood, valves), sound is partially reflected. Reflected pulses detected by transducer; time delay = distance (d = v×t/2). Thousands of pulses build a 2D/3D image. Non-ionising — safe for repeated imaging.

Why does a person in an empty hall experience more echo than in a furnished room?Medium
Show Answer

Empty hall: bare concrete/plaster walls highly reflective. Sound bounces multiple times with little absorption → long reverberation. Furnished room: carpets, furniture, curtains absorb sound → reflections die quickly → less reverberation, clearer direct sound.

Explain how bats use echoes to hunt prey in darkness. What properties of ultrasound help?Medium
Show Answer

Bats emit ultrasound pulses, time returning echo (d = v×t/2), and detect direction of echo arrival. Frequency shift (Doppler) reveals prey speed. Short wavelength (4–8 mm) allows detection of small insects. High frequency = short pulses = good temporal resolution for precise localisation.

How is ultrasound used to detect flaws in aircraft components? Why is this critical?Medium
Show Answer

Ultrasound pulses sent into metal. Internal cracks/voids reflect early echoes. Time-of-flight reveals crack depth; amplitude reveals size. NDT (non-destructive testing) — no need to cut. Aircraft experience cyclic stress → metal fatigue cracks develop. Regular ultrasonic inspection detects cracks before they reach critical size, preventing failures like Aloha Airlines 1988.

An ultrasound pulse travels through tissue (v=1540 m/s) and reflects from a kidney stone 6 cm deep. Find return time.Medium
Show Answer

t = 2d/v = 2×0.06/1540 = 7.79×10⁻⁵ s = 77.9 μs.

Why do geologists use seismic reflection to find oil deposits?Medium
Show Answer

Seismic waves penetrate rock layers. At boundaries between different density layers (rock, salt, oil reservoir), waves partially reflect. Reflected waves detected at surface. Time delay identifies layer depth; reflection pattern shows layered structure. Oil reservoirs have characteristic acoustic signatures different from surrounding rock.

Set C Hard – Advanced Reflection Problems

An ultrasound probe detects liver surface echo at 40 μs and tumour echo at 60 μs. (v=1540 m/s). Find depths and distance between them.Hard
Show Answer

Liver: d₁ = 1540×40×10⁻⁶/2 = 3.08 cm. Tumour: d₂ = 1540×60×10⁻⁶/2 = 4.62 cm. Tumour is 4.62 − 3.08 = 1.54 cm below the liver surface.

A ship detects two reflections: fish at 0.2 s and sea floor at 2 s. (v=1500 m/s). Find distances and explain why two echoes occur.Hard
Show Answer

Fish: d = 1500×0.2/2 = 150 m. Sea floor: d = 1500×2/2 = 1500 m. Two echoes because: partial reflection from fish shoal (some energy bounced back, some transmitted to sea floor which reflects the remaining energy). Each acoustic impedance boundary produces a partial echo.

Explain acoustic impedance and why ultrasound gel is essential for scanning.Hard
Show Answer

Acoustic impedance Z = density × sound speed. At a boundary, reflection coefficient R = ((Z₂−Z₁)/(Z₂+Z₁))². Air-skin: enormous impedance mismatch → >99% of ultrasound reflected before entering body. Gel (water-based) has similar Z to skin → eliminates air layer → maximum transmission into tissue. Without gel: virtually no ultrasound enters body — scan is impossible.

Explain acoustic shadowing and acoustic enhancement in ultrasound, with one clinical example of each.Hard
Show Answer

Acoustic shadowing: strongly reflecting/absorbing object (kidney stone, bone, gas) blocks ultrasound → dark shadow behind it. Clinical: shadow behind gallstone confirms stone diagnosis. Acoustic enhancement: anechoic fluid (cyst, bladder) transmits ultrasound better than surrounding tissue → echo behind cyst is brighter. Clinical: bright enhancement behind a cyst confirms fluid content (not solid mass).

Describe how a bat determines the size of prey from the intensity of returning echoes.Hard
Show Answer

Larger prey reflects more sound energy → stronger echo intensity (cross-sectional area ∝ reflected power). Bat's brain compares transmitted intensity to received intensity. Different object textures/shapes create characteristic echo spectral signatures. Hard smooth surfaces: strong coherent echo. Soft fuzzy insect bodies: weaker, more diffuse. Bats learn to distinguish prey species by echo quality, not just timing and intensity.

How does elastography (ultrasound stiffness mapping) detect liver fibrosis better than conventional ultrasound?Hard
Show Answer

Conventional ultrasound detects density differences. Fibrosis may have similar density to normal tissue → invisible. Elastography measures tissue stiffness (shear wave speed). A gentle push produces shear waves; stiffer tissue carries them faster. Fibrotic liver is 3–6× stiffer than normal. Stiffness map overlaid on ultrasound image detects early fibrosis before visible changes. Non-invasive alternative to liver biopsy in many cases.

Compare active and passive SONAR. Give one military and one marine biology application of each.Hard
Show Answer

Active SONAR: emits pulses, listens for echoes. Military: warship detects submarines. Marine bio: echo sounders count fish populations. Passive SONAR: only listens, no emission. Military: submarines listen for enemy engine sounds without revealing position. Marine bio: hydrophone arrays track whale song paths over vast ocean areas without disturbing animals.

Explain how contrast-enhanced ultrasound (CEUS) using microbubbles improves tumour detection over standard ultrasound.Hard
Show Answer

Microbubbles (1–10 μm, gas-filled lipid shells) injected IV remain in blood vessels (too large for capillaries). At resonant frequency, microbubbles vibrate non-linearly → produce harmonic frequencies (2f, 3f) not from background tissue → only blood gives strong signal. Maps real-time blood supply. Tumours have abnormal vascularity (neovascularisation) → detected even when invisible to standard ultrasound. No ionising radiation, no nephrotoxic contrast agents. Liver lesion characterisation accuracy ~90%.

Set A Easy – Doppler and Resonance Recall

Describe the Doppler effect.Easy
Show Answer

The Doppler effect is the change in observed frequency (pitch) of a wave when the source and observer are moving relative to each other. Approaching source = higher pitch; receding source = lower pitch.

Why does an ambulance siren sound higher pitched as it approaches and lower as it drives away?Easy
Show Answer

Approaching: sound waves compressed in front → shorter wavelength → more waves per second → higher frequency (higher pitch). Receding: waves stretched behind → fewer waves per second → lower frequency (lower pitch).

What is the natural (resonant) frequency of an object?Easy
Show Answer

The frequency at which an object naturally vibrates when disturbed. Every object has one or more resonant frequencies determined by its physical properties (size, shape, material, tension).

Define resonance.Easy
Show Answer

Resonance occurs when a driving frequency matches the natural frequency of an object. Energy is efficiently transferred, causing maximum amplitude vibration.

Give one example of resonance in everyday life.Easy
Show Answer

Examples: Opera singer shattering a glass at its natural frequency. Microwave oven heating water molecules. Tacoma Narrows bridge collapse (wind resonance). Tuning a radio (resonance with broadcast frequency).

How does a police speed gun use the Doppler effect?Easy
Show Answer

Gun emits microwave/radar pulses at known frequency. Pulses reflect off moving vehicle. Returned signal is Doppler-shifted. Speed gun measures frequency difference, calculates vehicle speed using the Doppler formula.

Does the Doppler effect apply to light? What evidence do we have?Easy
Show Answer

Yes. Light from receding galaxies is red-shifted (longer wavelength, lower frequency). Approaching sources are blue-shifted. Hubble (1929) used galactic redshifts as evidence for the expanding universe. Stars' Doppler shifts reveal orbital speeds and enable detection of exoplanets.

Why must pilots avoid flying at the natural resonant frequency of an aircraft's wings?Easy
Show Answer

At resonant frequency, vibration amplitude builds up rapidly. This can cause catastrophic structural failure — the wing exceeds material strength and breaks. Engine vibrations or aerodynamic turbulence matching wing natural frequency must be avoided in aircraft design.

Set B Medium – Doppler Calculations

A train whistle emits 400 Hz. Train moves toward observer at 34 m/s (v_sound=340 m/s). Find observed frequency.Medium
Show Answer

f_obs = f × v/(v − v_source) = 400 × 340/(340−34) = 400 × 340/306 ≈ 444 Hz. Observer hears higher pitch (444 Hz > 400 Hz).

Same train moves away at 34 m/s. Find observed frequency.Medium
Show Answer

f_obs = 400 × 340/(340+34) = 400 × 340/374 ≈ 363.6 Hz. Lower pitch as train recedes.

Explain how Doppler ultrasound measures blood flow velocity in the heart.Medium
Show Answer

Probe emits known frequency. Pulses reflect off red blood cells — moving cells Doppler-shift the reflected frequency. Moving toward probe: higher f. Moving away: lower f. Δf = (2 × f × v_blood × cosθ) / v_sound. From Δf, v_blood is calculated. Detects stenoses, valve problems, fetal circulation non-invasively.

Explain resonance in a wind instrument when a player blows across the opening.Medium
Show Answer

Blowing creates air turbulence exciting many frequencies. Only the frequency matching the tube's natural frequency (determined by tube length) is amplified by resonance — standing wave forms. Other frequencies cancel. Player changes finger positions → different effective tube length → different natural frequencies → different notes.

How does Doppler radar detect the speed and direction of approaching storms?Medium
Show Answer

Radar emits microwave pulses. Pulses reflect off water droplets. Moving storm cells Doppler-shift returned signals. Blueshift = rain moving toward radar; redshift = moving away. Magnitude of shift = velocity component. By scanning all directions, a 3D velocity map is built. Reveals rotation (tornadoes), wind shear, and approach speed — giving 15–30 min earlier tornado warnings.

Explain why suspension bridges can collapse due to resonance and what engineers do to prevent it.Medium
Show Answer

Tacoma Narrows bridge (1940): steady wind created vortex shedding at bridge's natural frequency (~0.2 Hz). Resonance built up oscillations → catastrophic failure. Prevention: stiffen deck (raise natural frequency), add aerodynamic fairings, install tuned mass dampers and fluid viscous dampers, use open-grid deck, and design natural frequency away from common wind frequencies.

How do astronomers use the Doppler effect to discover exoplanets?Medium
Show Answer

A planet orbiting a star makes the star wobble slightly. When star wobbles toward Earth, starlight is blue-shifted; wobbling away = red-shifted. By measuring tiny Doppler shifts in stellar spectral lines, astronomers calculate wobble velocity and period. From Kepler's laws, they infer the planet's mass and orbital period — without directly seeing the planet. Over 1000+ exoplanets discovered this way.

Calculate observed frequency when observer (stationary) hears source (700 Hz) at 50 m/s away and toward. (v=350 m/s). Comment on the change.Medium
Show Answer

Away: f = 700×350/(350+50) = 612.5 Hz. Toward: f = 700×350/(350−50) = 816.7 Hz. Change when passing = 816.7 − 612.5 = 204.2 Hz — a very noticeable pitch drop as the vehicle passes.

Set C Hard – Extended Doppler and Resonance

A star moves away at 6000 km/s. Hydrogen alpha line = 656 nm. (c=3×10⁵ km/s). Find observed wavelength.Hard
Show Answer

Δλ/λ = v/c = 6000/300000 = 0.02. Δλ = 0.02×656 = 13.12 nm. Observed λ = 656 + 13.12 = 669.12 nm (redshifted toward longer wavelength).

Doppler ultrasound shows blood flowing at 0.5 m/s toward probe. f=5 MHz (v_sound=1540 m/s). Find frequency shift.Hard
Show Answer

Δf = 2×f×v_blood/v_sound = 2×5×10⁶×0.5/1540 ≈ 3247 Hz ≈ 3.25 kHz. This is in the audible range — why Doppler blood flow sounds are audible in clinical settings.

A galaxy 400 Mpc away recedes (H₀=70 km/s/Mpc). Find redshift z for a 500 nm emission line.Hard
Show Answer

v = 70×400 = 28,000 km/s. z = v/c = 28,000/300,000 = 0.0933. Δλ = 0.0933×500 = 46.7 nm. Observed λ = 546.7 nm (shifted from blue-green toward yellow). Evidence for Hubble's expanding universe.

A bat and moth fly toward each other at 10 m/s each. Bat emits 80 kHz. (v=340 m/s). Find the frequency of echo heard by bat.Hard
Show Answer

Step 1 — moth receives bat's sound (both approaching): f_moth = 80000×(340+10)/(340−10) = 80000×350/330 = 84,848 Hz. Step 2 — bat receives echo from moth (still approaching): f_bat = 84848×(340+10)/(340−10) = 84848×350/330 ≈ 89,987 Hz ≈ 90 kHz. Double Doppler shift — bat detects nearly 10 kHz increase, revealing prey approach velocity.

Explain how MRI uses magnetic resonance of hydrogen nuclei to image soft tissue without ionising radiation.Hard
Show Answer

Strong magnetic field aligns hydrogen proton spins. RF pulse at Larmor frequency (resonance frequency in that field) flips proton spins. When RF pulse stops, protons relax back, emitting RF energy. Relaxation rate (T1/T2) differs between tissue types (fat, muscle, tumour). Computer processes signal intensities and timing to build 3D image distinguishing soft tissue types. No ionising radiation — uses radio waves and magnets. Superior to X-ray for soft tissue.

Explain the London Millennium Bridge resonance incident (2000) and how it was corrected.Hard
Show Answer

Bridge lateral natural frequency ~1.0 Hz. Walking produces lateral forces at ~0.9 Hz. When bridge moved, pedestrians adjusted their gait to match — positive feedback. Amplitude grew. Corrected by installing 37 fluid viscous dampers and 52 tuned mass dampers (pendulum masses vibrating 180° out of phase, absorbing energy). Reopened 2002 without wobble — £5 million solution. Lesson: dynamic loading analysis must include human-structure interaction.

A wine glass resonates at 440 Hz. A singer 3 m away sustains 440 Hz. Discuss whether the glass will shatter and what factors determine this.Hard
Show Answer

For shattering: resonance must build amplitude until stress exceeds glass's elastic limit (~50 MPa for soda-lime glass). Factors: (1) Quality factor Q of glass (how much energy retained per cycle). (2) Singer's sustained amplitude and precision (must be exact pitch). (3) Glass type — thin crystal (high Q) shatters much more easily than thick soda-lime. (4) Duration — needs 3–5 s sustained note. Possible with thin crystal wine glass close to powerful sustained voice. Typical conditions: unlikely without amplification. Confirmed possible in laboratory conditions with amplified voice.

Cosmological redshift vs. Doppler redshift: are they the same phenomenon? Explain the distinction.Hard
Show Answer

Doppler redshift: object moving through space away from observer — wavelength shifted by motion. Cosmological redshift: space itself is expanding — light waves stretched by the expansion of space as they travel. For nearby galaxies (z < 0.1), both give similar results. For distant galaxies (z > 0.1), cosmological redshift is dominant and the formula z = v/c breaks down. Full general relativistic treatment needed. Key distinction: Doppler is motion through space; cosmological is expansion of space itself. Both produce redshifted light but by different mechanisms.

Set A Easy – Ultrasound Basics

What is ultrasound? What frequency range?Easy
Show Answer

Ultrasound is sound with frequency above the upper limit of human hearing: f > 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). Medical ultrasound typically uses 1–20 MHz.

What is infrasound?Easy
Show Answer

Infrasound: frequencies below 20 Hz. Produced by earthquakes, large animals (elephants, whales), volcanic eruptions. Travels very long distances with little absorption.

Name three medical applications of ultrasound.Easy
Show Answer

1. Fetal scanning (prenatal imaging). 2. Kidney/gallstone detection and lithotripsy. 3. Echocardiography (heart imaging). 4. Doppler blood flow measurement. 5. Guided biopsy.

Why is ultrasound preferred over X-rays for imaging a fetus?Easy
Show Answer

Ultrasound does not use ionising radiation. X-rays can damage DNA — risky for rapidly dividing fetal cells. Ultrasound (pressure waves) has no ionising radiation, considered safe for repeated use during pregnancy.

What is lithotripsy?Easy
Show Answer

Lithotripsy uses focused high-energy ultrasound (shock waves) to shatter kidney or gallstones into small fragments that pass naturally. Non-invasive — no surgery required.

What is a piezoelectric transducer and what role does it play in ultrasound?Easy
Show Answer

A piezoelectric material converts electrical signals to mechanical vibrations (transmitting ultrasound) and vibrations back to electrical signals (receiving echoes). The same transducer acts as both emitter and detector.

Name two non-medical industrial applications of ultrasound.Easy
Show Answer

1. Ultrasonic cleaning (removing dirt from spectacles, jewellery by cavitation). 2. Non-destructive testing (detecting cracks in metal, concrete). 3. SONAR (depth-sounding). 4. Ultrasonic welding (joining plastics).

Why is gel applied between the ultrasound probe and skin?Easy
Show Answer

Air has very different acoustic impedance from skin — >99% of ultrasound would be reflected at an air-skin boundary. Water-based gel matches skin's acoustic impedance, eliminating the air layer and allowing maximum ultrasound transmission into the body.

Set B Medium – Ultrasound Applications

Explain step by step how a fetal ultrasound scan produces an image.Medium
Show Answer

1. Probe emits ultrasound pulse. 2. Pulse travels through gel and tissue. 3. At each tissue boundary (different acoustic impedance), partial echo reflected. 4. Probe receives echo; time delay measured: d = v×t/2. 5. Echo intensity = boundary density difference. 6. Computer repeats for thousands of scan lines. 7. 2D/3D image built from all echo data.

A 5 MHz probe is used. (v_tissue=1540 m/s). Calculate (a) wavelength, (b) minimum visible feature size.Medium
Show Answer

(a) λ = 1540/5×10⁶ = 3.08×10⁻⁴ m ≈ 0.3 mm. (b) Minimum feature ≈ λ = 0.3 mm.

Why does higher frequency ultrasound improve image resolution but reduce penetration depth?Medium
Show Answer

Higher f → shorter λ → smaller minimum detectable feature → better resolution. BUT higher frequency waves are absorbed/scattered more in tissue. Penetration depth decreases. Trade-off: 10–15 MHz for shallow structures (skin, thyroid); 2–5 MHz for deep structures (abdomen, heart).

How does HIFU (High Intensity Focused Ultrasound) destroy tumours without surgery?Medium
Show Answer

Multiple low-intensity beams from different angles converge on tumour. At focus: extremely high energy density → tissue heats to 65–85°C → thermal ablation (coagulative necrosis). Adjacent tissue: each beam passes through without significant heating. No incision, no ionising radiation, targeted destruction.

Explain ultrasonic cleaning of jewellery. What is cavitation?Medium
Show Answer

Jewellery in liquid solvent in ultrasonic bath (20–40 kHz). Rapid pressure fluctuations form and collapse microscopic bubbles (cavitation). Bubble collapse produces local temperatures ~5000 K and pressure spikes → micro-jets of liquid → dislodge dirt and grease from intricate surfaces. Reaches where brushes cannot.

Compare ultrasound and X-ray imaging in terms of radiation type, safety, tissue types imaged, and cost.Medium
Show Answer

Ultrasound: mechanical waves, no ionising radiation, safe for repeated use, excellent for soft tissue/fluid/fetus, real-time, portable, low cost (~₹500–2000). X-ray: ionising EM radiation, limited cumulative dose, excellent for bone/chest, static image, moderate cost (~₹500–5000). MRI: no radiation, best soft tissue, expensive (~₹5000–20000). Ultrasound is best for abdomen, pregnancy, guided procedures.

A fetal ultrasound scan echo returns in 26 μs (v=1540 m/s). How deep is the fetus?Medium
Show Answer

d = v×t/2 = 1540×26×10⁻⁶/2 = 0.02 m = 2 cm.

Why is ultrasound the most impactful imaging modality for expanding healthcare in rural India?Medium
Show Answer

Advantages: portable (solar-powered tablets), cheap (₹2–5 lakh vs MRI ₹80–200 lakh), no specialist required (AI-guided scanning), no ionising radiation (safe everywhere), real-time imaging. Key programmes: PMSSY, POCUS training for ASHA workers, Project ECHO teleconsultation. Critical for maternal health — remote fetal scanning reduces maternal mortality. Design a complete ultrasound kidney stone detection protocol. Include frequency choice, expected echo timing, and result interpretation. (v_tissue=1540 m/s)Hard

A 3 MHz probe is used vs a 7 MHz probe for liver scanning. The liver is at 8 cm depth. Which probe is more appropriate and why?Hard

Over 8 cm (16 cm round trip) = 56 dB total loss — signal too weak to detect reliably. 3 MHz: attenuation = 1.5 dB/cm. Over 16 cm round trip = 24 dB loss — manageable. Better signal at depth. Explain how 3D/4D ultrasound creates volume images and what "4D" means clinically.Hard
Show Answer

Parent bonding from fetal facial images.

Evaluate: "Ultrasound is completely safe with zero risks." Critically assess this statement.Hard
Show Answer

Largely true but not completely. Established risks: 1. Thermal: prolonged high-intensity scanning raises tissue temperature. WHO guidelines: limit rise to 1°C. 2. Mechanical: cavitation (bubble collapse) possible at high intensities in gas-containing tissue (lung, intestine). 3. HIFU deliberately destructive (not diagnostic US). Safety framework: ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable). Thermal index (TI) and mechanical index (MI) displayed on screens. No confirmed adverse fetal outcomes in 50+ years of diagnostic use. Contrast agents: rare anaphylaxis risk (~1:10,000). Conclusion: diagnostic ultrasound is among the safest medical imaging modalities — negligible risk compared to clinical benefit. Not "zero risk" but "acceptably safe."

Explain acoustic metamaterials and their potential applications in sound control.Hard

Acoustic metamaterials have sub-wavelength structured units engineered to have negative effective density or bulk modulus — properties not found in natural materials. These bend sound waves around an object (cloaking), redirect them, or completely absorb them. Applications: submarine stealth (redirect enemy SONAR), noise barriers that don't block airflow (concert halls, HVAC), protect sensitive equipment from therapeutic ultrasound, improve ultrasound imaging focus, reduce noise in aircraft cabins. Explain elastography (stiffness mapping) and its advantage over conventional ultrasound in detecting liver disease.Hard
