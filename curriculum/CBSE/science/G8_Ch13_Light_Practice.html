Set A — Recall & Identify
What is the angle of reflection if the angle of incidence is 35°?
Easy
Answer

35°

The angle of incidence always equals the angle of reflection — this is the first law of reflection.
What is meant by the 'normal' in reflection?
Easy
Answer

An imaginary line perpendicular to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence

The normal is the reference line from which angles of incidence and reflection are measured.
A ray of light falls perpendicularly on a mirror. What happens?
Easy
Answer

It reflects back along the same path

When a ray hits a mirror at 0° angle of incidence, the reflected ray retraces its path.
What type of reflection occurs on a rough surface?
Easy
Answer

Diffuse (irregular) reflection

Rough surfaces scatter light in many directions; this is why non-shiny objects are visible from all angles.
State the second law of reflection.
Easy
Answer

The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal all lie in the same plane

This law ensures reflection is a two-dimensional event in a defined plane.
Name the type of reflection that forms clear images.
Easy
Answer

Regular (specular) reflection

Smooth surfaces like mirrors produce regular reflection, which forms sharp images.
If a mirror is rotated by 10°, by how much does the reflected ray rotate?
Medium
Answer

20°

When the mirror rotates by θ, the normal rotates by θ, causing the reflected ray to rotate by 2θ.
Why can you see your face in a polished metal spoon but not in a painted wall?
Medium
Answer

The spoon has a smooth surface causing regular reflection; the wall is rough causing diffuse reflection

Smoothness of surface determines whether reflection is regular or diffuse.
A student says light reflects from the moon. Is the moon a source of light? Explain.
Hard
Answer

No — the moon reflects sunlight (diffuse reflection from its rough surface). It is not a self-luminous source.

The moon has no internal energy source. It reflects about 12% of the sunlight that hits it.
Design an experiment to verify the laws of reflection using a plane mirror, paper, and pins.
Hard
Answer

Place mirror on line, place incident pin, mark reflected pin position, measure angles with protractor — show i = r and coplanarity

MYP Criterion B: scientific investigation design using basic optics equipment.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A light ray makes an angle of 50° with the mirror surface. What is the angle of reflection?
Medium
Answer

40° (angle with normal = 90°−50° = 40°)

Angles are always measured from the normal, not the mirror surface.
Two mirrors are placed at 90° to each other. How many images of a candle placed between them are formed?
Medium
Answer

3 images

Formula: number of images = (360/angle) − 1 = (360/90) − 1 = 3.
Explain why the reflection of trees in a still lake looks inverted.
Medium
Answer

Water acts as a plane mirror; the image of objects above it is laterally and vertically inverted relative to observer

Reflection from a flat water surface follows the same laws as a plane mirror.
A periscope uses two mirrors at 45°. Trace the path of a light ray through the periscope.
Hard
Answer

Ray enters horizontally → reflects 90° downward → reflects 90° horizontally out to eye

Each mirror turns the ray 90°; the combination allows viewing over obstacles.
Why is the writing 'AMBULANCE' written in reverse on ambulance fronts?
Hard
Answer

To appear correctly in the rear-view mirror of vehicles ahead (mirror image corrects the reversal)

Mirrors cause lateral inversion — left-right reversal. Reversing the original text pre-corrects this.
A ray hits a plane mirror at 60° to the surface. Find the angle between incident and reflected rays.
Medium
Answer

60° (angle of incidence = 30°, angle of reflection = 30°, angle between them = 60°)

The angle between incident and reflected rays = 2 × angle of incidence.
If two parallel mirrors face each other, how many images are theoretically formed?
Hard
Answer

Infinite (in practice, limited by absorption)

Each reflection creates a new image that acts as object for the opposite mirror, repeating infinitely.
A flashlight beam hits a mirror at 45°. Describe the change in direction of the beam.
Medium
Answer

The beam is turned 90° from its original direction

Angle of incidence = 45° = angle of reflection; the total turning angle = 2 × 45° = 90°.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Why do we use mirrors in solar cookers? What design principle maximises heat collection?
Hard
Answer

Curved/concave mirrors concentrate parallel sun rays to a focal point; angling mirrors toward the sun maximises energy focus

MYP Criterion D: real-world application of reflection in sustainable energy technology.
Investigate: A lighthouse uses large mirrors to project light. Why are concave mirrors used instead of plane mirrors?
Hard
Answer

Concave mirrors can converge rays from a lamp at the focus into a parallel beam, projecting light much farther

Plane mirrors only redirect; concave mirrors with a lamp at focus produce a nearly parallel beam.
Reflect on the environmental impact: Replacing incandescent lighthouse bulbs with LEDs + mirrors. Analyse benefits.
Hard
Answer

Lower energy use, longer lifespan, same optical performance with mirrors — reduces carbon footprint and maintenance costs

MYP Criterion D: evaluating technological choices for environmental sustainability.

Set A — Recall & Identify
List the characteristics of an image formed by a plane mirror.
Easy
Answer

Virtual, erect, same size as object, laterally inverted, at same distance behind mirror as object is in front

These five properties fully describe plane mirror images in any exam.
What does 'virtual image' mean?
Easy
Answer

An image that cannot be formed on a screen; rays only appear to diverge from that point

Virtual images are formed by extending reflected rays backward — they don't actually meet.
A boy stands 3 m from a plane mirror. How far is the image from the mirror?
Easy
Answer

3 m behind the mirror

Image distance behind mirror = object distance in front of mirror for plane mirrors.
What is lateral inversion?
Easy
Answer

Left-right reversal of an image in a mirror (left side of object appears as right side of image)

Lateral inversion occurs because the mirror flips the direction perpendicular to its surface.
Why can't you project a plane mirror image onto a screen?
Easy
Answer

Because it is virtual — the reflected rays diverge and only appear to come from behind the mirror

Only real images (formed by actual convergence of rays) can be projected on screens.
Is the image in a plane mirror magnified or diminished?
Easy
Answer

Neither — it is the same size as the object (magnification = 1)

Plane mirrors neither converge nor diverge reflected rays, so image size = object size.
How does your image change as you walk toward a plane mirror?
Medium
Answer

The image also moves toward you at the same speed; the distance between you and your image decreases at twice your speed

Both you and your image travel at equal rates, so the gap closes at 2× your speed.
A full-length mirror is required to see your entire body. What is the minimum mirror height needed?
Medium
Answer

Half your height

Rays from head reach eye after reflecting at a point halfway between head and eye; rays from feet similarly.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A clock shows 10:25 in a mirror. What is the actual time?
Medium
Answer

1:35

Mirror reverses left-right; 10:25 reflected = 1:35 (think of clock face reversed horizontally).
Draw ray diagrams to locate the image of a point object placed 5 cm in front of a plane mirror.
Hard
Answer

Draw two rays from object: one perpendicular (reflects back), one at angle (reflects with equal angle); extend behind mirror — image is 5 cm behind

Ray diagram construction tests understanding of laws of reflection applied to image formation.
Explain why ambulances write their name reversed on the hood.
Medium
Answer

So it appears correctly in the rear-view mirror of cars ahead, which laterally inverts the image back to normal

Mirrors cause left-right reversal; pre-reversing the text compensates for this.
If you raise your right hand, which hand does your mirror image raise? Explain.
Medium
Answer

The image raises its left hand — because lateral inversion swaps left and right

The mirror image is a reflection, not a rotation, so left and right are exchanged.
A man 6 ft tall stands 10 ft from a plane mirror. Where exactly is his image, and what is its size?
Hard
Answer

Image is 10 ft behind the mirror (20 ft from the man), 6 ft tall, virtual, erect, laterally inverted

All plane mirror image properties applied together.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: You want to secretly observe people entering a room. Design a periscope system using plane mirrors.
Hard
Answer

Two mirrors at 45° angles: top mirror reflects incoming light downward through a tube; bottom mirror reflects it horizontally to the eye

MYP Criterion B: applying reflection laws in a functional device design.
Evaluate: If plane mirrors only produce virtual images, how are they still useful in practical applications?
Hard
Answer

Virtual images are directly visible (not needing screens); plane mirrors provide accurate reflections for grooming, navigation, safety (road mirrors), decoration

MYP Criterion D: assessing utility of virtual images in real life despite inability to project them.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a concave mirror?
Easy
Answer

A mirror with a reflecting surface that curves inward (like the inside of a spoon)

Concave mirrors converge parallel rays to a focal point in front of the mirror.
Define the principal focus of a concave mirror.
Easy
Answer

The point on the principal axis where parallel rays converge after reflection

The focal point is where the mirror concentrates incoming parallel light.
What is the relationship between radius of curvature (R) and focal length (f)?
Easy
Answer

f = R/2

The focal length is half the radius of curvature for any spherical mirror.
Name the sign convention used for spherical mirrors.
Easy
Answer

New Cartesian Sign Convention: incident light travels left to right; distances measured from pole; rightward/upward positive

Consistent sign convention prevents errors when applying the mirror formula.
What type of image does a concave mirror form when object is at focus?
Easy
Answer

No image formed (rays reflected parallel — image at infinity)

Rays from focus reflect as a parallel beam; they never converge to form an image.
Where must an object be placed for a concave mirror to form a real, inverted, same-sized image?
Easy
Answer

At the centre of curvature (C), i.e., at distance 2f

At C, the image is also at C — real, inverted, and equal in size to the object.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
An object is placed 30 cm from a concave mirror of focal length 15 cm. Find the image distance.
Medium
Answer

v = −30 cm (image at 30 cm in front, real and inverted)

1/v + 1/u = 1/f → 1/v + 1/(−30) = 1/(−15) → 1/v = −1/15 + 1/30 = −1/30 → v = −30 cm.
A concave mirror has focal length 20 cm. Object is at 10 cm. Describe the image.
Medium
Answer

Virtual, erect, magnified, behind mirror

Object is between F and pole; concave mirror acts as magnifying glass in this position.
Why are concave mirrors used in headlights of vehicles?
Medium
Answer

Light source placed at focus produces a parallel beam; concave mirror collimates the light for long-range illumination

The mirror converts diverging rays from bulb into parallel rays, projecting them far ahead.
A dentist uses a concave mirror. Where does the dentist place the tooth relative to the mirror?
Medium
Answer

Between the pole and focus (within focal length)

This produces a magnified, erect, virtual image — ideal for examining teeth.
Calculate magnification if object is 15 cm from concave mirror (f = 10 cm).
Hard
Answer

m = −v/u; find v first: 1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/(−10) − 1/(−15) = −3/30 + 2/30 = −1/30 → v = −30; m = −(−30)/(−15) = −2 (real, inverted, magnified ×2)

Apply mirror formula, then m = −v/u with signs.
A solar cooker uses a concave mirror of radius 1.4 m. Where should food be placed?
Hard
Answer

At the focal point: f = R/2 = 0.7 m from the mirror

Parallel sun rays converge at focus; maximum heat concentration occurs at f.
Compare images formed at object distance greater than, equal to, and less than focal length for a concave mirror.
Hard
Answer

>f: real, inverted (size varies by position); =f: no image (at infinity);

Three distinct cases cover the full behaviour of concave mirrors.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: Design an experiment to determine the focal length of a concave mirror using a distant object.
Hard
Answer

Aim concave mirror at a distant tree/window; adjust screen distance until sharp image forms; that distance ≈ focal length

Distant objects send essentially parallel rays; image forms at focus.
Evaluate the social and environmental benefit of solar cookers using concave mirrors in rural areas.
Hard
Answer

Reduces firewood use (deforestation), eliminates smoke (health), zero fuel cost — but depends on sunny weather and needs proper orientation

MYP Criterion D: technology assessment balancing benefits and limitations.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a convex mirror?
Easy
Answer

A mirror with an outward-curving reflective surface (like the back of a spoon)

Convex mirrors diverge reflected rays, so their focus and centre are behind the mirror (virtual).
What type of image does a convex mirror always form?
Easy
Answer

Virtual, erect, and diminished

Convex mirrors always diverge reflected rays; the image is behind the mirror and smaller than object.
Where is the focus of a convex mirror?
Easy
Answer

Behind the mirror (virtual focus — rays appear to diverge from this point)

The focus is virtual because reflected rays diverge and only appear to meet behind the mirror.
State one everyday use of convex mirrors.
Easy
Answer

Rear-view mirrors in vehicles (provides wider field of view)

Convex mirrors give a broader view of traffic, though objects appear smaller than actual.
What happens to the focal length sign of a convex mirror under the New Cartesian convention?
Easy
Answer

Positive (+f), because the focus is behind the mirror (in the direction of incident light)

By convention, distances behind the mirror (same side as incident light) are positive for convex mirrors.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Why are convex mirrors used as rear-view mirrors in vehicles despite producing smaller images?
Medium
Answer

They provide a wider field of view, allowing the driver to see more of the road behind

The trade-off: smaller image but much wider coverage — safety beats size accuracy.
A convex mirror has f = 15 cm. Object at 30 cm. Find image position.
Medium
Answer

v = +10 cm (behind mirror); image is virtual, erect, diminished

1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/15 − 1/(−30) = 1/15 + 1/30 = 3/30 = 1/10 → v = +10 cm.
Explain why convex mirrors are used at blind road corners.
Medium
Answer

Wide field of view allows drivers to see oncoming vehicles around the bend without being at the corner

Virtual, diminished images still provide positional awareness over a large area.
Compare: A concave mirror and convex mirror, each with f = 20 cm. Object at 40 cm. Find both image distances.
Hard
Answer

Concave: v = −40 cm (real, same size, in front); Convex: v = +13.3 cm (virtual, behind mirror, diminished)

Concave: 1/v + 1/(−40) = 1/(−20) → v = −40. Convex: 1/v + 1/(−40) = 1/20 → v ≈ +13.3.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: A driver prefers a flat mirror as a rear-view mirror for 'true size' images. Assess the trade-off vs convex mirror.
Hard
Answer

Flat mirror: accurate size but narrow field of view; Convex: wider coverage but underestimates distance — convex is safer for overall awareness

MYP Criterion D: evaluating technology trade-offs in road safety context.

Set A — Recall & Identify
Define refraction of light.
Easy
Answer

The bending of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another due to change in speed

Refraction occurs because light travels at different speeds in different media.
What happens to light when it passes from air to glass?
Easy
Answer

It slows down and bends toward the normal (toward the denser medium)

Denser media slow light; slowing causes the ray to bend toward the normal.
State Snell's law.
Easy
Answer

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ (ratio of sines of angles equals ratio of refractive indices)

Snell's law quantitatively describes refraction at any interface.
What is the refractive index of a medium?
Easy
Answer

n = speed of light in vacuum / speed of light in medium (n = c/v)

Higher refractive index = slower light = more bending toward normal.
Why does a pencil partially submerged in water appear bent?
Easy
Answer

Light from the submerged part refracts as it exits water into air, bending away from normal — the eye traces rays back in straight lines and perceives the pencil as bent

Refraction at the water-air interface causes the apparent displacement of the image.
A ray passes from glass to air. Which direction does it bend?
Easy
Answer

Away from the normal (bends outward, toward the surface)

Moving from denser to rarer medium, light speeds up and bends away from normal.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Light travels from air (n=1) into glass (n=1.5). Angle of incidence = 30°. Find angle of refraction.
Medium
Answer

sin r = sin 30° / 1.5 = 0.5/1.5 = 0.333 → r ≈ 19.5°

Apply Snell's law: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r.
Why does a swimming pool appear shallower than it is?
Medium
Answer

Light from the bottom refracts away from normal at water-air interface; observer's eye traces rays straight back, seeing the bottom at a higher (apparent) position

Apparent depth = real depth / refractive index of water ≈ real depth / 1.33.
A fish inside a pond looks up at a 90° cone of the outside world. Explain why.
Hard
Answer

Due to total internal reflection, light entering from outside is confined to a 48.8° half-angle cone (critical angle of water); outside the cone, fish sees only reflections from below

The 'Snell's window' effect: fish sees the entire outside world compressed into a circular window.
Calculate the apparent depth of a 2 m deep pool (n_water = 1.33).
Medium
Answer

Apparent depth = 2/1.33 ≈ 1.5 m

The pool appears 0.5 m shallower than actual — refraction raises the apparent bottom.
A ray enters a rectangular glass block and exits from the opposite side. Describe the net effect on its direction.
Medium
Answer

The ray exits parallel to the original direction but laterally displaced

The two refractions (entering and exiting) cancel in angle but leave a sideways shift.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: Design an experiment to measure the refractive index of a glass slab using pins.
Hard
Answer

Place pins on incident side, trace refracted ray inside glass using pins on exit side; measure i and r; n = sin i / sin r

MYP Criterion B: experimental design using Snell's law.
Evaluate: How does atmospheric refraction cause the sun to appear above the horizon after it has set?
Hard
Answer

Earth's atmosphere acts like a variable-density medium; light from sun bends gradually toward the ground as it passes through denser lower layers, reaching our eyes even when the sun is geometrically below horizon

This extends daylight by about 2 minutes; also causes twinkling of stars.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a convex lens?
Easy
Answer

A lens that is thicker at the center than at the edges; it converges parallel rays to a focal point

Also called a converging lens; used in magnifying glasses, cameras, human eyes.
Define the optical centre of a lens.
Easy
Answer

The central point of the lens through which rays pass without bending

Rays directed at the optical centre travel straight through without refraction.
What type of image does a convex lens form when the object is beyond 2f?
Easy
Answer

Real, inverted, diminished

Beyond 2f, the image forms between f and 2f on the other side — smaller than object.
What is the power of a lens?
Easy
Answer

P = 1/f (in dioptres, D), where f is focal length in metres

Positive power = converging lens; higher power = shorter focal length = stronger lens.
A convex lens has focal length 25 cm. Calculate its power.
Easy
Answer

P = 1/0.25 = +4 D

Convert cm to metres first: 25 cm = 0.25 m.
Where should an object be placed for a convex lens to produce a magnified image?
Easy
Answer

Between the optical centre and the principal focus (within focal length)

Within f, the convex lens acts as a magnifying glass producing virtual, erect, enlarged image.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Object is 30 cm from convex lens (f = 20 cm). Find image position and nature.
Medium
Answer

v = +60 cm; real, inverted, magnified (m = −2)

Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f → 1/v − 1/(−30) = 1/20 → 1/v = 1/20 − 1/30 = 1/60 → v = 60 cm.
Explain how a simple magnifying glass works.
Medium
Answer

Object placed inside focal length; convex lens forms virtual, erect, magnified image that eye can see directly

The eye perceives the virtual image as if it were at the near point — making objects appear larger.
A camera uses a convex lens with f = 5 cm to photograph an object 2 m away. Find the image distance.
Hard
Answer

1/v = 1/f + 1/u → signs: u = −200 cm; 1/v = 1/5 + 1/(−200) = 40/200 − 1/200 = 39/200 → v ≈ 5.13 cm

Image forms just beyond f on the film side — real, inverted, highly diminished.
Two convex lenses of power +3D and +4D are placed in contact. Find the combined focal length.
Medium
Answer

P_total = 3+4 = 7D; f = 1/7 ≈ 0.143 m = 14.3 cm

Powers of lenses in contact add directly.
Why does a magnifying glass make text appear larger?
Medium
Answer

By forming a virtual, magnified image of text beyond 25 cm (least distance of distinct vision), allowing eye to see more detail

At 25 cm, the eye can resolve more detail than when the object is closer without a lens.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: How does a compound microscope achieve greater magnification than a simple magnifying glass?
Hard
Answer

Objective lens (short f) forms real magnified intermediate image; eyepiece (longer f) acts as magnifier of this image — total magnification = product of both magnifications

Two-stage magnification overcomes the limit of a single lens.
Evaluate: Online reading glasses use +2D lenses. A person needs +3D. What are the risks of incorrect power?
Hard
Answer

Incorrect power strains eye muscles, may worsen vision long-term; object may not be in focus — always get prescription checked

MYP Criterion D: responsible use of optical technology and healthcare implications.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a concave lens?
Easy
Answer

A lens thinner at the centre than edges that diverges parallel rays

Also called a diverging lens; focal length is negative.
What is the sign of focal length of a concave lens?
Easy
Answer

Negative (−f)

By convention, the focal length of a diverging lens is negative.
A concave lens always forms what type of image?
Easy
Answer

Virtual, erect, and diminished — regardless of object position

Concave lenses always diverge rays; image is always on same side as object, smaller.
What eye defect is corrected using a concave lens?
Easy
Answer

Myopia (short-sightedness / near-sightedness)

A concave lens spreads parallel rays so they converge on the retina instead of in front of it.
Calculate the power of a concave lens with focal length −50 cm.
Easy
Answer

P = 1/(−0.5) = −2 D

Negative power confirms it is a diverging/concave lens.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Object 20 cm from concave lens (f = −15 cm). Find image position.
Medium
Answer

v = −8.57 cm (virtual, on same side as object, diminished)

1/v = 1/f + 1/u = 1/(−15) + 1/(−20) = −4/60 − 3/60 = −7/60 → v = −60/7 ≈ −8.57 cm.
Explain how a concave lens corrects myopia.
Medium
Answer

Myopic eye converges rays in front of retina; concave lens diverges rays before entry, shifting focal point back to retina

The concave lens pre-diverges parallel rays so the myopic eye focuses them correctly.
A person is −3 D myopic. What focal length lens is needed?
Medium
Answer

f = 1/(−3) ≈ −0.33 m = −33.3 cm

The prescription power is the required lens power; negative confirms concave lens.
Compare the images formed by concave and convex lenses when an object is at 2f.
Hard
Answer

Concave: virtual, erect, diminished (always); Convex: real, inverted, same size (specifically at 2f)

Object position doesn't change the concave lens behaviour, but dramatically changes convex lens output.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: A myopic student's glasses have lenses of −4 D. How far can they see clearly without glasses?
Hard
Answer

Far point = 1/4 = 0.25 m = 25 cm — they can only see clearly up to 25 cm

Power of corrective lens = −1/far point; so far point = 1/4 D = 0.25 m.
Evaluate: Screen use is increasing myopia rates in children. Suggest optical and behavioural interventions.
Hard
Answer

Optical: proper glasses/contacts; Behavioural: 20-20-20 rule (20 min screen → 20 sec look at 20 ft away), outdoor time — outdoor light may slow myopia progression

MYP Criterion D: science, technology and societal health interconnected.

Set A — Recall & Identify
Name the parts of the human eye and their functions.
Easy
Answer

Cornea (refracts light), Iris (controls pupil size), Lens (fine-focuses), Retina (detects image), Optic nerve (sends signals to brain)

The eye is a complex optical system with these key components working together.
What is the least distance of distinct vision for a normal human eye?
Easy
Answer

25 cm

Closer than 25 cm, the eye cannot focus clearly; this is the near point.
What is accommodation in the context of the eye?
Easy
Answer

The ability of the eye to adjust its focal length by changing the shape of the lens using ciliary muscles

Ciliary muscles contract to increase lens curvature (shorter f) for near objects; relax for far objects.
Which cells in the retina detect colour?
Easy
Answer

Cone cells (3 types: red, green, blue sensitive)

Rod cells detect light intensity (night vision); cone cells detect colour.
What is the blind spot?
Easy
Answer

The point on the retina where the optic nerve exits; has no photoreceptors, so no image is detected here

Objects whose image falls on the blind spot are invisible — a real gap in vision.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A person can see clearly only up to 1 m. Identify the defect and the corrective lens.
Medium
Answer

Myopia (near-sightedness); corrective lens: concave, P = −1/1 = −1 D

Far point is 1 m; needed lens has power = −1/(far point in m).
A person cannot read a book held closer than 1 m. Identify the defect and correction.
Medium
Answer

Hypermetropia (far-sightedness); corrective lens: convex, P = 1/0.25 − 1/1 = 4−1 = +3 D

Near point is 1 m; person needs help converging rays from 25 cm to focus on retina.
Why do we need two eyes for depth perception?
Medium
Answer

Two eyes see slightly different images (parallax); brain combines these to compute distance/depth

Binocular vision uses the angle difference between two images to triangulate depth.
A student in a dark room has enlarged pupils. Explain.
Medium
Answer

In dim light, the iris dilates the pupil to let in more light for better vision

This is a reflex action controlled by the autonomic nervous system.
Explain why the sky appears blue and sunsets appear red.
Hard
Answer

Blue light (short wavelength) scatters most by atmosphere (Rayleigh scattering) — reaches eyes from all directions; at sunset, light travels longer path, blue scattered away, leaving red/orange

Scattering is inversely proportional to wavelength^4 — blue scatters ~10× more than red.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is dispersion of light?
Easy
Answer

The splitting of white light into its component colours (spectrum) when it passes through a prism

Dispersion occurs because different colours have different refractive indices in glass.
State the colours of the visible spectrum in order.
Easy
Answer

Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red (VIBGYOR)

Red has the longest wavelength (~700 nm); violet has shortest (~400 nm).
Which colour deviates most when white light passes through a prism?
Easy
Answer

Violet (highest refractive index → most bending)

Higher refractive index = more slowing = more bending; violet light bends most.
What is a rainbow, and how is it formed?
Easy
Answer

A meteorological and optical phenomenon caused by dispersion of sunlight by water droplets in the atmosphere

Each raindrop acts as a tiny prism, refracting, dispersing, and internally reflecting sunlight.
What did Newton demonstrate by passing a spectrum through an inverted prism?
Easy
Answer

That white light is a mixture of all colours (recombination restores white light)

Newton's two-prism experiment proved white light is composite, not pure.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Why does violet light deviate more than red in a prism?
Medium
Answer

Violet has shorter wavelength, travels slower in glass, has higher refractive index → bends more at each surface

The refractive index of glass varies with wavelength — this is dispersion.
Explain the formation of a rainbow using the physics of refraction, dispersion, and reflection.
Hard
Answer

Sunlight enters droplet (refraction+dispersion), internally reflects off back, exits (refraction again) — each colour exits at different angle; observer sees arc of colours at ~40-42°

Red exits at 42°, violet at 40° — observer sees red at top of arc, violet at bottom.
Why is the sky red at sunrise/sunset but blue during the day?
Hard
Answer

At sunrise/sunset, sunlight travels through maximum atmosphere — blue light scattered away, only red/orange remains; midday, shorter path, blue scattered from all directions reaches eye

Rayleigh scattering intensity ∝ 1/λ⁴ — blue scatters far more than red.
A glass slab (unlike a prism) does not disperse white light. Explain why.
Medium
Answer

The two parallel faces of a slab recombine the dispersed rays — each colour is refracted twice in opposite ways, cancelling dispersion. Prism's non-parallel faces don't recombine.

Parallel faces means equal and opposite deviations cancel; prism's angled faces allow colours to separate.
How does the secondary rainbow form, and why is its colour order reversed?
Hard
Answer

Light undergoes two internal reflections in raindrop — extra reflection reverses colour order; secondary rainbow is at ~50-53°, dimmer than primary

Double reflection causes order reversal: violet at top, red at bottom in secondary rainbow.

Evaluate: LED white lights don't produce rainbows through a prism. Why? What does this reveal about LED vs sunlight?
Hard

LEDs produce narrow wavelength spikes — not a continuous spectrum; sunlight contains all visible wavelengths continuously, producing full VIBGYOR through a prism

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is total internal reflection (TIR)?
Easy
Answer

When light travelling from denser to rarer medium hits the boundary at an angle exceeding the critical angle, it reflects entirely back into the denser medium

TIR requires: denser to rarer medium AND angle of incidence > critical angle.
What is the critical angle?
Easy
Answer

The minimum angle of incidence (in denser medium) at which total internal reflection occurs

At exactly the critical angle, refracted ray travels along the boundary (90° to normal).
How do optical fibres use TIR?
Easy
Answer

Light enters at one end and undergoes repeated TIR along the length of the fibre, emerging at the other end with minimal loss

The core has higher n than cladding; TIR keeps light inside the core.
Name one medical use of optical fibres.
Easy
Answer

Endoscopes — used to view internal organs without surgery

Fibre optic bundles transmit images from inside the body to a camera outside.
What is a periscope and how does it work?
Easy
Answer

A device using two mirrors at 45° to allow viewing over or around obstacles; light zigzags via reflection between the mirrors

Used in submarines, military observation posts, and fun viewing devices.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Calculate the critical angle for glass (n = 1.5).
Medium
Answer

sin C = 1/n = 1/1.5 = 0.667 → C ≈ 41.8°

Critical angle formula: sin C = n_rarer / n_denser = 1/1.5 for glass-air.
Explain why a diamond sparkles more than glass.
Medium
Answer

Diamond has very high n (≈2.42), giving critical angle of only 24.4° — most light inside undergoes TIR and is redirected out the top, creating sparkle

Diamonds are cut precisely to maximise TIR and direct light out through the top facets.
How does an endoscope transmit images? Explain the role of two fibre bundles.
Hard
Answer

Illumination bundle carries light IN; image bundle carries reflected light OUT; TIR keeps light in each fibre — coherent bundle (ordered fibres) preserves image integrity

Medical endoscopes have rigid and flexible versions using both mirror and fibre optics.
Why can data travel faster through optical fibres than through copper wires?
Hard
Answer

Light travels at ~2×10⁸ m/s in glass; electromagnetic waves in copper much slower + more interference; fibre carries multiple wavelengths (wavelength-division multiplexing)

Optical fibres have revolutionised internet speeds — backbone of modern internet.
