Identifying Chemical Reactions

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

State five signs that indicate a chemical reaction has occurred.Easy
Show Answer

Colour change, gas evolution (fizzing/bubbling), precipitate formation, temperature change, formation of a new substance with different properties.

Is dissolving sugar in water a physical or chemical change? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

Physical change — no new substance is formed; the sugar can be recovered by evaporating the water. Molecular structure of sugar is unchanged.

Is burning of magnesium a chemical or physical change? State one sign.Easy
Show Answer

Chemical change — a new substance (MgO) is formed. Sign: brilliant white light and heat produced.

What is a precipitate?Easy
Show Answer

An insoluble solid formed when two solutions react, separating out of solution.

What gas is produced when zinc reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid?Easy
Show Answer

Hydrogen gas (H₂).

Distinguish between an exothermic and an endothermic reaction.Easy
Show Answer

Exothermic: releases heat — temperature rises (e.g., combustion). Endothermic: absorbs heat — temperature falls (e.g., thermal decomposition of CaCO₃).

What is a reactant and what is a product in a chemical equation?Easy
Show Answer

Reactants: substances present before the reaction (left of arrow). Products: new substances formed (right of arrow).

State one difference between a physical and chemical change.Easy
Show Answer

Physical change: no new substance formed, usually reversible. Chemical change: new substances with different properties formed, usually irreversible.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why temperature change alone is not sufficient proof of a chemical reaction.Medium
Show Answer

Physical processes also change temperature — dissolving ammonium nitrate in water is endothermic but no new substance forms. Temperature change must be combined with other evidence to confirm a chemical reaction.

When lead nitrate solution is added to potassium iodide solution, a bright yellow precipitate forms. Identify the type of change and explain.Medium
Show Answer

Chemical change (double displacement/precipitation). Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2KI → PbI₂↓ + 2KNO₃. Lead iodide (yellow, insoluble) is a new substance with different properties.

Describe a test to confirm that the gas produced in a reaction is hydrogen.Medium
Show Answer

Collect the gas; bring a lit splint to the test tube mouth. Hydrogen burns with a squeaky "pop" sound.

Distinguish between reversible and irreversible chemical reactions with examples.Medium
Show Answer

Irreversible: combustion of wood — ash cannot become wood. Reversible: blue CuSO₄·5H₂O heated → white CuSO₄ + H₂O; adding water reverses it.

Explain how colour change when sulphur burns indicates a chemical reaction.Medium
Show Answer

Yellow sulphur burns to produce SO₂ (colourless, pungent gas). New substance with entirely different properties forms — evidence of chemical change.

Why does mass remain constant in a chemical reaction (closed container)?Medium
Show AnswerLaw of conservation of mass: atoms are neither created nor destroyed, only rearranged. Total atoms before and after are equal, so mass is conserved.

A student burns iron wool in oxygen and finds the product has greater mass than iron alone. Explain.Medium
Show Answer4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃. The product includes iron plus the mass of oxygen absorbed from air. Total mass (Fe + O₂) is conserved; product mass exceeds original iron mass because oxygen atoms are added.

Explain why an open container gives different mass results than a closed container when magnesium burns.Medium
Show AnswerOpen: Mg + O₂ from air → MgO; product appears heavier than original Mg alone (oxygen added). Closed: total mass of all substances (Mg + O₂) remains the same — conservation of mass is demonstrable only in the closed system.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Critically analyse why rusting of iron is a chemical change even though it looks like a slow surface change.Hard
Show AnswerRusting produces Fe₂O₃·nH₂O — a new substance entirely different from iron in properties (brittle, reddish-brown, non-conductive). Requires O₂ and H₂O; is exothermic and irreversible. These confirm chemical change, not merely surface alteration.

Evaluate the argument: "All physical changes are reversible."Hard
Show AnswerNot always true. Cutting paper and breaking glass are physical changes but are not easily reversible. Reversibility is not the defining criterion — the correct criterion is whether a new substance is formed (chemical) or not (physical).

A student argues cooking an egg is physical because "it just changes shape." Evaluate.Hard
Show AnswerIncorrect. Heat denatures proteins — irreversible structural change at the molecular level; new texture and properties emerge; the egg cannot be uncooked. New chemical bonds form in the protein network — this is a chemical change.

Explain how Lavoisier's experiments with mercury oxide established the Law of Conservation of Mass.Hard
Show AnswerLavoisier heated HgO in a sealed flask: 2HgO → 2Hg + O₂. He measured mass before and after in the sealed system and found it unchanged. He showed combustion required oxygen. His quantitative experiments (1774) established matter is neither created nor destroyed in reactions.

Analyse a situation where colour change does NOT indicate a chemical reaction, and one where it DOES.Hard
Show AnswerDoes not: mixing red and blue food colouring → purple — physical blending; no new molecule formed; separable by chromatography. Does indicate: CuSO₄ solution turning colourless when iron is added — Cu²⁺ ions replaced by Fe²⁺ (new substance, new ionic environment). Key: check if a new substance with different chemical properties is formed.

Discuss how calorimetry can quantify energy changes in chemical reactions and relate this to bond breaking and forming.Hard
Show AnswerCalorimetry measures q = mcΔT. Energy is absorbed to break bonds in reactants and released when new bonds form in products. Net ΔH = bond breaking energy − bond forming energy. Exothermic if products have stronger bonds. Calorimetry provides experimental validation of theoretical bond energy calculations.

Evaluate how activation energy explains why some exothermic reactions need a spark to start.Hard
Show AnswerActivation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy needed to break reactant bonds and initiate the reaction. Even in exothermic reactions, reactants must first overcome Ea. A spark provides this initial energy; the heat released then sustains the reaction (especially combustion). Catalysts lower Ea, allowing reactions without external energy input.

A student claims photosynthesis is not a chemical reaction because "plants don't use chemicals." Critically refute this.Hard
Show AnswerIncorrect. Plants use CO₂ and H₂O as chemical reactants; light drives the reaction; glucose and O₂ are produced — entirely new substances. 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ is a textbook chemical equation. The student confuses everyday meaning of "chemical" with the scientific meaning — all matter is chemical.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

State the law that requires chemical equations to be balanced.Easy
Show Answer

Law of Conservation of Mass — atoms are neither created nor destroyed; equal numbers of each type on both sides.

What is the difference between a coefficient and a subscript in a chemical equation?Easy
Show AnswerCoefficient: number before a formula (e.g., 2H₂) — number of molecules. Subscript: number within a formula (e.g., H₂O) — atoms per molecule. Only coefficients are changed when balancing.

Balance: H₂ + O₂ → H₂OEasy
Show Answer2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

What do the state symbols (s), (l), (g), and (aq) mean?Easy
Show Answer(s) solid; (l) liquid; (g) gas; (aq) aqueous — dissolved in water.

Balance: Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃Easy
Show Answer4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃

What does the arrow (→) represent in a chemical equation?Easy
Show Answer"Yields" or "produces" — direction of reaction from reactants to products.

Balance: Mg + HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂Easy
Show AnswerMg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂

Why must subscripts NOT be changed when balancing equations?Easy
Show AnswerChanging subscripts would create a different compound (e.g., H₂O → H₃O changes water to hydronium). Only coefficients adjust the number of formula units.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Balance and classify: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂Medium
Show AnswerAlready balanced (1:1:1). Classification: thermal decomposition. Ca=1, C=1, O=3 on each side. ✓

Balance: Al + CuSO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + CuMedium
Show Answer2Al + 3CuSO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 3Cu. Check: 2Al, 3Cu, 3SO₄ each side. ✓

Balance: CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂OMedium
Show AnswerCH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. C=1, H=4, O=4 each side. ✓

Balance: Na + H₂O → NaOH + H₂Medium
Show Answer2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂. Na=2, H=4, O=2 each side. ✓

Write and balance the equation for complete combustion of ethanol (C₂H₅OH).Medium
Show AnswerC₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O. C=2, H=6, O=7 each side. ✓

Explain why ionic equations are sometimes preferred over full molecular equations.Medium
Show AnswerIonic equations show only the species that actually change (spectator ions cancelled), revealing the actual chemistry — e.g., any acid-base neutralisation simplifies to H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O. More general and informative.

Balance: Fe₂O₃ + CO → Fe + CO₂ (blast furnace).Medium
Show AnswerFe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂. Fe=2, O=6, C=3 each side. ✓

Balance: N₂ + H₂ → NH₃ (Haber process).Medium
Show AnswerN₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃. N=2, H=6 each side. ✓

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Explain how the mole concept connects balanced equations to actual masses of reactants and products, using 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O as an example.Hard
Show AnswerCoefficients give mole ratios: 2 mol H₂ + 1 mol O₂ → 2 mol H₂O. Using molar masses: 4 g H₂ + 32 g O₂ → 36 g H₂O. This allows calculation of exact masses needed or produced — the basis of stoichiometry.

A student balanced N₂ + H₂ → NH₃ as N₂ + H₃ → NH₃. Identify and correct the error.Hard
Show AnswerError: changed subscript H₂ to H₃ — not a real formula for hydrogen gas. Only coefficients may be changed. Correct: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃. N=2, H=6 both sides. ✓

Derive the mass of iron produced from 160 g of Fe₂O₃ using Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂.Hard
Show AnswerM(Fe₂O₃) = 160 g/mol → 1 mol Fe₂O₃. Ratio 1:2, so 2 mol Fe = 2 × 56 = 112 g Fe produced.

Explain the concept of limiting reagent and apply it: 4 g H₂ and 32 g O₂ in 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.Hard
Show AnswerLimiting reagent: reactant completely consumed first. 4 g H₂ = 2 mol; 32 g O₂ = 1 mol. Required ratio H₂:O₂ = 2:1; available ratio = 2:1 — both exactly consumed. Neither is limiting in this case; 36 g H₂O produced.

Explain the difference between theoretical yield and actual yield, and what causes the gap.Hard
Show AnswerTheoretical yield: maximum mass of product from stoichiometry (100% conversion assumed). Actual yield: mass actually collected. Actual < theoretical due to side reactions, incomplete reaction, product lost during purification, impure reactants. % yield = (actual/theoretical) × 100%.

Balance combustion of octane (C₈H₁₈) and calculate CO₂ from burning 1 L of petrol (density 0.74 g/mL, assume pure octane).Hard
Show Answer2C₈H₁₈ + 25O₂ → 16CO₂ + 18H₂O. Mass of 1 L = 740 g; M(C₈H₁₈)=114 g/mol → 6.49 mol. Each mol C₈H₁₈ → 8 mol CO₂. CO₂ = 6.49 × 8 × 44 ≈ 2,284 g ≈ 2.3 kg CO₂ per litre of petrol.

Critically evaluate why balanced equations are described as "models" rather than exact descriptions at the molecular level.Hard
Show AnswerBalanced equations show overall reactants/products but not: reaction mechanism, intermediates, transition states, rate, conditions, side reactions, or energy landscape. They are simplified macroscopic models — powerful for quantitative calculations but unable to represent molecular dynamics, kinetics, or mechanistic pathways.

A mixture of 5 g H₂ and 40 g O₂ reacts to form water. Calculate the mass of water formed and identify which reactant is in excess.Hard
Show Answer5 g H₂ = 2.5 mol; 40 g O₂ = 1.25 mol. Required ratio H₂:O₂ = 2:1 → 2.5 mol H₂ needs 1.25 mol O₂. Both exactly consumed (2.5:1.25 = 2:1). Water = 2.5 mol × 18 = 45 g. Neither is in excess — stoichiometric mixture.

Types of Reactions

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Define a combination (synthesis) reaction and give an example.Easy
Show AnswerTwo or more substances combine into one product (A + B → AB). Example: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO.

Define a decomposition reaction and give an example.Easy
Show AnswerOne compound breaks into two or more simpler substances (AB → A + B). Example: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂.

What is a thermal decomposition reaction? Give one NCERT example.Easy
Show AnswerDecomposition caused by heating. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (heating limestone).

What is a photolytic (photochemical) decomposition? Give an example.Easy
Show AnswerDecomposition caused by light. 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂ (silver chloride in sunlight).

What is an electrolytic decomposition? Give an example.Easy
Show AnswerDecomposition by passing electricity. 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrolysis of water).

Classify: 2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂. State the type and energy involved.Easy
Show AnswerThermal decomposition (requires heat, MnO₂ catalyst). Endothermic.

Is burning a candle a combination or decomposition reaction?Easy
Show AnswerCombination (combustion): wax hydrocarbons + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O.

What is a double displacement (metathesis) reaction? Give an example.Easy
Show AnswerTwo ionic compounds exchange ions (AB + CD → AD + CB). Na₂SO₄ + BaCl₂ → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why all combustion reactions are exothermic combination reactions.Medium
Show AnswerCombustion is the rapid reaction of fuel + oxygen (combining reactants to form products). Products (CO₂, H₂O) have stronger bonds than reactants — energy released as heat and light (exothermic).

Explain the industrial importance of thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate.Medium
Show AnswerCaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ produces quicklime (CaO) — essential for cement, mortar, steel desulphurisation, water treatment, and paper. India is a major producer; limestone quarrying and lime kilns are major industries.

Classify and write a balanced equation for barium chloride + sodium sulphate, identifying the precipitate.Medium
Show AnswerDouble displacement (precipitation): BaCl₂(aq) + Na₂SO₄(aq) → BaSO₄(s)↓ + 2NaCl(aq). Precipitate: barium sulphate (white).

Distinguish between thermal and photolytic decomposition with two examples each.Medium
Show AnswerThermal: (1) CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂; (2) 2Pb(NO₃)₂ → 2PbO + 4NO₂ + O₂. Photolytic: (1) 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂; (2) 2AgBr → 2Ag + Br₂.

What type of reaction is neutralisation? Write a balanced equation and explain what is formed.Medium
Show AnswerDouble displacement (or ionic: H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O). HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l). A salt and water are formed; acid and base properties neutralised.

Explain why AgBr is used in photographic film, referring to its decomposition type.Medium
Show Answer2AgBr → 2Ag + Br₂ (photolytic). Areas with more light produce more silver (darker image). Differential decomposition creates the photographic image.

Classify and explain: Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe (thermite reaction).Medium
Show AnswerSingle displacement and redox: Al (more reactive than Fe) displaces iron. Extremely exothermic (~2500°C). Used for welding railway tracks. Al oxidised (+3); Fe reduced (0).

Explain why decomposition reactions are generally endothermic.Medium
Show AnswerBreaking bonds in the compound requires energy input; fewer new bonds form in products. Net energy absorbed (endothermic). Photolytic decomposition absorbs light energy; still endothermic overall.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Analyse why classifying reactions into combination/decomposition/displacement/redox is an oversimplification, using the thermite reaction as an example.Hard
Show AnswerThermite is simultaneously: single displacement (Al displaces Fe), redox (Al oxidised; Fe reduced), and exothermic. Most reactions fit multiple categories. Classification is a pedagogical tool, not an absolute taxonomy — real reactions are complex multi-step processes.

Evaluate the industrial and environmental significance of electrolysis of water as a clean energy carrier.Hard
Show Answer2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂. Powered by renewables = "green hydrogen" — zero-carbon fuel. Burning H₂ produces only water. Challenges: energy-intensive (~50 kWh/kg H₂); hydrogen storage requires high pressure; fuel cell costs falling. Green hydrogen is a key decarbonisation strategy for industry.

Discuss how decomposition of ozone (O₃ → O₂ + O•) relates to CFCs and ozone layer depletion.Hard
Show AnswerCFCs photolytically decompose in stratosphere, releasing Cl•. Cl• catalyses O₃ decomposition: Cl• + O₃ → ClO• + O₂; ClO• + O• → Cl• + O₂. One Cl• destroys up to 100,000 O₃ molecules. Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFCs; ozone layer slowly recovering.

Critically assess the claim: "Combination reactions are always exothermic."Hard
Show AnswerFalse. N₂ + O₂ → 2NO is endothermic (ΔH = +180 kJ/mol) — occurs during lightning. Bond enthalpy determines sign of ΔH, not reaction type. Most combinations are exothermic but not all.

Explain how photosynthesis and respiration represent inverse reactions cycling carbon in the biosphere.Hard
Show AnswerPhotosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (endothermic, synthesis, stores solar energy). Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O (exothermic, decomposition/oxidation, releases ATP). Thermodynamic inverses; together cycle carbon between CO₂ and organic molecules.

Analyse how the Haber process (ammonia synthesis) and blast furnace (iron extraction) illustrate different reaction types requiring different industrial conditions.Hard
Show AnswerHaber: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ — reversible combination; equilibrium must be managed (200 atm, 450°C, Fe catalyst). Blast furnace: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂ — displacement/reduction; irreversible at high temperature (1600°C). Reaction type informs choice of conditions.

Evaluate how drug metabolism in the liver involves the same reaction types studied in NCERT Ch 1.Hard
Show AnswerPhase I (decomposition/oxidation by cytochrome P450): drugs broken down into more polar metabolites. Phase II (combination/conjugation with glucuronic acid/glutathione): make metabolites water-soluble for excretion. Same fundamental reaction types — applied biochemically. Understanding them predicts drug interactions and dosing.

Compare three experiments: heating CuCO₃; mixing CuSO₄ + NaOH; burning copper in air. Classify each, write balanced equations, compare oxygen's role.Hard
Show Answer(1) CuCO₃ → CuO + CO₂: thermal decomposition; no O₂ involved — oxygen released from within compound. (2) CuSO₄ + 2NaOH → Cu(OH)₂↓ + Na₂SO₄: double displacement; no O₂ involved. (3) 2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO: combination/oxidation; O₂ is a reactant. O₂ is produced in (1), absent in (2), and consumed in (3) — showing oxygen plays very different roles depending on reaction type.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Define a single displacement reaction.Easy
Show AnswerA more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound: A + BC → AC + B.

What happens when zinc is added to copper sulphate solution? Write the equation.Easy
Show AnswerZn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu. Blue colour fades; reddish copper deposits on zinc.

Why can copper NOT displace zinc from zinc sulphate solution?Easy
Show AnswerCopper is less reactive than zinc (lower in reactivity series) — cannot displace a more reactive metal.

Write the reactivity series from most to least reactive (K to Au, include H).Easy
Show AnswerK > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > (H) > Cu > Ag > Au

What observation confirms displacement when iron nails are placed in copper sulphate solution?Easy
Show AnswerIron nail becomes coated with reddish-brown copper; blue colour of CuSO₄ solution fades as Cu²⁺ is displaced.

Write the equation for displacement of hydrogen by sodium from water.Easy
Show Answer2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑

Can silver displace hydrogen from dilute HCl? Explain.Easy
Show AnswerNo — silver is below hydrogen in the reactivity series and is less reactive than hydrogen.

What type of reaction is thermite: Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe?Easy
Show AnswerSingle displacement — aluminium (more reactive) displaces iron from iron oxide. Also a redox reaction.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Will magnesium displace aluminium from aluminium chloride solution? Explain and write the equation.Medium
Show AnswerYes — Mg is above Al in reactivity series. 3Mg + 2AlCl₃ → 3MgCl₂ + 2Al. Mg has greater tendency to lose electrons, reducing Al³⁺ to Al metal.

Explain at the ionic level why iron displaces copper from CuSO₄.Medium
Show AnswerIonic: Fe(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Fe²⁺(aq) + Cu(s). Fe donates 2 electrons to Cu²⁺, reducing it to copper metal; Fe is oxidised to Fe²⁺.

Describe how displacement reactions are used to extract copper from CuSO₄ solution using scrap iron.Medium
Show AnswerFe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu. Scrap iron added to copper sulphate (from ore leaching); copper precipitates out and is collected. Low-energy, low-cost hydrometallurgy method.

Explain why hydrogen is placed in the reactivity series between lead and copper even though it is not a metal.Medium
Show AnswerHydrogen's position reflects which metals can displace it from acids (above H) and which cannot (below H: Cu, Ag, Au). It serves as a reference point for predicting acid reactions of metals.

A student adds copper to AgNO₃ solution and observes silvery crystals. Write the equation and explain.Medium
Show AnswerCu + 2AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag. Copper (above Ag in reactivity) displaces silver. Silver crystals deposit; solution turns blue (Cu²⁺ ions).

Explain why gold and platinum are found as native metals while iron is found as ore.Medium
Show AnswerGold/platinum: lowest reactivity — do not combine with O₂, water, or acids; found as free metals. Iron: highly reactive — readily combines with O₂ and H₂O to form oxides/carbonates — always found as compounds in Earth's crust.

Predict: will aluminium displace iron from FeCl₃ solution? Write the balanced equation.Medium
Show AnswerYes — Al is above Fe in reactivity series. 2Al + 2FeCl₃ → 2AlCl₃ + 2Fe (if Fe³⁺); or Al + FeCl₂ → AlCl₂ + Fe (if Fe²⁺). Common balanced form: 2Al + 3FeCl₂ → 2AlCl₃ + 3Fe.

Explain the corrosion of an iron nail in terms of displacement principles.Medium
Show AnswerIron (high reactivity) is oxidised by O₂ and H₂O: Fe effectively "displaces" H from water in an electrochemical process. Unlike noble metals (Cu, Au) below Fe in reactivity series, iron readily loses electrons — driving corrosion. Reactivity series position directly predicts corrosion susceptibility.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Explain why the reactivity series is ultimately based on reduction potential (electrode potential) values.Hard
Show AnswerStandard electrode potential (E°) measures tendency to lose/gain electrons. More negative E° = more reactive. K: E° = −2.93 V; Au: E° = +1.52 V. The reactivity series is an empirical ranking consistent with electrochemical measurements.

Critically analyse why aluminium appears unreactive in everyday life despite being high in the reactivity series.Hard
Show AnswerAl reacts rapidly with O₂ to form a thin, adherent, impervious Al₂O₃ passivation layer. This prevents further oxidation kinetically even though Al is thermodynamically very reactive. Unlike iron's flaky rust, Al₂O₃ is self-sealing.

Discuss galvanic corrosion and why connecting iron pipes to copper pipes in plumbing accelerates iron corrosion.Hard
Show AnswerTwo different metals in electrical contact in an electrolyte form a galvanic cell. Fe (more reactive, higher in series) = anode (oxidised): Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻. Copper = cathode (protected). Current flows, accelerating iron dissolution. Solution: dielectric fittings or sacrificial zinc anodes.

Evaluate the cementation process for copper extraction as an industrial application of displacement reactions.Hard
Show AnswerAcid mine drainage (dilute CuSO₄) flows over scrap iron: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu. Advantages: no smelting; works on dilute solutions; uses waste iron. Limitations: impure product; iron-rich acidic effluent (environmental hazard). Used for tailings pond remediation.

Analyse the thermite reaction in terms of: reactivity series position, oxidation states, energy change, and industrial application.Hard
Show AnswerFe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe. Reactivity: Al above Fe (displaces Fe). Oxidation states: Al: 0→+3 (oxidised); Fe: +3→0 (reduced). ΔH = −852 kJ/mol; reaches ~2500°C. Application: welding railway tracks in situ — no external power needed.

Explain why the reactivity series cannot reliably predict reactions at very high temperatures or in non-aqueous systems.Hard
Show AnswerReactivity series is based on aqueous chemistry at ~25°C. At high temperatures: thermodynamics change (entropy dominates); carbon reduces many metal oxides at 1600°C even if below them in aqueous series. Non-aqueous systems have different solvation and stability — predictions from aqueous series may not apply.

Discuss how displacement reactions in biology (competitive inhibition) parallel chemical displacement concepts.Hard
Show AnswerIn chemistry: more reactive substance displaces less reactive one. In competitive inhibition: inhibitor with higher affinity for enzyme active site displaces normal substrate. Stronger binding (lower Kᵢ) = more complete displacement. The principle — one entity displacing another due to greater affinity — applies from chemistry to biochemistry.

Critically evaluate whether a universal reactivity series can be constructed for non-metals analogous to metals.Hard
Show AnswerYes, partially. Halogens have an analogous displacement series: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂ (based on electronegativity and reduction potential). Cl₂ + 2KBr → 2KCl + Br₂. Used industrially to extract bromine from seawater. Other non-metals are harder to rank consistently due to greater variety of bonding and reaction types — a universal non-metal series is impractical.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

What does "OIL RIG" stand for?Easy
Show AnswerOIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons); Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).

Define oxidation in terms of (a) oxygen, (b) hydrogen, (c) electrons.Easy
Show Answer(a) Gain of oxygen. (b) Loss of hydrogen. (c) Loss of electrons.

Define reduction in terms of (a) oxygen, (b) hydrogen, (c) electrons.Easy
Show Answer(a) Loss of oxygen. (b) Gain of hydrogen. (c) Gain of electrons.

In CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O — which substance is oxidised and which is reduced?Easy
Show AnswerH₂ is oxidised (gains oxygen → H₂O). CuO is reduced (loses oxygen → Cu).

What is a redox reaction?Easy
Show AnswerA reaction where both oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously — one species loses electrons and another gains them.

In Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu, which element is oxidised?Easy
Show AnswerZinc — Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (loses electrons; oxidised).

What is an oxidising agent? Give one example.Easy
Show AnswerOxidises another substance while itself being reduced. Example: O₂, CuO, KMnO₄.

What is a reducing agent? Give one example.Easy
Show AnswerReduces another substance while itself being oxidised. Example: H₂, carbon, zinc, iron.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

In Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂: identify oxidising and reducing agents with justification.Medium
Show AnswerCO is oxidised (C: +2→+4 in CO₂) → CO is the reducing agent. Fe₂O₃ is reduced (Fe: +3→0) → Fe₂O₃ is the oxidising agent. Used in blast furnace to extract iron.

Assign oxidation states to each element in K₂Cr₂O₇.Medium
Show AnswerK = +1; O = −2; Let Cr = x: 2(+1) + 2x + 7(−2) = 0 → 2 + 2x − 14 = 0 → x = +6. Cr = +6.

Explain why combustion is always a redox reaction.Medium
Show AnswerFuel carbon is oxidised (e.g., C: 0→+4 in CO₂). O₂ is reduced (0→−2 in CO₂/H₂O). Electron transfer occurs, qualifying combustion as a redox reaction.

In H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl, identify what is oxidised and reduced.Medium
Show AnswerH: 0→+1 (oxidised; H₂ is reducing agent). Cl: 0→−1 (reduced; Cl₂ is oxidising agent).

How does oxidation state help in balancing redox equations?Medium
Show AnswerOxidation states identify which atoms change. Total increase in oxidation state (electrons lost) must equal total decrease (electrons gained) — conservation of electrons provides the constraint to balance the equation systematically.

Explain why bleaching powder acts as an oxidising agent to remove stains.Medium
Show AnswerCa(OCl)Cl releases HOCl in water, which releases nascent oxygen [O]. This oxidises coloured organic molecules, breaking their conjugated double-bond systems (chromophores). Oxidised products are colourless — stain removed.

Explain cellular respiration (C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O) as a redox reaction.Medium
Show AnswerGlucose C is oxidised (average C oxidation state rises toward +4 in CO₂); glucose is the reducing agent. O₂ is reduced (0→−2 in H₂O); O₂ is the oxidising agent. Electrons transferred via electron transport chain, driving ATP synthesis.

In electrolysis of water, which electrode has oxidation and which has reduction?Medium
Show AnswerAnode (+): 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ (oxidation). Cathode (−): 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂ (reduction). Twice the volume of H₂ is produced compared to O₂.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Derive the oxidation state of Mn in KMnO₄ and explain why it is a powerful oxidising agent.Hard
Show AnswerK=+1, O=−2 (×4=−8): +1 + x − 8 = 0 → x = +7. Mn is at its highest common oxidation state — readily accepts electrons (reduced to +2 in acid: MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O). This large reduction potential makes KMnO₄ a powerful oxidising agent.

Compare the two definitions of oxidation (O gain vs electron loss) — show where they agree and one example where only the electron definition applies.Hard
Show AnswerBoth agree: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO — Mg gains oxygen AND loses electrons. Electron definition only: 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl — Na is oxidised (Na→Na⁺+e⁻) but gains chlorine, not oxygen. Electron definition is more general and applies to all redox reactions.

Analyse the role of redox in the Daniell cell (zinc-copper electrochemical cell).Hard
Show AnswerAnode (Zn): Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (OIL — oxidation). Cathode (Cu): Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (RIG — reduction). Electrons flow from Zn to Cu through external wire, producing ~1.1 V. Spontaneous redox (more reactive Zn displacing Cu) converts chemical energy to electrical energy.

Explain oxidation state changes in the industrial Contact Process for sulphuric acid.Hard
Show AnswerStep 1: S + O₂ → SO₂ (S: 0→+4). Step 2: 2SO₂ + O₂ → 2SO₃ (S: +4→+6; V₂O₅ catalyst). Step 3: SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (not redox). Progressive oxidation of S from 0→+4→+6 using O₂ as oxidising agent is the core redox chemistry.

Discuss disproportionation reactions (one substance simultaneously oxidised and reduced), using H₂O₂ decomposition as an example.Hard
Show Answer2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂. O in H₂O₂ = −1. In H₂O: O = −2 (reduced). In O₂: O = 0 (oxidised). The same molecule is simultaneously oxidised and reduced — the substance acts as its own oxidising and reducing agent. Cl₂ + NaOH is another example (Cl: 0→−1 and +1 simultaneously).

Evaluate the importance of redox reactions in biological systems: photosynthesis, respiration, and nitrogen fixation.Hard
Show AnswerPhotosynthesis: H₂O oxidised; CO₂ reduced to glucose — solar energy stored. Respiration: glucose oxidised; O₂ reduced — ATP released. Nitrogen fixation: N₂ reduced (0→−3 in NH₃) by nitrogenase. Life is essentially organised redox chemistry — all metabolism involves controlled electron transfer to capture, store, and release energy.

Analyse why superoxide (O₂⁻) is dangerous in living cells, relating to its redox properties as a reactive oxygen species (ROS).Hard
Show AnswerSuperoxide has an unpaired electron — extremely reactive radical. Oxidises DNA, lipids (lipid peroxidation), and proteins. Superoxide dismutase (SOD) dismutates it: 2O₂⁻ + 2H⁺ → H₂O₂ + O₂. Catalase destroys H₂O₂. Failure of antioxidant systems → oxidative stress → cancer, ageing, neurodegeneration. All rooted in aberrant redox chemistry.

Critically evaluate whether oxidation state is a real physical property or a bookkeeping convention, and why this matters.Hard
Show AnswerOxidation state is a formal bookkeeping convention — electrons are assigned to the more electronegative atom, not reflecting actual charge distribution. In CO₂, C is not literally +4. This matters: the concept is a powerful tool for identifying redox reactions and balancing equations, but treating it as a real charge can mislead, especially in covalent and transition metal complexes where actual electron density (measured by XPS) often differs from formal oxidation states.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

What is corrosion?Easy
Show AnswerGradual deterioration of metals by reaction with moisture, oxygen, and other environmental substances — a slow oxidation process.

Write the overall equation for the rusting of iron.Easy
Show Answer4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃ → 2Fe₂O₃·3H₂O (hydrated iron oxide = rust)

Name three conditions required for iron to rust.Easy
Show AnswerIron, water (moisture), and oxygen — all three must be present. Salt accelerates rusting.

Name four methods to prevent rusting of iron.Easy
Show AnswerGalvanising (zinc coating), painting, oiling/greasing, alloying (stainless steel), electroplating, sacrificial anode.

What is galvanising?Easy
Show AnswerCoating iron or steel with zinc to prevent rust; zinc acts as a barrier and as a sacrificial anode (being more reactive, corrodes preferentially).

What is rancidity?Easy
Show AnswerSpoilage of fats and oils due to oxidation — they develop unpleasant smell and taste as fatty acid chains break down into aldehydes and ketones.

Name two methods to prevent rancidity in food.Easy
Show AnswerAdding antioxidants (vitamin E, BHA/BHT); flushing packaging with nitrogen gas.

Why do chip packets contain nitrogen instead of air?Easy
Show AnswerNitrogen is inert — does not react with fats. It displaces oxygen, preventing oxidation of oils in chips (rancidity) and maintaining freshness.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain the electrochemical mechanism of rusting using anode and cathode half-reactions.Medium
Show AnswerAnode (less pure iron): Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation). Cathode (O₂-rich area): O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻ (reduction). Fe²⁺ + OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₂ → Fe(OH)₃ → Fe₂O₃·nH₂O (rust). Water = electrolyte; salt increases conductivity and accelerates corrosion.

Explain how galvanising protects iron even after the zinc coating is scratched.Medium
Show AnswerZinc is higher in reactivity series than iron. When scratched, Zn acts as anode (Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻; corrodes sacrificially) while iron remains cathode (protected). Continues as long as any zinc is in contact with iron.

Why does stainless steel not rust unlike plain iron?Medium
Show AnswerStainless steel contains ≥10.5% chromium. Chromium forms a thin, adherent, self-repairing Cr₂O₃ passive layer that excludes O₂ and moisture. Unlike rust (flaky, porous, exposes fresh metal), Cr₂O₃ adheres tightly and reforms if scratched.

Explain why rancidity is classified as a chemical change, not a physical change.Medium
Show AnswerOxidation of fats produces new compounds (aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids) with different structures, smells, and flavours. Original fat molecules are chemically altered — bonds broken and new bonds formed. Irreversible chemical change.

Compare the corrosion of iron and aluminium at the molecular level, explaining why aluminium is more durable.Medium
Show AnswerIron: Fe₂O₃ (rust) is flaky, porous, non-adherent — crumbles, exposing fresh iron (corrosion accelerates). Aluminium: Al₂O₃ is thin, hard, adherent, self-limiting — seals the surface. Al is more reactive than Fe but far more durable due to its oxide protection.

Describe three ways antioxidants prevent rancidity in food, citing specific chemicals.Medium
Show Answer1. Chain-breaking: BHA/BHT donate H• to lipid radicals, stopping chain reactions. 2. Oxygen scavengers: vitamin C reacts with O₂ preferentially. 3. Metal chelators: EDTA binds Fe²⁺/Cu²⁺ that catalyse oxidation, rendering them inactive.

Explain how salt (NaCl) accelerates rusting using electrochemical principles.Medium
Show AnswerSalt solution (Na⁺ + Cl⁻) is a much better electrolyte than pure water, enabling faster ion flow between anodic and cathodic areas on iron. Greater current flow = faster iron oxidation. Explains why cars corrode faster near coastlines and on salt-treated winter roads.

Explain why a scratch in tin-plated iron is more dangerous than a scratch in galvanised iron.Medium
Show AnswerTin is less reactive than iron. If scratched, iron (more reactive) becomes the anode and corrodes rapidly while tin is the cathode — galvanic acceleration. Galvanised iron: zinc (more reactive than Fe) provides sacrificial protection even when scratched — iron remains cathode and is protected.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate the economic and engineering significance of corrosion globally, and assess the effectiveness of different prevention strategies.Hard
Show AnswerCorrosion costs ~$2.5 trillion/year globally (~3.4% of GDP). Most cost-effective: coatings (painting, galvanising) — cheap, scalable. Cathodic protection (sacrificial anodes, impressed current) for buried pipelines and ship hulls. Corrosion-resistant alloys for aerospace/chemical applications. Better inspection and maintenance could prevent 25–35% of costs.

Analyse the chemistry of cathodic protection used to protect iron pipelines and ship hulls.Hard
Show AnswerSacrificial anode: blocks of Mg or Zn (more reactive than Fe) attached to iron. In the galvanic cell formed, Mg/Zn is the anode (oxidised; corrodes); iron is the cathode (protected). Impressed current: external DC supply makes iron permanently cathodic. Both apply the principle that the cathode is protected in a galvanic cell.

Discuss the chemistry of lipid peroxidation in biological systems and its role in ageing and disease.Hard
Show AnswerROS (superoxide, OH•) initiate chain reactions on membrane phospholipids (PUFAs): L• + O₂ → LOO• → abstracts H from adjacent PUFA → propagation. Products (4-HNE, malondialdehyde) damage DNA and proteins. Chronic lipid peroxidation contributes to cardiovascular disease (oxidised LDL → foam cells → atherosclerosis), neurodegeneration, and cellular ageing.

Critically assess whether "green" corrosion inhibitors (plant extracts) can replace conventional toxic inhibitors in industry.Hard
Show AnswerConventional inhibitors (chromates) are effective but toxic/carcinogenic. Green inhibitors: polyphenols and alkaloids from plant extracts adsorb on metal surfaces, forming a protective monolayer. Lab studies show 70–95% inhibition efficiency. Challenges: inconsistent composition between batches; instability at high temperatures/pH; scale-up costs; regulatory approval. Promising for mild environments; not yet reliable replacements for harsh industrial conditions.

Evaluate the role of antioxidants in human health, connecting food chemistry (rancidity prevention) to cellular biochemistry.Hard
Show AnswerIn food, antioxidants (vitamin E, C, BHA) prevent lipid oxidation. In the body: vitamin E intercepts lipid peroxyl radicals in cell membranes; vitamin C regenerates vitamin E; glutathione neutralises ROS. Antioxidant-rich diets (Mediterranean) are linked to reduced CVD and cancer risk. However, high-dose supplements have often failed in clinical trials — food sources are superior to isolated supplements.

Discuss how nanomaterial coatings (graphene, ZnO nanoparticles) are advancing corrosion protection beyond traditional galvanising.Hard
Show AnswerGraphene: single-atom-thick, impermeable to most molecules — even one layer provides measurable corrosion protection by blocking O₂ and H₂O. ZnO nanoparticles in paint: provide barrier protection plus photocatalytic activity killing biofilm bacteria (microbially-induced corrosion). Self-healing coatings: microcapsules of inhibitor rupture on scratching, sealing defects. These technologies extend service life, especially in marine and aerospace applications.

Analyse the corrosion of the Statue of Liberty (copper → green patina) and explain why the green patina is actually protective.Hard
Show Answer2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO (black), then CuO + CO₂ + H₂O → Cu₂(OH)₂CO₃ (malachite, green). In polluted air, CuSO₄ forms → brochantite (Cu₄(OH)₆SO₄, also green). Unlike rust, copper carbonate/sulphate patina is adherent, dense, and impermeable — self-limiting corrosion. Only ~0.005 mm corroded in 130+ years. Analogous to aluminium's passivation layer.

Critically evaluate: "Corrosion and rancidity are essentially the same chemical process occurring in different materials."Hard
Show AnswerSubstantially correct. Both are oxidation by atmospheric oxygen; both are spontaneous and accelerated by heat/moisture/metal catalysts. Differences: corrosion is electrochemical (ionic mechanism, inorganic products); rancidity is free radical chain reaction (organic mechanism, organic products). Underlying principle — oxidation by O₂ — is identical. The analogy is chemically sound and useful for understanding both processes.
