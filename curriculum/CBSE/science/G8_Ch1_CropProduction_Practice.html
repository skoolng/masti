Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Name the season for Kharif crops and give two examples.Easy
Show Answer

Kharif crops grow June–November (rainy season). Examples: paddy (rice) and maize.

What are Rabi crops? Name three examples.Easy
Show Answer

Rabi crops grow November–April (winter season). Examples: wheat, mustard, and pea.

Define the term "crop".Easy
Show Answer

A crop is a large number of plants of the same kind grown together on a piece of land for food, fibre, or other uses.

What tool is used for the first step of soil preparation (tilling)?Easy
Show Answer

A plough is used to till and loosen the soil.

Name a Kharif crop that is a major source of edible oil.Easy
Show Answer

Groundnut is a Kharif crop and a major source of edible oil.

What is the purpose of harrowing the soil?Easy
Show Answer

Harrowing breaks clods and levels the soil after ploughing, making it suitable for sowing.

Is wheat a Kharif or Rabi crop? State the reason.Easy
Show Answer

Wheat is a Rabi crop because it is sown in November and harvested in April; it needs cool winters.

State one purpose of preparing (tilling) the soil before sowing.Easy
Show Answer

Tilling loosens the soil, allowing air and water to reach plant roots more easily.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain why paddy needs to be grown in a flooded field.Medium
Show Answer

Paddy (rice) requires warm, humid conditions and standing water to thrive. Flooded fields suppress weeds, maintain moisture, and suit the plant's aquatic growth habit.

How does deep ploughing benefit the soil before a Rabi crop is planted?Medium
Show Answer

Deep ploughing exposes sub-soil pests to sunlight (killing them), improves aeration, increases water retention, and allows roots to penetrate deeper.

Compare Kharif and Rabi crops with respect to sowing season and water requirement.Medium
Show Answer

Kharif: sown June–July, depend on monsoon rains, need high water. Rabi: sown November, need cool dry weather, lower water requirement.

Why is crop rotation between Kharif and Rabi seasons beneficial?Medium
Show Answer

Rotating crops replenishes soil nutrients (legumes fix nitrogen), breaks pest/disease cycles, and improves soil structure.

A farmer has sandy soil with poor water retention. Which type of soil preparation would you suggest and why?Medium
Show Answer

Mixing compost/manure into the soil during preparation increases organic matter content, improving water retention and nutrient availability.

Differentiate between a food crop and a cash crop with one example each.Medium
Show Answer

Food crop: grown mainly for eating (e.g., rice). Cash crop: grown mainly for sale/profit (e.g., cotton, tobacco).

Why do Rabi crops not rely heavily on rainfall?Medium
Show Answer

Rabi crops grow in the dry cool winter season after the monsoon ends. Farmers use irrigation to supply water, so they do not depend on rain.

State the difference between a plough and a harrow in terms of function.Medium
Show Answer

A plough cuts and turns the soil deeply (primary tillage). A harrow breaks clods, levels, and smooths the surface (secondary tillage).

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate how the classification of crops into Kharif and Rabi affects a farmer's annual planning and livelihood.Hard
Show Answer

The two-season system allows year-round land use, income diversification, and risk spreading. Kharif failure (drought/flood) can be partially offset by a good Rabi harvest, providing income stability.

Analyse how soil preparation practices have changed with mechanisation and discuss one advantage and one disadvantage.Hard
Show Answer

Tractors replace bullocks for faster, deeper tillage across large areas (advantage: efficiency). Disadvantage: heavy machinery compacts sub-soil, reducing porosity and water infiltration over time.

Design an argument for why a monoculture of a single Kharif crop poses environmental risks.Hard
Show Answer

Monocultures deplete specific nutrients, create large uniform habitats for specialist pests, require higher pesticide use, reduce biodiversity, and increase vulnerability to a single disease outbreak wiping out the entire harvest.

Using your knowledge of soil science, explain why ploughing the same field repeatedly to the same depth can create a "plough pan".Hard
Show Answer

Repeated ploughing at identical depth compacts soil just below the plough line, forming a hard impermeable layer (plough pan) that blocks root growth and water drainage.

A region experiences unpredictable rainfall. Evaluate which crop season (Kharif or Rabi) poses greater risk and suggest a mitigation strategy.Hard
Show Answer

Kharif poses greater risk as it depends on monsoon; unpredictable rain causes drought or flooding. Mitigation: build small check dams for water harvesting, use drought-resistant varieties, and supplement with drip irrigation.

Discuss how the seasonal separation of Kharif and Rabi crops reflects adaptation to India's monsoon climate.Hard
Show Answer

India's monsoon delivers heavy rain Jun–Sep. Kharif crops evolved/were selected to exploit this moisture. Rabi crops suit the dry, cool post-monsoon period. This temporal separation maximises land use within the monsoon cycle.

Critique the practice of zero-tillage farming from both a productivity and environmental perspective.Hard
Show Answer

Zero-tillage preserves soil structure, reduces erosion and carbon loss, saves fuel/labour (environmental + economic benefits). However, weeds may increase and soil compaction from heavy traffic is not broken up, potentially reducing yields without herbicide use.

Propose how traditional knowledge of crop seasons could be integrated with modern climate data to improve agricultural planning.Hard
Show Answer

Traditional calendars record long-term seasonal patterns; modern satellite/climate data provide real-time forecasts. Combining both allows farmers to predict optimal sowing dates, adjust variety selection, and respond to shifting monsoon onset due to climate change.

Sowing and Irrigation

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What is a seed drill and what is its advantage over broadcasting?Easy
Show Answer

A seed drill sows seeds at uniform depth and spacing in rows, reducing wastage and ensuring even germination unlike broadcasting which scatters seeds randomly.

State two characteristics of a good-quality seed.Easy
Show Answer

Good seeds are uniform in size, disease-free, and have high germination percentage (above 90%).

Name the most water-efficient method of irrigation.Easy
Show Answer

Drip irrigation is the most water-efficient method, saving up to 70% water compared to flood irrigation.

What is irrigation?Easy
Show Answer

Irrigation is the artificial supply of water to crops through canals, wells, pipes, or sprinklers when rainfall is insufficient.

Name one traditional and one modern source of irrigation in India.Easy
Show Answer

Traditional: wells/canals. Modern: tube wells / drip irrigation systems.

What does "germination percentage" mean for seeds?Easy
Show Answer

It is the percentage of seeds in a batch that successfully sprout under standard conditions; higher % means better quality seeds.

How does a sprinkler irrigation system work?Easy
Show Answer

Water is pumped through pipes and sprayed through rotating nozzles over crops, mimicking rainfall from above.

Why should seeds be selected before sowing?Easy
Show Answer

Selecting healthy, disease-free seeds with high germination rate ensures better crop yield and reduces gaps in the field.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Compare drip and sprinkler irrigation in terms of water use and suitable crops.Medium
Show Answer

Drip: delivers water directly to roots, saves ~70% water, suits row crops (tomatoes, sugarcane). Sprinkler: covers wide area, suits cereals and lawns but loses more water to evaporation.

Why is over-irrigation harmful to crops?Medium
Show Answer

Over-irrigation waterloggs the soil, cutting off oxygen to roots (root rot), and causes leaching of nutrients. Long-term, it leads to soil salinisation.

Explain the "float test" used to select seeds before sowing.Medium
Show Answer

Seeds are placed in water; hollow/damaged seeds float (rejected) while healthy dense seeds sink (selected for sowing).

A farmer uses flood irrigation for wheat. Suggest why this is inefficient and what he should switch to.Medium
Show Answer

Flood irrigation wastes water through runoff and evaporation. Switching to sprinkler irrigation would reduce water use by ~30–50% while evenly covering the wheat field.

How does sowing depth affect seed germination?Medium
Show Answer

Too shallow: seeds dry out or are eaten by birds. Too deep: seedlings cannot push through soil to reach sunlight. Optimal depth ensures warmth, moisture, and accessible sunlight.

Why is row sowing preferred over broadcasting for most crops?Medium
Show Answer

Row sowing allows uniform spacing, easier weeding and irrigation management, better sunlight access for each plant, and higher yields per unit area.

Explain how drip irrigation conserves water at the molecular/process level.Medium
Show Answer

Drip delivers water drop-by-drop directly to the root zone, minimising surface evaporation, eliminating runoff, and reducing weed growth between rows (less water reaches non-crop areas).

What is the relationship between seed germination rate and crop yield?Medium
Show Answer

Higher germination rate means more plants grow per unit area, reducing gaps in the field, leading to uniform crop stands and higher overall yield.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the social and economic barriers that prevent Indian farmers from adopting drip irrigation despite its efficiency.Hard
Show Answer

High installation cost is unaffordable for marginal farmers. Lack of technical knowledge, maintenance challenges, small fragmented land holdings making system installation uneconomical, and limited government subsidy reach are key barriers.

Analyse how groundwater depletion caused by over-irrigation threatens future food security in Punjab and Haryana.Hard
Show Answer

Paddy cultivation requires ~1,500L/kg water. Tube wells draw water faster than recharge, dropping water tables. Future scarcity will make irrigation unaffordable, threatening wheat/rice production in India's breadbasket states.

Design an experiment to test whether pre-soaking seeds in water for 12 hours improves germination rate compared to dry sowing.Hard
Show Answer

Control: 50 dry seeds on moist cotton in trays. Experimental: 50 seeds soaked 12 hrs then placed identically. Same temperature, light, moisture. Count germinated seeds after 7 days. Compare percentages. Repeat 3 times for reliability.

Using MYP systems thinking, explain how switching an entire district to drip irrigation could have second-order effects on the local ecosystem.Hard
Show Answer

Reduced waterlogging would lower wetland area, affecting migratory birds and amphibians. Lower groundwater use would allow aquifer recovery. Less agricultural runoff would reduce eutrophication in rivers. Soil salinity may decrease, improving soil health long-term.

Critically assess the claim: "GM (genetically modified) seeds with herbicide resistance are the best solution to seed selection challenges."Hard
Show Answer

GM seeds offer uniform high germination and resist specific herbicides (reducing weeding cost). However, they are expensive, create dependency on corporations, risk gene flow to wild relatives, and may reduce crop genetic diversity, increasing vulnerability to new pathogens.

Compare canal irrigation and tube well irrigation in terms of sustainability for a semi-arid region.Hard
Show Answer

Canal irrigation depends on river flow (surface water), sustainable if rivers are not over-extracted but subject to seasonal variation. Tube wells tap groundwater which recharges slowly; over-extraction is unsustainable. Canals are more sustainable in semi-arid regions if well-managed.

A government policy mandates that all rice farmers replace flood irrigation with drip systems. Evaluate likely outcomes over 10 years.Hard
Show Answer

Positive: 50–70% water saved, groundwater tables recover, reduced soil salinisation. Negative: high initial investment burden, rice yields may drop initially as farmers learn the system, weed control harder without flooding. Net outcome positive if subsidies and training provided.

Justify why seed certification programmes are essential for national food security.Hard
Show Answer

Certified seeds guarantee minimum germination rates, genetic purity, disease-free status, and variety performance. This ensures predictable yields across millions of farms, prevents disease outbreaks from infected seeds, and maintains varietal diversity in the national seed bank.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What is manure? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

Manure is organic material from decomposed plant/animal waste that adds nutrients to soil. Example: compost or farmyard manure (FYM).

What are fertilisers? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

Fertilisers are synthetic or manufactured chemicals that supply specific nutrients (N, P, K) to plants quickly. Example: urea (nitrogen fertiliser).

Name the three major nutrients supplied by NPK fertilisers.Easy
Show Answer

Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Potassium (K).

How does manure improve soil texture?Easy
Show Answer

Manure adds organic matter that binds soil particles, improving aeration in clay soils and water retention in sandy soils.

What is compost?Easy
Show Answer

Compost is decomposed organic waste (kitchen scraps, plant material) used as a natural fertiliser to enrich soil.

State one advantage of fertilisers over manures.Easy
Show Answer

Fertilisers are concentrated, act fast, and supply specific nutrients in precise amounts for maximum plant growth.

What is green manure?Easy
Show Answer

Green manure is a crop (like dhaincha or sunhemp) that is grown and then ploughed back into the soil to add organic matter and nitrogen.

Why is excessive use of chemical fertilisers harmful to water bodies?Easy
Show Answer

Excess fertiliser washes into rivers/lakes (runoff), causing algal blooms (eutrophication) that deplete oxygen and kill aquatic life.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Compare manure and fertilisers with respect to nutrient content, cost, and effect on soil health.Medium
Show Answer

Manure: low/slow nutrients, cheap/free, improves soil structure and microbial life. Fertilisers: high/fast nutrients, costly, improve yield quickly but degrade soil structure over time and cause pollution.

Explain the role of Rhizobium bacteria in reducing the need for nitrogen fertilisers.Medium
Show Answer

Rhizobium lives in root nodules of legumes and fixes atmospheric N₂ into ammonia (NH₃) usable by plants. After the crop, soil N remains higher, reducing fertiliser need for the next crop.

A farmer applies excess urea to his paddy field. Describe two negative consequences.Medium
Show Answer

1. Nitrogen runoff causes eutrophication in nearby water bodies, depleting oxygen. 2. Excess nitrogen causes "scorching" (leaf burn) and disrupts soil microbial balance.

How does composting convert kitchen waste into useful manure?Medium
Show Answer

Microorganisms decompose organic kitchen waste (bacteria, fungi) breaking complex molecules into simpler nutrients (N, P, K). After 2–3 months, the material becomes dark, crumbly, nutrient-rich compost.

Why is Vermicomposting considered superior to regular composting?Medium
Show Answer

Earthworms break down organic matter faster, produce finer compost with higher nutrient availability, improve soil aeration, and the castings contain beneficial microbes that enhance soil life.

Explain why a farmer growing legumes (peas) needs less nitrogen fertiliser for the next crop.Medium
Show Answer

Legume roots harbour Rhizobium bacteria that fix atmospheric nitrogen into the soil. After harvest, decaying roots release this fixed nitrogen, enriching the soil for subsequent crops.

What is the difference between a macro-nutrient and a micro-nutrient for plants? Give one example each.Medium
Show Answer

Macro-nutrients needed in large quantities (e.g., nitrogen for protein synthesis). Micro-nutrients needed in tiny amounts (e.g., zinc for enzyme function).

Describe how eutrophication occurs as a result of fertiliser use.Medium
Show Answer

Fertiliser runoff carries nitrates/phosphates into lakes/rivers → algae grow rapidly (algal bloom) → algae decompose using up dissolved oxygen → fish and aquatic organisms die from hypoxia.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the long-term consequences of India's Green Revolution fertiliser-heavy farming model on soil health.Hard
Show Answer

Decades of heavy urea/DAP use have acidified Punjab soils, reduced organic matter, disrupted microbial ecosystems, and caused micronutrient deficiencies (zinc, iron). Yields are now stagnating despite increased inputs, indicating soil degradation.

Analyse the trade-off between using chemical fertilisers for immediate high yield versus manures for long-term soil fertility.Hard
Show Answer

Chemical fertilisers maximise short-term yield (feeding growing population now) but degrade soil structure, require increasing doses over time, and pollute water. Manures build soil health slowly; yields are lower initially but sustainable long-term. Integrated use is optimal.

Design a nutrient management plan for a small organic farm that avoids synthetic fertilisers entirely.Hard
Show Answer

Rotate legumes (fix N) → apply vermicompost (NPK + microbes) → use green manure crops (Sesbania) between seasons → apply bone meal for phosphorus, wood ash for potassium → test soil annually and adjust organically.

Critically assess whether biofertilisers (Rhizobium/Azotobacter inoculants) can fully replace synthetic nitrogen fertilisers at industrial scale.Hard
Show Answer

Biofertilisers fix 20–200 kg N/ha/year which is insufficient for high-yield varieties needing 120–200 kg N/ha. They work best in combination with reduced chemical N input. Full replacement would lower yields significantly, threatening food security at current population levels.

A MYP student claims "organic farming is always more sustainable than conventional farming." Evaluate this claim using evidence.Hard
Show Answer

True: organic farming improves soil health, biodiversity, and reduces water pollution. False: organic yields are 20–25% lower, requiring more land for same output (potentially deforesting more area). Life-cycle analysis shows trade-offs; neither system is universally "better."

Explain how soil pH affects nutrient availability and why over-fertilisation can lock out nutrients even when present in soil.Hard
Show Answer

Nutrients dissolve optimally at pH 6–7. Excess nitrogen fertiliser acidifies soil; at pH below 5.5, phosphorus becomes insoluble (locked to aluminium/iron), iron becomes toxic, and microbial activity drops — plants show deficiency symptoms despite high soil nutrient content.

Using systems thinking, map how chemical fertiliser use in fields connects to dead zones in the ocean.Hard
Show Answer

Farm runoff (N, P) → rivers → coastal areas → algal blooms → decomposition consumes O₂ → hypoxic "dead zones" form (e.g., Gulf of Mexico). Fish populations collapse, affecting fisheries and coastal communities. Full feedback loop damages the same food system the fertiliser was meant to enhance.

Propose a government policy that would incentivise balanced fertiliser use while maintaining food production targets.Hard
Show Answer

Subsidise soil testing kits so farmers apply only what's needed. Cap urea subsidies, redirect savings to manure/biofertiliser support. Mandate nutrient management plans for large farms. Reward organic certification with premium market access. Penalise excess fertiliser use via soil health cards.

Weed and Pest Control

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Define weeds and give two examples.Easy
Show Answer

Weeds are unwanted plants growing among crops that compete for nutrients, water, and sunlight. Examples: wild grasses (Phalaris), parthenium (Congress grass).

What are pesticides? Name two types.Easy
Show Answer

Pesticides are chemicals used to kill organisms harmful to crops. Types: insecticides (kill insects), herbicides (kill weeds), fungicides (kill fungi).

What is the main problem with using chemical pesticides on crops?Easy
Show Answer

Pesticides kill beneficial organisms, accumulate in food chains (bioaccumulation), and can contaminate soil and water.

Name one physical/mechanical method of weed control.Easy
Show Answer

Manual weeding (pulling weeds by hand) or hoeing between crop rows are physical methods of weed control.

What is Integrated Pest Management (IPM)?Easy
Show Answer

IPM is a strategy that combines biological, cultural, mechanical, and minimal chemical methods to control pests in an eco-friendly way.

Give one example of biological pest control.Easy
Show Answer

Releasing ladybirds (ladybugs) to eat aphids on crop plants is a biological control method.

Why do weeds reduce crop yield?Easy
Show Answer

Weeds compete with crops for water, nutrients, sunlight, and space, reducing the resources available for the crop plants to grow.

What is bioaccumulation of pesticides?Easy
Show Answer

Bioaccumulation is the build-up of pesticides (like DDT) in an organism's tissues over time. It increases up the food chain (biomagnification), harming top predators.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain how crop rotation helps reduce pest populations without chemicals.Medium
Show Answer

Rotating crops breaks pest life cycles. Pests adapted to one crop cannot survive when a different crop is planted, reducing their populations naturally without chemical intervention.

A farmer notices her wheat crop is infested with aphids. Suggest an IPM approach.Medium
Show Answer

IPM approach: introduce predatory ladybirds (biological), remove heavily infested plants (mechanical), plant aphid-repelling plants nearby (cultural), apply neem oil spray (natural pesticide) only if infestation is severe.

Differentiate between a herbicide and an insecticide in terms of target organism and an example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Herbicide targets unwanted plants/weeds (e.g., 2,4-D). Insecticide targets insects (e.g., chlorpyrifos). Both are pesticides but with different target organisms.

Explain why DDT was banned despite being very effective at killing mosquitoes.Medium
Show Answer

DDT is fat-soluble and persistent; it bioaccumulates in food chains (biomagnifies). It thinned eggshells in birds of prey causing population collapse, and residues entered human food and breast milk, causing health risks.

How does mulching help in weed control?Medium
Show Answer

Mulching covers the soil surface between crop rows with straw, plastic, or organic material, blocking sunlight from reaching weed seeds and preventing germination without chemicals.

Why is timing of weeding critical in crop production?Medium
Show Answer

Weeds are most competitive in the "critical period" (first 3–6 weeks). Weeding after crops establish wastes effort; before this window, weeds take resources from young, vulnerable seedlings causing maximum yield loss.

Describe how Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) is used as a biological pesticide.Medium
Show Answer

Bt produces crystal proteins (Cry toxins) that are only toxic to specific insect larvae (e.g., caterpillars). When sprayed on crops, it kills target pests while being safe for humans, birds, and other insects.

Compare chemical weed control and manual weeding in terms of cost, speed, and environmental impact.Medium
Show Answer

Chemical (herbicides): fast, low labour cost, covers large areas; environmental risk (soil/water pollution). Manual weeding: slow, labour-intensive, costly at scale; no chemical pollution, preserves soil biology.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Analyse how the widespread use of herbicide-resistant GM crops creates a "superweeds" problem.Hard
Show Answer

When herbicide-resistant GM crops are grown, farmers apply more herbicide liberally. Natural selection favours weed variants with resistance genes. Over generations, "superweeds" emerge that cannot be killed by the same herbicide, requiring stronger or multiple chemicals — escalating the chemical arms race.

Evaluate IPM as a comprehensive solution to both yield protection and environmental conservation.Hard
Show Answer

IPM reduces chemical use (lower pollution, safer food), preserves natural predators, lowers resistance development. Limitations: requires more knowledge, monitoring, and management skill; may not control sudden severe outbreaks; biological agents can take time to establish. Overall superior to sole reliance on chemicals but needs training.

Using biomagnification, explain why eagles and other apex predators suffered most from DDT even though they never directly consumed DDT-sprayed crops.Hard
Show Answer

DDT in water → absorbed by algae (0.000003 ppm) → small fish eat algae (0.5 ppm) → large fish eat small fish (2 ppm) → eagles eat large fish (25+ ppm). Each step concentrates DDT 10–100x. Apex predators accumulate lethal doses without any direct exposure to the source.

Design a field study to compare crop yield and biodiversity under IPM versus conventional pesticide management over one growing season.Hard
Show Answer

Two adjacent fields of equal size, same crop variety and soil. IPM field: biological controls + threshold-based minimal pesticides. Conventional: standard spray calendar. Measure: crop yield (kg/ha), insect species count (traps), beneficial insect populations, soil microbial diversity. Analyse after harvest. Repeat three seasons for reliability.

Critically discuss the ethical implications of using glyphosate (Roundup) in herbicide-resistant GM soybean farming.Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: higher yields, lower tillage reducing erosion. Ethical concerns: WHO classified glyphosate as "probable carcinogen"; corporate control over seed supply (Monsanto/Bayer); soil microbiome disruption (kills beneficial organisms); economic dependency of farmers; trade concentration threatening food sovereignty.

Propose how traditional Indian farming practices (like intercropping and neem-based pesticides) can be incorporated into modern sustainable agriculture.Hard
Show Answer

Intercropping (sorghum + pigeonpea) confuses pests, reduces monoculture vulnerability. Neem (azadirachtin) is an effective biopesticide, biodegradable, and cheap. Integrating these practices with modern IPM monitoring reduces synthetic pesticide use by 40–60% in field trials. Government certification of neem products would support wider adoption.

A country bans all synthetic pesticides. Predict the likely short-term and long-term agricultural outcomes.Hard
Show Answer

Short-term: crop losses 20–40% as pest populations surge; food prices rise; imports increase. Long-term (5–10 years): natural predator populations recover, soil health improves, farmers develop IPM expertise, biodiversity increases. Net food security depends on speed of transition and government support during adjustment period.

How does the concept of "economic threshold" in IPM prevent unnecessary pesticide application?Hard
Show Answer

Economic threshold is the pest population level at which the crop yield loss exceeds the cost of control. Below this level, it is uneconomical to spray. This prevents prophylactic pesticide use, reducing chemical load, resistance development, and costs while maintaining profitability.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What is threshing?Easy
Show Answer

Threshing is the process of separating grain from stalks and husks after harvesting, by beating the crop or using a threshing machine.

What is winnowing?Easy
Show Answer

Winnowing is the process of separating grain from chaff by pouring the mixture in a breeze or using a fan, so light chaff blows away and heavy grains fall.

Name two conditions required for proper grain storage.Easy
Show Answer

Grains should be stored in dry, cool, rodent-proof, and insect-proof conditions to prevent spoilage.

What is a silo?Easy
Show Answer

A silo is a large airtight cylindrical tower used to store bulk quantities of grain under controlled (dry, cool, pest-free) conditions.

What is combine harvesting?Easy
Show Answer

A combine harvester is a machine that reaps, threshes, and winnows the crop in a single operation in the field.

Name two organisms that can damage stored grains.Easy
Show Answer

Rodents (rats and mice) and insects (weevils, moths) damage stored grains.

Why must grain moisture be reduced before storage?Easy
Show Answer

Moisture encourages fungal growth (mould) and insect activity which can spoil grains rapidly. Dry grain (moisture below 14%) stores safely.

What is the function of a granary?Easy
Show Answer

A granary is a storehouse for threshed grain, designed to protect it from rain, humidity, pests, and rodents.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain why post-harvest losses are a major food security concern in India.Medium
Show Answer

India loses 10–20% of harvested grain to poor storage (pests, moisture, rodents). This means producing more food than actually reaches consumers. Reducing these losses could feed millions more without growing extra crops.

Compare traditional (clay pot) grain storage with modern silos.Medium
Show Answer

Clay pots: cheap, low capacity, some insulation, not rodent-proof for large quantities. Silos: large capacity, airtight, temperature-controlled, rodent and insect proof; expensive to build but far lower post-harvest losses.

How does fumigation protect stored grain from insects?Medium
Show Answer

Fumigants (e.g., aluminium phosphide) release toxic gas in sealed storage that kills insects at all life stages (eggs, larvae, adults) without leaving harmful residue when properly aerated before use.

A farmer harvests paddy in monsoon season. What specific storage challenge does he face and how should he address it?Medium
Show Answer

Monsoon brings high humidity, which causes grain to absorb moisture, promoting fungal growth. He should sun-dry the grain to below 14% moisture, use airtight metal bins, and add moisture absorbers before sealing for storage.

Describe the sequence of operations from standing crop to stored grain.Medium
Show Answer

Harvesting (cut crop) → Threshing (separate grain from stalk) → Winnowing (remove chaff) → Drying (reduce moisture) → Cleaning/grading → Storage in silo/granary.

Why are hermetic (airtight) bags effective for small-scale grain storage by farmers?Medium
Show Answer

Hermetic bags trap the grain in a sealed environment. Insects consume available oxygen and die; CO₂ builds up to lethal levels for pests. No chemicals needed, cheap, and effective for storage up to 6–12 months.

How do rodents cause both direct and indirect losses to stored grain?Medium
Show Answer

Direct: rats eat and carry away grain. Indirect: they contaminate grain with droppings, urine, and hair (causing disease risk), and damage storage structures allowing moisture and more pests to enter.

Explain why cold storage is especially important for fruits and vegetables compared to grains.Medium
Show Answer

Fruits and vegetables have high moisture content and continue metabolic activity (ripening/rotting) after harvest. Cold temperatures slow enzyme activity and microbial growth, extending shelf life from days to weeks.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate India's Food Corporation of India (FCI) storage infrastructure in the context of minimising post-harvest losses.Hard
Show Answer

FCI stores ~80 million tonnes but outdated open-air storage and CAP storage in many states leads to 10–15% losses. Modern silos cover only a fraction of needs. Governance, logistics, and investment gaps mean grain rots while people go hungry — a policy failure as much as a technical one.

Analyse how food losses at the harvest and storage stage differ between high-income and low-income countries.Hard
Show Answer

Low-income countries: losses are mostly at harvest/storage stage (poor infrastructure, no cold chain, pest infestation). High-income countries: losses mostly at retail and consumer stage (over-purchasing, cosmetic standards rejection). Both waste ~1/3 of food but at different points in the chain.

Design a cost-effective grain storage solution for a small farmer (1 hectare, no electricity) in rural India.Hard
Show Answer

Solution: PICS (Purdue Improved Crop Storage) triple-layer hermetic bags. Steps: sun-dry grain to <14% moisture, fill bags in dry conditions, seal all three layers, store on raised platform (off damp ground). Cost ~₹200 per bag, no fumigants, effective 12+ months. Affordable, low-tech, proven technology.

Critically evaluate the use of chemical fumigants versus modified atmosphere storage as grain preservation methods.Hard
Show Answer

Fumigants (phosphine) are cheap, effective against all pests, but toxic to humans if improperly handled, and resistance is developing in some insect populations. Modified atmosphere (CO₂/N₂): no residues, no resistance, consumer-friendly, but expensive equipment needed. For national-scale storage, fumigants remain practical; modified atmosphere suits premium/organic markets.

How does reducing post-harvest loss contribute more efficiently to food security than increasing crop production?Hard
Show Answer

Growing more crops requires land, water, fertiliser, and labour inputs. Reducing post-harvest loss "creates" food from existing production at much lower cost. Eliminating India's 10% grain loss would feed ~130 million people annually — more efficient than equivalent area expansion of farmland.

Evaluate the social and economic factors that prevent small-scale farmers from investing in better storage technology.Hard
Show Answer

Lack of capital for upfront investment, low awareness of modern options, distress selling immediately at harvest (debt pressure means no time to store for better prices), lack of market linkages, and inadequate extension services all prevent technology adoption even when it is cost-effective long-term.

Propose how a school or community can reduce food waste using principles of good storage science.Hard
Show Answer

Apply FIFO (first in, first out) for food stocks. Store dry grains in airtight containers. Refrigerate perishables immediately. Compost unavoidable waste. Track waste amounts weekly to identify patterns. Educate students on moisture, temperature, and pest prevention. Connect learning to NCERT crop storage principles.

Assess whether digitisation of grain storage management (IoT sensors, humidity monitors) is a viable solution for India's storage problem.Hard
Show Answer

IoT sensors can detect temperature, humidity, and pest activity in silos in real time, enabling early intervention. Viable for FCI mega-silos and state warehouses. Not yet viable for 100 million small farmers without smartphones and electricity. A tiered solution is needed: IoT for government/large storage, PICS bags for small farmers.

Explain two positive and two negative outcomes of the Green Revolution in India.Medium

How does growing GMO Bt cotton reduce pesticide use?Medium

Compare organic and conventional farming with respect to yield, cost, and environmental impact.Medium
Show Answer

Organic: lower yields (~20% less), higher input labour, better soil health, lower pollution. Conventional: higher yields, lower per-unit cost, greater environmental impact (pesticides, fertilisers, water use).

How does loss of crop biodiversity threaten food security?Medium
Show Answer

Monocultures of few HYV varieties are genetically uniform. A single new pathogen or climate shift can destroy entire regional harvests. Biodiversity provides genetic options for resistance and adaptation.

Explain the concept of "sustainable development" as applied to agriculture.Medium
Show Answer

Sustainable agriculture meets today's food needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs — maintaining soil health, water availability, biodiversity, and economic viability.

How does agroforestry help in climate change adaptation?Medium
Show Answer

Trees provide shade (reducing heat stress on crops), hold water in soil, sequester carbon, break wind, and provide additional income — making farms more resilient to climate extremes.

Describe how permaculture differs from conventional farming in its approach to crop management.Medium
Show Answer

Permaculture designs farming systems that mimic natural ecosystems with diverse perennial plants, minimal soil disturbance, closed nutrient cycles, and no synthetic inputs — compared to conventional annual monocultures with external inputs.

Why is crop genetic diversity maintained in seed banks important for future food security?Medium
Show Answer

Seed banks preserve thousands of crop varieties including ancient landraces with unique resistance genes. Future breeders can access these to develop new varieties resistant to emerging diseases or climate conditions that existing crops cannot tolerate.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Critically evaluate the Green Revolution's legacy: did it solve India's food problem or create new problems?Hard
Show Answer

It solved acute hunger (self-sufficiency achieved by 1970s). However, it concentrated benefits in irrigated regions (Punjab/Haryana), left dryland farmers behind, degraded soils, depleted water, reduced crop diversity, and created health impacts. A partial success with serious unintended consequences requiring correction.

Analyse the ethical debate surrounding Golden Rice (GM rice with beta-carotene) as a solution to vitamin A deficiency in developing countries.Hard
Show Answer

Pro: could prevent 500,000 cases of childhood blindness/year; GM modification is precise and targeted. Anti: dietary diversity (vegetables) is a more sustainable and culturally appropriate solution; corporate control of seed; unknown long-term effects; undermines push for food system diversity rather than silver-bullet GM solutions.

Evaluate whether India can feed its projected 1.7 billion population by 2050 through sustainable farming alone.Hard
Show Answer

Sustainable farming's ~20% lower yields per hectare vs. conventional means significant land expansion would be needed. However, reducing food waste (40% of food wasted), shifting diets (less meat), and combining precision technology with agroecological practices could close the gap. Unlikely purely organically; a hybrid approach is more realistic.

Discuss how climate change will alter the Kharif–Rabi crop calendar and what adaptation strategies farmers will need.Hard
Show Answer

Delayed monsoon onset shifts Kharif sowing windows. Rising temperatures shorten Rabi growing season. More intense rain increases flood risk. Farmers need heat-tolerant varieties, flexible irrigation, updated agronomic calendars based on climate forecasts, and crop insurance to manage these risks.

Design a sustainable farming model for a 2-hectare farm in semi-arid Telangana that addresses water scarcity, soil health, and income stability.Hard
Show Answer

Components: drip-irrigated Kharif crop (groundnut/pigeonpea intercrop); rain water harvesting pond; vermicompost unit from crop residues; Rabi vegetables for local market; 20% land in agroforestry (teak/neem); solar pump for irrigation. This diversifies income, builds soil, conserves water, and reduces climate risk.

Assess the role of farmer cooperatives in transitioning rural India from conventional to sustainable agriculture.Hard
Show Answer

Cooperatives pool resources for collective drip irrigation systems, shared organic certification costs, bulk purchase of biofertilisers, and market access for premium organic produce. They reduce individual financial risk of transition. Successful examples: Amul (dairy), VDSA (Andhra organic). Key barrier: formation and governance challenges in fragmented farming communities.

Using the UN SDG framework, explain how sustainable agriculture contributes to at least three different Sustainable Development Goals.Hard
Show Answer

SDG 2 (Zero Hunger): sustainable yields feed populations long-term. SDG 6 (Clean Water): drip irrigation and no-chemical farming protect water quality. SDG 13 (Climate Action): carbon farming, agroforestry, and reduced synthetic input emissions lower agriculture's carbon footprint. SDG 15 (Life on Land): biodiversity preservation through organic and diverse farming systems.

Critically compare the "Gene Revolution" (GMOs) and the "Agroecological Revolution" as competing visions for the future of farming.Hard
Show Answer

Gene Revolution: technology-intensive, corporate-driven, high-yield precision modifications; suits large commercial farms; risks biodiversity loss and corporate dependency. Agroecological Revolution: knowledge-intensive, farmer-driven, ecosystem-based; suits small farms; addresses root causes of unsustainability but requires significant knowledge transfer. Both have evidence of success; ideal future likely blends precision biotechnology within agroecological frameworks.
