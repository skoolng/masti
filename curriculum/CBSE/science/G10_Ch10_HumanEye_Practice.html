Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

Name the transparent front surface of the eye that first refracts incoming light.
Show Answer

The cornea is the transparent curved surface at the front of the eye. It performs the initial and major bending (refraction) of light entering the eye.

Easy
What is the function of the iris in the human eye?
Show Answer

The iris is the coloured muscular diaphragm that controls the size of the pupil, thereby regulating the amount of light entering the eye.

Easy
Define the pupil and explain what causes its size to change.
Show Answer

The pupil is the circular aperture (opening) in the centre of the iris. In bright light the iris muscles contract to make the pupil smaller; in dim light they relax to make it larger, allowing more light in.

Easy
What is the role of the crystalline lens in the eye?
Show Answer

The crystalline lens is a flexible, transparent biconvex structure that fine-tunes focus by changing its curvature (accommodation) to form a sharp image on the retina.

Easy
Name the fluid that fills the space between the cornea and the lens.
Show Answer

Aqueous humour – a clear, watery fluid that maintains the shape of the front of the eye and supplies nutrients to the cornea and lens.

Easy
Where on the retina is vision sharpest, and what cells are found there?
Show Answer

Vision is sharpest at the fovea (yellow spot), which is densely packed with cone cells responsible for colour and fine-detail vision in bright light.

Easy
What is the blind spot and why does it produce no vision?
Show Answer

The blind spot (optic disc) is the point where the optic nerve exits the eye. It has no photoreceptors (no rods or cones), so light falling on it produces no visual signal.

Easy
State the function of the vitreous humour.
Show Answer

Vitreous humour is the jelly-like fluid filling the large space between the lens and the retina. It maintains the spherical shape of the eyeball and transmits light to the retina.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

Trace the path of a ray of light from an object outside the eye to the optic nerve, naming each structure it passes through.
Show Answer

Light enters through the cornea → passes through aqueous humour → through the pupil → refracted by the crystalline lens → passes through vitreous humour → strikes retinal photoreceptors (rods/cones) → signal travels along the optic nerve to the brain.

Medium
Why is the image formed on the retina inverted? How does the brain handle this?
Show Answer

The cornea and lens act as a convex lens system, forming a real, inverted image on the retina (beyond 2F focusing). The brain (visual cortex) processes and reinterprets the signals so we perceive the world right-side up.

Medium
Distinguish between rods and cones in terms of location, stimulus detected, and function.
Show Answer

Rods: distributed across the peripheral retina; sensitive to low light intensity; detect black-and-white/dim-light vision. Cones: concentrated at the fovea; require bright light; detect colour (three types for red, green, blue) and fine detail.

Medium
Explain why aqueous humour is more important for eye health than vitreous humour.
Show Answer

Aqueous humour is continuously produced and drained, supplying nutrients and oxygen to the avascular cornea and lens. Vitreous humour is largely static gel. Blocked drainage of aqueous humour raises intra-ocular pressure causing glaucoma, damaging the optic nerve.

Medium
What structural feature of the cornea allows it to refract light without a lens?
Show Answer

The cornea's curved, dome-shaped surface creates an interface between air (n ≈ 1.00) and the aqueous humour (n ≈ 1.34). This large difference in refractive index causes significant bending of light even before it reaches the lens, accounting for about 70% of the eye's total refracting power.

Medium
A person can see in a dimly lit room but struggles to distinguish colours. Which photoreceptors are working and which are not? Why?
Show Answer

Rods are active — they are highly sensitive and function in low light. Cones are inactive because they need bright light to respond. This is why colour vision is poor in dim conditions (scotopic vision).

Medium
How does the choroid layer contribute to clear vision?
Show Answer

The choroid is a dark, pigmented layer between the retina and sclera. Its dark pigment absorbs stray light inside the eye, preventing internal reflection and scattering that would blur the image on the retina. It also supplies blood to the outer retina.

Medium
Compare the refracting power of the cornea and the crystalline lens. Which contributes more?
Show Answer

The cornea contributes about +43 dioptres (roughly 70% of total power) because of the large refractive index change at the air-cornea boundary. The lens contributes about +15–25 dioptres (varying with accommodation). The cornea is the primary refracting surface; the lens fine-tunes focus.

Medium

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

The total refracting power of the eye is approximately 60 D. Explain how this value changes when viewing a near object versus a distant object, and which structure is responsible.
Show Answer

When viewing a distant object, the lens is flattened (minimum curvature) and total power is ~60 D. For a near object, ciliary muscles contract, the lens becomes more curved and thicker, increasing its power by up to ~15 D, raising total power to ~75 D. The crystalline lens alone changes; the cornea's contribution stays constant.

Hard
Cataract causes the lens to become opaque. Predict two effects on vision and explain the surgical correction used today.
Show Answer

Effects: (1) Reduced light transmission — less light reaches the retina, causing dim, hazy vision. (2) Loss of transparency causes scattering, producing glare and blurred images, especially at night. Surgical correction: phacoemulsification — the cloudy lens is broken up by ultrasound and removed; an artificial intra-ocular lens (IOL) with fixed focal power is implanted in its place.

Hard
Evaluate how the eye's optical system differs from a simple camera, focusing on how each forms and adjusts focus.
Show Answer

Camera: fixed lens shape; focus adjusted by moving the lens closer/farther from the sensor; aperture controlled by mechanical diaphragm; sensor is flat. Eye: adjusts focus by changing the curvature (shape) of the lens (accommodation) — lens-to-retina distance is fixed; aperture controlled by the iris (muscle); retina is curved. The eye's system is more compact and dynamic; the camera provides higher resolution and no chromatic aberration correction issues.

Hard
Why do some people who undergo LASIK surgery no longer need spectacles? Relate your answer to the refracting power of the cornea.
Show Answer

LASIK uses a laser to reshape the cornea — it changes the curvature of the cornea permanently. Since the cornea provides ~70% of the eye's refracting power, correcting its curvature corrects the refractive error (myopia/hypermetropia/astigmatism) at source, eliminating the need for an external corrective lens (spectacles or contacts).

Hard
Using MYP Criterion B (Inquiring and Designing), design an experiment to measure the blind spot location in the human eye without any equipment.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: The blind spot exists at a specific angular position where the optic nerve exits. Method: Draw a cross (x) and a dot (.) 6 cm apart on paper. Cover the left eye. Hold the paper ~30 cm from the right eye, focusing on the cross. Slowly bring the paper closer — the dot disappears at a specific distance (blind spot). Variables: Distance (independent), visibility of dot (dependent), eye used (controlled). Result: The blind spot is ~15 degrees temporal to the fovea. No equipment needed beyond paper and pen.

Hard
Explain why nocturnal animals (e.g., owls) have a much higher rod-to-cone ratio than humans, and predict one trade-off this creates.
Show Answer

Nocturnal animals need maximum sensitivity in low-light conditions, so a very high rod density allows them to detect even single photons. Rods also have larger photoreceptive fields and pool signals, amplifying dim light. Trade-off: Poor colour vision and reduced fine-detail (acuity) in daylight, because cone density is low. Owls compensate by having a large eye diameter (larger pupil = more light) but fixed eyes requiring head rotation for tracking.

Hard
The intra-ocular pressure (IOP) of a healthy eye is 10–21 mmHg. Explain what maintains this pressure and what happens when it is chronically elevated.
Show Answer

IOP is maintained by the balance between aqueous humour production (by the ciliary body) and drainage (through the trabecular meshwork into Schlemm's canal). If drainage is blocked, fluid accumulates → elevated IOP → glaucoma: the raised pressure compresses and damages the optic nerve fibres, causing progressive peripheral vision loss, potentially leading to blindness if untreated.

Hard
Analyse the statement: "The eye is the most sophisticated optical instrument." Discuss two ways it surpasses man-made optical systems and two ways it falls short.
Show Answer

Surpasses: (1) Dynamic accommodation — changes lens shape in milliseconds, no mechanical parts needed. (2) Adaptive aperture — pupil responds to light levels continuously in real time, far faster than mechanical shutters. Falls short: (1) Chromatic aberration — the lens disperses different wavelengths to slightly different focal points; camera lenses with achromatic doublets correct this. (2) Limited resolution — ~1 arc-minute; high-quality telescopes/microscopes achieve far higher angular resolution. Also, the eye cannot zoom or record.

Accommodation

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

Define accommodation of the eye.
Show Answer

Accommodation is the ability of the eye to adjust its focal length by changing the curvature of the crystalline lens, allowing it to form sharp images of objects at different distances.

Easy
What happens to the ciliary muscles when the eye focuses on a near object?
Show Answer

The ciliary muscles contract, releasing tension on the suspensory ligaments. The elastic lens then becomes thicker and more curved (more convex), increasing its refracting power to focus near objects.

Easy
Define the near point of the human eye.
Show Answer

The near point is the closest distance at which the eye can see an object clearly. For a normal adult eye it is approximately 25 cm (the least distance of distinct vision).

Easy
What is the far point of a normal (emmetropic) eye?
Show Answer

The far point of a normal eye is infinity. The eye can see clearly up to an infinite distance when the lens is in its most flattened (relaxed) state.

Easy
Which structure changes shape during accommodation — the cornea or the lens?
Show Answer

The crystalline lens changes shape. The cornea's curvature is fixed; only the lens is flexible enough to change curvature under the action of the ciliary muscles.

Easy
State what happens to the suspensory ligaments when ciliary muscles contract.
Show Answer

When ciliary muscles contract, the ciliary body moves inward, reducing tension on the suspensory ligaments. The ligaments become slack, allowing the elastic lens to bulge (become more convex) due to its own elasticity.

Easy
What is the range of vision of a normal human eye?
Show Answer

The range of vision is from the near point (~25 cm) to the far point (infinity — effectively very large distances). Any object within this range can be brought into sharp focus by accommodation.

Easy
Name the unit used to measure the power of a lens.
Show Answer

The unit is the dioptre (D). Power P = 1/f, where f is focal length in metres. A converging lens has positive power; a diverging lens has negative power.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

Explain, step by step, how the eye shifts focus from a distant tree to a book 30 cm away.
Show Answer

1. For the distant tree: ciliary muscles relax — suspensory ligaments taut — lens flattened, long focal length. 2. Looking at the book: ciliary muscles contract — ligaments slacken — lens bulges, shorter focal length, higher power. 3. The increased power converges the diverging rays from the near book onto the retina, forming a sharp image.

Medium
Calculate the power of the eye-lens needed to focus an object at 25 cm. Assume the image is formed 2.5 cm from the lens (eye).
Show Answer

Using lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f. Object u = −25 cm = −0.25 m; image v = +2.5 cm = +0.025 m. 1/f = 1/0.025 − 1/(−0.25) = 40 + 4 = 44 D. This is the maximum power of the eye for near vision.

Medium
Why does prolonged reading cause eye strain? Relate to the ciliary muscles.
Show Answer

During prolonged reading, the ciliary muscles remain continuously contracted to maintain the high-power lens shape needed for near vision. Sustained muscle contraction causes fatigue, leading to eye strain (asthenopia), headache, and blurred vision. Rest allows the muscles to relax.

Medium
The near point of a child is 7 cm and of an elderly person is 200 cm. Compare their accommodation abilities.
Show Answer

The child has a highly elastic, flexible lens capable of very large curvature changes — near point much closer than the standard 25 cm, showing excellent accommodation. The elderly person's lens has lost elasticity (presbyopia) — cannot increase curvature sufficiently, so near point has receded far beyond 25 cm, indicating very poor accommodation ability.

Medium
Why is the near point of a young child closer than 25 cm, while 25 cm is used as the standard least distance of distinct vision?
Show Answer

A young child's lens is highly elastic and can curve more, so they can focus on objects closer than 25 cm. However, 25 cm is the distance at which the average adult can comfortably sustain reading without undue eye strain. It is a physiological convention for standard vision.

Medium
What is the power of the lens when the eye looks at a very distant object? Assume image distance = 2.5 cm.
Show Answer

Object at infinity: u = infinity, so 1/u = 0. 1/f = 1/v = 1/0.025 = 40 D. This is the minimum power of the eye, corresponding to the fully relaxed, flattened lens.

Medium
Explain why accommodation is considered an active (energy-consuming) process.
Show Answer

Accommodation requires the ciliary muscles to contract, which consumes ATP (cellular energy). The resting state is the lens flattened (far vision) — maintained by passive tension in the suspensory ligaments. Near vision requires active muscle work, which is why sustained near work fatigues the eye.

Medium
A person's accommodation amplitude decreases from 14 D at age 10 to 1 D at age 60. What does this tell you about the lens over time?
Show Answer

The lens becomes progressively less elastic and more rigid with age (due to protein changes — crystallin hardening). By age 60, the lens can barely change curvature regardless of ciliary muscle effort, severely reducing the range over which focus can be adjusted. This is presbyopia.

Medium

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

The power of accommodation of the human eye is defined as the difference between maximum and minimum lens power. A person has a near point of 10 cm and a far point of infinity. Calculate the accommodation amplitude.
Show Answer

Min power (far, v = 2.5 cm): P_min = 1/0.025 = 40 D. Max power (near, u = −10 cm, v = +2.5 cm): 1/f = 1/0.025 + 1/0.10 = 40 + 10 = 50 D. Amplitude = 50 − 40 = 10 D.

Hard
Critically evaluate this statement: "The eye and a camera are equivalent optical systems." Where does accommodation make them fundamentally different?
Show Answer

A camera focuses by moving the lens (changing lens-to-sensor distance) — the lens shape stays fixed. The eye focuses by changing lens shape (accommodation) — the lens-to-retina distance is fixed. This is a fundamental architectural difference. The camera requires mechanical movement; the eye uses biological muscle and elasticity. The eye's system is more robust for a fixed-size organ, but the camera can use multiple lens elements to correct aberrations impossible for the single natural lens.

Hard
Explain the Helmholtz model of accommodation and how it is supported by observations in presbyopia.
Show Answer

Helmholtz proposed: resting ciliary muscle → taut zonules → lens flattened (distance). Contracted ciliary muscle → slack zonules → lens rounds up due to its own elasticity (near vision). In presbyopia, this model predicts that even when ciliary muscles contract fully, the stiffened lens does not round up — which is exactly what is observed. The zonules slacken but the lens stays flat, confirming that lens elasticity, not muscle strength, is the limiting factor in ageing.

Hard
Why cannot the eye maintain focus on both a near and a far object simultaneously? Link to the single fixed focal length at any instant.
Show Answer

At any instant the lens has one specific curvature, hence one focal length f. The lens equation 1/v − 1/u = 1/f allows only one object distance u to produce a focused image at the fixed retinal distance v. Two objects at different distances have different u values and would require different f values simultaneously — physically impossible with one lens. The eye rapidly shifts between focal lengths (saccadic accommodation) creating the illusion of simultaneous focus.

Hard
An ophthalmologist measures that a patient's near point has shifted from 25 cm to 100 cm. Calculate the power of the reading lens required to restore the near point to 25 cm.
Show Answer

The lens must form a virtual image of an object at 25 cm at the patient's near point (100 cm) on the same side. u = −0.25 m, v = −1.00 m (virtual, same side). 1/f = 1/v − 1/u = 1/(−1.00) − 1/(−0.25) = −1 + 4 = +3 D. A +3 D convex lens is needed.

Hard
How does depth of field relate to the pupil size, and how does this interact with accommodation in bright versus dim light?
Show Answer

A small pupil (bright light) increases depth of field — more distances appear in focus simultaneously (like a small camera aperture). This reduces the demand for accommodation as slight mis-focus is less noticeable. A large pupil (dim light) reduces depth of field, making the eye more sensitive to focus error and increasing the need for precise accommodation. However, in dim light rods dominate and the brain tolerates more blur, partially compensating.

Hard
Design an MYP Criterion B investigation to determine how accommodation speed changes with age using volunteer subjects aged 10, 30, and 60.
Show Answer

Research question: How does age affect the speed of accommodation? Method: Volunteer reads a standard text card at 25 cm; card is quickly swapped with one at 4 m; time until clear is measured with a stopwatch. Repeat 5 times each direction. Variables: Age group (independent), time to focus (dependent), lighting/text size/eye health (controlled). Expected data: Age 10 fastest; age 60 slowest. Analysis: ANOVA or t-test between groups. Ethics: consent, no strain induced.

Hard
Bifocal lenses have two regions of different power. Explain using optics principles why this design helps a presbyopic patient with both near and distance vision.
Show Answer

A presbyopic patient cannot accommodate — their lens stays near-flat. Lower portion of the bifocal has a convex (+) lens of power needed for near vision (e.g., +2.5 D), converging near rays onto the retina. Upper portion has a weaker lens (or plano lens) for distance. The patient looks down through the near-zone for reading and straight ahead/up through the distance zone — mimicking the accommodation the natural lens can no longer perform.

Myopia (Short-sightedness)

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

Define myopia.
Show Answer

Myopia (short-sightedness) is a refractive defect in which a person can see near objects clearly but cannot see distant objects clearly because the image of a distant object is focused in front of the retina.

Easy
Where does the image form in a myopic eye for a distant object?
Show Answer

In a myopic eye, the image of a distant object forms in front of the retina (between the lens and retina), not on it, causing the image to appear blurred.

Easy
State two physical causes of myopia.
Show Answer

(1) The eyeball is too long (axial myopia) — the retina is further back than normal. (2) The lens is too curved (too powerful) — converges light too strongly so it focuses before reaching the retina.

Easy
What type of lens is used to correct myopia?
Show Answer

A concave (diverging) lens is used. It diverges the incoming parallel rays before they enter the eye, so the eye's own optical system then focuses them on the retina.

Easy
What is the far point of a myopic eye?
Show Answer

The far point of a myopic eye is closer than infinity — it is the farthest point the myopic eye can see clearly without correction. For example, a person with −2 D myopia has a far point of 50 cm.

Easy
Does the power of the corrective lens for myopia carry a positive or negative sign? Why?
Show Answer

Negative (−) power, because the corrective lens is concave (diverging). A diverging lens has a negative focal length, hence negative power in dioptres.

Easy
A person with myopia wears spectacles of power −3 D. What is the focal length of the lens?
Show Answer

f = 1/P = 1/(−3) = −0.33 m = −33 cm. The negative sign confirms it is a concave (diverging) lens.

Easy
Name two lifestyle/environmental factors believed to contribute to increasing rates of myopia in children.
Show Answer

(1) Excessive near work (reading, screen time) — keeps ciliary muscles contracted, possibly encouraging axial elongation. (2) Insufficient outdoor time — bright outdoor light and viewing distant objects are thought to slow eyeball elongation.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

A myopic student can see clearly up to a maximum distance of 50 cm. Calculate the power of the corrective lens needed.
Show Answer

Far point = 50 cm = 0.50 m. The lens must form a virtual image of a distant object (u = infinity) at the far point (v = −0.50 m). 1/f = 1/v − 1/u = 1/(−0.50) − 0 = −2.0 D. Power = −2.0 D (concave lens, f = −50 cm).

Medium
Describe (as a ray diagram description) how image formation differs in a myopic eye vs a normal eye for a distant object at 5 m.
Show Answer

Normal eye: parallel rays from 5 m refracted by the relaxed lens system — focused exactly on the retina — sharp image. Myopic eye: same rays refracted by the overpowered/over-long system — focus forms in front of the retina — by the time light reaches retina, rays have diverged into a blur circle — fuzzy image.

Medium
Explain why myopic people can read without spectacles but cannot see a whiteboard clearly.
Show Answer

In myopia the far point is close (e.g., 30 cm). Near objects at reading distance (25–30 cm) are within the range of clear vision — the eye focuses them on the retina without extra accommodation. Distant objects (e.g., a whiteboard at 3 m) are beyond the far point and form images in front of the retina. So near vision is fine; distance vision is blurred.

Medium
A myopic eye has a far point of 80 cm. Find the focal length and nature of the lens required to correct the defect.
Show Answer

u = infinity (distant object), v = −80 cm = −0.80 m (virtual image at far point). 1/f = 1/(−0.80) − 0 = −1.25. f = −0.80 m = −80 cm. Nature: concave (diverging) lens, P = −1.25 D.

Medium
Why is myopia described as an error in the axial length of the eye rather than a "disease"?
Show Answer

Myopia is a refractive error — the eye is anatomically normal but dimensionally mis-matched. The eyeball is longer than needed for the cornea and lens power. There is no disease process (infection, inflammation, degeneration) in simple myopia. It is a variation in growth, hence correctable with an appropriate lens without treating any pathology.

Medium
A person wears −4 D spectacles. What is their far point? Can they see an object at 20 cm without spectacles?
Show Answer

Far point = 1/4 = 0.25 m = 25 cm. Can they see an object at 20 cm? Yes — 20 cm is within their 25 cm far point, so they can see it clearly without glasses.

Medium
Explain the role of the concave lens in shifting the image back onto the retina for a myopic eye.
Show Answer

The concave lens diverges parallel rays from a distant object, making them appear to originate from a point closer to the eye (the far point). When the myopic eye receives these diverging rays, it converges them less than parallel rays — the convergence point shifts backward onto the retina. The lens effectively compensates for the eye's excess converging power.

Medium
A myopic student cannot see the board clearly beyond 1 m. A classmate with normal vision can. Compare the far points and what this implies about their eyeball lengths.
Show Answer

Myopic student's far point = 1 m. Normal student's far point = infinity. The myopic student's eyeball is longer than normal — the retina is too far from the lens. For a given lens power, a longer eyeball means parallel rays converge before reaching the retina. The normal student's axial length perfectly matches the lens power, so parallel rays focus exactly on the retina.

Medium

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

High myopia (>−6 D) is associated with retinal detachment risk. Using eye anatomy, explain why an elongated eyeball increases this risk.
Show Answer

An abnormally elongated eyeball stretches the retina and choroid over a larger surface. The retinal tissue becomes thinner, especially at the periphery. The vitreous humour may pull on the stretched retina at weak points, creating tears or holes. Fluid entering through these tears can detach the retina from the choroid. The increased axial length also puts mechanical stress on the retina-choroid adhesion, raising detachment risk substantially above −6 D.

Hard
A child's myopia progresses from −1 D at age 8 to −4 D at age 14. Calculate the change in far point and explain why this progression is clinically significant.
Show Answer

Far point at −1 D: 1/1 = 100 cm. Far point at −4 D: 1/4 = 25 cm. The clear distance range has shrunk dramatically. Clinically significant: rapid axial elongation during childhood increases the lifetime risk of high myopia complications (glaucoma, retinal detachment, macular degeneration). Early intervention (orthokeratology, atropine drops) can slow progression.

Hard
Evaluate: "LASIK surgery permanently cures myopia." Is this statement accurate? Justify.
Show Answer

Partially accurate but not fully. LASIK reshapes the cornea to compensate for the excess refractive power. It corrects the optical defect — patients typically achieve 20/20 vision. However, it does not shorten the elongated eyeball — the underlying axial length remains increased. Therefore, the structural risk factors (retinal thinning, detachment risk) persist. "Cured" is misleading; "optically corrected" is more accurate.

Hard
A myopic student (far point 40 cm) also has a near point of 10 cm. What power spectacle lens corrects their distance vision? After wearing this lens, where is their new near point?
Show Answer

Distance correction: P = −1/0.40 = −2.5 D. With the −2.5 D lens, for an object at distance d to produce an image at the original near point (−10 cm = −0.10 m): 1/(−0.10) = 1/d + (−2.5) → −10 + 2.5 = 1/d → 1/d = −7.5 → d = −0.133 m ≈ 13.3 cm. New near point ≈ 13 cm (slightly farther than 10 cm without glasses).

Hard
Using MYP Criterion D (Reflecting on the Impacts of Science), discuss the societal implications of the global myopia epidemic, particularly among school-age children in East Asia.
Show Answer

Scale: Up to 90% of urban East Asian teenagers are myopic — a dramatic rise over 50 years. Social impact: academic pressure + indoor lifestyles + screen use linked to progression. Economic impact: cost of spectacles, surgery, treatment of complications; lost productivity. Health impact: high myopia risk of blindness from retinal detachment/glaucoma rises. Policy response: some countries mandate outdoor school time (Taiwan, China). MYP reflection: Science provides evidence; policy and individual behaviour must also change — a systems-level problem requiring interdisciplinary solution.

Hard
Derive an expression for the power of the corrective lens in terms of the far point distance d (in metres) for a myopic eye.
Show Answer

The lens must form a virtual image of a distant object (u = infinity) at the far point (v = −d). Lens formula: 1/f = 1/v − 1/u = 1/(−d) − 0 = −1/d. Therefore P = −1/d dioptres. For example, far point = 0.5 m — P = −2 D. The corrective power equals the reciprocal of the far point distance (in metres), with a negative sign.

Hard
Compare the optical principles behind spectacle correction, contact lens correction, and LASIK correction of myopia.
Show Answer

All three reduce the effective refracting power of the myopic eye system. Spectacles: concave lens ~12 mm in front of the eye diverges rays; vertex distance effect means spectacle power differs slightly from contact power. Contact lenses: sit directly on the cornea (~0 mm vertex); same principle but power adjusted for zero vertex distance; also correct some corneal astigmatism. LASIK: permanently ablates corneal stroma to flatten curvature — reduces corneal power at source; no external device needed; irreversible. All produce the same optical outcome (image on retina) by different means.

Hard
A myopic person can read at 15 cm without glasses. After putting on −2.5 D glasses, where is the nearest object they can read?
Show Answer

Without glasses, near point = 15 cm. With −2.5 D glasses (f = −0.4 m), for the image to appear at −0.15 m: 1/(−0.15) = 1/u + 1/(−0.4). 1/u = −6.667 + 2.5 = −4.167. u = −0.24 m = −24 cm. New near point ≈ 24 cm — farther than 15 cm, as expected (distance glasses make near reading slightly harder).

Hypermetropia & Presbyopia

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

Define hypermetropia.
Show Answer

Hypermetropia (far-sightedness/long-sightedness) is a refractive defect where a person can see distant objects more clearly than near objects. The image of a near object is focused behind the retina.

Easy
State the two physical causes of hypermetropia.
Show Answer

(1) The eyeball is too short — the retina is closer to the lens than normal. (2) The lens has insufficient curvature (too weak) — it cannot converge rays enough to focus on the retina.

Easy
What type of lens corrects hypermetropia?
Show Answer

A convex (converging) lens is used to correct hypermetropia. It converges rays before entering the eye, effectively bringing the focal point forward onto the retina.

Easy
Define presbyopia and state its cause.
Show Answer

Presbyopia is an age-related loss of accommodation. It is caused by the gradual hardening and loss of elasticity of the crystalline lens, and weakening of ciliary muscles, making it impossible to focus on near objects. It typically becomes noticeable after age 40–45.

Easy
Where does the image form in a hypermetropic eye for a near object?
Show Answer

In a hypermetropic eye, the image of a near object forms behind the retina (the convergence point would lie beyond the retinal surface), producing a blurred image on the retina.

Easy
What is the near point of a hypermetropic eye compared to a normal eye?
Show Answer

In hypermetropia, the near point is farther than 25 cm from the eye (can be 50 cm, 1 m, or more), unlike the normal near point of 25 cm. The person cannot read comfortably at normal reading distance.

Easy
Why do presbyopic patients sometimes need both reading and distance glasses?
Show Answer

Presbyopia eliminates accommodation — the lens stays nearly fixed in curvature. If the resting eye is also hypermetropic or emmetropic, the person needs a convex lens for near vision. If they are also myopic, they may still see well at distance but need glasses for reading. Bifocals address both needs simultaneously.

Easy
A hypermetropic patient is prescribed +2.5 D lenses. What is the focal length?
Show Answer

f = 1/P = 1/2.5 = +0.40 m = +40 cm. The positive sign and value confirm a convex (converging) lens.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

A hypermetropic person has a near point of 1 m. Calculate the power of the corrective lens to allow reading at 25 cm.
Show Answer

The lens must form a virtual image of a book at 25 cm at the near point (1 m) on the same side. u = −0.25 m, v = −1.00 m. 1/f = 1/(−1.00) − 1/(−0.25) = −1 + 4 = +3 D. A convex lens of +3 D is required.

Medium
Distinguish between myopia and hypermetropia using: (a) which distances are unclear, (b) image position, (c) corrective lens.
Show Answer

(a) Myopia: distance unclear; Hypermetropia: near unclear. (b) Myopia: image in front of retina; Hypermetropia: image behind retina. (c) Myopia: concave (−) lens; Hypermetropia: convex (+) lens.

Medium
Explain why a young person with mild hypermetropia may not need spectacles but an older person with the same degree of hypermetropia does.
Show Answer

A young person with a flexible, elastic lens can over-accommodate — contract ciliary muscles strongly enough to add extra power and focus near objects despite the structural weakness. As they age, presbyopia reduces accommodation, so the same degree of hypermetropia can no longer be compensated. Spectacles then become necessary.

Medium
Why is presbyopia considered a natural ageing process rather than a disease?
Show Answer

Presbyopia results from the universal biological ageing of the crystalline lens — gradual loss of water content, crystallin protein cross-linking, and stiffening. Every human being who lives long enough will develop it. There is no infection, inflammation, or abnormal pathology involved. It is an expected physiological change, like greying hair, treated by corrective lenses not by curative medicine.

Medium
A presbyopic patient's near point is 60 cm and far point is infinity. What lens power allows them to read at 25 cm?
Show Answer

u = −0.25 m, v = −0.60 m. 1/f = 1/(−0.60) − 1/(−0.25) = −1.667 + 4 = +2.33 D. A convex lens of approximately +2.33 D is required.

Medium
Explain why the corrective lens for hypermetropia produces a virtual, erect, magnified image of a near object.
Show Answer

The convex corrective lens is used with the object inside its focal length (object at 25 cm, focal length ~33–40 cm for +2.5–3 D). A convex lens with object inside f acts as a simple magnifier, producing a virtual, erect, magnified image farther from the lens (at the near point of the hypermetropic eye). This virtual image is then focused by the eye's own system on the retina.

Medium
Compare the near points of: (a) a normal adult, (b) a 10-year-old child, (c) a hypermetropic adult, (d) a presbyopic person aged 60.
Show Answer

(a) Normal adult: ~25 cm. (b) Child age 10: ~7–10 cm (highly elastic lens). (c) Hypermetropic adult: >25 cm (e.g., 50 cm–1 m). (d) Presbyopic age 60: may be >1–2 m (severely reduced accommodation). The near point recedes progressively with age due to lens stiffening, and is closer than normal in youth due to high lens elasticity.

Medium
Why might a person with severe hypermetropia experience eyestrain even when looking at distant objects?
Show Answer

In severe hypermetropia, even rays from distant objects require the eye to accommodate slightly to focus them on the retina (the resting, unaccommodated lens is still too weak). The ciliary muscles must remain partially contracted at all times, including for distance vision, causing constant muscle effort and fatigue (eyestrain) even without near work.

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

A person has a near point of 75 cm. They are prescribed +3 D glasses. Verify whether this fully corrects near vision to 25 cm.
Show Answer

With +3 D (f = +0.333 m), object at u = −0.25 m: 1/v = 1/f + 1/u = 3 + (−4) = −1. v = −1.00 m = −100 cm. Image is virtual at 100 cm. Their near point is 75 cm, and the image is at 100 cm which is farther than their near point — so the image is beyond their near point; they cannot see it clearly. +3 D over-corrects. A +2.33 D lens places the image at exactly −75 cm, which is correct.

Hard
Critically compare myopia, hypermetropia, and presbyopia in terms of cause, affected age group, and optical correction principles.
Show Answer

Myopia: long eyeball or over-curved lens; children/young adults; corrected by concave lens (reduces convergence). Hypermetropia: short eyeball or weak lens; can be congenital or any age; corrected by convex lens (adds convergence). Presbyopia: stiff, inelastic lens due to ageing; 40+ years; corrected by convex lens for near; may need bifocals if myopia coexists. Key distinction: myopia/hypermetropia are structural; presbyopia is a dynamic (accommodation) failure. All three involve incorrect image placement on the retina but for different reasons.

Hard
Using MYP Criterion D, discuss the equity implications of uncorrected hypermetropia in children in low-income countries.
Show Answer

Academic impact: Children with uncorrected hypermetropia struggle with reading — near work is painful/blurred. Without diagnosis, they are labelled as slow learners. Equity issue: In high-income countries, school vision screening identifies hypermetropia early; glasses are subsidised. In low-income settings, lack of optometrists, cost of glasses, and poor screening means millions of children receive no correction. Science-society link: WHO estimates 12 million children are visually impaired due to uncorrected refractive error. Solutions: community vision camps, low-cost spectacles (e.g., Aurolabs), teacher training.

Derive the expression for the power of the corrective lens for hypermetropia in terms of the person's near point (d_n) and the standard reading distance (25 cm).
Show Answer

Object at standard distance: u = −0.25 m. Lens must form virtual image at near point: v = −d_n (m). 1/f = 1/v − 1/u = −1/d_n + 4. Therefore P = 4 − 1/d_n dioptres. For d_n = 1 m: P = 4 − 1 = +3 D. For d_n = 0.5 m: P = 4 − 2 = +2 D. Power increases as the near point gets farther.

Hard
A 55-year-old patient has near point 80 cm and far point 2 m. Describe their condition and calculate the power of lenses for (a) reading at 25 cm and (b) distance vision.
Show Answer

Far point = 2 m (not infinity) → still myopic. Near point = 80 cm → presbyopia limiting near vision. Combined: myopic presbyopia. (a) Reading lens: u = −0.25 m, v = −0.80 m. 1/f = 1/(−0.80) − 1/(−0.25) = −1.25 + 4 = +2.75 D. (b) Distance lens: u = infinity, v = −2.00 m. P = −0.5 D (concave). Bifocals: upper −0.5 D for distance, lower +2.75 D for reading.

Hard
Why does accommodation in hypermetropia actually worsen headaches more than in myopia? Link to sustained ciliary muscle effort.
Show Answer

In hypermetropia, the person must accommodate even for distant objects — the ciliary muscles are never fully relaxed. For near work, they must accommodate even more strongly. This sustained, above-normal ciliary muscle contraction causes prolonged fatigue and reflex headaches (especially frontal). In myopia, the person sees near objects without accommodation; ciliary muscles are relaxed for their comfortable distance. Only forced distance viewing causes strain. Therefore, hypermetropia tends to cause more chronic headache from constant over-accommodation.

Hard
How does the treatment of presbyopia with multifocal intra-ocular lenses (IOLs) after cataract surgery differ from bifocal spectacles in principle?
Show Answer

Bifocal spectacles: two separate zones — user must physically move gaze (down for near, straight for distance) to use each zone. Multifocal IOLs: implanted directly inside the eye; use diffractive or refractive optics to simultaneously create multiple focal points from the same aperture — the brain selects the in-focus image. No head movement needed. However, multifocal IOLs can cause halos and glare at night because multiple images are always formed. Bifocals have no such issue but require learned gaze patterns.

Hard
A 14-year-old with +4 D hypermetropia has no symptoms. A 45-year-old with the same +4 D has severe reading difficulty. Explain this using accommodation amplitude data.
Show Answer

A 14-year-old has an accommodation amplitude of ~12–14 D. The +4 D hypermetropia requires 4 D of extra accommodation for distance and ~8 D total for reading — easily within range. So they compensate fully. A 45-year-old has amplitude ~5–6 D. Distance now requires 4 D accommodation (leaving only 1–2 D for near work), and reading (requiring ~8 D total) exceeds total accommodation amplitude — impossible to focus on near objects. Same structural defect, completely different symptom profile due to age-related loss of amplitude.

Dispersion of Light

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

Define dispersion of light.
Show Answer

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its constituent colours (spectrum) when it passes through a prism or any medium whose refractive index varies with wavelength. White light is composed of seven colours: VIBGYOR.

Easy
Write the order of colours in the visible spectrum from least to most deviated when white light passes through a prism.
Show Answer

Least deviated to most deviated: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet (ROYGBIV). Red bends least, violet bends most.

Easy
Why does violet light bend more than red light in a glass prism?
Show Answer

Violet light has a shorter wavelength and higher frequency than red. The refractive index of glass is higher for shorter wavelengths (normal dispersion), so violet light slows down more in glass and is bent (refracted) more at the surface.

Easy
What is the band of colours formed by a prism called?
Show Answer

The band of colours is called the spectrum (or visible spectrum). It ranges from red (~700 nm) to violet (~400 nm) and contains all wavelengths visible to the human eye.

Easy
State Newton's experiment with two prisms related to recombination of white light.
Show Answer

Newton used a first prism to disperse white light into a spectrum, then placed a second inverted prism in the path of the spectrum. The second prism recombined all colours, producing white light again, proving that white light is a mixture of all spectral colours.

Easy
Which colour of visible light has the highest frequency?
Show Answer

Violet has the highest frequency (~7.5 × 10^14 Hz) in the visible spectrum. Higher frequency = shorter wavelength = greater refractive index in glass = more bending.

Easy
What is meant by the refractive index of a medium? How does it relate to the speed of light?
Show Answer

The refractive index (n) of a medium is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum (c) to the speed of light in that medium (v): n = c/v. A higher n means light travels slower in that medium and bends more at the interface.

Easy
Name the scientist who first studied the dispersion of white light through a glass prism.
Show Answer

Sir Isaac Newton first systematically studied dispersion using a glass prism around 1666, demonstrating that white light is composed of a spectrum of colours.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

Explain why a glass prism produces a spectrum but a glass slab does not.
Show Answer

In a glass slab, the two faces are parallel. Different wavelengths exit at different lateral positions but travel in the same direction as the incident ray — they do not separate angularly. In a prism, the two refracting faces are non-parallel (angled). Different wavelengths exit at different angles, producing an angular spread — a visible spectrum. The geometry of non-parallel faces is key.

Medium
The refractive index of glass for red light is 1.51 and for violet light is 1.53. Calculate the difference in the angle of refraction for a ray incident at 30° on a glass surface.
Show Answer

Using Snell's law: sin θ_r = sin 30° / n. For red: sin θ_r = 0.5/1.51 = 0.3311 → θ = 19.33°. For violet: sin θ_v = 0.5/1.53 = 0.3268 → θ = 19.07°. Difference = 0.26°. This small but real angular difference causes the visible separation in a prism.

Medium
Explain the formation of a pure spectrum using a convex lens placed after the prism.
Show Answer

Without a lens, colours mix because each diverging beam spreads. Placing a convex lens after the prism converges each colour to its own focus — since each has a slightly different wavelength, they focus at slightly different positions on a screen, forming a pure spectrum with sharply defined colour bands rather than overlapping fuzzy colours.

Medium
Why is the sky dark for astronauts even when the Sun is shining?
Show Answer

The blue sky colour depends on Rayleigh scattering of sunlight by the atmosphere. In space above the atmosphere, there are no air molecules to scatter light. Light from the Sun travels in straight lines without scattering — the rest of the sky appears completely black. Stars are also visible during the day from space.

Medium
A diamond has a very high refractive index (~2.42). How does this relate to its brilliance and the splitting of white light (fire)?
Show Answer

High n → small critical angle (~24°) → total internal reflection of light entering the diamond. Light bounces internally many times before exiting. Additionally, high dispersion separates colours by a large angle (more "fire" than glass). The combination of TIR (brilliance — white sparkle) and strong dispersion (fire — coloured flashes) gives diamonds their distinctive appearance.

Medium
If monochromatic red light enters a prism, will a spectrum be produced? Explain.
Show Answer

No spectrum will be produced. Dispersion requires the presence of multiple wavelengths. Monochromatic red light has only one wavelength. The prism refracts it at a single fixed angle — it emerges as a single red ray, deviated but not split. Spectrum formation requires polychromatic (white) light.

Medium
Explain the concept of angular dispersion of a prism and what factors affect it.
Show Answer

Angular dispersion = difference in deviation between violet and red light through the prism. Factors: (1) Apex angle of prism — larger angle → greater deviation → greater dispersion. (2) Material of prism — higher difference in refractive indices between violet and red → greater angular dispersion (flint glass disperses more than crown glass). (3) The dispersive power is a property of the material, independent of the angle for small apex angles.

Medium
Why is the spectrum produced by a prism described as VIBGYOR from bottom to top when white light enters from the left (apex pointing up)?
Show Answer

At a prism surface, lower-wavelength light (violet) bends more toward the base of the prism. With apex pointing up, violet bending most toward the base appears at the bottom of the spectrum; red, bending least, appears at the top. Reading bottom-to-top gives VIBGYOR. The standard NCERT convention shows violet at the bottom of the spectrum band.

Medium

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

Explain how an achromatic doublet eliminates chromatic aberration, linking to the principle of dispersion.
Show Answer

A single convex lens disperses light (different focal lengths for different wavelengths — chromatic aberration). An achromatic doublet combines a convex crown glass lens and a concave flint glass lens cemented together. Crown glass converges light; flint glass (higher dispersion) diverges the colours. By choosing the right combination, the angular dispersions cancel (equal and opposite) while the net converging power remains. Red and blue are brought to the same focus — eliminating colour fringing.

Hard
The deviation through a prism of apex angle A is given by delta = (i1 + i2) - A. Derive this relation and explain what happens at minimum deviation.
Show Answer

At prism: refraction at first face: i1 → r1; refraction at second face: r2 → i2. Geometry of prism: r1 + r2 = A. Deviation at each face: d1 = i1 − r1; d2 = i2 − r2. Total deviation: delta = d1 + d2 = (i1 + i2) − (r1 + r2) = (i1 + i2) − A. At minimum deviation: the ray passes symmetrically (i1 = i2, r1 = r2 = A/2). The refracted ray inside the prism is parallel to the base. n = sin((A + D_min)/2) / sin(A/2).

Hard
The speed of red light in glass is 2.0 × 10^8 m/s and violet light is 1.96 × 10^8 m/s. Calculate the refractive indices and explain the difference in deviation.
Show Answer

c = 3 × 10^8 m/s. n_red = 3/2.0 = 1.50. n_violet = 3/1.96 = 1.531. Violet has a higher refractive index. By Snell's law: sin θ_t = sin θ_i / n → smaller refracted angle for violet → violet is deviated more at each surface → the prism bends violet more than red, producing dispersion.

Hard
Analyse why the human eye itself suffers from chromatic aberration and why this rarely causes noticeable blur in normal vision.
Show Answer

The eye's lens has different refractive indices for different wavelengths. Blue light is focused slightly in front of red — chromatic aberration. However, the eye's yellow macular pigment absorbs blue light, reducing the blue-focus error. The pupil as a small aperture increases depth of field; retinal cone spacing limits resolution; the brain applies neural processing to compensate. The combined effect makes chromatic aberration in the eye optically present but perceptually negligible in most conditions.

Hard
Using MYP Criterion B, design an experiment to measure the angular dispersion of two different glass prisms (crown vs. flint glass) and determine which disperses more.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Flint glass has greater dispersion than crown glass. Setup: Pass a narrow beam of white light through each prism at the same angle of incidence (30°). Project the spectrum on a screen 50 cm away. Measure positions of red and violet spectral lines. Calculate angular separation = arctan(separation/distance). Variables: Prism material (independent), angular separation (dependent), angle of incidence/screen distance (controlled). Expected result: Flint glass gives larger separation. Safety: use low-power LED, not UV sources.

Hard
Explain how a spectroscope uses a prism to analyse the composition of stars, linking dispersion to astrophysics.
Show Answer

A spectroscope passes starlight through a narrow slit, collimates it with a lens, and disperses it with a prism (or diffraction grating). Each element in the star's atmosphere absorbs specific wavelengths (absorption spectroscopy). The resulting spectrum shows dark lines (Fraunhofer absorption lines) at characteristic wavelengths unique to each element. By identifying these line positions, astronomers determine the star's chemical composition, temperature, velocity (Doppler shift), and density — all from light analysis using dispersion principles.

Hard
A prism of apex angle 60° is made of glass with n = 1.5 for yellow light. Find the angle of minimum deviation for yellow light.
Show Answer

At minimum deviation: n = sin((A + D)/2) / sin(A/2). A = 60°, sin(30°) = 0.5. 1.5 = sin((60° + D)/2) / 0.5 → sin((60° + D)/2) = 0.75 → (60° + D)/2 = 48.59° → 60° + D = 97.18° → D = 37.18° ≈ 37.2°.

Hard
Why do oil films on water and soap bubbles show rainbow colours without a prism? Relate to thin film interference and distinguish it from dispersion.
Show Answer

Oil/soap films produce colour by thin film interference, not dispersion. White light reflects from the top and bottom surfaces of the thin film. The path difference between these reflections depends on film thickness and wavelength. For a given thickness, certain wavelengths undergo constructive interference (appear bright) while others experience destructive interference (appear dark). As film thickness varies, different wavelengths are enhanced in different regions — rainbow colours. This is a wave optics phenomenon; the prism is a geometric optics (refraction) phenomenon — fundamentally different mechanisms producing similar visual effects.

Scattering of Light & Rainbow

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

State the Tyndall effect.
Show Answer

The Tyndall effect is the scattering of a beam of light by colloidal particles in a medium, making the path of the beam visible. Named after John Tyndall — e.g., a beam of sunlight visible in dusty air or smoke.

Easy
Why does the sky appear blue?
Show Answer

The sky appears blue because of Rayleigh scattering. Sunlight contains all colours; shorter wavelengths (blue, ~450 nm) are scattered much more (proportional to 1/λ^4) by atmospheric molecules than longer wavelengths (red). The scattered blue light reaches our eyes from all directions of the sky, making the sky appear blue.

Easy
Why does the Sun appear red at sunrise and sunset?
Show Answer

At sunrise/sunset, sunlight travels through a much longer path of the atmosphere. Most of the blue and shorter wavelengths are scattered away before reaching the observer. Mainly red and orange wavelengths (least scattered) survive to reach the eye, making the Sun appear red/orange.

Easy
State Rayleigh's scattering law regarding the dependence of scattering on wavelength.
Show Answer

Rayleigh's law: The intensity of scattered light is inversely proportional to the fourth power of the wavelength: I is proportional to 1/λ^4. Shorter wavelengths (blue/violet) are scattered far more than longer wavelengths (red), explaining blue sky and red sunsets.

Easy
How is a rainbow formed? Name the two optical processes involved.
Show Answer

A rainbow is formed when sunlight enters spherical water droplets in the atmosphere. The two processes are: (1) Refraction — light bends on entering and exiting the droplet. (2) Total internal reflection — light reflects off the back inner surface of the droplet. Together, these disperse sunlight into a spectrum seen as a rainbow arc.

Easy
In a primary rainbow, which colour appears on the outside (top) and which on the inside (bottom)?
Show Answer

In a primary rainbow: Red is on the outside (top) at ~42° and violet is on the inside (bottom) at ~40°. This is because red light undergoes less deviation inside the droplet than violet.

Easy
Why does the sky near the horizon appear whitish rather than deep blue?
Show Answer

Near the horizon, sunlight has passed through more atmosphere and multiple scattering events. All wavelengths are scattered multiple times and mixed together, producing nearly white light. The multiple scattering washes out the blue preferential effect seen for a single scattering event when looking higher in the sky.

Easy
Give two everyday examples of the Tyndall effect.
Show Answer

(1) Sunlight entering a dusty room through a window — the beam of light is visible because dust particles scatter it. (2) Headlight beams visible in foggy weather — fog droplets scatter the headlight beam, making it visible as a cone of light.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

Using Rayleigh's law (I proportional to 1/λ^4), compare the scattering of blue (λ = 450 nm) and red (λ = 700 nm) light. Calculate the ratio of scattering intensities.
Show Answer

I_blue/I_red = (λ_red/λ_blue)^4 = (700/450)^4 = (1.556)^4 ≈ 2.42 × 2.42 ≈ 5.85. Blue light is scattered about 5.8 times more than red light, explaining the strong blue appearance of the sky.

Medium
Explain why the Moon has a black sky even during its "day" despite the Sun shining.
Show Answer

The Moon has no atmosphere. Rayleigh scattering requires gas molecules to scatter sunlight in all directions. Without an atmosphere, no scattering occurs. Sunlight travels in straight lines to the surface; the sky in other directions remains completely dark — black. Only the Sun itself appears as a bright disc; everything else is dark.

Medium
Why are danger signals and traffic stop lights red in colour? Relate to Rayleigh scattering.
Show Answer

Red light has the longest wavelength in the visible spectrum and is therefore scattered least by atmospheric particles (fog, dust, rain). It can travel longer distances through the atmosphere without losing intensity or being redirected. This makes red the most visible colour from a distance in poor weather — essential for danger/stop signals that must be seen at maximum range.

Medium
Explain the formation of a secondary rainbow. How does it differ from a primary rainbow in colour order and brightness?
Show Answer

A secondary rainbow forms when light undergoes two internal reflections inside the water droplet (compared to one in the primary). This reverses the colour order: violet is on the outside and red on the inside. The secondary rainbow appears at ~51° (compared to ~42° for primary). Two reflections mean more light is lost — the secondary rainbow is dimmer than the primary and appears above the primary.

Medium
A beam of white light passes through a colloidal solution of milk in water. What would you observe from the side and from the end of the beam, and why?
Show Answer

From the side: the beam appears blue/bluish-white because shorter wavelengths (blue) are scattered laterally more than longer wavelengths. From the end (looking through the solution): the transmitted light appears reddish/orange because blue wavelengths have been scattered away, leaving mainly the longer (red/orange) wavelengths. This is analogous to the blue sky (side-scattered) and red sunset (transmitted).

Medium
Why can we see a rainbow only when the Sun is behind us and rain is in front?
Show Answer

Rainbow formation requires sunlight to enter water droplets, reflect internally, and exit back toward the observer. The geometry means the dispersed light from droplets returns at ~40–42° to the direction of the incoming sunlight — i.e., back toward the Sun's direction. For the observer to see this returned light, the Sun must be behind them and the rain droplets in front (anti-solar direction).

Medium
Mars has a very thin atmosphere (mostly CO2). Predict the colour of the Martian sky at noon, giving reasons.
Show Answer

Mars's sky is pinkish/butterscotch-brown, not blue. The thin CO2 atmosphere alone would produce a faint blue, but suspended iron oxide (rust) dust particles in the atmosphere are much larger than atmospheric molecules — they scatter all wavelengths relatively equally (Mie scattering, not Rayleigh) and absorb blue, transmitting reddish tones. The dominant scattering particles (dust) cause the pink/brownish colour.

Medium
Why does smoke from a fire appear bluish when viewed against a dark background, but yellowish/whitish against a bright background?
Show Answer

Fresh smoke contains very fine particles. These small particles undergo Rayleigh-type scattering, preferentially scattering blue light. Against a dark background, the scattered blue light is visible (bluish smoke). Against a bright/white background, the viewer sees the transmitted light — blue has been scattered away, leaving longer wavelengths (yellow/red), so the smoke appears yellowish/white.

Medium

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

Analyse why Rayleigh scattering (λ^-4) applies to molecules but Mie scattering applies to larger particles. What is the sky colour implication for a planet with large aerosol particles?
Show Answer

Rayleigh scattering applies when particle size is much smaller than wavelength of light (molecules, ~0.3 nm; visible λ ~400–700 nm). The oscillating electric field induces a dipole in the small particle, which re-radiates with strong wavelength dependence (λ^-4). Mie scattering applies when particle size ≈ wavelength (dust, pollen, water droplets). Mie scattering is nearly wavelength-independent — all colours scattered equally. Implication: a planet with large aerosol particles (like Venus with sulfuric acid clouds) would have a white/grey/yellowish sky with no blue colour preference.

Hard
Explain geometrically why a primary rainbow is always seen at approximately 42° from the anti-solar point.
Show Answer

Consider a ray entering a spherical water droplet (n ≈ 1.33). At minimum deviation, the ray undergoes two refractions and one TIR. Using geometry: total deviation D = 180° + 2i − 4r (where r from Snell: sin i = n sin r). Minimising D with respect to i: dD/di = 0. Solving numerically for n = 1.33: i ≈ 59.4°, r ≈ 40.4°, D_min ≈ 137.5°. Rainbow angle from anti-solar point = 180° − 137.5° ≈ 42.5° ≈ 42°. This minimum deviation angle is where rays are most concentrated — forming the bright rainbow band.

Hard
Using MYP Criterion C (Processing and Evaluating), discuss whether the Tyndall effect is a suitable lab experiment to distinguish a true solution from a colloid. Evaluate its limitations.
Show Answer

Method: Pass a laser beam through the sample in a dark room. Observe from the side. Result: Colloid — beam visible (Tyndall effect). True solution — no beam visible. Strengths: Simple, no special equipment, fast, non-destructive. Limitations: (1) Very dilute colloids may show faint Tyndall effect. (2) Suspension (not colloid) also shows Tyndall effect — cannot distinguish colloid from suspension. (3) Qualitative, not quantitative. (4) Coloured solutions may confound observation. Better combined with particle size measurement or filtration test.

Hard
The dark band between the primary and secondary rainbow is called Alexander's dark band. Explain its origin using ray optics.
Show Answer

Primary rainbow forms at ~42°; secondary at ~51° from the anti-solar point. In the primary, minimum deviation sends light to angles less than 42° (inside the primary bow). In the secondary, light exits at angles greater than 51° (outside the secondary bow). The region between 42° and 51° receives no direct rainbow rays from droplets — light from droplets in this angular range cannot reach the observer via either rainbow-forming path. This angular gap appears darker than the surrounding sky — Alexander's dark band.

Hard
Climate scientists use atmospheric scattering data to infer aerosol levels. Explain how a sudden change from blue to milky-white sky could indicate increased particulate pollution.
Show Answer

A pure blue sky indicates Rayleigh scattering dominance — only gas molecules (size much less than λ). A milky-white hazy sky indicates Mie scattering dominance — larger particles (aerosols, PM2.5 pollutants, smoke, dust; size ≈ λ). Mie scattering is wavelength-independent — all colours scattered equally → white/grey appearance. The shift from blue to milky-white directly indicates increased concentration of large particulate matter (aerosol optical depth increase). Satellite sensors measure sky radiance at multiple wavelengths to quantify aerosol types and concentrations — used in PM2.5 monitoring and climate modelling.

Hard
Using MYP Criterion D, evaluate the statement: "Atmospheric scattering is both essential for life and a marker of environmental damage." Discuss with specific examples.
Show Answer

Essential for life: (1) Rayleigh scattering distributes solar energy across the sky — without it, only the Sun's disc would be bright; extreme temperature gradients would result. (2) Scattered light enables photosynthesis even in shaded areas. (3) The atmosphere partially scatters harmful UV. Marker of environmental damage: (1) Increased haze/white sky = aerosol pollution (PM2.5 from factories, vehicles, crop burning — major issue in North India). (2) Reduced visibility = smog (photochemical or industrial). (3) Volcanic eruptions inject aerosols → colourful skies globally but also crop yield reduction. Balanced view: natural scattering is life-sustaining; anthropogenic enhancement is harmful. Science informs policy; individual and collective action required.

Hard
Why does a rainbow appear as an arc rather than a full circle, and under what conditions can a full circular rainbow be seen?
Show Answer

A rainbow is a cone of light centred on the anti-solar point, with half-angle ~42°. For a ground-based observer, the ground cuts off the lower half of this cone — only the arc above the horizon is visible. A full circular rainbow can be seen from an aircraft, a tall waterfall with spray, or from high altitude above the rain — when the full cone's circumference is visible below the observer. Pilots and skydivers sometimes observe full circular rainbows.

Hard
Analyse the difference between the Tyndall effect and Rayleigh scattering in terms of particle size, mechanism, and observable outcomes.
Show Answer

Rayleigh scattering: particles (gas molecules) much smaller than λ; induced dipole radiation; strong λ^-4 wavelength dependence; explains blue sky/red sunset; occurs in clean atmosphere. Tyndall effect: colloidal particles (1–100 nm; size comparable to visible λ in some cases); less wavelength dependent than Rayleigh; the beam path is visible in colloids. Key distinction: Rayleigh is for molecular-scale scatterers with strong colour dependence; Tyndall is for colloidal-scale scatterers — the beam visibility effect. Both are forms of elastic scattering but at different size regimes with different wavelength dependencies.
