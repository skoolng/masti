Set A Knowledge & Recall Easy

What is the endocrine system? Easy
Show Answer

The endocrine system is a network of ductless glands that secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the bloodstream to regulate body functions.

What does "ductless gland" mean? Easy
Show Answer

A ductless gland has no tube or duct; it releases its secretions (hormones) directly into the surrounding blood capillaries.

Which gland is called the master gland and why? Easy
Show Answer

The pituitary gland is called the master gland because it produces hormones that control and regulate the activities of all other endocrine glands.

Where is the pituitary gland located in the body? Easy
Show Answer

The pituitary gland is a pea-sized gland located at the base of the brain, in a bony socket called the sella turcica.

Name any three endocrine glands in the human body. Easy
Show Answer

Three endocrine glands are: (1) Pituitary gland, (2) Thyroid gland, and (3) Adrenal gland. Others include pancreas, ovaries, and testes.

What is a hormone? Easy
Show Answer

A hormone is a chemical messenger produced by an endocrine gland, secreted into the blood, and carried to a target organ where it brings about a specific response.

How do hormones travel to their target organs? Easy
Show Answer

Hormones are secreted directly into the bloodstream and travel through the circulatory system to reach their specific target organs or tissues.

Give one difference between endocrine glands and exocrine glands. Easy
Show Answer

Endocrine glands are ductless and secrete hormones directly into the blood; exocrine glands have ducts and release secretions (like sweat, saliva, or digestive juices) onto a surface or into a cavity.

Set B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain the concept of "feedback mechanism" in the endocrine system using one example. Medium
Show Answer

In a feedback mechanism, the output of a process regulates its own production. Example: When blood calcium drops, the parathyroid gland releases PTH to raise it; once calcium levels normalise, PTH secretion decreases. This prevents over- or under-correction.

Why is blood used as the transport medium for hormones rather than a duct system? Medium
Show Answer

Blood reaches every cell in the body, allowing hormones to simultaneously affect multiple distant target organs. A duct would restrict the hormone to a specific local area and would require separate ducts for every target organ.

How does the pituitary gland control other endocrine glands? Give one specific example. Medium
Show Answer

The pituitary releases tropic hormones (stimulating hormones) that travel to other glands and stimulate them to produce their own hormones. Example: TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone) from the pituitary stimulates the thyroid gland to produce thyroxine.

A student says, "Since hormones are chemicals, they must act instantly." Is this correct? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

This is incorrect. Unlike nerve impulses (which act in milliseconds), hormones travel slowly via blood and their effects may take minutes, hours, or even days to appear. However, hormonal effects tend to be longer-lasting than nerve responses.

Distinguish between the endocrine system and the nervous system as means of body coordination. Medium
Show Answer

The nervous system uses electrical impulses through nerves for fast, short-lived, localised responses. The endocrine system uses chemical hormones through blood for slower, longer-lasting, widespread responses affecting multiple organs at once.

Why are hormones needed only in very small quantities to produce their effects? Medium
Show Answer

Hormones are highly specific and potent chemical messengers. They bind to complementary receptor proteins on target cells with great precision. Even tiny amounts trigger amplified biochemical responses inside the cell, so large quantities are unnecessary.

The pancreas is both an endocrine and exocrine gland. Explain this dual role. Medium
Show Answer

The pancreas acts as an exocrine gland by secreting digestive enzymes (e.g., lipase, amylase) through the pancreatic duct into the small intestine. It also acts as an endocrine gland because its islets of Langerhans produce insulin and glucagon, which are secreted directly into the blood to regulate blood sugar.

If the pituitary gland stops working, how would this affect other endocrine glands? Medium
Show Answer

Without pituitary tropic hormones, other endocrine glands such as the thyroid, adrenal cortex, ovaries, and testes would receive no stimulation and would cease to function normally. This would cause widespread hormonal deficiencies affecting growth, metabolism, reproduction, and stress response.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Analysis & Evaluation Hard

[MYP B] Design an investigation to test whether stress increases hormone levels. Identify variables and a measurable indicator. Hard
Show Answer

Independent variable: level of stress (e.g., a mild maths test vs. rest). Dependent variable: cortisol concentration in saliva. Controlled variables: time of day, diet, age and gender of participants. Measurable indicator: salivary cortisol kit reading in nmol/L before and after the stressor. A control group rests quietly during the same period. Multiple trials improve reliability.

[MYP D] Evaluate the claim: "The endocrine system is more important than the nervous system." Use evidence to support and refute this. Hard
Show Answer

Support: hormones regulate long-term processes (growth, reproduction, metabolism) that nerves cannot. Refute: without the nervous system, rapid survival responses (reflexes, heartbeat regulation, muscle movement) would fail, and even the endocrine system is partly regulated by the hypothalamus–pituitary axis which requires brain signals. Both systems are essential and interdependent; neither is "more important" in absolute terms.

A child's growth is abnormally slow. What endocrine dysfunction could cause this, and what treatment might be used? Hard
Show Answer

Insufficient growth hormone (GH) from the pituitary gland causes dwarfism (pituitary dwarfism). The condition can also result from low thyroxine (cretinism if present from birth). Treatment: synthetic recombinant human growth hormone (rHGH) injections during childhood can stimulate normal growth if administered before the growth plates close.

[MYP D] Analyse how disruption of the endocrine system by environmental pollutants (endocrine disruptors) could affect human health. Hard
Show Answer

Endocrine disruptors (e.g., BPA in plastics, pesticides like DDT) mimic or block natural hormones. They can bind to hormone receptors and trigger false responses or block real hormones. Consequences include early puberty in girls, reduced sperm count, thyroid dysfunction, and increased cancer risk. This is significant because even low doses over long periods can cause permanent damage, especially in developing foetuses and children.

Compare the speed and duration of response of the endocrine and nervous systems using a real-life scenario of someone touching a hot stove. Hard
Show Answer

Touching a hot stove triggers an immediate nerve-mediated reflex arc (withdrawal in <0.1 s). The nervous system then signals the hypothalamus, which activates the adrenal glands to release adrenaline (endocrine response, takes seconds to minutes). The adrenaline response (raised heart rate, dilated pupils, heightened alertness) persists for minutes after the nerve signals stop — showing that endocrine responses are slower but more prolonged.

[MYP B] You find that plant A grows faster with a chemical treatment than plant B without. Explain why this alone is insufficient to conclude the chemical acts as a hormone. Hard
Show Answer

To confirm the chemical acts as a hormone, we would need: (1) evidence it is produced internally by plant cells, not externally applied; (2) evidence it is transported through the plant's vascular system to act at a distance from the production site; (3) a dose–response curve showing effectiveness at very low concentrations; and (4) controls ruling out confounding variables (light, water, temperature). One observation of faster growth does not establish any of these criteria.

Explain why athletes who misuse anabolic steroids (synthetic sex hormones) may experience shrinkage of their own hormone-producing glands over time. Hard
Show Answer

The body uses negative feedback: when external anabolic steroids raise blood hormone levels, the pituitary detects this and reduces secretion of LH and FSH. Without LH stimulation, the testes (or ovaries) receive no signal to produce their own hormones and begin to atrophy (shrink) from disuse. This is a direct consequence of the feedback regulation built into the endocrine system.

[MYP D] "Hormones are chemical signals, yet they only affect certain cells." Explain the molecular basis for this specificity and evaluate its importance. Hard
Show Answer

Specificity arises from receptor proteins on target cells that have a complementary shape to a particular hormone (lock-and-key model). Only cells bearing the correct receptor respond. This is vital: it prevents every circulating hormone from triggering every cell, which would cause chaos. It allows precise control — e.g., FSH stimulates only gonadal cells while leaving liver cells unaffected. Loss of specificity (e.g., mutated receptors) can cause conditions like hormone insensitivity syndrome.

Set A Knowledge & Recall Easy

Name the hormone produced by the pituitary gland that controls growth. Easy
Show Answer

Growth Hormone (GH), also called somatotropin, is produced by the pituitary gland and stimulates growth of bones and muscles.

What does FSH stand for and which gland produces it? Easy
Show Answer

FSH stands for Follicle-Stimulating Hormone. It is produced by the pituitary gland and stimulates the ovaries (in females) or testes (in males).

Which hormone regulates the body's metabolic rate? Easy
Show Answer

Thyroxine, produced by the thyroid gland, regulates the body's metabolic rate — controlling how quickly cells use energy from food.

Which gland produces adrenaline? Easy
Show Answer

The adrenal glands, located on top of the kidneys, produce adrenaline (also called epinephrine).

What is the "fight-or-flight" response? Easy
Show Answer

The fight-or-flight response is a set of rapid physiological changes triggered by adrenaline when an organism perceives danger — it prepares the body to either confront the threat or escape from it.

What mineral is essential for the thyroid gland to produce thyroxine? Easy
Show Answer

Iodine is essential for the production of thyroxine. Deficiency of iodine in the diet leads to goitre (enlargement of the thyroid gland).

Name two physical effects of adrenaline on the body. Easy
Show Answer

Two effects of adrenaline: (1) Increased heart rate and blood pressure to deliver more oxygen to muscles. (2) Dilation of pupils to improve vision. Other effects include increased breathing rate and release of glucose from liver stores.

What condition results from excess growth hormone secretion during childhood? Easy
Show Answer

Excess GH during childhood causes gigantism — abnormally tall stature due to over-stimulation of bone and tissue growth before growth plates close.

Set B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain how adrenaline prepares the body for physical exertion during an emergency. Describe at least three changes. Medium
Show Answer

Adrenaline: (1) Increases heart rate to pump more oxygenated blood to muscles. (2) Dilates airways (broncholes) to increase oxygen intake. (3) Causes liver to convert glycogen to glucose, providing instant energy. (4) Redirects blood flow away from digestive organs to skeletal muscles. (5) Dilates pupils for better visual awareness.

What is goitre? How is it related to thyroxine production? Medium
Show Answer

Goitre is the abnormal enlargement of the thyroid gland, visible as a swelling in the neck. When dietary iodine is insufficient, the thyroid cannot synthesise enough thyroxine. The pituitary responds by releasing more TSH to stimulate the thyroid, causing the gland to grow larger in an attempt to produce more hormone — resulting in goitre.

Distinguish between the roles of FSH and LH in the human reproductive system. Medium
Show Answer

FSH (Follicle-Stimulating Hormone) stimulates the maturation of egg follicles in the ovary (females) or sperm production in the testes (males). LH (Luteinising Hormone) triggers ovulation (the release of a mature egg) in females and stimulates testosterone production in males. Both are secreted by the pituitary.

How does an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism) affect a child's physical and mental development? Medium
Show Answer

Insufficient thyroxine slows down the metabolic rate in all body cells. In children, this causes cretinism: stunted physical growth, delayed bone development, intellectual disability, and lethargy. Early diagnosis and hormone replacement therapy can prevent permanent damage if started in the first weeks of life.

A person experiences a surge of adrenaline during a roller-coaster ride. Explain the sequence of events from stimulus to body response. Medium
Show Answer

1. The brain perceives the thrill/fear stimulus. 2. The hypothalamus activates the sympathetic nervous system. 3. Nerve signals reach the adrenal medulla, triggering adrenaline secretion. 4. Adrenaline enters the bloodstream and binds to receptors throughout the body. 5. Result: racing heart, rapid breathing, heightened alertness, "butterflies" (digestive blood redirected). The effects subside once the stimulus ends and adrenaline is broken down.

Why must iodised salt be used in cooking? What public health problem does it prevent? Medium
Show Answer

Iodised salt provides dietary iodine, which is essential for thyroxine synthesis. In iodine-deficient regions, supplementing salt with potassium iodate prevents endemic goitre and cretinism. It is one of the most cost-effective public health interventions — the WHO estimates it prevents millions of cases of intellectual disability worldwide.

Explain why a person with diabetes mellitus does not have normal blood glucose control. Which hormone is involved? Medium
Show Answer

The hormone insulin, produced by beta cells in the pancreatic islets, allows cells to take up glucose from the blood. In Type 1 diabetes, the immune system destroys beta cells, so no insulin is produced and blood glucose rises dangerously (hyperglycaemia). In Type 2 diabetes, cells become resistant to insulin's signal. Without proper glucose uptake, cells are starved of energy while glucose accumulates in blood.

How does the pituitary gland act as the link between the nervous system and the endocrine system? Medium
Show Answer

The pituitary gland is directly connected to the hypothalamus (part of the brain) by a stalk. The hypothalamus detects nervous signals (e.g., stress, light cycles) and releases releasing hormones that travel to the pituitary, which then secretes tropic hormones to activate other endocrine glands. This neuroendocrine axis converts brain signals into systemic hormonal responses.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Analysis & Evaluation Hard

[MYP D] Evaluate the ethical and health implications of athletes using synthetic human growth hormone (HGH) for performance enhancement. Hard
Show Answer

HGH increases muscle mass and reduces fat, giving competitive advantage. Health risks include acromegaly (abnormal bone growth), cardiomegaly (enlarged heart), joint pain, insulin resistance, and increased cancer risk. Ethically, it violates fair-play principles, is banned by WADA, and sets a dangerous precedent for younger athletes. While medically necessary for GH-deficient children, its misuse commodifies health and undermines sporting integrity.

[MYP B] Design an experiment to show that the thyroid gland affects the metabolic rate of tadpoles. List all variables and expected results. Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: thyroxine treatment accelerates tadpole metamorphosis. Groups: Control (plain water), Experimental 1 (water + thyroxine), Experimental 2 (water + thyroid-blocking drug). Measured variable: time (days) to complete metamorphosis to frog. Controlled variables: temperature, water pH, food supply, light cycle, tadpole age and species. Expected: thyroxine group metamorphoses earliest; blocked-thyroid group slowest or fails to metamorphose, confirming thyroxine's role in metamorphosis regulation.

Analyse why chronic stress can be harmful to long-term health using your knowledge of adrenaline and cortisol. Hard
Show Answer

Short-term adrenaline/cortisol release is beneficial (mobilising energy, suppressing inflammation acutely). However, chronic stress keeps cortisol elevated, which: (1) suppresses the immune system, increasing susceptibility to infections; (2) causes prolonged raised blood pressure, increasing cardiovascular disease risk; (3) disrupts sleep and appetite; (4) impairs memory (hippocampal damage); (5) causes prolonged hyperglycaemia, raising Type 2 diabetes risk. The system evolved for short-term threats, not sustained modern stressors.

[MYP D] A student claims that since insulin can now be produced by genetically engineered bacteria, diabetes is "cured." Critically evaluate this claim. Hard
Show Answer

The claim overstates the case. Recombinant insulin (from bacteria engineered with the human insulin gene) allows effective management of Type 1 diabetes — it is not a cure. Patients still require daily injections or pump therapy, blood glucose monitoring, and dietary management. The underlying autoimmune destruction of beta cells continues. True cures being researched include islet cell transplantation, beta cell regeneration, and closed-loop artificial pancreas systems. Recombinant insulin is a transformative treatment, not a cure.

Compare and contrast the effects of hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid) and hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) on the body. Hard
Show Answer

Hyperthyroidism: excess thyroxine → increased metabolism → weight loss despite increased appetite, rapid heart rate, anxiety, heat intolerance, tremors, bulging eyes (Graves' disease). Hypothyroidism: insufficient thyroxine → slowed metabolism → weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance, depression, dry skin, goitre (if due to iodine deficiency). Both disrupt homeostasis; one accelerates processes, the other depresses them. Treatment differs: hyperthyroidism requires antithyroid drugs or radioiodine; hypothyroidism requires synthetic thyroxine (levothyroxine).

[MYP B] A researcher measures GH levels in students at different times of day. Predict the pattern and explain what variables must be controlled. Hard
Show Answer

Predicted pattern: GH peaks during deep sleep (approximately 11pm–2am in adolescents) and has smaller peaks after exercise. Daytime levels are generally low. Controls: participants must follow the same sleep schedule, dietary intake, exercise levels, and stress levels. Blood samples must be taken at identical times. GH levels vary with age, sex, and body composition, so participants should be matched for these. Multiple samples per person improve reliability against individual pulsatile GH release.

Explain how the hypothalamus-pituitary-thyroid axis maintains thyroxine at a stable level through negative feedback. Draw a flow diagram in words. Hard
Show Answer

Flow: Low blood thyroxine → hypothalamus detects drop → releases TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) → TRH reaches pituitary → pituitary releases TSH → TSH stimulates thyroid → thyroid produces thyroxine → blood thyroxine rises → hypothalamus and pituitary detect rise → reduce TRH and TSH output → thyroid reduces thyroxine production → equilibrium restored. This negative feedback loop keeps thyroxine within a narrow optimal range for stable metabolism.

[MYP D] Assess the advantages and limitations of hormone therapy for treating endocrine disorders, using two specific conditions as examples. Hard
Show Answer

Example 1 — Hypothyroidism: synthetic levothyroxine restores normal metabolism cheaply and effectively; limitation: lifelong daily dose required, and over/under-dosing must be carefully monitored. Example 2 — Type 1 Diabetes: insulin therapy prevents life-threatening hyperglycaemia; limitation: does not cure the underlying autoimmune cause, requires injections (patient burden), and hypoglycaemia from overdose can be fatal. Overall, hormone therapy manages rather than cures most endocrine disorders, requires ongoing monitoring, and may have side effects; but for many conditions it is life-saving and markedly improves quality of life.

Set A Knowledge & Recall Easy

Where is testosterone produced in males? Easy
Show Answer

Testosterone is produced by the Leydig cells in the testes (the male gonads).

Name the primary female sex hormone and the organ that produces it. Easy
Show Answer

Oestrogen (estrogen) is the primary female sex hormone; it is produced mainly by the ovaries.

List two secondary sexual characteristics in males caused by testosterone. Easy
Show Answer

Two male secondary sexual characteristics: (1) Growth of facial and body hair (beard, chest hair). (2) Deepening of the voice (laryngeal growth). Others include increased muscle mass, broader shoulders, and growth of the penis and testes.

List two secondary sexual characteristics in females caused by oestrogen. Easy
Show Answer

Two female secondary sexual characteristics: (1) Development of breasts. (2) Widening of the hips (pelvis broadening). Others include the onset of menstruation and growth of pubic/underarm hair.

What is the menstrual cycle? Approximately how long does one cycle last? Easy
Show Answer

The menstrual cycle is the monthly hormonal cycle in females that prepares the uterus for possible pregnancy. It involves egg maturation, ovulation, uterine lining build-up, and shedding (menstruation) if fertilisation does not occur. One cycle lasts approximately 28 days on average.

What is puberty? Easy
Show Answer

Puberty is the period of adolescence during which the reproductive system matures and secondary sexual characteristics develop, triggered by increased production of sex hormones.

Which hormone from the pituitary stimulates testosterone production in males? Easy
Show Answer

Luteinising Hormone (LH) from the pituitary gland stimulates the Leydig cells in the testes to produce testosterone.

What is the role of progesterone in the menstrual cycle? Easy
Show Answer

Progesterone, produced by the corpus luteum after ovulation, maintains and thickens the uterine lining to prepare it for implantation of a fertilised egg. If fertilisation does not occur, progesterone levels fall and menstruation begins.

Set B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain why secondary sexual characteristics begin to appear at puberty and not earlier in life. Medium
Show Answer

Before puberty, the hypothalamus and pituitary secrete only small amounts of GnRH and gonadotropins (FSH/LH). At puberty, rising body mass and hormonal signals (possibly leptin from fat tissue) trigger the hypothalamus to increase GnRH pulses, stimulating the pituitary to release higher levels of FSH and LH. These in turn stimulate the gonads to produce sex hormones (testosterone or oestrogen) in sufficient quantities to trigger secondary sexual changes.

Describe the hormonal events that occur during a typical 28-day menstrual cycle. Medium
Show Answer

Days 1–5: Menstruation (uterine lining sheds). Days 6–13: FSH rises → follicle matures → oestrogen rises → uterine lining rebuilds. Day 14: LH surge → ovulation (egg released). Days 15–28: Corpus luteum forms → progesterone rises → lining thickens ready for implantation. If no fertilisation: corpus luteum degenerates → progesterone falls → menstruation restarts cycle.

Both males and females produce both testosterone and oestrogen. Explain why, and what determines the phenotype (male or female appearance). Medium
Show Answer

The adrenal glands in both sexes produce small amounts of sex hormones. The ratio matters: males have much higher testosterone (from testes) than oestrogen, driving male characteristics. Females have much higher oestrogen (from ovaries) than testosterone, driving female characteristics. In females, small amounts of testosterone contribute to libido and muscle function; in males, small oestrogen amounts are needed for bone density and sperm maturation.

Explain what happens hormonally if a released egg is fertilised and implants in the uterus. Medium
Show Answer

After fertilisation and implantation, the developing embryo secretes hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin), which prevents the corpus luteum from degenerating. The corpus luteum continues to produce progesterone, maintaining the uterine lining and preventing menstruation. hCG is detected by pregnancy tests. As the placenta develops, it takes over progesterone production around week 10–12.

How does the contraceptive pill prevent pregnancy? Name the hormones involved. Medium
Show Answer

Combined oral contraceptive pills contain synthetic oestrogen and progesterone. These hormones use negative feedback to suppress the pituitary's release of FSH and LH. Without FSH, follicles do not mature; without LH, there is no ovulation surge. No egg is released, so fertilisation cannot occur. The pill also thickens cervical mucus and thins the uterine lining as additional barriers.

What is menopause? Explain the hormonal changes that cause it. Medium
Show Answer

Menopause is the natural cessation of menstruation, typically occurring around age 45–55. As women age, the ovaries become less responsive to FSH and LH, and their supply of egg follicles is exhausted. Oestrogen and progesterone levels fall sharply. The pituitary increases FSH output (trying to stimulate the unresponsive ovaries), resulting in high FSH levels. Symptoms include hot flushes, mood changes, and bone density loss (osteoporosis risk).

Why do adolescent boys experience mood swings and emotional changes during puberty? Medium
Show Answer

Rising testosterone levels during puberty affect not just physical development but also brain chemistry. Testosterone and its derivatives influence neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, dopamine) in areas governing mood, impulse control, and reward. Additionally, the prefrontal cortex (responsible for rational decision-making) is still developing throughout adolescence, so emotional responses from the limbic system often dominate, resulting in heightened emotional reactivity and mood variability.

Why do female athletes sometimes experience cessation of menstruation (amenorrhoea) during intense training? Medium
Show Answer

Extreme physical training combined with low body fat reduces leptin signals that normally maintain the hypothalamus–pituitary–ovary axis. The hypothalamus suppresses GnRH pulses, reducing FSH and LH, which in turn reduces oestrogen production. The cycle cannot proceed normally without adequate hormonal signals, so menstruation stops. This is the "female athlete triad" (low energy intake, low bone density, amenorrhoea) and can cause long-term bone and reproductive health problems.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Analysis & Evaluation Hard

[MYP D] Evaluate the use of hormone replacement therapy (HRT) for menopausal women. Consider benefits and risks. Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: relieves hot flushes, night sweats, mood changes; prevents osteoporosis; reduces risk of colorectal cancer; improves quality of life. Risks: slightly increased risk of breast cancer (combined HRT), blood clots (deep vein thrombosis), stroke, and endometrial cancer (if oestrogen-only HRT without progesterone). Current guidance: short-term HRT at lowest effective dose has an acceptable risk–benefit ratio for most women; the decision should be individualised based on personal and family medical history. Not all risks are equal — overstated fears from a 2002 study led many women to forgo beneficial treatment unnecessarily.

[MYP B] Design a study to compare the age of puberty onset between urban and rural adolescents in India. What variables would you measure? Hard
Show Answer

Dependent variable: age at onset of puberty (first menstruation for girls; testicular volume ≥ 4 mL for boys using Prader orchidometer). Independent variable: urban vs. rural setting. Controlled variables: ethnic background, family income (proxy for nutrition), BMI. Additional variables to record: diet (caloric intake, protein, fat), physical activity, endocrine disruptor exposure, screen time and sleep. Sample: 200+ adolescents aged 8–16 per group. Statistical test: Mann–Whitney U or t-test for age of pubertal milestones. Ethics: parental consent, confidentiality, IRB approval.

Analyse the concept of "precocious puberty" — its hormonal causes and the social/psychological impact on affected children. Hard
Show Answer

Precocious puberty (onset before 8 in girls, 9 in boys) results from premature activation of the HPG axis (central precocious puberty) or from sex hormone secretion independent of the pituitary (peripheral precocious puberty — e.g., tumours, congenital adrenal hyperplasia). Hormonal cause: early FSH/LH release or autonomous sex hormone production. Physical: early growth spurt followed by premature growth plate closure → shorter adult height. Psychological: body image issues, social isolation from peers who are not yet pubescent, increased risk of depression and early sexual activity. Treatment: GnRH analogues suppress puberty until an appropriate age.

[MYP D] "Gender is purely determined by sex hormones." Critically assess this statement using scientific evidence. Hard
Show Answer

Partially true but reductive. Sex hormones play a significant role: testosterone promotes male characteristics, oestrogen promotes female ones. However: (1) Gender identity involves complex interactions of genetics (XX/XY), hormones, brain structure, and social environment. (2) Intersex conditions (e.g., congenital adrenal hyperplasia, androgen insensitivity syndrome) demonstrate that hormones alone do not always determine gender phenotype. (3) Neurological studies suggest brain organisation during foetal development (partly influenced by hormones) differs between males and females, but with significant overlap. (4) Gender identity — a person's internal sense of self — is distinct from biological sex and is not fully explained by hormone levels alone.

Explain how anabolic steroid abuse in female athletes causes masculinisation (virilisation). Which hormones are involved? Hard
Show Answer

Anabolic steroids are synthetic androgens (testosterone derivatives). When females use them, exogenous androgens bind androgen receptors throughout the body, driving male secondary sexual characteristics: voice deepening (laryngeal growth), clitoral enlargement, facial and body hair growth, acne, and menstrual irregularities. The excess androgens suppress the HPG axis via negative feedback, reducing oestrogen and causing amenorrhoea. Some effects (voice change) may be irreversible after long-term use. Liver damage, cardiovascular risks, and psychiatric effects ("roid rage") also occur.

[MYP B] Plan an experiment to determine whether oestrogen affects bone density using laboratory rats. Include ethical considerations. Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: oestrogen deficiency decreases bone mineral density (BMD). Groups: Group 1 — intact female rats (control); Group 2 — ovariectomised rats (low oestrogen); Group 3 — ovariectomised + oestrogen supplementation. Measured variable: BMD by dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) at 12 weeks. Controlled variables: age, diet (Ca, phosphorus, Vit D standardised), cage conditions. Ethics: minimum number of animals; analgesic post-surgery; approved by Institutional Animal Ethics Committee; replacement alternatives (cell cultures) considered first; humane endpoints defined.

Explain the concept of endocrine disruption specifically in the context of oestrogen-mimicking chemicals (xenoestrogens) found in plastics. Hard
Show Answer

Xenoestrogens (e.g., BPA, phthalates) are synthetic chemicals with structures similar to oestrogen. They bind to oestrogen receptors and activate or block them, disrupting normal hormonal signalling. In males: reduced sperm count, testicular developmental abnormalities. In females: early puberty, increased breast cancer risk, polycystic ovary syndrome. In foetuses: disrupted reproductive tract development. The concern is that regulatory "safe" dose limits are based on traditional toxicology, but endocrine disruptors may have non-linear dose responses — low doses can sometimes cause greater effects than high doses — challenging conventional risk assessment models.

[MYP D] Compare the health implications of early puberty vs. late puberty for both males and females. What role does the endocrine system play in each scenario? Hard
Show Answer

Early puberty (endocrine system activates HPG axis prematurely): Females — higher risk of breast cancer, shorter adult height, psychological stress from early bodily changes. Males — early muscle development but premature growth plate closure, potential social challenges. Late puberty (delayed HPG axis activation): Females — delayed menstruation, lower bone density risk; generally less harmful medically. Males — social stigma, potential underlying pathology (hypogonadism, chronic illness). Both extremes require investigation. Endocrine cause of late puberty in boys often involves low LH/FSH (hypogonadotropic hypogonadism), treatable with GnRH or gonadotropin therapy. Early puberty may be idiopathic or due to tumours producing ectopic hormones.

Set A Knowledge & Recall Easy

What is the sex chromosome combination in a human female? Easy
Show Answer

A human female has the sex chromosome combination XX — two X chromosomes, one inherited from each parent.

What is the sex chromosome combination in a human male? Easy
Show Answer

A human male has the sex chromosome combination XY — one X chromosome (from the mother) and one Y chromosome (from the father).

Which parent determines the sex of the child — the mother or the father? Explain briefly. Easy
Show Answer

The father determines the sex of the child. The mother can only contribute an X chromosome (since she is XX). The father contributes either an X (producing a girl, XX) or a Y (producing a boy, XY) from his sperm.

What is the probability that a child will be a boy? Easy
Show Answer

The probability is 50% (1 in 2) because the father produces equal numbers of X-bearing and Y-bearing sperm, and either type has an equal chance of fertilising the egg.

How many chromosomes does a normal human cell contain? How many are sex chromosomes? Easy
Show Answer

A normal human cell contains 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). Of these, 2 are sex chromosomes (either XX or XY); the remaining 44 are autosomes.

What type of chromosomes determines traits other than sex? Easy
Show Answer

Autosomes (non-sex chromosomes) determine traits other than biological sex. Humans have 22 pairs of autosomes.

If a couple already has two daughters, what is the probability that their next child will be a son? Easy
Show Answer

The probability is still 50%. Each conception is an independent event; the sex of previous children does not influence the probability of the next child's sex.

What is the SRY gene and what does it do? Easy
Show Answer

The SRY (Sex-determining Region of Y) gene is located on the Y chromosome. It triggers development of testes in the embryo. Without SRY, the default developmental pathway produces female anatomy, even in XY individuals who lack a functional SRY gene.

Draw a Punnett square to show the possible sex chromosome combinations of the children of a normal couple. Medium
Show Answer

Mother (XX) × Father (XY). Punnett square: Top row headers: X (from father), Y (from father). Side headers: X (from mother), X (from mother). Results: XX, XY, XX, XY. Ratio: 1 XX (girl) : 1 XY (boy) = 50% girls, 50% boys.

Explain why blaming a woman for the sex of her child is scientifically incorrect. Medium
Show Answer

A woman (XX) can only donate an X chromosome to every egg she produces. The sex of the child is determined by which sperm fertilises the egg. A Y-bearing sperm produces a boy; an X-bearing sperm produces a girl. Since the woman contributes no Y chromosome, she cannot be responsible for producing a son or a daughter — responsibility lies entirely with the father's sperm.

What is sex-linked inheritance? Give one example of a sex-linked condition. Medium
Show Answer

Sex-linked inheritance refers to traits controlled by genes located on the sex chromosomes (usually the X chromosome). Because males have only one X chromosome, a recessive allele on the X has no masking dominant allele and will always be expressed. Example: haemophilia A (inability to clot blood properly), caused by a recessive allele on the X chromosome, affects males far more often than females.

In birds, sex determination is the reverse of mammals (females are ZW, males are ZZ). Why does this make evolutionary sense? Medium
Show Answer

Regardless of which sex is heterogametic (ZW or XY), the fundamental principle is the same: one sex contributes two different sex chromosomes, producing offspring of roughly equal sex ratio. Bird evolution happened independently of mammal sex-determination mechanisms. The ZW system in birds arose from different ancestral chromosomes and provides the same 50:50 offspring ratio, which is evolutionarily stable (Fisher's principle: rare sex gets a reproductive advantage, maintaining 50:50).

Explain the social problem of female foeticide in India and how it relates to a misunderstanding of sex determination biology. Medium
Show Answer

Female foeticide (abortion of female foetuses after sex-selective testing) has occurred in India, often driven by a preference for male children and the mistaken belief that the mother is responsible for the baby's sex. Scientifically, this is wrong — the father's sperm determines the sex (as explained above). The practice has caused severely skewed sex ratios in some regions. India's Pre-Conception and Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques (PCPNDT) Act prohibits sex-selective abortion and prenatal sex determination.

What is Turner syndrome and Klinefelter syndrome? How do they arise? Medium
Show Answer

Turner syndrome (45, XO): a female with only one X chromosome, caused by non-disjunction (failure of sex chromosomes to separate) during meiosis. Effects: short stature, infertility, heart defects. Klinefelter syndrome (47, XXY): a male with an extra X chromosome. Effects: taller stature, reduced fertility, some breast tissue development. Both arise from non-disjunction errors during gamete formation, resulting in abnormal chromosome numbers.

If both X chromosomes in a female carry the gene for colour blindness, will she be colour-blind? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

Yes. If both X chromosomes carry the recessive colour-blindness allele (she is homozygous recessive, X^cX^c), she will be colour-blind. Females are usually carriers (X^cX) because one normal dominant allele masks the recessive. But when both alleles are recessive, there is no masking, and colour blindness is expressed — though this is rare in females.

Why are males called the "heterogametic sex"? What practical consequence does this have? Medium
Show Answer

Males produce two types of gametes (sperm): X-bearing and Y-bearing, making them heterogametic. Females produce only X-bearing eggs, making them homogametic. The practical consequence: the type of sperm that fertilises the egg determines the child's sex. It also means males express all X-linked recessive alleles (since they have no second X to mask them), making X-linked recessive conditions like haemophilia and colour blindness far more common in males.

[MYP D] Evaluate whether sex is binary (male/female only) from a biological perspective. Use chromosome, hormone, and anatomical evidence. Hard
Show Answer

Traditional binary view: XX = female, XY = male. Evidence against strict binary: (1) Chromosome variation — XXY, XYY, XO, XXX exist naturally. (2) SRY mutations — XY individuals with non-functional SRY develop as female (complete androgen insensitivity syndrome). (3) Intersex conditions — ambiguous genitalia, congenital adrenal hyperplasia. Biologically, sex is best described as a bimodal distribution rather than a strict binary, with most individuals at one of two peaks but significant natural variation existing along a spectrum.

Analyse the evolutionary advantage of having two sexes with a 50:50 ratio rather than all-female asexual reproduction. Hard
Show Answer

Asexual reproduction (all-female) doubles reproductive output per individual. However, sexual reproduction creates genetic diversity through recombination, which: (1) accelerates adaptation to changing environments; (2) creates novel allele combinations that may confer resistance to new pathogens (Red Queen hypothesis); (3) purges harmful mutations more efficiently. The 50:50 ratio is maintained by Fisher's principle: if one sex is rarer, parents of that sex produce more offspring on average, conferring selection advantage until the ratio returns to equality. Despite the "cost of males" (halving reproduction rate), genetic diversity benefits outweigh this cost in variable environments.

[MYP D] Critically assess the ethics of sex selection technologies (e.g., sperm sorting, PGT-A). When, if ever, are these justified? Hard
Show Answer

Medical justification: sex selection to prevent sex-linked genetic diseases (haemophilia, Duchenne muscular dystrophy) has strong ethical support — parents avoid passing severe conditions to sons. Social sex selection (choosing sex for preference) is highly controversial: arguments for include reproductive autonomy and family balancing. Arguments against: commodification of children, reinforcing gender stereotypes, potential to skew sex ratios (as seen with selective abortion in some cultures), slippery slope toward designer babies. Most countries allow medical sex selection but prohibit social sex selection. The debate requires balancing reproductive autonomy against societal harm.

Explain the difference between genetic sex, gonadal sex, hormonal sex, and phenotypic sex. Give an example where these may not align. Hard
Show Answer

Genetic sex: sex chromosomes (XX/XY). Gonadal sex: presence of ovaries or testes. Hormonal sex: balance of oestrogen/testosterone. Phenotypic sex: external anatomy and secondary sexual characteristics. Example of misalignment — Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (CAIS): Genetic sex XY, gonadal sex male (testes present internally), hormonal sex: testosterone produced but cells cannot respond (androgen receptor mutation), phenotypic sex: female external anatomy (vagina, female body shape). The person appears female and identifies as female. This demonstrates sex is multi-dimensional and not solely determined by chromosomes.

What is genomic imprinting and how does it relate to sex determination? Give one medically relevant example. Hard
Show Answer

Genomic imprinting is the phenomenon where the expression of certain genes depends on which parent they were inherited from — not just which allele is present. Some imprinted genes are relevant to sex-linked conditions: example — Angelman syndrome arises when the maternally inherited copy of chromosome 15q11-q13 is deleted or silenced (the paternal copy is already imprinted/silenced). Prader-Willi syndrome results from loss of the paternally inherited copy. These are not sex-determination genes per se, but demonstrate that parent-of-origin genetic expression affects development. In sex determination context, certain Y-linked genes involved in spermatogenesis are imprinted differently depending on transmission pattern.

[MYP D] "The father who carries an X-linked recessive disease gene will never pass it to his sons." Evaluate this statement with a genetic cross diagram described in words. Hard
Show Answer

The statement is TRUE. A father with an X-linked recessive trait (e.g., haemophilia) has genotype X^h Y. He passes his Y chromosome to sons and his X^h to daughters. Sons receive their X from their mother. Cross: X^h Y (father) × X^N X^N (unaffected mother). Offspring: X^N X^h (carrier daughters), X^N Y (unaffected sons). None of the sons inherit the father's X^h allele. However, all daughters become carriers — and their sons (the father's grandsons) have a 50% chance of being affected. So while sons are safe, the disease skips a generation through carrier daughters.

What is metamorphosis? Give one example of an animal that undergoes complete metamorphosis. Easy
Show Answer

Metamorphosis is a dramatic biological process of transformation from a larval form to the adult form. Example: a frog (tadpole → froglet → adult frog) or a butterfly (egg → caterpillar → pupa → butterfly) undergoes complete metamorphosis.

Which hormone controls metamorphosis of a tadpole into a frog? Easy
Show Answer

Thyroxine (produced by the tadpole's thyroid gland) controls metamorphosis from a tadpole into a frog. It triggers resorption of the tail, limb growth, lung development, and other changes.

What is ecdysone and what does it do in insects? Easy
Show Answer

Ecdysone is a steroid hormone produced by the prothoracic glands of insects. It triggers moulting (ecdysis) — the shedding of the old exoskeleton — allowing growth and metamorphosis in insects.

What is seasonal breeding? Give one animal example. Easy
Show Answer

Seasonal breeding is the tendency of animals to mate and reproduce at specific times of year, triggered by hormonal responses to environmental cues (day length, temperature). Example: deer breed in autumn (rutting season) when testosterone peaks in males due to shortening day length.

What environmental signal often triggers seasonal hormonal changes in animals? Easy
Show Answer

Photoperiod (day length — the number of hours of light per day) is the most common environmental signal that triggers seasonal hormonal changes in animals, especially seasonal breeding cycles.

Name the hormone from the pituitary that controls metamorphosis indirectly in amphibians. Easy
Show Answer

TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone) from the tadpole's pituitary gland stimulates the thyroid gland to produce thyroxine, which directly drives metamorphosis.

What is the role of juvenile hormone (JH) in insect development? Easy
Show Answer

Juvenile hormone (JH), produced by the corpora allata glands behind the brain, maintains the larval (immature) characteristics during moulting. High JH keeps the insect as a larva; when JH drops and ecdysone dominates, metamorphosis to the adult form occurs.

Give one example of a hormone affecting animal behaviour. Easy
Show Answer

Testosterone in male animals increases aggressive and territorial behaviour, especially during breeding season. Example: rooster crowing and fighting behaviour is reduced significantly by castration (removal of testosterone source).

Set B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain the hormonal control of metamorphosis in a frog. What would happen if you blocked thyroxine production in a tadpole? Medium
Show Answer

As the tadpole matures, TSH from its pituitary stimulates thyroxine production. Rising thyroxine triggers: tail resorption, limb bud development, lung formation, eye repositioning, jaw changes, and skin transformation. If thyroxine is blocked (e.g., by anti-thyroid drugs), the tadpole would remain in larval form indefinitely — it would continue to grow as a large tadpole but fail to metamorphose. This was demonstrated experimentally and shows that thyroxine is essential and sufficient for metamorphosis.

Compare insect metamorphosis (ecdysone/JH system) with amphibian metamorphosis (thyroxine system). Medium
Show Answer

Insects: ecdysone (steroid, from prothoracic gland) drives moulting; juvenile hormone suppresses adult development; low JH + ecdysone → metamorphosis. Amphibians: thyroxine (iodine-based amino acid derivative, from thyroid) drives metamorphosis; regulated by TSH from pituitary. Similarity: both use hormones to coordinate a complex developmental transition involving remodelling of body structures. Difference: insects undergo complete metamorphosis with a pupal stage; amphibians undergo gradual metamorphosis without a pupal stage. Both systems respond to developmental timing signals.

Why do migratory birds begin their long journeys in autumn? What hormonal mechanism is involved? Medium
Show Answer

Shortening day length (photoperiod) in autumn is detected by the bird's pineal gland and retina, which signal the hypothalamus. The hypothalamus adjusts GnRH, TSH, and melatonin secretion patterns. These changes trigger: fattening (hyperphagia) for fuel stores, gonadal regression, restlessness (Zugunruhe), and navigation behaviour. Melatonin from the pineal gland plays a key role in synchronising biological clocks with seasonal light changes, cuing migration readiness.

Explain how dairy farmers use hormones to increase milk production in cows. Is this practice safe for consumers? Medium
Show Answer

Recombinant bovine somatotropin (rBST/rBGH) — a synthetic growth hormone — is injected into dairy cows to increase milk yield by 10–15%. It works by increasing IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1) in cows, stimulating mammary gland activity. Consumer safety debate: the EU and many countries ban rBST; the FDA in the US approved it as safe, citing milk composition is unchanged. Critics note marginally elevated IGF-1 in milk may stimulate cell proliferation, raising theoretical cancer concerns. Long-term human safety data remains contested.

What is hibernation, and which hormones are involved in triggering it in bears? Medium
Show Answer

Hibernation is a state of metabolic depression and reduced body temperature adopted in winter to conserve energy when food is scarce. In bears (torpor rather than true hibernation): declining day length and cooling temperatures reduce thyroid activity (lowering metabolic rate) and increase insulin sensitivity (fat storage before winter). Cortisol and prolactin levels fluctuate to regulate fat metabolism during the torpor period. Melatonin from the pineal gland synchronises the circadian clock with seasonal change.

How does the use of pesticides that mimic insect juvenile hormone represent a targeted pest control strategy? Medium
Show Answer

Insect growth regulators (IGRs) such as methoprene mimic juvenile hormone. When larvae are exposed, JH levels stay artificially high, preventing metamorphosis to the reproductive adult stage. The larvae moult into sterile adults or die because the normal developmental switch is disrupted. This is highly target-specific (only insects with JH receptors are affected) and is safer for vertebrates and the environment than broad-spectrum insecticides. Used in mosquito larvae control and flea products.

What is the role of melatonin in regulating seasonal reproduction in sheep? Medium
Show Answer

Sheep are short-day breeders (breed in autumn/winter). The pineal gland secretes melatonin during darkness. As nights lengthen in autumn, melatonin secretion duration increases. Prolonged melatonin signal activates the hypothalamus to increase GnRH pulsatility, stimulating LH/FSH release and thus activating the reproductive cycle. Farmers can manipulate breeding season by using artificial light or melatonin implants to shift the reproductive season for earlier lambing.

Explain how salmon undergo physiological changes when migrating from fresh water to the ocean, and the role of hormones in these changes. Medium
Show Answer

Salmon must switch from freshwater (low salt) to marine (high salt) osmoregulation. This process (smoltification) is triggered by: cortisol (activates salt-secreting chloride cells in gills), growth hormone (increases gill Na+/K+-ATPase expression for active salt excretion), and thyroid hormones (coordinate overall metabolic changes). Smoltification is triggered by photoperiod (increasing day length in spring), preparing salmon for oceanic migration. The reverse process (anadromous return) when spawning also involves hormonal coordination.

[MYP D] Evaluate the impact of climate change on animal hormonal cycles, particularly seasonal breeding. Hard

Many seasonal breeders use temperature and day length as hormonal cues. Climate change alters temperature patterns while day length remains constant (governed by Earth's tilt). Show Answer

Many invertebrates use diffuse neuroendocrine systems rather than discrete endocrine glands. Explain the neuroendocrine mechanism by which a queen bee suppresses reproduction in worker bees. Hard
Show Answer

Queen bees produce queen mandibular pheromone (QMP), a chemical mixture that: (1) directly inhibits worker ovary development by suppressing vitellogenin (egg yolk protein) gene expression; (2) reduces juvenile hormone levels in workers, keeping them in their sterile worker developmental pathway; (3) suppresses GnRH-equivalent peptides in worker brains. Describe the hormonal basis of parental behaviour in mammals, focusing on oxytocin and prolactin. Hard
Show Answer

Oxytocin (from posterior pituitary): released during birth and suckling; promotes bonding between mother and infant; stimulates milk let-down reflex; activates neural reward circuits making infant care pleasurable. [MYP D] Analyse how the evolution of hormonal systems across animal phyla supports the theory of common descent. Hard
Show Answer

Many hormones are structurally conserved across phyla. Insulin-like peptides exist in insects, nematodes, and vertebrates — suggesting a common ancestral signalling molecule. Divergences (like the ZW vs. XY system) show how independent evolutionary lineages can solve the same problem differently.

Why do adolescents need more calcium in their diet than adults? Easy
Show Answer

During adolescence, bones are growing rapidly in length and density. Calcium is the key mineral required for building strong bones and teeth. Adequate calcium intake during adolescence establishes peak bone mass, which reduces the risk of osteoporosis in later life.

Why do adolescent girls need more iron than adolescent boys? Easy
Show Answer

Adolescent girls lose iron monthly during menstruation. Iron is required for haemoglobin synthesis; without sufficient iron, anaemia can develop, causing fatigue, poor concentration, and weakened immunity. Boys also need iron for muscle growth, but their requirements are lower since they do not have monthly blood loss.

What is the role of protein in the diet of a growing adolescent? Easy
Show Answer

Proteins provide amino acids that are the building blocks for growth of muscle, organs, enzymes, and hormones. During the adolescent growth spurt, protein demands are high to support tissue synthesis, including muscle mass development (especially in males).

What is Type 1 diabetes and how is it treated? Easy
Show Answer

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system destroys the insulin-producing beta cells of the pancreas. Without insulin, blood glucose cannot enter cells and rises dangerously. It is treated with daily insulin injections or an insulin pump, combined with blood glucose monitoring and a balanced diet.

What is Type 2 diabetes and how is it managed? Easy
Show Answer

Type 2 diabetes is a metabolic condition in which body cells become resistant to insulin's action, and/or the pancreas produces insufficient insulin. It is managed with dietary changes (low-glycaemic, high-fibre diet), weight loss, regular exercise, and oral medications (e.g., metformin). Advanced cases may require insulin therapy.

Name two good dietary sources of calcium for adolescents. Easy
Show Answer

Two good sources of calcium: (1) Dairy products — milk, cheese, yoghurt. (2) Green leafy vegetables — spinach, broccoli, kale. Others include fortified soy milk, tofu, and fish with edible bones (sardines, salmon).

What is anaemia and which dietary deficiency causes it? Easy
Show Answer

Anaemia is a condition in which the blood has insufficient healthy red blood cells or haemoglobin to carry adequate oxygen to tissues. The most common dietary cause is iron deficiency, which reduces haemoglobin synthesis. Symptoms include fatigue, pale skin, shortness of breath, and poor concentration.

Why should adolescents avoid excessive consumption of sugar and refined carbohydrates? Easy
Show Answer

Excessive sugar and refined carbohydrates cause rapid blood glucose spikes, promoting insulin resistance over time. They contribute to obesity (which is a risk factor for Type 2 diabetes), tooth decay, and energy crashes. They also displace nutrient-rich foods needed for growth, potentially causing micronutrient deficiencies.

Set B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain why both under-nutrition and over-nutrition are health problems for adolescents. Give one consequence of each. Medium
Show Answer

Under-nutrition: inadequate caloric or nutrient intake impairs growth, delays puberty, weakens immunity, and causes anaemia or bone weakness. Example consequence: stunted height from protein-energy malnutrition. Over-nutrition: excessive caloric intake leading to obesity increases the risk of Type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, joint problems, and mental health issues. Example consequence: insulin resistance and pre-diabetes in obese teenagers, now increasingly common globally.

Explain the hormonal connection between stress, poor sleep, and weight gain in adolescents. Medium
Show Answer

Stress elevates cortisol, which promotes fat storage (especially abdominal), increases appetite for high-calorie foods, and impairs insulin sensitivity. Poor sleep (common in adolescents who sleep 6–7 hours instead of 9) reduces leptin (satiety hormone) and increases ghrelin (hunger hormone), making overeating more likely. Combined, chronic stress and poor sleep create a hormonal environment that promotes weight gain independent of caloric intake alone.

Why is Vitamin D important alongside calcium for bone health, and how can adolescents ensure adequate Vitamin D? Medium
Show Answer

Vitamin D is required for calcium absorption in the small intestine. Without sufficient Vitamin D, dietary calcium is poorly absorbed even if intake is high. Rickets (soft, curved bones) can result in children; osteomalacia in adults. Sources: sunlight exposure (skin synthesises Vitamin D3 from UV-B light), fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), egg yolks, fortified dairy products. Adolescents who spend most time indoors or cover skin for cultural/religious reasons are particularly at risk of Vitamin D deficiency.

Describe what happens to blood glucose levels after eating a meal, and how the body restores normal levels. Name all hormones involved. Medium
Show Answer

After a meal: blood glucose rises (hyperglycaemia). The pancreatic beta cells detect this and secrete insulin. Insulin causes: cells to take up glucose, liver to convert glucose to glycogen (glycogenesis), fat cells to store glucose as fat. Blood glucose falls. If it falls too low (hypoglycaemia): pancreatic alpha cells secrete glucagon, which stimulates liver to break down glycogen to glucose (glycogenolysis) and convert amino acids/fats to glucose (gluconeogenesis). Adrenaline also raises blood glucose during stress. Normal fasting blood glucose is 70–100 mg/dL.

How does regular physical exercise benefit adolescent health from a hormonal perspective? Medium
Show Answer

Exercise: (1) Increases insulin sensitivity, reducing Type 2 diabetes risk. (2) Stimulates GH and testosterone release, promoting muscle and bone growth. (3) Reduces cortisol (stress hormone) over the long term, improving mood and sleep. (4) Increases endorphins and serotonin, reducing depression and anxiety risk. (5) Increases bone density by stimulating osteoblast activity. WHO recommends 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity daily for adolescents.

What is the body mass index (BMI) and why are separate BMI charts used for adolescents compared to adults? Medium
Show Answer

BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)². It estimates body fat based on weight relative to height. Adult BMI has fixed thresholds: <18.5 underweight, 18.5–24.9 normal, ≥25 overweight. Adolescents undergo rapid changes in body composition (muscle, fat, bone) with puberty, and both weight and height change simultaneously. Age- and sex-specific BMI-for-age percentile charts account for these normal developmental changes; a 14-year-old's "healthy" BMI range is different from a 17-year-old's. Using adult thresholds would incorrectly classify many healthy adolescents.

Explain why consuming alcohol during adolescence is particularly harmful compared to adulthood. Medium
Show Answer

The adolescent brain is still developing, particularly the prefrontal cortex (maturation completes around age 25). Alcohol interferes with neurotransmitter systems (GABA, glutamate) and disrupts the development of neural connections. Consequences of adolescent alcohol use: impaired memory and learning, higher risk of addiction (adolescent brains are more vulnerable to dependence), hormonal disruption (alcohol interferes with GH, testosterone, oestrogen secretion), liver damage at lower doses, and greater risk of accidents and risky behaviour due to impaired judgement.

Describe the importance of mental health as a component of adolescent wellbeing. How do hormones contribute to mood disorders in adolescence? Medium
Show Answer

Adolescence is the peak onset period for many mental health conditions (depression, anxiety, eating disorders). Hormonal fluctuations — particularly rapid changes in oestrogen/testosterone and their effects on serotonin and dopamine pathways — contribute to mood instability. Cortisol dysregulation from academic/social stress further impairs mood and sleep. The WHO estimates that 50% of all mental health conditions begin by age 14. Addressing sleep, nutrition, physical activity, and social connection can mitigate hormonal contributions to mental health disorders, making holistic adolescent health frameworks essential.

Longitudinal cohort study: Recruit 500 adolescents aged 12–14. Randomise: Group A (high calcium: ≥1300 mg/day through diet + dairy); Group B (normal diet: ~600 mg/day). Measure BMD (DEXA) at baseline (age 12), 18, and 30. Controlled variables: Vitamin D status, physical activity, sex, ethnicity, smoking status. Challenges: (1) 18-year follow-up → high dropout rates; (2) Ethical limitations on strict dietary control for decades; (3) Confounders accumulate over time; (4) Diet changes with life events; (5) Supplementation may be needed if Group A cannot reach targets through food alone. Alternative: retrospective cohort or case-control study using dietary recall, though recall bias is a limitation.

Analyse the hormonal and nutritional factors that contribute to eating disorders (anorexia nervosa, bulimia) in adolescents. Hard
Show Answer

Hormonal factors: serotonin dysregulation (affects appetite and mood); cortisol elevation (stress-driven eating suppression); altered GH/IGF-1 in severely malnourished states leads to stunted growth; oestrogen deficiency from amenorrhoea causes bone loss. Leptin falls with extreme weight loss, theoretically driving further food restriction (paradoxically, as leptin normally promotes eating when low, but cognitive override in anorexia overrides this signal). Nutritional consequences: protein-energy malnutrition, hypokalaemia (from purging in bulimia — cardiac arrhythmia risk), anaemia, osteoporosis. Multifactorial aetiology: genetic predisposition, sociocultural pressures (thin ideal), perfectionistic personality traits, and adverse childhood experiences all contribute alongside hormonal dysregulation.

[MYP D] "The medicalisation of adolescence — treating normal developmental changes as diseases — does more harm than good." Critically assess this view. Hard
Show Answer

Argument for medicalisation: early identification of Type 2 diabetes, eating disorders, PCOS, and depression allows earlier intervention and better outcomes. Preventive medicine (vaccines, nutritional screening) saves lives. Against: over-pathologising normal mood swings as clinical depression or hormonal fluctuations as PCOS may lead to over-prescription, stigma, and loss of confidence in normal self-regulation. Pharmaceutical industry incentives may inflate diagnostic thresholds. Adolescent risk-taking is developmentally appropriate, not pathological. Balance: evidence-based criteria for clinical intervention are essential; preventive frameworks should address social determinants of health rather than solely medicalising normal variation.

Explain how iron deficiency anaemia specifically affects academic performance in adolescents. Link cellular mechanisms to observable outcomes. Hard
Show Answer

Iron is essential for: (1) Haemoglobin synthesis — reduced haemoglobin lowers oxygen delivery to the brain; (2) Myelin formation — iron is required for myelin (nerve insulation), affecting neural signal speed; (3) Neurotransmitter synthesis — dopamine and serotonin synthesis requires iron-dependent enzymes; (4) Mitochondrial electron transport — iron-sulphur proteins in the electron transport chain are iron-dependent; neuronal energy production falls. Observable outcomes: fatigue, poor attention span, slower processing speed, reduced working memory, difficulty concentrating, poor performance in timed tasks and executive function tests. Supplementation trials show significant improvement in cognitive performance in previously iron-deficient adolescents.

Research question: Is there a significant difference in calcium intake between vegetarian and non-vegetarian Grade 8 students? Data collection: 3-day food diary (including one weekend day) completed by 40 students (20 veg, 20 non-veg). Diary entries converted to nutrient data using a validated Indian food composition database (IFCT-2017). Statistical test: independent samples t-test or Mann–Whitney U (if data non-normal). Ethical safeguards: (1) Parental consent and student assent; (2) Anonymised data — no names on diaries; (3) No judgement of dietary choices; (4) Nutritional counselling available for all students regardless of results; (5) Ethics review by school administration. Limitation: self-reporting underestimates actual intake (social desirability bias).

Critically analyse the effectiveness of school-based nutrition education programmes in changing adolescent eating behaviour. What factors limit their impact? Hard
Show Answer

Evidence for effectiveness: knowledge-based programmes increase nutritional literacy; interactive cooking and behaviour-change interventions show modest improvements in fruit/vegetable intake; programmes addressing social norms can reduce stigma around body image. Limitations: (1) Knowledge–behaviour gap: knowing something is healthy does not ensure behaviour change; (2) Home food environment dominates — if nutritious food is unavailable or unaffordable at home, school education has limited reach; (3) Peer pressure and media advertising for junk food are powerful countervailing forces; (4) Food deserts (low-income areas lacking fresh produce) create structural barriers; (5) Short-term programmes show knowledge gains that rarely persist over 1+ years without ongoing reinforcement. Systemic approaches (school canteen policies, pricing incentives for healthy food, restricting junk food advertising to children) are more effective than education alone.

[MYP D] Using your knowledge of insulin, evaluate the claim that artificial sweeteners are a healthy alternative to sugar for adolescents with Type 2 diabetes risk. Hard
Show Answer

Initial case for sweeteners: they provide sweetness without glucose, avoiding blood glucose spikes and insulin demand. Useful for reducing caloric intake in obese adolescents. Evidence against: (1) Some studies suggest artificial sweeteners (e.g., saccharin, sucralose) alter gut microbiome composition, potentially impairing glucose metabolism indirectly; (2) "Cephalic phase insulin response" — the sweet taste alone may trigger insulin release before glucose arrives, potentially causing compensatory hunger; (3) May perpetuate preference for intensely sweet tastes, making healthful whole foods less palatable; (4) Long-term safety data in children is limited. Current consensus: occasional use is acceptable, but whole-food dietary patterns (reducing all added sweeteners) are preferable for long-term metabolic health in adolescents with Type 2 diabetes risk.
