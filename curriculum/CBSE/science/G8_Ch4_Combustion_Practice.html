Combustion Definition

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Define combustion.Easy
Show Answer

Combustion is a chemical reaction between a fuel and oxygen that releases heat and light energy. It is an exothermic oxidation reaction.

Write the general word equation for complete combustion of a hydrocarbon fuel.Easy
Show Answer

Fuel (hydrocarbon) + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Heat + Light

Name the three conditions necessary for combustion to occur.Easy
Show Answer

Fuel (combustible material), Oxygen (supporter of combustion), and Heat (ignition temperature must be reached). These three form the "fire triangle."

What is ignition temperature?Easy
Show Answer

The ignition temperature (ignition point) is the minimum temperature to which a substance must be heated before it can catch fire and start burning on its own.

What type of energy change occurs during combustion?Easy
Show Answer

Chemical energy stored in the fuel is converted to thermal (heat) energy and light energy during combustion. It is exothermic — energy is released to the surroundings.

Does combustion require the fuel to be in any specific state? Give an example.Easy
Show Answer

Most fuels burn as gases or vapours (even solid/liquid fuels vaporise before burning). Example: candle wax melts and vaporises; the wax vapour burns, not the solid wax.

Is combustion a physical change or a chemical change? Give a reason.Easy
Show Answer

Combustion is a chemical change — new substances (CO₂ and water) are formed, the reaction is irreversible, and energy is permanently released.

Write the balanced chemical equation for the combustion of methane (natural gas).Easy
Show Answer

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + energy. Methane burns completely in excess oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water vapour.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain why all combustion reactions are exothermic but not all exothermic reactions are combustion.Medium
Show Answer

Combustion specifically involves a fuel reacting with oxygen to release energy — all combustion is exothermic. But other reactions (neutralisation, condensation) also release heat without involving oxygen or combustion. Combustion is a subset of exothermic reactions.

Explain how lowering the ignition temperature of a fuel affects fire safety risks.Medium
Show Answer

Lower ignition temperature means the fuel can catch fire at lower temperatures — even from warm surfaces or ambient heat. This makes it more hazardous to store and handle. E.g., petrol (ignition temp ~246°C) is more dangerous than diesel (~250°C) which is more dangerous than wood (~300°C).

Describe an experiment to show that oxygen is necessary for combustion.Medium
Show Answer

Light two identical candles. Cover one with a glass jar (sealing the air). The covered candle goes out as it depletes the oxygen inside; the uncovered candle keeps burning. Conclusion: oxygen is necessary for combustion.

Why does a fuel need to be heated to its ignition temperature before it will burn, even though the reaction releases energy?Medium
Show Answer

Activation energy is needed to break the initial chemical bonds in the fuel molecule, starting the reaction. Once started, the exothermic reaction releases far more energy than was put in — sustaining itself. The initial heat input is the "activation energy" to overcome the energy barrier.

How does the combustion of coal in a power station differ from combustion of natural gas in terms of products and efficiency?Medium
Show Answer

Coal combustion: produces CO₂, SO₂, NOₓ, particulates, ash — large amounts of pollutants. Natural gas: mainly CO₂ and H₂O — far fewer pollutants. Thermal efficiency: modern gas turbines achieve 55–60% vs coal plants at 33–40%. Natural gas is both cleaner and more efficient.

Explain why a campfire requires continuous oxygen supply but an oil lamp requires less active ventilation.Medium
Show Answer

Campfire: large open flame with high fuel consumption needs rapid oxygen supply from surrounding air currents. Oil lamp: small controlled flame with a wick that regulates fuel supply; only small oxygen flow needed for steady low-intensity combustion — ambient air diffusion is sufficient.

Why is it incorrect to say "the paper burns" — what actually burns during paper combustion?Medium
Show Answer

Paper (cellulose) is a solid; solids do not directly combust. Heat decomposes the paper (pyrolysis) releasing volatile gases (CO, CH₄, various organics). These gases then ignite and burn. The solid paper is converted to gas before actual combustion — visible as the flame hovers above the paper surface.

Balance the combustion equation for propane (C₃H₈) used in LPG cylinders.Medium
Show Answer

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O. Check: C: 3=3 ✓, H: 8=8 ✓, O: 10=6+4=10 ✓. Balanced.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Analyse the energy transformations in a coal-fired power station from the formation of coal to electrical energy reaching your home.Hard
Show Answer

Ancient solar energy (photosynthesis) → stored as chemical energy in coal (millions of years) → combustion converts chemical → thermal energy (heat) → steam (thermal → kinetic energy) → turbine (kinetic → mechanical energy) → generator (mechanical → electrical energy) → transmission lines → home appliances (electrical → light/heat/mechanical). Each conversion loses energy as heat. Total efficiency from coal energy to home use: ~30–35%.

Using the concept of activation energy, explain why some fuels burn more readily than others.Hard
Show Answer

Activation energy is the minimum energy needed to start the combustion reaction (break fuel bonds). Fuels with weaker chemical bonds (white phosphorus: P-P bonds weak) have low activation energy — ignite at room temperature. Fuels with strong bonds (wood, coal: C-C and C-H bonds) require more heating to reach ignition. The activation energy directly determines a fuel's ignition temperature.

Evaluate the argument that hydrogen is the "perfect fuel" for replacing fossil fuels based on its combustion chemistry.Hard
Show Answer

Combustion: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O + energy. Products: only water — zero CO₂, zero SO₂, zero particulates. High calorific value (120,000 kJ/kg vs petrol's 45,000). However: highly flammable (low ignition temperature 500°C, wide flammability range 4–75% in air), requires liquid at -253°C (cryogenic storage) or high-pressure tanks. Currently produced mostly from natural gas (grey hydrogen, not clean). "Perfect" in combustion chemistry, but challenges in production, storage, and safety must be overcome.

Propose how understanding combustion chemistry can guide the design of more efficient engines.Hard
Show Answer

Higher compression ratios increase temperature before ignition, enabling more complete combustion and higher thermodynamic efficiency (Carnot principle: efficiency increases with higher temperature differential). Fuel injection optimises air-fuel ratio for complete combustion, reducing CO and unburnt hydrocarbons. Catalytic converters oxidise remaining CO and hydrocarbons post-combustion. Turbocharging recycles exhaust energy. Combined, these convert combustion knowledge into engines achieving 40%+ thermal efficiency.

Critically evaluate whether combustion will still be used for energy generation in 2075, and if so, in which sectors.Hard
Show Answer

Direct electrification will eliminate combustion in most sectors by 2075: cars, trains, home heating (heat pumps), most industry. Remaining combustion: aviation (SAF or hydrogen combustion in jet turbines), shipping (green ammonia or hydrogen combustion), high-temperature industrial processes (cement, glass), and as backup power generation. Combustion using carbon-neutral fuels (biofuels, green hydrogen, synthetic fuels) will remain; fossil fuel combustion should be near-zero. The fire triangle survives, but the fuel changes fundamentally.

Analyse how the development of the steam engine (based on combustion) changed human history and whether a similar transformation is happening with electric motors.Hard
Show Answer

Steam engine (1760s–1800s): amplified human mechanical power by 100x, enabling industrialisation, urbanisation, railways, and global trade — reshaping economies, societies, and ecology. Electric motors (1880s onwards, accelerating now): convert electrical energy from renewables to mechanical work with 90%+ efficiency vs steam's 10–15%. EVs, industrial robots, and renewable energy systems represent a comparable transformation. The shift from combustion to electrification may be as historically significant as the original Industrial Revolution — but if powered by renewables, far more environmentally benign.

Design an investigation to find the calorific value of two different fuels (candle wax and ethanol) using simple school lab equipment.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Known mass of fuel in spirit lamp/candle. Measure mass before/after burning. Heat fixed volume of water (100 mL) in beaker; record temperature rise. Calculate: Energy = mass of water × 4.2 J/g°C × temperature rise. Calorific value = energy released / mass of fuel burned. Compare for candle wax and ethanol. Controls: same water volume, same conditions, repeat 3 times. Expected: ethanol ~27,000 kJ/kg; candle (paraffin) ~42,000 kJ/kg. Discuss: heat losses to environment as major source of error — actual values will be lower than published values.

Using thermodynamics, explain why a fuel cell is fundamentally more efficient than combustion for converting chemical energy to work.Hard
Show Answer

Combustion converts chemical energy → thermal energy → mechanical work. Carnot limit: efficiency = 1 - T(cold)/T(hot). For a car engine (~600°C hot, ~100°C cold): max 60%, practical 25–35%. Fuel cell converts chemical energy directly to electrical energy via electrochemical reactions, bypassing thermal conversion. Not subject to Carnot limit: theoretical efficiency 83%, practical 50–60%. This is why hydrogen fuel cell vehicles are more efficient than hydrogen combustion engines — combustion is an unnecessary and lossy intermediate step.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Name the three types of combustion.Easy
Show Answer

Rapid combustion (e.g., burning candle), spontaneous combustion (e.g., white phosphorus at room temperature), and explosive combustion (e.g., LPG, gunpowder).

What is spontaneous combustion? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

Spontaneous combustion occurs when a substance catches fire on its own at room temperature, without an external ignition source. Example: white phosphorus ignites spontaneously in air; coal dust heaps can self-ignite.

What is explosive combustion? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

Explosive combustion involves extremely rapid combustion producing a large volume of hot gas very quickly, creating a shockwave. Example: LPG gas leak explosion, gunpowder, fireworks.

In which type of combustion is a match or spark required?Easy
Show Answer

Rapid combustion requires an external ignition source (match, spark) to provide initial activation energy to reach the fuel's ignition temperature.

Why is white phosphorus stored under water?Easy
Show Answer

White phosphorus undergoes spontaneous combustion in air at room temperature. Storing it under water prevents contact with oxygen, stopping the reaction.

Give two examples of rapid combustion in everyday life.Easy
Show Answer

Burning of a candle, burning of LPG on a gas stove, burning of wood in a fireplace — these are all rapid combustion requiring an ignition source.

What makes explosive combustion different from rapid combustion in terms of speed?Easy
Show Answer

Explosive combustion is extremely rapid — it occurs in milliseconds, releasing energy so fast that it creates an overpressure shockwave. Rapid combustion is relatively slower, releasing energy over seconds to hours.

What is coal dust explosion and why is it dangerous in mines?Easy
Show Answer

Coal dust suspended in air forms an explosive mixture. A single spark can trigger instantaneous combustion of the entire dust cloud, causing a violent explosion in the confined mine space.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain why a fine powder of a substance burns faster and more explosively than a solid block of the same substance.Medium
Show Answer

A fine powder has vastly greater surface area exposed to oxygen. More simultaneous reaction sites means faster combustion rate. When dispersed as dust in air (with each particle surrounded by oxygen), combustion can be near-instantaneous — explaining coal dust and flour mill explosions.

Describe the conditions under which a can of petrol could undergo explosive combustion rather than just rapid combustion.Medium
Show Answer

Petrol in an open can burns with a visible flame (rapid combustion). If petrol vapour accumulates in an enclosed space and mixes with air within flammability limits (1.4–7.6%), a spark can ignite all the vapour simultaneously — explosive combustion. This is why petrol must never be stored near sparks or flames in enclosed spaces.

Compare the ignition temperature of petrol and diesel and explain the practical safety implications.Medium
Show Answer

Petrol ignition temperature: ~246°C; flash point: -43°C (vapours ignite at room temperature). Diesel ignition temperature: ~250°C; flash point: ~55°C. Petrol vapours form at room temperature — highly flammable. Diesel must be heated significantly before vapours ignite. This makes petrol significantly more dangerous for storage and handling than diesel.

Explain why a wet haystack can undergo spontaneous combustion even without any spark.Medium
Show Answer

Wet hay encourages microbial activity (bacteria, fungi) inside the stack. Microbial respiration releases heat (exothermic biological process). In the insulated interior of a large haystack, this heat cannot escape — temperature builds up over days until the dry inner hay reaches its ignition temperature, igniting without any external flame.

Why is it critical to eliminate all ignition sources when dealing with an LPG gas leak?Medium
Show Answer

LPG (propane/butane) has a flammability range of 1.8–8.5% in air. A leak fills the kitchen air with this explosive mixture. Any spark (light switch, mobile phone, static electricity) within this range triggers explosive combustion. This is why the first action during a gas leak is to NOT turn any electrical switches on/off and to ventilate immediately.

Describe the chemical difference between deflagration and detonation as two types of explosive combustion.Medium
Show Answer

Deflagration: combustion wave travels at subsonic speeds (below speed of sound). Detonation: combustion wave travels at supersonic speeds, creating an extremely powerful shockwave. TNT and high explosives detonate; gas leaks typically deflagrate. Detonation is far more destructive.

How does the oxygen concentration in air affect the type and intensity of combustion?Medium
Show Answer

Normal air (21% O₂): regular combustion. Oxygen-enriched air (30%+): dramatically faster, hotter combustion — materials not normally flammable in air can burn (hospital fire risk with pure oxygen). Oxygen-depleted air (<16%): incomplete combustion, smouldering, produces more CO. The oxygen concentration directly controls combustion rate and completeness.

Explain why fireworks involve explosive combustion and describe the chemical components responsible.Medium
Show Answer

Fireworks contain an oxidiser (potassium nitrate, KNO₃ — provides oxygen), a fuel (carbon, charcoal, sulphur), and metal salts for colour (barium → green, strontium → red, copper → blue). When ignited, the oxidiser rapidly releases oxygen for explosive combustion of the fuel, releasing energy faster than from air alone — creating the rapid burst and colours.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the safety systems required in an LPG bottling plant to prevent explosive combustion incidents.Hard
Show Answer

Systems needed: continuous gas leak detectors calibrated below LEL (Lower Explosive Limit); forced ventilation to prevent accumulation; explosion-proof electrical equipment (no sparking); grounding of all metal equipment (prevents static electricity); automated shutdown valves if leak detected; no smoking/open flames; regular pressure testing of cylinders; trained emergency response teams. Each layer of safety reduces risk of reaching explosive concentrations — defence in depth approach.

Analyse how spontaneous combustion of coal waste piles represents both an environmental and safety hazard.Hard
Show Answer

India has hundreds of coal waste fires, some burning for decades. Environmental: continuous CO₂, CO, SO₂, and particulate emission with no energy capture — pure atmospheric pollution. Safety: underground fires create subsidence (ground collapse), releasing toxic fumes into nearby villages, and can spread to active mines. Jharia (Jharkhand) coal fire: burning since 1916, displacing 600,000 people. Chemical solution: seal with inert material (clay) to deny oxygen; physical: mine out burning material. Extremely difficult to extinguish.

Design a safe procedure for a school experiment demonstrating the difference between slow and rapid combustion using steel wool.Hard
Show Answer

Setup: in fume cupboard or outdoors. Fine steel wool (large surface area) versus a steel nail (small surface area). Hold each with tongs. Expose each to candle flame: steel nail glows faintly (slow oxidation), fine steel wool rapidly glows orange and burns (rapid combustion — hugely increased surface area). Safety: safety goggles, heat-resistant mat, tongs only, fire extinguisher nearby, no loose clothing. Discussion: surface area is the controlled variable proving its role in combustion rate.

Critically assess whether explosive combustion in car engines (internal combustion) can be made sustainable through synthetic fuels.Hard
Show Answer

Internal combustion engines use controlled explosive combustion (power stroke in cylinder). Synthetic fuels (e-fuels) made from CO₂ + green H₂ could make this process carbon-neutral — CO₂ released when burned equals CO₂ used in synthesis. Technically feasible: Porsche's eFuel plant in Chile. Concerns: energy efficiency very low (~13% renewable electricity → e-fuel → car motion vs ~77% for EV); extremely expensive ($10+/litre currently). Best suited for existing fleet and aviation/motorsport, not for replacing EVs in new car market.

Analyse the chemistry behind the explosion in the 2020 Beirut port disaster involving ammonium nitrate.Hard
Show Answer

2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃) stored unsafely. Initial fire raised temperature until NH₄NO₃ began decomposing (200°C): NH₄NO₃ → N₂O + 2H₂O (exothermic). At higher temperatures: 2NH₄NO₃ → 2N₂ + 4H₂O + O₂ — massive gas volume release caused detonation. The self-sustaining reaction (produces its own oxygen) explains why water cooling was insufficient. The explosion killed 218, injured 7,000, and devastated 6 km radius — illustrating how spontaneous/explosive combustion chemistry creates catastrophic risk when materials are improperly stored.

Evaluate whether traditional fireworks should be banned in major Indian cities on Diwali, using evidence from combustion chemistry and public health.Hard
Show Answer

Combustion chemistry: fireworks release fine particulates (PM1, PM2.5), heavy metals (barium, strontium, aluminium), SO₂, NOₓ, and CO. Delhi AQI spikes from 150 to 999+ post-Diwali night. Health evidence: acute respiratory distress increase, asthma hospitalisations surge, long-term PM2.5 exposure damages lungs. Cultural argument: fireworks are integral to Diwali celebrations, cultural rights. Balanced solution: permit only "green crackers" (reduced metal/particulate content), restrict to 2-hour window, ban Class 1–3 explosives. Complete ban is enforceable only with alternative celebratory options and community buy-in.

Using principles of chemistry, explain why hydrogen-air mixtures are more dangerous than petrol-air mixtures at similar concentrations.Hard
Show Answer

Hydrogen flammability range: 4–75% in air (very wide). Petrol vapour: 1.4–7.6% (much narrower). Hydrogen minimum ignition energy: 0.017 mJ (virtually any spark ignites it). Petrol: 0.24 mJ. Hydrogen flame speed is 7x faster than petrol (265 cm/s vs 37 cm/s) — more likely to detonate rather than deflagrate. Combined: hydrogen can explode over a much wider range of concentrations, with less energy needed for ignition, and burns/detonates faster. Special H₂ safety protocols and hydrogen-specific detection/venting systems are mandatory.

Propose design principles for a safe hydrogen fuel cell car that addresses the explosive combustion risks of hydrogen storage.Hard
Show Answer

Storage: Type IV carbon fibre reinforced tank rated for 700 bar; designed to vent upward (H₂ is lighter than air — dissipates safely unlike petrol vapour which pools). Detection: continuous H₂ sensors in car cabin with automatic cutoff valve on tank. Crash protection: tank in central protected zone (Toyota Mirai design). Venting: pressure relief devices vent H₂ away from hot engine parts. Standards: UN GTR13 governs H₂ vehicle safety. Real-world data: Toyota Mirai has driven millions of km with zero hydrogen-related fires — the safety engineering is proven.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Name the three zones of a candle flame from innermost to outermost.Easy
Show Answer

Innermost dark zone (unburnt wax vapour), middle luminous yellow zone (incomplete combustion — hot glowing carbon/soot particles), outer blue zone (complete combustion — hottest part).

Which zone of a flame is the hottest?Easy
Show Answer

The outer blue zone is the hottest because this is where complete combustion occurs — maximum fuel is oxidised, releasing maximum energy. Temperature can exceed 1,400°C here.

What causes the yellow colour in the middle zone of a candle flame?Easy
Show Answer

The yellow luminous colour is caused by glowing, incandescent carbon particles (soot) produced by incomplete combustion in the middle zone where oxygen supply is limited.

What is the innermost (dark) zone of a flame made of?Easy
Show Answer

The innermost dark zone contains unburnt fuel vapour (in a candle, this is unburnt wax vapour). The temperature here is lowest; no combustion occurs — it is just hot fuel gas rising.

Why do Bunsen burners used in labs have a blue flame?Easy
Show Answer

When the air hole of a Bunsen burner is fully open, excess oxygen is available for complete combustion of gas (methane) — producing a hot blue non-luminous flame. When the hole is closed, incomplete combustion causes a yellow luminous flame (like a candle).

Why is a yellow flame less efficient for heating than a blue flame?Easy
Show Answer

Yellow flame: incomplete combustion produces soot and CO (energy not fully released). Blue flame: complete combustion, maximum energy is extracted from the fuel. Blue flame is therefore hotter and more fuel-efficient.

Name one practical application where knowledge of flame zones is important.Easy
Show Answer

Goldsmithing/jewellery making: use the outer blue zone of a blowtorch flame (hottest) for melting metals. Laboratory work: Bunsen burner blue flame for more efficient and controlled heating of samples.

What two products does the middle zone of a flame produce in incomplete combustion?Easy
Show Answer

Incomplete combustion in the middle zone produces carbon monoxide (CO) — a poisonous gas — and carbon particles (soot/C), which glow yellow-orange when incandescent.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain the experimental evidence that can prove the three zones of a candle flame have different temperatures.Medium
Show Answer

Hold a copper wire horizontally through each zone: inner zone — wire barely heated. Middle zone — wire glows and may turn red-hot. Outer zone — wire glows most brightly (highest temperature). Alternatively, place a white card briefly across the flame — soot marks show the unburnt inner zone (no soot), middle (soot ring), outer (clean).

Describe the classic experiment to show that the innermost zone contains unburnt fuel vapour.Medium
Show Answer

Insert a glass tube into the inner dark zone of a candle. Wax vapour travels through the tube. Hold a lit match at the other end of the tube — the wax vapour ignites, producing a small flame away from the candle. This proves the dark zone contains combustible but unburnt vapour.

Why does a candle flame have a teardrop shape?Medium
Show Answer

Hot combustion gases expand, become less dense, and rise by convection. Cooler air rushes in from below. This convection current draws the flame upward, elongating it into a teardrop shape. In microgravity (space), where convection is absent, flames are spherical — confirming that the teardrop is caused by gravity-driven convection.

Explain why a goldsmith uses the outer zone of a flame to melt gold but would NOT use the yellow middle zone.Medium
Show Answer

The outer blue zone is hottest (complete combustion, ~1,400°C) — sufficient to melt gold (1,064°C). The yellow middle zone contains soot particles that would deposit carbon on the gold, discolouring and contaminating the jewellery. The outer zone is both hotter and cleaner.

Compare the chemical reactions occurring in the inner, middle, and outer zones of a candle flame.Medium
Show Answer

Inner zone: no combustion — just thermal decomposition/vaporisation of wax. CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ → wax vapour. Middle zone: incomplete combustion — limited O₂. CₓHᵧ + limited O₂ → CO + C(soot) + H₂O. Outer zone: complete combustion — excess O₂. CₓHᵧ + excess O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + maximum heat.

How does the flame structure of a Bunsen burner differ from a candle and why?Medium
Show Answer

Bunsen with air hole open: premixed flame — gas and air mix before burning, producing uniform blue cone with inner reducing zone and outer oxidising zone. Candle: diffusion flame — fuel vapour and oxygen diffuse together at the reaction zone, creating separate zones. Bunsen can be controlled to produce pure reducing (inner) or oxidising (outer) conditions, unlike a candle.

Why does a candle flame waver/flicker in a breeze but tends to go out in a strong wind?Medium
Show Answer

Light breeze: moves flame sideways but provides more oxygen — flame flickers but burns. Strong wind: cools the flame below ignition temperature and disrupts the fuel vapour supply to the combustion zone — these effects overcome the extra oxygen benefit, extinguishing the flame. This is the principle of blowing out a candle.

Explain how incomplete combustion produces carcinogens and why this is a concern with biomass cooking fires.Medium
Show Answer

Incomplete combustion of organic matter produces polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) — known carcinogens — along with benzene, formaldehyde, and CO. Indoor biomass fires with poor ventilation expose cooks (primarily women) chronically to these compounds, causing lung cancer, COPD, and cardiovascular disease at rates comparable to heavy smoking.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Analyse what happens to a flame's structure in microgravity and what this reveals about the role of gravity in combustion on Earth.Hard
Show Answer

In microgravity (ISS experiments): flames are spherical (not teardrop), dimmer, cooler, and burn more slowly. No convection removes combustion products or supplies fresh oxygen — diffusion alone supplies O₂. The outer blue complete combustion zone disappears because soot reaches the outer surface and the flame becomes soot-free (cooler, less luminous). On Earth, gravity-driven convection is fundamental to the structure, efficiency, and temperature distribution of flames — without it, combustion is dramatically different.

Critically evaluate the practice of using the colour of a flame as a diagnostic tool in chemical analysis (flame tests).Hard
Show Answer

Flame test principle: metal ions in outer (hot) zone of Bunsen flame absorb energy and emit specific wavelengths as electrons return to ground state. Reliable for: identifying Na (yellow), K (lilac), Ca (brick-red), Cu (blue-green), Li (crimson). Limitations: mixtures are difficult (Na yellow overwhelms others); some elements emit similar colours (Ba and Ca both in red-green region); requires pure samples; subjective colour interpretation. Modern alternative: atomic absorption/emission spectroscopy is objective and quantitative — but flame tests remain fast, cheap, and educational.

Evaluate how understanding the three zones of flame improved the design of gas cookers and industrial burners.Hard
Show Answer

Pre-mixed burners (like Bunsen) were designed based on understanding that premixing fuel + air before ignition creates a hotter, cleaner outer zone flame with no soot. Industrial burners use staged combustion: primary zone is fuel-rich (complete carbon combustion to CO) then secondary air injection ensures CO → CO₂ completion. Catalytic burners (used in camping stoves) use metal catalysts to achieve complete combustion at lower temperatures — building on incomplete combustion understanding. Each improvement directly reduces pollution and improves efficiency based on flame zone chemistry.

Design an investigation to determine which zone of a candle flame produces the most soot, using quantitative measurements.Hard
Show Answer

Method: carefully hold pre-weighed white filter paper squares in each zone (inner, middle, outer) for exactly 2 seconds using tongs. Weigh after each exposure. Soot deposits appear as black marks; mass difference = soot collected. Variables: same time, same paper mass, same candle (wait for stable flame). Hypothesis: middle zone produces most soot. Also test: observe colour of soot marks (middle = black carbon soot; inner = yellow wax residue; outer = very little soot). Repeat 5 times for reliability. Note: safety — brief exposure only, have water nearby.

Using atomic emission spectra theory, explain why different metal salts produce different coloured flames.Hard
Show Answer

Each metal has a unique electron configuration. In the flame, thermal energy excites outer electrons to higher energy levels. When electrons return to ground state, they emit photons of specific wavelengths corresponding to the exact energy gap between levels (ΔE = hf). Na: 589 nm (yellow). Cu: 510 nm (green-blue). These wavelengths are unique to each element's electron structure — an atomic fingerprint. This is the basis of spectroscopy, which revolutionised astronomy (identifying stellar compositions from afar) and analytical chemistry.

Analyse how soot from incomplete combustion forms and why it contributes to climate change differently from CO₂.Hard
Show Answer

Soot (black carbon, BC) forms when fuel carbon doesn't fully oxidise to CO₂ — carbon clusters aggregate into nano-particles. Climate impact: BC absorbs sunlight (not just traps IR like CO₂) — warms atmosphere directly. When deposited on snow/ice (Himalayas, Arctic), it reduces reflectivity (albedo), dramatically accelerating melting. Atmospheric lifetime: only 1–2 weeks (vs CO₂: 100+ years) — reducing BC emissions has immediate climate benefits. Reducing BC from cookstoves and diesel vehicles is one of the fastest near-term climate interventions available.

Critically evaluate whether eliminating the luminous zone of flames in industrial burners is always desirable from an engineering perspective.Hard
Show Answer

Eliminating luminous (yellow) zone: reduces soot, increases efficiency, cleaner emissions — desirable for most applications. However: in some industrial processes (glass furnaces, cement kilns), the radiant heat from luminous flames (from incandescent carbon particles) is essential for heat transfer to furnace walls/product. A non-luminous blue flame would provide convective but not radiative heat — inadequate for these applications. Engineers must sometimes deliberately maintain partial incomplete combustion zones to achieve required radiant heat flux — demonstrating that "more complete = better" is not universally true in industrial combustion engineering.

Propose how real-time monitoring of flame colour and shape could be used in smart home safety systems to detect dangerous incomplete combustion.Hard
Show Answer

Smart gas stove with camera + computer vision: continuous image analysis of Bunsen-type flame. Yellow/orange flame indicates incomplete combustion (CO production) — system automatically increases air supply or sounds alarm. CO sensor confirms detection independently. Machine learning trained on normal vs. abnormal flame patterns. Integration with smart home: auto-ventilates kitchen fan, alerts user via app, cuts gas supply if CO reaches dangerous levels. This combines flame structure chemistry with IoT safety technology, potentially preventing ~4,000 CO-related deaths annually in India alone.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Define calorific value of a fuel.Easy
Show Answer

Calorific value is the amount of heat energy released when one kilogram of a fuel is completely burned. It is measured in kJ/kg or kJ/g.

Which fuel has the highest calorific value — LPG, petrol, coal, or wood?Easy
Show Answer

LPG has the highest calorific value (~55,000 kJ/kg), followed by petrol (~45,000), coal (~25,000–33,000), and wood (~17,000 kJ/kg).

What unit is calorific value measured in?Easy
Show Answer

Calorific value is measured in kilojoules per kilogram (kJ/kg) or megajoules per kilogram (MJ/kg). Sometimes kJ/g or kcal/g is also used.

Why does wood have a lower calorific value than LPG?Easy
Show Answer

Wood contains water, cellulose with oxygen already incorporated (less chemical energy to release), and ash — less energy per kg than LPG which is a pure, dense hydrocarbon with maximum C-H bond energy per kilogram.

What is the approximate calorific value of hydrogen gas?Easy
Show Answer

Hydrogen has the highest calorific value of any fuel: approximately 120,000 kJ/kg (120 MJ/kg) — about 3x more energy per kilogram than petrol.

Using thermodynamic principles, explain why the theoretical maximum efficiency of any heat engine is limited, regardless of fuel calorific value.Hard
Show Answer

Carnot efficiency: η = 1 - T_cold/T_hot (temperatures in Kelvin). For a car engine (T_hot ~873 K, T_cold ~373 K): max η = 1 - 373/873 = 57%. Actual efficiency ≈ 25–35% due to friction, heat losses. Increasing fuel calorific value increases available energy but cannot exceed the Carnot limit. Using the concept of calorific value, explain why dietary fat contains more than double the Calories of the same mass of carbohydrate.Hard
Show Answer

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What is the fire triangle?Easy
Show Answer

The fire triangle shows the three elements required for fire: fuel, heat (above ignition temperature), and oxygen. Remove any one side and fire cannot start or will be extinguished.

How does water extinguish most fires?Easy
Show Answer

Water absorbs large amounts of heat (high specific heat capacity and latent heat of evaporation), cooling the burning material below its ignition temperature — removing the "heat" side of the fire triangle.

How does a CO₂ fire extinguisher work?Easy
Show Answer

CO₂ (carbon dioxide) is heavier than air and displaces oxygen around the fire. Removing oxygen removes the O₂ side of the fire triangle, smothering the flame.

Why should water NEVER be used to extinguish an electrical fire?Easy
Show Answer

Water conducts electricity. Spraying water on live electrical equipment creates a path for electric current to flow through the water to the person holding the extinguisher — causing electrocution.

Why should water NOT be used on oil/grease fires?Easy
Show Answer

Water immediately vaporises on contact with hot oil, creating a rapid expansion of steam that splatters burning oil droplets in all directions — dramatically spreading the fire ("fireball effect").

Name the fire extinguisher suitable for electrical fires.Easy
Show Answer

CO₂ (carbon dioxide) fire extinguishers are safe for electrical fires because CO₂ is non-conductive and leaves no residue on equipment.

How does sand extinguish a small fire?Easy
Show Answer

Sand smothers the fire by covering the fuel, preventing oxygen from reaching it — removing the oxygen side of the fire triangle.

What does the PASS technique stand for in fire extinguisher use?Easy
Show Answer

Pull (pin), Aim (at base of fire), Squeeze (handle), Sweep (side to side). This is the standard technique for safe and effective fire extinguisher use.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain why a fire blanket is more appropriate than water for extinguishing a small chip pan (oil) fire.Medium
Show Answer

A fire blanket smothers the fire by cutting off oxygen supply — no contact with the burning oil. Water on hot oil causes steam explosion and fire spread. Fire blanket is safe to apply from the front of the pan by sliding it over the pan (do not throw — avoids spreading oil).

Describe the chain reaction mechanism of combustion and explain how halon fire suppressants interrupt it.Medium
Show Answer

Combustion involves free radical chain reactions (H•, OH• radicals propagate the flame). Halon gases (bromine-containing compounds) release bromine radicals that scavenge these chain-carrying radicals, breaking the chain reaction. This is the 4th element of the "fire tetrahedron" (fuel, heat, O₂, chain reaction). Modern replacements: clean agent gases (FM-200, Novec) work by similar mechanisms.

Explain why forest fires spread faster uphill than downhill.Medium
Show Answer

Heat rises (convection) — preheats and dries the unburnt vegetation ahead of the fire uphill, lowering its ignition temperature. Burning embers travel uphill in hot air. Wind pushed by the fire tends to blow uphill. Combined: uphill fires can spread at 10-20× the speed of downhill fires — critical for firefighter safety decisions.

Design a school fire drill procedure using knowledge of the fire triangle and fire safety principles.Medium
Show Answer

Close (do not lock) all doors behind (slows fire spread by reducing O₂). Assemble at designated muster point upwind of building. Report to principal. Do not re-enter until all-clear given. Science link: closing doors removes oxygen supply to fire (fire triangle), buying time for evacuation.

A kitchen fire is caused by a gas leak igniting. List the correct sequence of emergency actions.Medium
Show Answer

1. Do NOT operate any electrical switches (sparks could trigger explosion). 2. Turn off gas supply at the main valve. 3. Open all windows and doors (ventilate). 4. Evacuate all people from the building. 5. Call fire services (from outside). 6. Use CO₂ extinguisher only if fire is small and you can safely do so while standing at the exit. Never re-enter to retrieve belongings.

Explain why using a foam extinguisher is appropriate for petrol fires but not electrical fires.Medium
Show Answer

Foam creates a blanket over burning petrol, excluding oxygen and cooling the fuel surface (suitable for Class B fires). However, foam contains water — it conducts electricity. Using foam on live electrical equipment risks electrocution to the user. CO₂ or dry powder must be used for electrical fires.

How does building fire compartmentalisation use science principles to slow fire spread?Medium
Show Answer

Fire-resistant walls, floors, and sealed doors divide buildings into compartments. Fire needs continuous oxygen supply and fuel to spread. Compartment walls (fire rating: 30–120 minutes) slow spread by reducing O₂ access. Automatic fire doors self-close on heat activation. This gives occupants time to evacuate and fire services time to respond before structural collapse.

Why do wildfires intensify dramatically under drought conditions?Medium
Show Answer

Drought: moisture content of vegetation drops from ~150% to below 30% (fine fuels). Lower moisture → less heat needed to ignite (lower effective ignition temperature). More fuel is available (dead dry vegetation). Low humidity dries out any green vegetation ahead of the fire. Wind speeds often higher in drought. All three fire triangle elements are maximised simultaneously — explaining the catastrophic intensity of drought-fuelled wildfires (Australia 2019, Canada 2023).

Explain the chemical mechanism by which nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) form during combustion despite nitrogen not being a fuel.Medium
Show Answer

At combustion temperatures (above 1,200°C), the N₂ in air dissociates into N atoms, which react with O₂: N₂ + O₂ → 2NO (thermal NOₓ). This is unavoidable in any high-temperature air combustion. NO further oxidises to NO₂ in air. Engine manufacturers use exhaust gas recirculation (EGR) and three-way catalysts to reduce NOₓ post-combustion.

It can reduce CO, HC, and NOₓ emissions by ~90–95% but only works effectively when hot (above 300°C) and with correct air-fuel ratio.

Current Hyderabad public bus fleet: ~3,300 diesel buses. Each diesel bus emits ~15g PM/km; 1,000 electric buses emit zero tailpipe emissions. If each bus travels 200 km/day: diesel = 1,000 × 200 × 15g = 3 tonnes PM/day eliminated. Hyderabad's total transport PM2.5 contribution: ~800 tonnes/year. 1,000 electric buses would reduce transport PM2.5 by ~1,000 tonnes/year (~125% more than needed, considering fleet replacement). Grid emissions from charging: ~3 tonnes CO₂/bus/year (Indian grid) vs ~80 tonnes CO₂/bus/year for diesel. Net: 1,000 electric buses would meaningfully reduce both PM2.5 and CO₂ — a viable, impactful investment with 15–20 year operating cost savings paying for the price premium.
