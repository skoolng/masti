SET A Recall – States of Matter

Name the three common states of matter.Easy
Show Answer

The three common states of matter are solid, liquid, and gas. A fourth state — plasma — exists at extremely high temperatures but is not commonly encountered in everyday life.

What is the shape and volume of a solid?Easy
Show Answer

A solid has a fixed (definite) shape and a fixed volume. Its particles are tightly packed and cannot move freely, only vibrating in place.

What is the shape and volume of a liquid?Easy
Show Answer

A liquid has no fixed shape (it takes the shape of its container) but has a fixed volume. Particles can flow past each other but remain in contact.

What is the shape and volume of a gas?Easy
Show Answer

A gas has no fixed shape and no fixed volume — it expands to fill any container. Gas particles move rapidly and are far apart from each other.

Can a solid be compressed easily? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

No. In a solid, particles are already tightly packed together. There is very little space between them, so the solid cannot be compressed further.

Give two examples each of solids, liquids, and gases at room temperature.Easy
Show Answer

Solids: iron, wood. Liquids: water, mercury. Gases: oxygen, carbon dioxide. These examples are all at standard room temperature (approximately 25°C).

What happens to the volume of a gas when it is moved to a larger container?Easy
Show Answer

A gas expands to fill the entire volume of its new container. Gas particles move freely in all directions and spread out until they are uniformly distributed throughout the available space.

Which state of matter has the highest energy? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

Gas. Gas particles have the most kinetic energy — they move fastest and most freely. This is why they can spread out and fill any container, unlike solids and liquids.

SET B Apply – States of Matter

Explain why liquid water takes the shape of its container but a block of ice does not.Medium
Show Answer

Ice (solid) has a rigid lattice of particles that hold their fixed positions, maintaining a definite shape regardless of container. Liquid water's particles can slide past each other freely, allowing the liquid to flow and conform to the shape of any container while maintaining a fixed total volume.

A student blows air into a balloon. The balloon expands. Which property of gases explains this?Medium
Show Answer

Gases have no fixed volume and expand to fill available space. The air molecules inside the balloon move in all directions, exerting pressure on the balloon walls. As more air is added, more molecules exert greater outward pressure, expanding the balloon until the rubber's elastic tension balances the gas pressure.

Steam (water vapour) burns more severely than boiling water at the same temperature (100°C). Explain why, using knowledge of states of matter.Medium
Show Answer

Steam has more energy than liquid water at 100°C because it carries the latent heat of vaporisation — the energy used to change state from liquid to gas. When steam contacts skin, it releases this extra energy as it condenses back to liquid water, causing more severe burns than liquid water at the same temperature.

Why is it possible to pour sand into any shape (like a liquid) even though sand is made of solid particles?Medium
Show Answer

Sand is made of individual solid grains that can slide past each other due to gravity. This gives it some properties similar to a liquid (pourable), but each grain is still a solid with fixed shape and volume. Sand is not a true liquid — it cannot flow upward or fill a sealed container.

Explain why gases can be compressed but liquids and solids cannot (or only very slightly).Medium
Show Answer

In gases, particles are far apart with large empty spaces between them. Compression pushes the particles closer together into these spaces. In liquids and solids, particles are already in contact with very little space between them, so very little further compression is possible without extremely high pressure.

A perfume bottle is opened in one corner of a room. Soon the scent is detected across the room. Which property of gases explains this?Medium
Show Answer

Gases expand and fill their container — the room. Perfume molecules (in gaseous form) move rapidly in random directions (random motion/diffusion), spreading throughout the room until they are uniformly distributed. This process is called diffusion and is evidence that gas particles are in constant rapid motion.

Why does water remain as a liquid at room temperature while oxygen is a gas?Medium
Show Answer

The state of a substance at room temperature depends on the forces between its particles and the energy of the particles. Water molecules have strong intermolecular forces (hydrogen bonds), keeping them as a liquid at room temperature. Oxygen molecules have very weak intermolecular forces, so their particles have enough energy to escape as a gas at room temperature.

A student claims that a gas has more mass than a solid because it fills more space. Evaluate this claim.Medium
Show Answer

The claim is false. Mass depends on the number and type of particles, not the volume they occupy. A gas expands to fill space because its particles move far apart, but the total mass of the gas remains the same. Equal masses of solid and gas contain the same number of atoms — the gas just takes up much more volume.

SET C Investigate – States of Matter (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an experiment to demonstrate that water can change state in both directions (solid→liquid→gas and reverse). Identify energy changes at each step.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Start with ice in a beaker on a hot plate; record temperature and observe changes. Solid→liquid (melting): temperature stays at 0°C while ice melts (latent heat of fusion absorbed). Liquid→gas (evaporation/boiling): temperature rises to 100°C then stays constant while boiling (latent heat of vaporisation absorbed). Reverse: collect steam in a cold beaker — condenses to liquid (energy released). Place in freezer — freezes to solid (energy released). Energy absorbed during heating; energy released during cooling — demonstrating reversibility and conservation of energy.

Critically evaluate the limitations of classifying all matter as solid, liquid, or gas. What substances are difficult to classify?Hard
Show Answer

Limitations: many substances do not fit neatly into three categories. Examples: Non-Newtonian fluids (cornstarch + water — solid-like under impact, liquid-like when still), gels (have fixed shape like solids but high water content), foams (gas trapped in liquid), liquid crystals (used in LCD screens — between solid and liquid order). Plasma is a fourth state. The three-state model is a useful simplification but does not capture the full range of matter's behaviour.

Analyse what happens at the particle level when water is heated from 20°C to 120°C (steam). Describe each phase change and the energy involved.Hard
Show Answer

20°C→100°C (liquid): particles gain kinetic energy, vibrate and move faster, temperature rises. At 100°C (boiling): temperature stops rising; energy (latent heat of vaporisation = 2260 J/g) is used to overcome intermolecular forces, converting liquid to gas — not to raise temperature. 100°C→120°C (gas): steam particles gain kinetic energy again, temperature rises. Total energy = energy to heat liquid (m×c×ΔT) + latent heat + energy to heat steam.

A student observes that a glass of ice water stays at 0°C until all the ice melts, even though heat is being applied. Explain this observation and its practical applications.Hard
Show Answer

The heat energy is being used as latent heat of fusion — energy needed to break the lattice bonds in ice and change the state from solid to liquid, not to raise the temperature. The temperature remains at 0°C (the melting point) until all ice is melted. Practical application: this is how ice is used to keep food cold in coolers — the large amount of latent heat absorbed keeps the temperature stable at 0°C for a long time.

Compare the compressibility of air in a bicycle pump with the near-incompressibility of water in a hydraulic braking system. Analyse why engineers choose each for their specific applications.Hard
Show Answer

Air (gas): large spaces between particles allow significant compression — perfect for bicycle pumps where storing energy by compression and releasing it is the goal. Water (liquid): virtually incompressible because particles are already in contact — perfect for hydraulic brakes where pressing the pedal must immediately and reliably transmit force through the fluid without losing any motion to compression. Compressible fluids would make brake response delayed and inconsistent — dangerous in safety-critical systems.

Investigate (theoretically) how the behaviour of matter in the states of solid, liquid, and gas changes on the Moon (1/6 of Earth's gravity). Predict which properties change and which remain the same.Hard
Show Answer

Properties that do NOT change with gravity: melting/boiling points (determined by intermolecular forces and temperature, not gravity), particle speed, density of a given substance, intermolecular forces. Properties that DO change: the behaviour of liquids — without strong gravity, liquids form spherical droplets due to surface tension rather than pooling flat. Gases would still expand to fill containers. The Moon has no atmosphere because its gravity is too weak to retain gas molecules — light molecules escape into space. On Earth, gravity helps retain the atmosphere.

Evaluate the scientific and practical significance of the discovery of plasma as the fourth state of matter. Where is plasma found and why is it important?Hard
Show Answer

Plasma is an ionised gas where electrons are separated from nuclei — occurring at very high energies. Found in: the Sun and stars (fusion reactions), lightning, neon signs, and plasma TVs. Scientific significance: plasma makes up 99% of visible matter in the universe. Practical importance: fusion reactors (e.g. ITER) aim to use plasma for clean, nearly unlimited energy. Understanding plasma behaviour is essential for space travel (spacecraft re-entry plasma sheath) and materials processing (plasma etching in semiconductor manufacture).

A student designs an investigation to find the melting point of wax by slowly heating it and recording temperature vs. time. Predict the shape of the temperature–time graph, identify what happens at the plateau, and explain what information this gives about the material.Hard
Show Answer

Graph shape: rising straight line (solid wax heating), then horizontal plateau (melting — temperature constant despite continued heating), then rising line again (liquid wax heating). The plateau represents the melting point — where supplied heat goes entirely into latent heat of fusion (breaking crystal lattice bonds), not into raising temperature. This gives: (1) the exact melting point of the wax; (2) the purity (impurities lower and broaden the melting point range); (3) the latent heat can be calculated from the plateau duration and power input.

SET A Recall – Particle Model

Describe the arrangement of particles in a solid.Easy
Show Answer

In a solid, particles are tightly packed in a regular, ordered arrangement (lattice). They vibrate in fixed positions but cannot move freely — this gives solids their definite shape and volume.

Describe the arrangement of particles in a liquid.Easy
Show Answer

In a liquid, particles are close together but in a random, irregular arrangement. They can slide past each other, allowing liquids to flow and take the shape of their container.

Describe the arrangement of particles in a gas.Easy
Show Answer

In a gas, particles are far apart, moving rapidly in random directions. They are much more spread out than in a solid or liquid, which is why gases can expand to fill any container.

What type of particle motion occurs in a solid?Easy
Show Answer

Particles in a solid vibrate about fixed positions. They have low kinetic energy compared to liquids and gases, and cannot change their average position.

How does increasing temperature affect particle motion?Easy
Show Answer

Increasing temperature gives particles more kinetic energy, causing them to move faster. In solids, vibrations become more vigorous; in liquids, particles move faster; in gases, particles move faster and hit container walls with greater force.

Why can gases be compressed while solids cannot?Easy
Show Answer

Gas particles are far apart with large empty spaces between them — compression moves particles closer into these spaces. Solid particles are already tightly packed with virtually no space between them, leaving no room for compression.

What is diffusion and which states of matter show it?Easy
Show Answer

Diffusion is the spreading of particles from a region of high concentration to low concentration due to random particle motion. It occurs in both gases and liquids. Gases diffuse faster than liquids because their particles move more rapidly.

Name the scientist whose observation of pollen grains provided the first indirect evidence for the particle model.Easy
Show Answer

Robert Brown (1827) observed random motion of pollen grains in water (Brownian motion). This provided the first observational evidence that water consists of tiny, invisibly fast-moving particles.

SET B Apply – Particle Model

Use the particle model to explain why a solid block of ice melts when placed in a warm room.Medium
Show Answer

The warm air in the room transfers thermal energy to the ice particles, increasing their kinetic energy (vibrations). When the energy is sufficient to overcome the forces holding particles in the lattice, the ice melts — particles gain enough energy to slide past each other as a liquid.

Explain why a gas exerts pressure on the walls of its container, using the particle model.Medium
Show Answer

Gas particles move rapidly in random directions. When they collide with container walls, they exert a tiny force on the wall. The combined effect of billions of such collisions per second creates the measurable gas pressure. Increasing temperature increases particle speed and collision frequency, raising pressure.

A student drops a small amount of food colouring into still water. Slowly, the colour spreads throughout the water. Explain this using the particle model.Medium
Show Answer

Diffusion explains this. The coloured dye particles and water particles are both in random motion, constantly moving in all directions. Dye particles gradually spread from where they are concentrated (the drop) to areas of lower concentration throughout the water — driven by random particle movement, not a directed force.

Why does a gas fill its container uniformly rather than sinking to the bottom (unlike a liquid)?Medium
Show Answer

Gas particles have much higher kinetic energy than liquid particles and move rapidly in all directions. Gravity affects them, but their kinetic energy is so large that they constantly bounce off each other and the walls, spreading uniformly. Only at very low temperatures do gas molecules settle — at room temperature, thermal energy far exceeds gravitational potential energy differences.

Use the particle model to explain why smell travels faster on a hot day than a cold day.Medium
Show Answer

On a hot day, odour molecules (gas) have more kinetic energy and move faster. Diffusion rate is proportional to particle speed. Faster-moving particles spread through the air more quickly, reaching your nose in less time. On a cold day, lower kinetic energy means slower movement and slower diffusion.

Explain why cooking oil and water do not mix, using knowledge of particle interactions.Medium
Show Answer

Water molecules are polar (have slightly positive and negative ends) and strongly attract other water molecules (hydrogen bonding). Oil molecules are non-polar and attract other oil molecules through weaker forces. When mixed, water molecules preferentially attract each other and exclude the oil, which groups together — this is why oil and water separate into distinct layers.

A bicycle tyre feels hard even though it is filled with gas. Explain using the particle model.Medium
Show Answer

Gas molecules inside the tyre are in constant rapid motion. They collide with the inner wall of the tyre continuously, exerting outward pressure. The pressure from many billions of collisions per second creates the firm feeling of the inflated tyre. The tyre wall provides inward elastic force balancing the outward gas pressure.

Use the particle model to explain the difference in density between a solid and a gas of the same material.Medium
Show Answer

Density = mass ÷ volume. In a solid, particles are tightly packed — the same number of particles occupy a very small volume, giving high density. In a gas, the same particles are spread far apart — they occupy a much larger volume, giving low density. The mass is the same; the difference is entirely in the volume the particles occupy.

SET C Investigate – Particle Model (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an experiment to demonstrate diffusion of two gases (e.g., ammonia and hydrochloric acid) meeting in a glass tube. Predict where the product forms and explain using the particle model.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Soak one cotton wool plug in ammonia solution and another in hydrochloric acid solution. Simultaneously plug each end of a long glass tube. Observe where white smoke (ammonium chloride) forms. Prediction: white smoke forms closer to the HCl end because ammonia (NH₃, lighter, smaller molecule) diffuses faster than HCl (heavier). Particle model explanation: lighter particles have higher average speed at the same temperature (kinetic theory: KE = ½mv², so smaller m → higher v for same KE). The ring of white smoke marks where equal numbers of both molecules meet.

Critically evaluate the particle model of matter as a scientific model. What does it explain well and what are its limitations?Hard
Show Answer

Explains well: states of matter, diffusion, gas pressure, Brownian motion, thermal expansion, and state changes. Limitations: treats all particles as identical hard spheres — ignores different molecular shapes (e.g., H₂O's bent shape that gives it unusual properties), intermolecular force complexity, quantum effects at atomic scale, and cannot account for non-Newtonian fluids or liquid crystal behaviour. The model is a useful simplification — powerful for Grade 6-10 chemistry but must be refined at higher levels (molecular orbital theory, quantum mechanics).

A student investigates diffusion rate of food colouring in hot vs. cold water. Describe a fair test, what to measure, and expected results with particle-level explanation.Hard
Show Answer

Fair test: same volume of water (100 mL), same amount of food colouring (1 drop), same container size. Variables: independent = water temperature (10°C, 20°C, 40°C, 60°C); dependent = time for colour to spread uniformly (or radius of colour spread after a fixed time). Expected: higher temperature → faster diffusion. Particle explanation: higher temperature → greater kinetic energy → faster particle motion → faster spreading of dye particles from high to low concentration regions.

Analyse how the particle model explains why a balloon inflated outdoors in winter shrinks when brought indoors in summer (and vice versa). What variables are involved?Hard
Show Answer

Actually, a balloon brought from cold outdoor (winter) air to warm indoor air would expand, not shrink — because warmer indoor air warms the gas inside, increasing particle kinetic energy and thus pressure, expanding the balloon. If the reverse (hot outdoor summer to cold air-conditioned indoors): balloon shrinks because gas particles slow down, reducing pressure and volume. Variables: temperature (directly affects kinetic energy), volume (balloon is flexible so pressure stays roughly constant — Charles's Law: V ∝ T at constant pressure), and external air pressure.

Propose an investigation to determine the size of an oil molecule using the oil drop experiment. Describe the method, assumptions, and calculation.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Place one drop of oleic acid in ethanol (known volume, e.g. 1 mm³ of diluted solution). Drop onto water surface dusted with lycopodium powder. Measure diameter of oil patch (d). Calculation: Volume of oil = known amount from dilution. Assume the oil spreads to a monolayer (one molecule thick). Thickness = volume ÷ area = volume ÷ (π(d/2)²). Assumption: oil forms a perfectly flat circular monolayer one molecule thick. Result: approximately 10⁻¹⁰ m (1 nm) — the order of magnitude of a molecule. Limitations: oil patch not perfectly circular, powder may interfere.

Evaluate the evidence that led scientists to develop the particle model of matter. List at least three key experimental observations and explain what each revealed.Hard
Show Answer

1) Brownian motion (Robert Brown, 1827): random movement of visible particles in fluid showed invisible particles in constant random motion — evidence for particle nature of fluids. 2) Diffusion: spreading of gases/liquids without stirring showed particles self-move from high to low concentration. 3) Compression of gases: gases can be compressed significantly, showing large empty spaces between gas particles. 4) Thermal expansion: solids, liquids, and gases expand when heated, showing particles move further apart as energy increases. 5) Definite melting/boiling points: show particles require a specific energy to change the nature of their interactions. Together, these observations converged on the particle model as the most consistent explanation.

Compare and contrast the kinetic theory explanations for gas pressure and osmotic pressure in liquids. What is fundamentally different about these two pressures despite both arising from particle motion?Hard
Show Answer

Gas pressure: caused by kinetic impacts of gas molecules on container walls — direct momentum transfer. Depends on temperature and number of molecules per unit volume. Osmotic pressure: arises from diffusion of solvent (water) molecules through a semipermeable membrane from low to high solute concentration, driven by concentration gradients. Water molecules pass through the membrane more easily than solute molecules. While both ultimately arise from random particle motion, gas pressure is about collision force, while osmotic pressure is about net movement direction due to concentration difference — a thermodynamic driving force toward equilibrium.

Using the particle model, analyse why solid carbon dioxide (dry ice) sublimates directly to gas without becoming liquid at atmospheric pressure, while water melts to liquid before boiling.Hard
Show Answer

Sublimation in dry ice occurs because at atmospheric pressure, the intermolecular forces between CO₂ molecules are weak — not strong enough to maintain the liquid state. The energy needed to go directly from solid to gas (sublimation energy) is less than the energy needed to maintain the liquid phase at this pressure. Water has much stronger intermolecular forces (hydrogen bonds), which are strong enough to hold molecules in the liquid state at atmospheric pressure over a 100°C range. The difference is entirely in the relative strengths of intermolecular forces and how they interact with thermal energy at atmospheric pressure.

SET A Recall – Physical Properties

What is hardness as a property of a material?Easy
Show Answer

Hardness is a material's resistance to being scratched or dented. The Mohs scale rates hardness from 1 (talc, softest) to 10 (diamond, hardest). A harder material scratches a softer one.

What is lustre?Easy
Show Answer

Lustre describes how well a material reflects light — how shiny it appears. Metals like gold, silver, and copper have high lustre. Non-metals are generally dull (no lustre).

What does solubility mean?Easy
Show Answer

Solubility is the ability of a substance (solute) to dissolve in a solvent (usually water). Sugar and salt are highly soluble in water; sand and oil are not soluble in water.

What is thermal conductivity?Easy
Show Answer

Thermal conductivity is a material's ability to conduct (transfer) heat. Metals like copper and aluminium are good thermal conductors. Non-metals like wood and plastic are poor conductors (insulators).

What is electrical conductivity?Easy
Show Answer

Electrical conductivity is a material's ability to allow electric current to flow through it. Metals are good electrical conductors. Non-metals (rubber, plastic, wood) are poor conductors (insulators).

What is transparency in materials?Easy
Show Answer

Transparency (or optical transmittance) is how well a material allows light to pass through it. Glass and clear plastic are transparent. Wood and metal are opaque. Frosted glass is translucent (partially transparent).

What is flexibility/elasticity in a material?Easy
Show Answer

Flexibility is the ability to bend without breaking. Elasticity is the ability to return to original shape after being stretched or compressed. Rubber is both flexible and elastic; glass is rigid and brittle.

Name one material with high hardness and one with low hardness.Easy
Show Answer

Diamond has the highest hardness (10 on Mohs scale). Talc has the lowest hardness (1 on Mohs scale) — it can be scratched with a fingernail.

SET B Apply – Physical Properties

A student needs to choose a material for a frying pan handle. Which property is most important and why?Medium
Show Answer

Low thermal conductivity (insulating property) is most important. The handle must not transfer heat from the hot pan to the hand. Wood or plastic handles are used because they are poor heat conductors — metals would be dangerous as they transfer heat rapidly to the user's hand.

Explain why glass is used for windows and not wood or metal.Medium
Show Answer

Glass is transparent, allowing light to pass through and illuminate the room while providing a physical barrier against wind and rain. Wood and metal are opaque — they would block light entirely. Glass also has adequate strength and hardness for this application.

A scientist needs to test the hardness of two unknown minerals. Describe how they could use the scratch test to compare them.Medium
Show Answer

Attempt to scratch Mineral A's surface with Mineral B, then vice versa. Whichever mineral scratches the other is harder. If Mineral A scratches B but B does not scratch A, then A is harder. If neither scratches the other, they have equal hardness. This is the basis of the Mohs hardness scale.

Copper is used for electrical wiring. Name the two key properties that make it suitable and explain why no other property in this list matters as much.Medium
Show Answer

High electrical conductivity (allows current to flow with minimal resistance) and flexibility (can be bent and formed into wires without breaking). Properties like lustre, hardness, and transparency are irrelevant because the wire is insulated and hidden. Conductivity and formability directly determine function.

Explain why salt dissolves in water but sand does not, using knowledge of particle interactions.Medium
Show Answer

Salt (NaCl) is an ionic compound. Water is a polar molecule. The positive water molecules attract chloride ions and the negative parts attract sodium ions, pulling the lattice apart — this is dissolution. Sand (SiO₂) has very strong covalent bonds in a giant lattice structure that water molecules cannot break apart. The interaction between water and sand is too weak to separate the sand particles.

A material is described as 'translucent'. How does this differ from transparent and opaque? Give one example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Transparent: light passes through clearly with no scattering (e.g. clear glass — objects behind are clearly visible). Translucent: light passes through but is scattered, making objects behind blurry (e.g. frosted glass or baking paper). Opaque: no light passes through at all (e.g. wood or metal). The difference lies in how much light scattering occurs within the material.

Why is rubber used for tyres rather than glass, even though glass might be harder?Medium
Show Answer

Rubber has high flexibility and elasticity — it deforms under impact and springs back, absorbing shocks and maintaining grip on road surfaces. Glass is rigid and brittle — it would shatter on impact. For tyres, flexibility, friction (grip), and durability under repeated deformation are the key properties, not hardness.

A student tests four substances for solubility in water: sugar, sand, oil, and copper sulfate. Predict the results and explain each.Medium
Show Answer

Sugar: soluble — its molecules interact with polar water molecules, breaking apart and dispersing. Sand: insoluble — giant covalent structure with bonds too strong for water to break. Oil: insoluble — non-polar oil molecules are not attracted to polar water molecules. Copper sulfate: soluble — ionic compound, water molecules attract its ions and pull them from the crystal lattice.

SET C Investigate – Physical Properties (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an investigation to rank five unknown materials by hardness using only the materials themselves and a copper coin (hardness ~3 Mohs). State your method, variables, and how you would construct a hardness ranking.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Attempt to scratch each material with the copper coin; record whether it scratches. Then use harder materials to scratch softer ones in a round-robin comparison. Variables: independent = material being tested; dependent = scratch result (yes/no); controlled = same force applied, same area of scratch, same observer. Construct ranking by: if A scratches B, A is harder than B. Use transitivity (if A>B and B>C, then A>C). Coin at hardness 3 splits materials into >3 (coin cannot scratch) and <3 (coin scratches). Limitations: pressure variation between scratches, surface contaminants.

Critically evaluate whether electrical conductivity is a fixed property of a material or whether it can be changed. Use examples to support your answer.Hard
Show Answer

Conductivity can change with: temperature (metal conductivity decreases as temperature rises — more thermal vibrations scatter electrons; semiconductor conductivity increases with temperature); doping (adding impurities to silicon dramatically increases its conductivity — basis of all transistors); state of matter (mercury: liquid at room temp, conducts; many non-conductors become conductors when dissolved as ions in water — e.g. salt solution). Therefore electrical conductivity is not a fixed intrinsic property but depends on temperature, composition, and physical state. This is exploited in electronics (transistors, sensors) and industry (electrolytes in batteries).

An engineer needs a material for an aeroplane wing that must be: strong, lightweight, slightly flexible (to absorb turbulence), non-corrosive, and thermally stable. Analyse how modern composite materials (e.g. carbon fibre reinforced polymer) meet these requirements better than aluminium.Hard
Show Answer

Aluminium: strong, lightweight, conductive, slightly flexible, resists corrosion with oxide layer. Limitation: still heavier than ideal; fatigue cracking over time. Carbon fibre (CFRP): stronger than steel by weight, lighter than aluminium, very stiff, does not corrode, thermally stable to high temperatures. The polymer matrix provides slight flexibility to absorb shocks. CFRP has a higher strength-to-weight ratio than any metal, reduces fuel consumption, and does not suffer fatigue in the same way as metals. Limitation: more expensive to manufacture and harder to repair. Used in Boeing 787 and Airbus A350.

Propose an investigation to determine which of five liquids (water, ethanol, vegetable oil, salt solution, vinegar) has the highest and lowest surface tension. Describe your method, measurements, and expected ranking.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Use a dropper to place carefully sized drops on a hydrophobic surface. More surface tension → drop forms a more spherical shape with larger contact angle (height to width ratio). Measure drop height and width with ruler; calculate h/w ratio. Alternatively: count drops from a dropper until 1 mL is reached — more drops = smaller drops = higher surface tension. Expected ranking (highest to lowest surface tension): water > salt solution (ions increase surface tension) > vinegar > ethanol > vegetable oil. Controlled variables: same dropper tip, same volume dispensed, same surface, room temperature.

Analyse the relationship between a material's lustre and its electrical conductivity. Is there a pattern? Explain the reason using atomic structure.Hard
Show Answer

Yes, there is a strong pattern: materials with high lustre (metals — gold, silver, copper, aluminium) are also excellent electrical conductors. The reason lies in their atomic structure: metals have delocalised electrons (a 'sea' of free electrons). These free electrons: (1) reflect light efficiently → giving lustre; (2) carry electrical charge freely when a voltage is applied → giving conductivity. Non-metals lack delocalised electrons — their electrons are tightly bound in bonds, so they neither reflect light well (dull) nor conduct electricity well (insulators). The same electron structure causes both properties.

Design an experiment to investigate whether temperature affects the rate at which sugar dissolves in water. Include a hypothesis, method, variables, and safety considerations.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Higher temperature increases dissolution rate because particles have more kinetic energy, collide more frequently with solute, and break it apart faster. Method: Measure 50 mL water at temperatures 10°C, 20°C, 30°C, 40°C, 50°C. Add exactly 5 g sugar to each. Time how long until fully dissolved (no visible crystals), stirring at the same rate. Variables: independent = temperature; dependent = time to dissolve; controlled = volume of water, mass of sugar, same stirring rate and method. Safety: hot water safety, use tongs for hot containers, thermometer care. Expected: dissolution time decreases as temperature increases (inverse relationship).

A new synthetic material claims to have the following properties: hardness 8 Mohs, transparent, electrically conductive, flexible, and soluble in water. Critically evaluate whether this set of properties is scientifically plausible. Identify any contradictions.Hard
Show Answer

Several contradictions: (1) Hardness 8 + flexible: high-hardness materials are typically brittle (e.g. diamonds, corundum) — combining extreme hardness with flexibility is very unusual (though graphene has unique properties). (2) Transparent + electrically conductive: most transparent materials are non-conductors (glass, plastic). Conducting materials like metals are opaque. Indium tin oxide (ITO) is a rare exception (used in touchscreens) but is brittle, not flexible. (3) Soluble in water + hard: highly soluble materials (salts) are typically low-hardness ionic crystals. A Mohs 8 soluble material would be unprecedented. Verdict: while individual combinations exist in special materials, this combination of all five properties together is not currently plausible with known materials.

Evaluate the social and scientific impact of the discovery of superconductivity (zero electrical resistance below a critical temperature). What are the current limitations and future potential?Hard
Show Answer

Discovery: Heike Kamerlingh Onnes (1911) found mercury has zero resistance below 4.2 K (−269°C). Scientific impact: fundamentally changed understanding of electron behaviour in materials; led to BCS theory (Nobel Prize). Current applications: MRI scanner superconducting magnets, maglev trains (Japan's SCMaglev), particle accelerators (LHC at CERN). Limitations: require extreme cooling (liquid helium or nitrogen), making them expensive and bulky. Room-temperature superconductors remain elusive despite promising recent claims (not yet reproducibly confirmed). Future potential: lossless electricity transmission (power grids waste ~5–10% as heat in resistance), ultra-fast computers with quantum processors, and ultra-efficient motors — potentially transforming global energy use.

EPR is a policy where manufacturers are legally responsible for the end-of-life management of their products and packaging. Under EPR, companies must fund collection and recycling systems, design products for easy disassembly, and meet take-back requirements. This incentivises designing products that use less material, are easier to recycle, and last longer. For electronics (e-waste): manufacturers pay for collection and proper recycling of rare earth metals and hazardous components. For packaging: producers fund recycling infrastructure and are incentivised to reduce packaging weight and complexity.

How does the circular economy model differ from the linear 'take-make-dispose' model? Give one example from the textile industry.Medium
Show Answer

Linear economy: extract raw material → make product → dispose after use (landfill/incineration). Resources used once and discarded. Circular economy: design for reuse, repair, remade, and recycle — keeping materials in use as long as possible, extracting maximum value, with minimal waste. Textile example: H&M's garment collection scheme — used clothes are collected in stores, sorted, resold as second-hand, repurposed as industrial rags, or shredded into insulation fibre. The circular design extends material life and reduces demand for new cotton and synthetic fibre production.

Analyse why recycling rates for plastics are much lower than for metals like aluminium and steel. What properties of each material explain this difference?Medium
Show Answer

Metals: can be melted and recast with no loss of quality (aluminium recycled 95%+ energy savings; steel 74% savings). Recycled metal is identical to virgin metal — easy to sell. Plastic: there are 7+ different types (PET, HDPE, PVC, etc.) that cannot be mixed. Sorting is labour-intensive. Recycled plastic is often lower quality than virgin plastic (downcycling). Some plastics (PVC, polystyrene) release toxins when heated for recycling. Contamination (food residue) degrades quality. Result: only PET bottles and HDPE containers are widely recycled; most other plastics go to landfill or incineration despite collection. Solution: design all plastics for recyclability (mono-material, no mixed polymers, easy to clean).

Design an investigation to compare the biodegradation rate of four materials (cotton cloth, plastic bag, newspaper, jute bag) buried in soil. Describe method, measurements, timeline, and expected results.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Cut identical 5 cm × 5 cm squares of each material. Weigh each (initial mass). Bury at 10 cm depth in identical soil conditions (same pot, same soil, same watering). Exhume samples at 2-week intervals for 12 weeks. Rinse, dry, reweigh. Calculate mass loss (%) at each interval. Variables: independent = material type; dependent = % mass loss; controlled = soil type, burial depth, temperature, moisture level, sample size. Expected results: newspaper fastest (cellulose + chemical treatment breaks down in weeks); cotton next (natural cellulose fibres, months); jute (months to 1 year); plastic essentially unchanged at 12 weeks (centuries in real environment). Plot mass retained (%) vs. time for each material.

Critically evaluate the environmental trade-offs of three packaging options for delivering food: polystyrene, PLA bioplastic, and recycled cardboard. Consider production, use, and end-of-life for each.Hard
Show Answer

Polystyrene: excellent insulation, lightweight, cheap. Production: from petrochemicals (non-renewable). End-of-life: virtually non-biodegradable, difficult to recycle, ocean pollutant. PLA bioplastic: made from corn starch (renewable). Production: land use, water, pesticides, energy for fermentation and polymerisation. End-of-life: only biodegrades in industrial composters at 55–70°C, NOT in home compost or natural environments — often ends up in landfill as standard plastic. Recycled cardboard: renewable, widely recyclable, home-compostable. Production: if from virgin pulp, requires tree cutting; recycled cardboard uses much less energy and water. Limitation: poor moisture resistance. Verdict: recycled cardboard has the most genuinely sustainable lifecycle when properly recycled or composted, but insulation performance is lower than polystyrene. PLA's 'biodegradable' marketing can be misleading without composting infrastructure.

Analyse the life cycle of a smartphone in terms of materials sustainability. Identify the key environmental challenges at each stage.Hard
Show Answer

Raw material extraction: cobalt (Democratic Republic of Congo — child labour, habitat destruction), lithium (Chile/Argentina — water-intensive mining in desert), rare earth elements (toxic processing waste in China). Manufacturing: high energy use, chemical solvents, greenhouse gas emissions, short production cycle (new model every year). Use phase: electricity consumption, planned obsolescence (software updates making older models slow). End of life: only 20% of e-waste is formally recycled globally. The other 80% is dumped, often in developing countries (Ghana, Nigeria) where it is informally burned or acid-leached, releasing toxic heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium) into soil and water. Solutions: design for repairability (modular phones like Fairphone), mandatory e-waste collection, longer software support, and stricter supply chain ethics standards.

Evaluate the potential of mycelium-based composites (grown from fungi on agricultural waste) as a sustainable replacement for polystyrene packaging. Consider properties, production, scale-up challenges, and environmental impact.Hard
Show Answer

Properties: mycelium composites can be grown in any mould shape, are lightweight, have comparable thermal insulation to polystyrene (R-value similar), good compressive strength, fully home-compostable in 45 days, fire-resistant, and non-toxic. Production: agricultural waste (hemp shiv, cotton hulls) inoculated with fungus, grown in 5–7 days at room temperature, then heat-treated to stop growth. Very low energy input — no petroleum, no high-temperature processing. Scale-up challenges: production is slower than injection-moulded polystyrene (days vs. seconds), product lifetime is shorter if exposed to moisture, currently higher cost per unit (though improving). Environmental impact: carbon-negative if agricultural waste is used (captures carbon in the mycelium mass). Fully circular: end-of-life composting returns nutrients to soil. Verdict: highly promising — already commercially available (Ecovative Design, used by Dell and IKEA) but requires scale-up investment to compete with polystyrene pricing.

Critically analyse whether 'green washing' in materials marketing (e.g. calling a product 'eco-friendly' or 'sustainable') undermines genuine sustainability efforts. Give three examples and propose how consumers and regulators could address this.Hard
Show Answer

Examples of greenwashing: 1) PLA bioplastic bags labelled 'biodegradable' — only in industrial composters, not in nature or home compost. 2) 'Recyclable' packaging — technically recyclable but no local recycling facility accepts it. 3) 'Organic cotton' clothing from brands that use toxic dyes, ship from overseas, and have short product lifespans — partially negating the sustainability of the cotton. Impact: greenwashing erodes consumer trust, gives a false sense of progress, and allows companies to profit from sustainability branding without genuine change. Addressing this: Consumer level — check third-party certifications (e.g. Cradle-to-Cradle, Global Organic Textile Standard). Regulatory level — mandate full lifecycle disclosure on labels; fine companies for false environmental claims (EU Green Claims Directive 2023 is a step in this direction); require third-party verification before 'eco-friendly' labelling is permitted.

Compare the environmental impact of building a house in wood (timber frame) versus reinforced concrete in terms of embodied carbon, operational energy, end-of-life, and long-term sustainability.Hard
Show Answer

Embodied carbon (production): Concrete production = ~0.2 kg CO₂ per kg; reinforced concrete house ≈ 50–80 tonnes CO₂. Timber house: trees absorb CO₂ during growth; a timber house stores ~50 tonnes CO₂ in its structure, and manufacturing adds ~5–10 tonnes CO₂. Net carbon: timber house can be carbon-negative at the construction stage. Operational energy: timber frame provides better natural insulation, reducing heating/cooling energy over building lifetime. End-of-life: concrete is difficult to recycle (downcycled into road fill at best); timber can be reused, recycled, composted, or burned for energy. Long-term sustainability: sustainable forestry (FSC certification) ensures trees regrow. Concrete uses finite limestone and sand. Verdict: life cycle analysis consistently shows timber has 30–60% lower carbon footprint than concrete. Countries like Norway and Finland are building multi-storey timber buildings (mass timber/CLT) to reduce construction sector emissions.

Design and justify a sustainability scoring system (rubric) to compare and rank materials for use in a school's new outdoor furniture. Include at least five sustainability criteria, explain how you would weight each, and apply your rubric to compare wood, recycled plastic lumber, and steel.Hard
Show Answer

Criteria and weightings: (1) Carbon footprint of production (25%) — lower is better. (2) End-of-life recyclability/biodegradability (20%) — circular options score higher. (3) Durability/longevity (20%) — longer lasting = fewer replacements = less resource use. (4) Use of renewable or recycled content (20%) — renewable/recycled sources score higher. (5) Maintenance requirements (15%) — low maintenance reduces chemical use and labour over time. Scoring (1–5 scale, 5=best): Wood (FSC certified): C-footprint 4, end-of-life 5 (composts/reuses), durability 3 (needs treatment), renewable 5, maintenance 3. Score = 4(25)+5(20)+3(20)+5(20)+3(15) = 100+100+60+100+45 = 405/500. Recycled plastic lumber: C-footprint 4 (from waste), end-of-life 3 (recyclable if sorted), durability 5 (lasts 50 years), renewable 3 (recycled but from fossil base), maintenance 5 (no treatment). Score = 4(25)+3(20)+5(20)+3(20)+5(15) = 100+60+100+60+75 = 395/500. Steel: C-footprint 2 (high emissions), end-of-life 5 (100% recyclable), durability 5, renewable 2 (mining), maintenance 3 (needs painting). Score = 2(25)+5(20)+5(20)+2(20)+3(15) = 50+100+100+40+45 = 335/500. Recommendation: FSC wood wins slightly due to biodegradability and renewability, but recycled plastic lumber is a close second if durability is prioritised for outdoor use.
