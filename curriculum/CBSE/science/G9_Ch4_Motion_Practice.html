Distance vs Displacement

📺 Recommended Videos

NCERT / CBSE Distance and Displacement – Class 9

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Define distance. State whether it is a scalar or vector quantity. Easy
Show Answer

Distance is the total length of the path travelled by an object, regardless of direction. It is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude only, no direction.

Define displacement. How does it differ from distance? Easy
Show Answer

Displacement is the shortest straight-line distance between the starting point and the ending point, measured in a specific direction. Unlike distance, displacement is a vector quantity (it has both magnitude and direction). Displacement can be zero even when distance is non-zero.

A student walks 5 m east, then 5 m west back to the start. What is the (a) distance, and (b) displacement? Easy
Show Answer

(a) Distance = 5 + 5 = 10 m (total path length).
(b) Displacement = 0 m (start and end at the same point).

Give two examples of scalar quantities and two examples of vector quantities. Easy
Show Answer

Scalar: distance, speed, mass, temperature.
Vector: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force.

Can displacement ever be greater than distance? Explain. Easy
Show Answer

No. Displacement is always less than or equal to distance. The straight-line path (displacement) can never be longer than the actual path taken (distance). They are equal only when the object moves in a straight line without reversing direction.

What is the SI unit for both distance and displacement? Easy
Show Answer

The SI unit for both distance and displacement is the metre (m).

A car travels 3 km north and then 4 km east. What is the total distance travelled? Easy
Show Answer

Total distance = 3 km + 4 km = 7 km.

Using the same journey as Q7 (3 km north, 4 km east), calculate the magnitude of the displacement. Easy
Show Answer

Displacement = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5 km, directed north-east from start.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A jogger runs around a circular track of radius 50 m and returns to the starting point after one complete lap. State the (a) distance and (b) displacement. Medium
Show Answer

(a) Distance = circumference = 2π × 50 ≈ 314 m.
(b) Displacement = 0 m (same start and end point).

Explain why a taxi meter records distance and not displacement. Medium
Show Answer

A taxi meter records the total path length (distance) because the fare depends on how far the vehicle has actually travelled along roads, not the straight-line difference between start and end.

A ball is thrown straight up 8 m and comes back down 8 m to the thrower's hand. Find (a) distance and (b) displacement. Medium
Show Answer

(a) Distance = 8 + 8 = 16 m.
(b) Displacement = 0 m (returns to origin).

A student walks 6 m north and then 8 m east. Calculate: (a) total distance, (b) magnitude of displacement. Medium
Show Answer

(a) Distance = 6 + 8 = 14 m.
(b) Displacement = √(6² + 8²) = √(36 + 64) = √100 = 10 m.

Why must displacement always include a direction when giving your answer? Medium
Show Answer

Displacement is a vector quantity. Without direction, it is incomplete — "5 m north" and "5 m south" have the same magnitude but describe completely different positions. Direction distinguishes one from the other.

An athlete runs 100 m along a straight track and stops at the finish line. Compare their distance and displacement. Medium
Show Answer

Both distance and displacement are 100 m (in the direction of motion). When motion is in a straight line without any reversal, distance = magnitude of displacement.

Describe a real-life situation where a large distance corresponds to zero displacement. Medium
Show Answer

Example: A marathon runner who starts and finishes at the same point (e.g., a looped course). They cover ~42 km of distance but their displacement is 0 m.

A bee flies 12 m south and then 5 m east. Find the magnitude of its displacement. Medium
Show Answer

Displacement = √(12² + 5²) = √(144 + 25) = √169 = 13 m south-east.

SET C Extended (Hard)

A person walks 10 m north, then 6 m east, then 10 m south. Calculate (a) total distance, (b) magnitude of displacement, and (c) direction of displacement from start. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Distance = 10 + 6 + 10 = 26 m.
(b) Net north-south: 10 N − 10 S = 0; net east: 6 m. Displacement = 6 m east.
(c) Direction = due east.

Explain why two journeys with identical distances can have very different displacements. Use a worked example. Hard
Show Answer

Displacement depends on the net change in position (start to end), not the total path. Example: Journey A goes 5 m north then 5 m south → distance 10 m, displacement 0. Journey B goes 10 m north → distance 10 m, displacement 10 m north. Same distance, completely different displacement.

A particle moves along a straight line: 20 m east, then 35 m west, then 15 m east. Find (a) total distance, (b) final displacement from start. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Distance = 20 + 35 + 15 = 70 m.
(b) Net position: +20 − 35 + 15 = 0 m. Displacement = 0 m.

Using a vector diagram, show how to find the resultant displacement when a boat travels 40 m east and then 30 m north. Include the angle from east. Hard
Show Answer

Draw 40 m east → then 30 m north from that endpoint. Resultant = √(40² + 30²) = √(1600 + 900) = √2500 = 50 m. Angle from east = tan⁻¹(30/40) = tan⁻¹(0.75) ≈ 36.9° north of east.

A student says: "If I run 500 m and end up where I started, my displacement was 500 m." Identify and correct the error in this statement. Hard
Show Answer

The student has confused distance with displacement. If they return to the starting point, the net change in position is zero, so displacement = 0 m. The 500 m is the total path length (distance), not displacement.

In a grid-based city, a car travels 3 blocks north, 4 blocks east, 3 blocks south, and 2 blocks west. If each block is 200 m, find (a) total distance and (b) net displacement. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Total blocks = 3+4+3+2 = 12; distance = 12 × 200 = 2400 m.
(b) N−S: 3−3 = 0; E−W: 4−2 = 2 blocks east = 2 × 200 = 400 m east.

Differentiate between the odometer and the GPS displacement reading on a car. Which records distance and which records displacement? Hard
Show Answer

The odometer records the total path length (distance) — it increases continuously regardless of direction. The GPS displacement reading (straight-line from start to current position) records the net change in position — a vector that changes based on start/end coordinates. The odometer records distance; GPS gives displacement magnitude.

A particle moves along a semicircular path of diameter 20 m. Calculate (a) distance travelled, (b) magnitude of displacement, and explain why they differ. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Distance = half circumference = π × r = π × 10 ≈ 31.4 m.
(b) Displacement = diameter = 20 m (straight line from one end of diameter to the other).
They differ because the particle followed a curved path (longer) rather than the straight-line route between start and end.

Speed and Velocity

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Write the formula for average speed. State its SI unit. Easy
Show Answer

Average speed = Total distance ÷ Total time. SI unit: metres per second (m/s).

Is speed a scalar or vector quantity? Justify your answer. Easy
Show Answer

Speed is a scalar quantity. It has magnitude only (e.g., 10 m/s) and does not include direction.

A car travels 120 km in 2 hours. Calculate its average speed in (a) km/h, (b) m/s. Easy
Show Answer

(a) Speed = 120 ÷ 2 = 60 km/h.
(b) 60 km/h × (1000/3600) = 16.7 m/s.

Convert 72 km/h to m/s. Easy
Show Answer

72 × (1000/3600) = 72 ÷ 3.6 = 20 m/s.

What is the difference between speed and velocity? Easy
Show Answer

Speed is the rate of change of distance (scalar). Velocity is the rate of change of displacement (vector — it includes both magnitude and direction).

Convert 15 m/s to km/h. Easy
Show Answer

15 × 3.6 = 54 km/h.

A train covers 300 m in 30 s. Find its speed. Easy
Show Answer

Speed = 300 ÷ 30 = 10 m/s.

Can an object have a constant speed but a changing velocity? Give an example. Easy
Show Answer

Yes. A car moving around a circular track at a constant speed has a continuously changing velocity because its direction changes. Speed is constant; velocity (direction component) varies.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A cyclist travels 200 m east in 25 s. State the velocity (magnitude and direction). Medium
Show Answer

Velocity = 200 ÷ 25 = 8 m/s east. (Must include direction for velocity.)

An athlete runs 400 m around a track in 50 s and returns to the start. Find (a) average speed and (b) average velocity. Medium
Show Answer

(a) Average speed = 400 ÷ 50 = 8 m/s.
(b) Displacement = 0 m, so average velocity = 0 ÷ 50 = 0 m/s.

A car travels 50 km at 100 km/h and the next 50 km at 50 km/h. Find the average speed for the whole journey. Medium
Show Answer

Time₁ = 50/100 = 0.5 h; Time₂ = 50/50 = 1 h. Total time = 1.5 h. Total distance = 100 km. Average speed = 100 ÷ 1.5 ≈ 66.7 km/h. (Not 75 km/h — you cannot simply average the speeds.)

Explain why a satellite orbiting Earth at 7800 m/s has a constant speed but not a constant velocity. Medium
Show Answer

The satellite travels in a circular orbit, so its speed (magnitude) remains constant at 7800 m/s. However, its direction continuously changes, meaning its velocity vector changes every instant — hence velocity is not constant.

A bus travels for 2 h at 60 km/h then stops for 30 min and finally travels 1 h at 80 km/h. Find total distance and average speed for the whole journey. Medium
Show Answer

Distance = (2 × 60) + (1 × 80) = 120 + 80 = 200 km. Total time = 2 + 0.5 + 1 = 3.5 h. Average speed = 200 ÷ 3.5 ≈ 57.1 km/h.

How long does it take a runner to cover 1500 m at 5 m/s? Medium
Show Answer

Time = distance ÷ speed = 1500 ÷ 5 = 300 s (5 minutes).

A bird flies at 20 m/s. How far does it travel in 45 seconds? Medium
Show Answer

Distance = speed × time = 20 × 45 = 900 m.

Distinguish between instantaneous speed and average speed. Give a practical example of each. Medium
Show Answer

Instantaneous speed is the speed at a particular moment in time (e.g., the speedometer reading of a car at a traffic light). Average speed is total distance divided by total time for a journey (e.g., covering 60 km in 1 hour = 60 km/h average, even though the car may have slowed and sped up).

SET C Extended (Hard)

Two trains start from the same station at the same time, moving in opposite directions at 60 km/h and 80 km/h. How far apart are they after 90 minutes? Hard
Show Answer

Relative speed = 60 + 80 = 140 km/h (opposite directions). Time = 90 min = 1.5 h. Distance apart = 140 × 1.5 = 210 km.

A swimmer crosses a river of width 80 m. She swims at 2 m/s perpendicular to the river bank while the current flows at 1.5 m/s. Find (a) time to cross, (b) resultant velocity magnitude. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Time = 80 ÷ 2 = 40 s (only the perpendicular component crosses the river).
(b) Resultant = √(2² + 1.5²) = √(4 + 2.25) = √6.25 = 2.5 m/s.

A car travels the first half of a journey in time t₁ and the second half in time t₂. Derive an expression for the average speed if both halves have the same distance d. Hard
Show Answer

Total distance = 2d. Total time = t₁ + t₂. Average speed = 2d/(t₁ + t₂). If v₁ = d/t₁ and v₂ = d/t₂, then avg speed = 2v₁v₂/(v₁ + v₂) — the harmonic mean of the two speeds.

Explain why a ball moving in uniform circular motion has zero average velocity over one complete revolution but a non-zero average speed. Hard
Show Answer

After one complete revolution, the ball returns to its starting position, so net displacement = 0. Average velocity = 0/T = 0. However, the ball has covered the full circumference (non-zero distance), so average speed = 2πr/T ≠ 0.

A race car completes a 5 km lap of a circular track in 2 min 30 s. Calculate (a) average speed, (b) average velocity (give reasoning). Hard
Show Answer

(a) Time = 150 s. Average speed = 5000 ÷ 150 ≈ 33.3 m/s.
(b) Displacement for one complete lap = 0 m, so average velocity = 0/150 = 0 m/s.

A plane flies 600 km north in 1 h, then 800 km east in 2 h. Find (a) average speed, (b) magnitude of average velocity. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Total distance = 600 + 800 = 1400 km. Total time = 3 h. Average speed = 1400 ÷ 3 ≈ 466.7 km/h.
(b) Net displacement = √(600² + 800²) = √(360000 + 640000) = √1000000 = 1000 km. Avg velocity = 1000 ÷ 3 ≈ 333.3 km/h north-east.

Convert 108 km/h to m/s and use it to find how far a car at this speed travels in 8.5 seconds. Hard
Show Answer

108 km/h ÷ 3.6 = 30 m/s. Distance = 30 × 8.5 = 255 m.

Two cyclists start 100 m apart and ride towards each other at 8 m/s and 12 m/s. How long before they meet, and how far has each ridden? Hard
Show Answer

Closing speed = 8 + 12 = 20 m/s. Time = 100 ÷ 20 = 5 s. Cyclist 1 covers: 8 × 5 = 40 m. Cyclist 2 covers: 12 × 5 = 60 m. (40 + 60 = 100 m ✓)

Acceleration

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Acceleration – Intro What is Acceleration?
Crash Course Physics Kinematics – Acceleration
Physics Online Acceleration and Free Fall
NCERT / CBSE Acceleration – Class 9 Science

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Define acceleration and state its SI unit. Easy
Show Answer

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. SI unit: m/s² (metres per second squared). Formula: a = (v − u) / t.

Is acceleration a scalar or vector quantity? Why? Easy
Show Answer

Acceleration is a vector quantity because it is derived from velocity (a vector), so it has both magnitude and direction.

A car increases its speed from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 5 s. Calculate the acceleration. Easy
Show Answer

a = (v − u) / t = (30 − 10) / 5 = 20 / 5 = 4 m/s².

What does a negative acceleration (deceleration) indicate about the motion of an object? Easy
Show Answer

A negative acceleration (also called deceleration or retardation) means the object is slowing down — its velocity is decreasing over time.

What is the value of gravitational acceleration (g) near Earth's surface? Easy
Show Answer

g = 9.8 m/s² (approximately 10 m/s² for simple calculations) directed downward toward Earth's centre.

A motorbike slows from 20 m/s to 0 m/s in 4 s. Find its deceleration. Easy
Show Answer

a = (0 − 20) / 4 = −20/4 = −5 m/s² (deceleration of 5 m/s²).

Give two real-life examples of objects that accelerate. Easy
Show Answer

1. A car pulling away from traffic lights (speeds up). 2. A ball thrown upward that slows, stops, and falls back (direction of velocity changes).

If an object moves at constant velocity, what is its acceleration? Easy
Show Answer

Acceleration = zero (0 m/s²). Constant velocity means no change in speed or direction, so Δv = 0, and a = Δv/t = 0.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A ball is dropped from rest and falls freely under gravity. What is its speed after 3 seconds? (g = 9.8 m/s²) Medium
Show Answer

v = u + at = 0 + 9.8 × 3 = 29.4 m/s downward.

Explain the difference between positive and negative acceleration with examples from everyday life. Medium
Show Answer

Positive acceleration: velocity increases in the direction of motion (e.g., car accelerating from a traffic light). Negative acceleration (deceleration): velocity decreases (e.g., a car braking before a stop sign). Both are accelerations; the sign indicates direction relative to velocity.

An object moving in a circle at constant speed: does it have acceleration? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

Yes — it has centripetal acceleration. Even though speed is constant, velocity changes direction continuously, so acceleration ≠ 0. The acceleration points toward the centre of the circle.

A rocket increases velocity from 500 m/s to 2000 m/s in 30 s. Calculate its acceleration. Medium
Show Answer

a = (2000 − 500) / 30 = 1500 / 30 = 50 m/s².

What is free fall? Under what conditions does an object experience free fall? Medium
Show Answer

Free fall is the motion of an object under the influence of gravity alone, with no air resistance or other forces. An object is in free fall when only gravitational force acts on it (e.g., a ball dropped in a vacuum, or approximately a ball dropped from a small height in air).

Calculate the time taken for a cyclist to accelerate from 2 m/s to 14 m/s at a constant acceleration of 3 m/s². Medium
Show Answer

t = (v − u) / a = (14 − 2) / 3 = 12 / 3 = 4 s.

A stone is thrown vertically upward at 19.6 m/s. Ignoring air resistance, how long does it take to reach maximum height? (g = 9.8 m/s²) Medium
Show Answer

At maximum height, v = 0. t = (v − u) / a = (0 − 19.6) / (−9.8) = 2 s.

Why is the unit of acceleration m/s² and not simply m/s? Medium
Show Answer

Acceleration = Δvelocity / time = (m/s) / s = m/s². It measures how much velocity (m/s) changes each second, so it is per second per second (per second squared).

SET C Extended (Hard)

A car decelerates uniformly from 25 m/s to rest. If the deceleration is 5 m/s², how far does it travel before stopping? Hard
Show Answer

Using v² = u² + 2as: 0 = 25² + 2(−5)s → 10s = 625 → s = 62.5 m.

A ball is thrown upward at 14.7 m/s. Find (a) max height, (b) total time in the air. (g = 9.8 m/s²) Hard
Show Answer

(a) At max height v = 0: s = (v²−u²)/(2a) = (0−14.7²)/(2×−9.8) = −216.09/(−19.6) = 11.025 m ≈ 11 m.
(b) Time up = 14.7/9.8 = 1.5 s; total time = 2 × 1.5 = 3 s.

Explain how an object can have zero velocity but non-zero acceleration. Give an example. Hard
Show Answer

At the top of its trajectory, a ball thrown upward has v = 0 momentarily, but gravity still acts (a = 9.8 m/s² downward). Velocity is zero at that instant, but acceleration is not. This is because acceleration depends on force acting, not on current velocity.

Two objects are dropped from the same height simultaneously — one is 2 kg, the other is 10 kg. Ignoring air resistance, which hits the ground first and why? Hard
Show Answer

Both hit the ground at the same time. In free fall, all objects accelerate at g = 9.8 m/s² regardless of mass (Galileo's principle). The gravitational force is larger on the heavier object, but so is its inertia (mass), cancelling out in the formula a = F/m = mg/m = g.

A car accelerates from rest at 2 m/s². Find its speed and distance covered after 8 s. Hard
Show Answer

v = u + at = 0 + 2 × 8 = 16 m/s.
s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½ × 2 × 64 = 64 m.

A train travelling at 30 m/s applies brakes and decelerates at 0.5 m/s². How long does it take to stop, and what distance does it cover? Hard
Show Answer

t = (v−u)/a = (0−30)/(−0.5) = 60 s.
s = (v²−u²)/(2a) = (0−900)/(−1) = 900 m.

Distinguish between uniform acceleration and non-uniform acceleration. Which do the equations of motion apply to? Hard
Show Answer

Uniform acceleration: constant rate of change of velocity (e.g., free fall in vacuum). Non-uniform acceleration: rate of change of velocity varies (e.g., car in traffic). The equations of motion (v=u+at, s=ut+½at², etc.) apply only to uniform acceleration.

A stone dropped from a cliff takes 4 s to reach the ground. Calculate (a) its speed on impact and (b) the height of the cliff. (g = 9.8 m/s²) Hard
Show Answer

(a) v = u + gt = 0 + 9.8 × 4 = 39.2 m/s.
(b) s = ut + ½gt² = 0 + ½ × 9.8 × 16 = 78.4 m.

Equations of Motion

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Kinematic Formulas and Projectile Motion Kinematic Equations – All 4
Crash Course Physics SUVAT Equations of Motion

NCERT / CBSE Equations of Motion – Class 9

SET A Foundational (Easy)

State all four equations of motion (SUVAT). Define each variable. Easy
Show Answer

1. v = u + at
2. s = ut + ½at²
3. v² = u² + 2as
4. s = ½(u + v)t
Where: u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time, s = displacement. All apply to uniform acceleration only.

Using v = u + at: if u = 0, a = 5 m/s², t = 6 s, find v. Easy
Show Answer

v = 0 + 5 × 6 = 30 m/s.

Using s = ut + ½at²: find s when u = 0, a = 10 m/s², t = 4 s. Easy
Show Answer

s = 0 × 4 + ½ × 10 × 4² = 0 + 5 × 16 = 80 m.

Using v² = u² + 2as: find v when u = 5 m/s, a = 3 m/s², s = 12 m. Easy
Show Answer

v² = 5² + 2 × 3 × 12 = 25 + 72 = 97. v = √97 ≈ 9.85 m/s.

Under what condition do the equations of motion hold true? Easy
Show Answer

The equations of motion hold only for uniform (constant) acceleration in a straight line.

A car starts from rest and accelerates at 4 m/s². What is its velocity after 10 s? Easy
Show Answer

v = u + at = 0 + 4 × 10 = 40 m/s.

Using s = ½(u + v)t: find s when u = 10 m/s, v = 30 m/s, t = 5 s. Easy
Show Answer

s = ½ × (10 + 30) × 5 = ½ × 40 × 5 = 100 m.

Which equation of motion would you use to find displacement if you know initial velocity, acceleration, and time? State the equation. Easy
Show Answer

Use s = ut + ½at² when initial velocity (u), acceleration (a), and time (t) are known.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A ball is thrown vertically upward at 20 m/s. Using equations of motion, find the maximum height. (g = 10 m/s²) Medium
Show Answer

At max height v = 0. Using v² = u² + 2as: 0 = 400 − 20s → s = 400/20 = 20 m.

A car travelling at 25 m/s brakes with deceleration 5 m/s². How far does it travel before stopping? Medium
Show Answer

v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 625 + 2(−5)s → 10s = 625 → s = 62.5 m.

A train starts from rest and reaches 54 km/h in 30 s. Find its acceleration and the distance covered. Medium
Show Answer

54 km/h = 15 m/s. a = (v−u)/t = 15/30 = 0.5 m/s². s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½ × 0.5 × 900 = 225 m.

Using the appropriate equation, find the time for a car to accelerate from 10 m/s to 50 m/s at 4 m/s². Medium
Show Answer

v = u + at → 50 = 10 + 4t → 4t = 40 → t = 10 s.

A stone is dropped from a 45 m tower. How long before it hits the ground? (g = 10 m/s²) Medium
Show Answer

s = ut + ½gt² → 45 = 0 + ½ × 10 × t² → t² = 9 → t = 3 s.

A motorcycle accelerates from 15 m/s to 35 m/s over 200 m. Find the acceleration. Medium
Show Answer

v² = u² + 2as → 35² = 15² + 2a × 200 → 1225 = 225 + 400a → 400a = 1000 → a = 2.5 m/s².

A ball rolls from rest down a 5 m slope with a = 2 m/s². Find its speed at the bottom. Medium
Show Answer

v² = u² + 2as = 0 + 2 × 2 × 5 = 20 → v = √20 ≈ 4.47 m/s.

Identify which SUVAT equation to use when time is not given. State the equation and give a worked example. Medium
Show Answer

Use v² = u² + 2as when time is not involved. Example: u = 0, a = 3 m/s², s = 24 m. v² = 0 + 2 × 3 × 24 = 144. v = 12 m/s.

SET C Extended (Hard)

A bullet is fired horizontally at 400 m/s. It strikes a wall 2 s later. Find (a) horizontal distance and (b) vertical drop (g = 10 m/s²). Hard
Show Answer

(a) Horizontal: s = 400 × 2 = 800 m.
(b) Vertical (free fall): s = ½ × 10 × 4 = 20 m.

Derive the third equation of motion v² = u² + 2as by eliminating t from the first two equations. Hard
Show Answer

From v = u + at → t = (v−u)/a. Substitute in s = ut + ½at²: s = u(v−u)/a + ½a[(v−u)/a]² = u(v−u)/a + (v−u)²/2a. Multiply through by 2a: 2as = 2u(v−u) + (v−u)² = 2uv−2u² + v²−2uv+u² = v²−u². Therefore v² = u² + 2as.

Two cars start side by side. Car A accelerates at 3 m/s² from rest. Car B starts at 12 m/s and decelerates at 1 m/s². At what time do they have the same velocity? Hard
Show Answer

v_A = 3t; v_B = 12 − t. Equal: 3t = 12 − t → 4t = 12 → t = 3 s. Common velocity = 9 m/s.

A rocket launched from rest achieves 500 m/s over 2 km. Find acceleration. Is this realistic for a rocket? Hard
Show Answer

v² = u² + 2as → 250000 = 0 + 2a × 2000 → a = 250000/4000 = 62.5 m/s². This is about 6.4g — plausible for powerful rockets (e.g., Space Shuttle peak ~ 3g, but military rockets can exceed 10g).

A car takes 8 s to travel 200 m and has a final speed of 30 m/s. Find (a) initial speed using s = ½(u+v)t, (b) acceleration. Hard
Show Answer

(a) 200 = ½(u + 30) × 8 → 200 = 4(u + 30) → u + 30 = 50 → u = 20 m/s.
(b) a = (v−u)/t = (30−20)/8 = 1.25 m/s².

A ball is thrown upward at 15 m/s from the top of a 20 m building. Find the total time to reach ground. (g = 10 m/s², take upward positive) Hard
Show Answer

Taking up as positive, displacement to ground = −20 m. s = ut + ½at²: −20 = 15t − 5t² → 5t² − 15t − 20 = 0 → t² − 3t − 4 = 0 → (t−4)(t+1) = 0 → t = 4 s (discard t = −1).

Explain why SUVAT equations cannot be directly applied to a car journey involving traffic stops and accelerations. What method should be used instead? Hard
Show Answer

SUVAT requires constant acceleration throughout. A car journey with traffic involves non-uniform acceleration (varying speeds, stops, turns). Instead, you should divide the journey into segments of uniform acceleration and apply SUVAT to each segment separately, or use a velocity–time graph and find the area under each section.

Two balls are dropped from the same height: one in vacuum, one in air. Compare their motion qualitatively. Which equation of motion applies to which? Hard
Show Answer

In a vacuum, the ball accelerates uniformly at g = 9.8 m/s² — SUVAT equations apply directly. In air, drag force increases with speed, so acceleration decreases — the ball reaches terminal velocity (non-uniform acceleration). SUVAT does not apply to the air case except as an approximation for very short falls where air resistance is negligible.

Distance–Time Graphs

SET A Foundational (Easy)

What does the gradient (slope) of a distance–time graph represent? Easy
Show Answer

The gradient of a distance–time graph represents the speed of the object. Gradient = Δdistance / Δtime = speed.

What does a horizontal line on a distance–time graph indicate? Easy
Show Answer

A horizontal line means the distance is not changing with time — the object is stationary (at rest).

How does a steeper slope on a distance–time graph compare to a gentler slope? Easy
Show Answer

A steeper slope means a greater gradient, which means a higher speed. A gentler slope means slower speed.

What type of motion does a straight line (not horizontal) on a distance–time graph represent? Easy
Show Answer

A straight, non-horizontal line represents uniform speed (constant speed) — distance increases by equal amounts in equal time intervals.

What does a curved line on a distance–time graph indicate? Easy
Show Answer

A curve indicates non-uniform speed — the object is accelerating or decelerating (speed is changing).

A distance–time graph shows distance rising from 0 to 80 m over 10 s in a straight line. Calculate the speed. Easy
Show Answer

Speed = gradient = Δd/Δt = 80/10 = 8 m/s.

Can distance ever decrease on a distance–time graph? Explain. Easy
Show Answer

No. Distance is always non-decreasing — it can only increase or remain constant (rest). Distance is the total path length and never decreases. (Displacement–time graphs can decrease.)

How would you find the speed from a curved distance–time graph at a specific point? Easy
Show Answer

Draw a tangent to the curve at that specific point, then calculate the gradient of the tangent. Gradient of tangent = instantaneous speed at that point.

A distance–time graph has three sections: slope from 0–5 s (0 to 40 m), flat from 5–8 s, slope down from 8–12 s. Describe the motion and find average speed for each section. Medium

Section 1 (0–5 s): Moving at speed = 40/5 = 8 m/s. Section 2 (5–8 s): Stationary (at rest). Section 3 (8–12 s): Distance decreases — returning toward start at 40/4 = 10 m/s (on a displacement-time graph; note a distance graph can't decrease, so this may represent a displacement-time graph — clarify context).

Compare the motions of two objects A and B on the same distance–time graph, where A has a steeper straight line than B. Medium
Show Answer

Object A is travelling at a higher constant speed than object B. Both have uniform (constant) speed. A covers more distance per unit time.

On a distance–time graph, what shape would represent (a) constant acceleration, and (b) constant deceleration? Medium
Show Answer

(a) Constant acceleration: a concave upward curve (gradient increases — slope gets steeper). (b) Constant deceleration: a concave downward curve (gradient decreases — slope gets less steep).

A cyclist's distance–time graph shows: 0 to 30 s: 0 to 150 m straight; 30 to 50 s: flat at 150 m; 50 to 70 s: 150 m to 0 m straight. Find the average speed for the whole journey (use distance only). Medium
Show Answer

Total distance = 150 + 0 + 150 = 300 m (out and back). Total time = 70 s. Average speed = 300/70 ≈ 4.29 m/s.

Explain why two parallel straight lines on a distance–time graph represent objects moving at the same speed. What does the gap between lines mean? Medium
Show Answer

Parallel lines have equal gradients, which means equal speeds. The gap (vertical distance) between the lines represents the difference in distance covered at any time — i.e., one object started with a head start or began later.

Using a distance–time graph, how can you tell if an object has a greater acceleration than another object? Medium
Show Answer

Both will show curves. The object with greater acceleration will have a more steeply curved line — it gains speed faster, so its gradient increases more rapidly.

Sketch a description of the distance–time graph for a ball bouncing repeatedly on a floor. Describe the key features. Medium
Show Answer

On a distance–time graph: a series of parabola-like curves, each with steeper gradient on descent (accelerating down) and gentler gradient on ascent (decelerating up). Each bounce peak occurs at the same distance (or slightly decreasing with energy loss). The curve flattens near the top (momentarily at rest).

A distance–time graph passes through (0,0), (4,20), and (10,50). Is the speed constant? Justify with calculation. Medium
Show Answer

Gradient from 0 to 4 s: 20/4 = 5 m/s. Gradient from 4 to 10 s: (50−20)/6 = 30/6 = 5 m/s. Both gradients are equal, so the speed is constant at 5 m/s. The graph is a straight line.

A distance–time graph has a curve given approximately by d = 2t². Find the instantaneous speed at t = 3 s by drawing the tangent (gradient method) or differentiation. Hard
Show Answer

Differentiating: dd/dt = 4t. At t = 3 s: speed = 4 × 3 = 12 m/s. (Tangent method: use points on d = 2t² near t=3, e.g. at t=2.5, d=12.5 and t=3.5, d=24.5 → gradient ≈ (24.5−12.5)/1 = 12 m/s.)

Two objects start at the same point. Object A has a straight-line distance–time graph with slope 6 m/s. Object B has a curved graph starting slowly. Describe qualitatively when and if B overtakes A. Hard
Show Answer

Initially A is ahead (constant speed 6 m/s). B accelerates, so its gradient increases over time. B overtakes A at the point where their distance–time curves intersect — when B's instantaneous speed exceeds A's and its total distance surpasses A's. After that crossing point, B is ahead. The exact time depends on B's acceleration profile.

Convert the following distance–time graph description into a velocity–time graph: 0–5 s: straight line slope 4 m/s; 5–10 s: horizontal; 10–15 s: straight line slope 8 m/s. Hard
Show Answer

v-t graph: 0–5 s: horizontal line at v = 4 m/s; 5–10 s: horizontal line at v = 0 m/s (at rest); 10–15 s: horizontal line at v = 8 m/s. (Each constant slope in d-t becomes a horizontal line in v-t.)

Explain how a distance–time graph for projectile motion (only vertical component) looks from launch to landing. Describe each phase. Hard
Show Answer

As the projectile rises, it decelerates (concave-down curve, decreasing gradient). At the peak, the gradient momentarily = 0 (instantaneous rest). On descent, it accelerates downward (concave-up curve, increasing gradient). The total curve is a smooth hill shape. Note: total distance keeps increasing; only displacement shows the hill symmetrically.

A train travels between two stations 5 km apart. For the first 2 km it accelerates uniformly, for the next 2 km it travels at constant speed, and for the last 1 km it decelerates to rest. Describe the distance–time graph shape for each segment. Hard
Show Answer

Segment 1 (0–2 km): curve with increasing gradient (accelerating). Segment 2 (2–4 km): straight line (constant speed, steeper slope). Segment 3 (4–5 km): curve with decreasing gradient (decelerating to rest — slope flattens to zero at 5 km).

Explain why a distance–time graph can never have a negative gradient, but a displacement–time graph can. Illustrate with an example. Hard
Show Answer

Distance only accumulates (total path length always increases or stays constant), so its graph can only have zero or positive gradient. Displacement measures net position change — if an object moves back toward the start, displacement decreases, giving a negative gradient on a displacement–time graph. Example: a car going forward then reversing: distance–time keeps rising; displacement–time rises then falls below previous value.

Two cyclists approach each other on a straight path. Sketch the shape of (a) their individual distance–time graphs from their own starting points, and (b) the distance between them vs time. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Each cyclist's individual distance–time graph shows a straight line with positive slope (constant speed away from their start). (b) The distance between them decreases linearly (straight line with negative slope), reaching zero when they meet. After the meeting, the distance between them increases linearly again (they pass each other).

The gradient of a distance–time curve changes from 5 m/s at t = 2 s to 15 m/s at t = 6 s. Estimate the average acceleration between these two times. Hard
Show Answer

Average acceleration = Δv / Δt = (15 − 5) / (6 − 2) = 10 / 4 = 2.5 m/s².

The area under a velocity–time graph represents the displacement of the object during that time interval.

A v-t graph shows a straight line from v = 0 at t = 0 to v = 20 m/s at t = 5 s. Calculate the acceleration. Easy
Show Answer

Acceleration = gradient = (20 − 0)/(5 − 0) = 4 m/s².

What shape on a velocity–time graph indicates uniform acceleration? Easy

A straight line with a positive slope indicates uniform (constant) acceleration.

On a v-t graph, a line slopes downward from 30 m/s to 0 m/s over 6 s. What does this represent? Easy
Show Answer

The downward slope represents uniform deceleration. The object is slowing down from 30 m/s to rest. Deceleration = (0−30)/6 = −5 m/s² (magnitude: 5 m/s²).

A v-t graph shows: 0–4 s: v rises from 0 to 20 m/s; 4–10 s: v constant at 20 m/s; 10–14 s: v drops to 0. Find (a) acceleration in each phase, (b) total displacement. Medium

How does a curved velocity–time graph differ from a straight-line v-t graph in terms of acceleration? Medium
Show Answer

A straight-line v-t graph = constant (uniform) acceleration. A curved v-t graph = non-uniform acceleration — the rate of change of velocity is itself changing over time.

On a v-t graph, the line crosses the time axis (v = 0) and continues with negative velocity. What does this mean physically? Medium
Show Answer

The object has momentarily stopped and then begun to move in the opposite direction. Negative velocity means motion is reversed compared to the positive direction defined.

A trapezoid is formed under a v-t graph with parallel sides of 20 m/s and 8 m/s and a height (time) of 5 s. Find the displacement. Medium
Show Answer

Area of trapezoid = ½(a + b) × h = ½(20 + 8) × 5 = ½ × 28 × 5 = 70 m.

From a v-t graph, how would you find the distance travelled rather than displacement when velocity is negative at some point? Medium
Show Answer

Find the area above the time axis (positive velocity phase) and below the axis (negative velocity phase) separately, then add the magnitudes of both areas. This gives total distance; displacement would be positive area minus negative area.

A car's v-t graph shows v = 0 at t = 0, rising linearly to 30 m/s at t = 10 s, then constant. Find (a) acceleration, (b) displacement after 15 s. Medium

A v-t graph shows: 0–3 s: v rises from 0 to 12 m/s; 3–7 s: v constant at 12 m/s; 7–10 s: v drops to −6 m/s. Find (a) accelerations in each phase, (b) total displacement, (c) total distance. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Phase 1: 12/3 = 4 m/s²; Phase 2: 0; Phase 3: (−6−12)/3 = −6 m/s².
(b) Areas: ½×3×12 + 4×12 + ½(12+0)×2 − ½×6×1 = 18+48+12−3 = 75 m (net). More carefully: at v=0 in phase 3: time from 7s when v crosses 0 = 12/6 = 2 s → at t=9 s, v=0; t=9–10: v goes to −6, area = ½×1×6 = 3 m (negative).

(c) Distance = 18+48+12+3 = 81 m.

Show Answer

Explain how you would determine if two objects will ever be at the same position using their velocity–time graphs, given they start at the same point. Hard
Show Answer

The objects are at the same position when their displacements from the start are equal. Calculate the area under each object's v-t graph up to each time t. Set area₁ = area₂ and solve for t. The time where the cumulative areas are equal is when they meet. You can also use the v-t graph: if one goes ahead (area₁ > area₂) but the other is faster (higher current velocity), they will eventually meet when the trailing object's extra speed accumulates enough area to catch up.

Show Answer

(b) Area at t = 4 s: Triangle above axis (0–2 s) = ½×2×20 = 20 m upward. Triangle below axis (2–4 s) = ½×2×20 = 20 m downward. Net displacement = 20 − 20 = 0 m (returns to start).

Show Answer

s = ∫₁⁴ 3t² dt = [t³]₁⁴ = 4³ − 1³ = 64 − 1 = 63 m.

Compare the information given by a distance–time graph and a velocity–time graph for the same journey. Which is more useful for calculating displacement, and why? Hard
Show Answer

A distance–time graph gives speed at any point (via gradient) and shows total path length. A velocity–time graph gives acceleration (gradient), displacement (area), and direction of motion. The velocity–time graph is more useful for displacement because displacement equals the net area under the v-t graph — it automatically accounts for direction (positive/negative velocity), whereas the d-t graph only shows cumulative total path.

A racing car's v-t graph shows velocity increasing from 0 to 60 m/s in 6 s (straight line), then constant for 10 s, then decreasing to 0 in 4 s. Calculate total distance. Should the driver have started braking earlier to stop within 800 m? Hard
Show Answer

Area = ½×6×60 + 10×60 + ½×4×60 = 180 + 600 + 120 = 900 m. Since 900 m > 800 m, the car goes past 800 m before stopping. Yes, the driver should have braked earlier — they needed to begin decelerating before the 800 m mark.

(b) s = ∫₀⁵ (2 + 3t) dt = [2t + 1.5t²]₀⁵ = (10 + 37.5) − 0 = 47.5 m.
