Research Questions

Set A Easy — Recall & Recognition

What is the correct format for a testable MYP research question?
Show Answer

"How does [independent variable] affect [dependent variable] in/on [controlled context]?" — e.g., "How does temperature affect the rate of photosynthesis in Elodea?"

Easy
Which research question is testable? (A) "Is pollution bad?" (B) "How does fertiliser concentration affect plant height?" (C) "Why do birds sing?"
Show Answer

(B) — it names a specific IV (fertiliser concentration) and a measurable DV (plant height). Options A and C involve opinion or complex causation not directly measurable.

Easy
Why must a research question avoid words like "best" or "better"?
Show Answer

Those words imply subjective judgement. A scientific RQ must be objectively testable with measurable data, not personal opinion.

Easy
What does MYP Criterion B assess in relation to research questions?
Show Answer

Criterion B (Inquiring and Designing) assesses whether you can formulate a focused, testable research question and design a valid experiment to answer it.

Easy
A student asks: "Do plants need light?" Improve this into a proper MYP research question.
Show Answer

"How does light intensity (lux) affect the rate of oxygen production (mL/min) in Elodea aquatic plants?" — adds specific IV, measurable DV, and named organism.

Easy
Why should a research question mention the specific organism or material being tested?
Show Answer

Specificity limits the scope and makes the experiment reproducible. "Plants" is too broad — "Phaseolus vulgaris seedlings" tells anyone exactly what to use.

Easy
State whether this RQ is opinion-based or testable: "How does sugar concentration affect yeast respiration rate (CO₂ bubbles/min)?"
Show Answer

Testable — it identifies IV (sugar concentration), DV (CO₂ bubbles per minute, measurable), and implies a controlled experiment.

Easy
List two features that make a research question scientifically valid.
Show Answer

1) Identifies one clear independent variable and one measurable dependent variable. 2) Is answerable through direct observation or experiment, not opinion.

Easy

Set B Medium — Application & Analysis

A student writes: "How does music affect memory?" Identify TWO problems and rewrite as a proper MYP RQ.
Show Answer

Problems: (1) "music" is not quantified (type? volume?), (2) "memory" is not defined as a measurable DV. Rewrite: "How does background music volume (dB) affect the number of words recalled correctly in a 20-word memory test?"

Medium
Explain why "How does exercise affect health?" is not a suitable MYP research question.
Show Answer

"Health" is multi-dimensional and not directly measurable. "Exercise" is undefined. A better RQ specifies: type of exercise, duration, and a single measurable health marker (e.g., resting heart rate bpm).

Medium
Construct a research question for an investigation into antibiotics and bacteria using the "How does X affect Y?" format.
Show Answer

"How does the concentration of amoxicillin (mg/mL) affect the diameter of the inhibition zone (mm) around E. coli colonies on agar plates?"

Medium
Distinguish between a research question and a hypothesis.
Show Answer

A research question asks what will be investigated (open-ended). A hypothesis is a specific, testable prediction of the expected outcome, written in "If…then…because" format.

Medium
Why is "How does temperature affect enzymes?" a weak research question for MYP Criterion B?
Show Answer

It lacks specificity: which enzyme? which reaction? what DV is measured? A stronger version: "How does temperature (10–60°C) affect the rate of amylase breaking down starch (measured by iodine colour change time, seconds)?"

Medium
A student investigates how salt concentration affects osmosis. Write a fully specified MYP research question.
Show Answer

"How does NaCl concentration (0–2 mol/L) affect the percentage change in mass of potato cylinders (g%) after 30 minutes of immersion?"

Medium
What is the difference between a broad question and a focused question? Give an example of each on the same topic.
Show Answer

Broad: "How does pollution affect fish?" — too many variables. Focused: "How does nitrate concentration (0–50 ppm) in water affect the gill beat rate (beats/min) of zebrafish larvae?" — one IV, one measurable DV.

Medium
Explain how the scope of a research question affects the design of an experiment.
Show Answer

A narrowly scoped RQ with one IV and one DV makes it possible to control all other variables and draw valid cause-effect conclusions. A broad RQ would require too many simultaneous manipulations, making results uninterpretable.

Medium

Set C Hard — MYP Criteria B & D

A student's RQ is: "How does the amount of sunlight affect how fast a plant grows?" Score this against MYP Criterion B strand 1 and suggest improvements.
Show Answer

Score 1–2/4: IV ("amount of sunlight") is unquantified, DV ("grows") is unmeasured, no organism named. Improved: "How does daily light exposure duration (4/8/12/16 h) affect the stem elongation rate (mm/day) of Phaseolus vulgaris seedlings over 14 days?"

Hard
Evaluate: can a research question about human psychology (e.g., happiness) ever meet MYP Criterion B requirements? Justify.
Show Answer

Yes, if happiness is operationalised as a measurable proxy — e.g., self-reported score on a validated 10-point scale — and the IV is controlled. The key is measurability and reproducibility, not the topic itself.

Hard
A student wants to investigate the effect of caffeine on concentration. Design a research question and identify TWO ethical considerations that affect how it can be tested.
Show Answer

RQ: "How does caffeine dose (0/50/100 mg) affect reaction time (ms) in 14-year-old students on a computer task?" Ethical issues: (1) informed parental consent required for minors; (2) caffeine doses must be safe — high doses excluded.

Hard
Explain why a research question with two independent variables cannot yield valid conclusions in a standard MYP experiment.
Show Answer

Changing two IVs simultaneously means you cannot attribute any observed change in the DV to either IV specifically. Confounding variables invalidate cause-effect claims. MYP experiments require all variables constant except one.

Hard
A student investigates whether playing video games improves hand-eye coordination. Critique the research question and rewrite it to achieve maximum marks on Criterion B.
Show Answer

Critique: "playing video games" is unspecified (genre, duration, frequency); "improves" implies a value judgement; "hand-eye coordination" needs an operational measure. Rewrite: "How does daily action video game play duration (0/30/60/90 min/day) affect ball-catch success rate (%) in a standardised 20-trial test after 4 weeks?"

Hard
Describe how a poor research question leads to low scores on BOTH Criterion B (design) and Criterion D (evaluation).
Show Answer

A vague RQ produces an ill-controlled experiment (low B) and in Criterion D evaluation you cannot assess reliability or validity because there was no clear prediction to compare results against. Weaknesses cascade through the whole investigation.

Hard
A student's RQ: "Does the colour of light affect plant growth?" What range of IV values should be specified, and why does range matter?
Show Answer

Specify discrete colours with wavelength (e.g., red 660 nm, green 530 nm, blue 450 nm, white) and equal intensity (lux). Range matters because too narrow a range may show no effect; too wide may introduce uncontrolled factors. Specified levels allow quantitative trend analysis.

Hard
Compare the quality of these two RQs and explain which would score 7–8 on MYP Criterion B: (A) "How does pH affect enzymes?" (B) "How does pH (3/5/7/9/11) affect the rate of hydrogen peroxide decomposition (mL O₂/min) by catalase in potato discs at 25°C?"
Show Answer

(B) scores 7–8: IV has specific values (5 levels), DV is quantitative and unit-specified, enzyme named, substrate named, temperature controlled, organism stated. (A) scores 1–2: no values, no DV measurement, no context.

Hypothesis Format

Set A Easy — Recall & Recognition

Write the three-part structure of an MYP hypothesis.
Show Answer

"If [independent variable is changed in this way], then [dependent variable will change in this way], because [scientific reasoning/mechanism]."

Easy
Is "Plants need sunlight to grow" a hypothesis or a statement? Explain.
Show Answer

It is a general statement, not a hypothesis. A hypothesis must predict a specific measurable outcome and include scientific reasoning in if-then-because format.

Easy
What is the difference between a hypothesis and a prediction?
Show Answer

A hypothesis includes scientific reasoning (the "because" clause). A prediction states only what will happen. Hypothesis = prediction + mechanism.

Easy
Why must a hypothesis be testable and falsifiable?
Show Answer

If a hypothesis cannot be proven wrong by an experiment, it cannot be scientifically evaluated. Falsifiability is a core requirement of scientific statements (Popper's criterion).

Easy
Complete this hypothesis: "If the concentration of sugar solution increases, then the rate of yeast respiration will _______ because _______."
Show Answer

"…will increase because yeast cells have more substrate molecules (glucose) available for cellular respiration, increasing the frequency of enzyme-substrate collisions."

Easy
Identify the flaw: "If I water plants more, then they will grow better."
Show Answer

"Grow better" is not measurable. "Water more" is not quantified. No scientific reasoning is given. Replace with specific IV amount, measurable DV (height in cm), and a biological mechanism.

Easy
Why is the "because" clause the most important part of an MYP hypothesis?
Show Answer

It links the prediction to scientific knowledge, demonstrating understanding rather than guessing. It is what distinguishes a scientific hypothesis from a random prediction.

Easy
State whether this is a valid hypothesis: "If temperature increases, then enzyme activity will first increase then decrease because enzymes denature above their optimum temperature."
Show Answer

Valid — it predicts a specific trend (rise then fall), names the mechanism (denaturation), and is testable with measurable enzyme activity data.

Easy

Set B Medium — Application & Analysis

Write a full hypothesis for: "How does light intensity affect the rate of photosynthesis in Elodea?"
Show Answer

"If light intensity increases (from 1000 to 5000 lux), then the rate of oxygen production by Elodea will increase, because more photons are available to drive the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis, producing more ATP and NADPH."

Medium
A student hypothesises: "If I exercise more, then my heart rate will go up." Evaluate this against MYP criteria and improve it.
Show Answer

Issues: "exercise more" unquantified; no scientific reasoning. Improved: "If exercise intensity increases (walking/jogging/running at 5/8/12 km/h), then resting heart rate recovery time (min) will increase, because higher intensity demands greater cardiac output to supply muscles with oxygen."

Medium
Explain why a hypothesis should predict direction of change (e.g., "will increase"), not just "will change."
Show Answer

Stating direction demonstrates prior scientific knowledge and creates a specific, falsifiable claim. "Will change" is trivially true — the hypothesis must be precise enough to be proved wrong by data.

Medium
Write a hypothesis for an investigation into salt concentration and osmosis in potato discs.
Show Answer

"If NaCl concentration increases (0–2 mol/L), then the mass of potato discs will decrease, because the water potential outside the cell becomes more negative, causing water to move out of cells by osmosis down the water potential gradient."

Medium
How does the scientific reasoning in a hypothesis link to the mark scheme for MYP Criterion B strand 2?
Show Answer

Criterion B strand 2 awards marks for "formulating a testable hypothesis and explaining it." The "because" clause must reference a scientific mechanism (not just repeat the prediction) to achieve 7–8 marks.

Medium
Compare a null hypothesis with a directional hypothesis. When would you use each?
Show Answer

Null hypothesis: "There is no significant effect of X on Y." Used when testing whether any relationship exists. Directional: predicts the direction of effect. MYP typically expects directional hypotheses; null hypotheses appear more in statistical analyses at DP level.

Medium
A student hypothesises that caffeine improves memory. Write this in correct if-then-because format with appropriate scientific reasoning.
Show Answer

"If caffeine dose increases (0/50/100 mg), then the number of words recalled in a 20-word test will increase, because caffeine blocks adenosine receptors in the brain, reducing drowsiness and increasing neural activity in the hippocampus."

Medium
What happens to a hypothesis if the experimental results do not support it? Does this mean the experiment failed?
Show Answer

No — a rejected hypothesis is still scientifically valuable. It eliminates a possible explanation and guides future inquiry. Science advances equally from disconfirmed and confirmed hypotheses.

Medium

Set C Hard — MYP Criteria B & D

A student's hypothesis: "If I add more fertiliser, plants will grow more because fertiliser is good for plants." Score this for Criterion B and rewrite to achieve 7–8 marks.
Show Answer

Score 1–2: reasoning ("fertiliser is good") is circular. Rewrite: "If nitrate fertiliser concentration increases (0/2/4/6 g/L), then stem height after 3 weeks will increase, because nitrate ions are absorbed for amino acid synthesis, enabling faster protein production and cell growth."

Hard
Explain why a hypothesis must be written before data collection, and what the consequence is of writing it after seeing results.
Show Answer

Writing a hypothesis first ensures the experiment is designed to test it fairly. Writing it after results (HARKing — Hypothesising After Results are Known) is a form of scientific fraud that makes any result appear to "confirm" the hypothesis.

Hard
For the enzyme investigation below, write both a directional hypothesis and an alternative (null) hypothesis. RQ: "How does pH affect amylase activity?"
Show Answer

Directional: "If pH increases from 2 to 10, then amylase activity (measured by starch hydrolysis rate) will peak at pH 7 then decline, because deviations from amylase's optimum pH (7) alter the ionisation of active site residues, reducing enzyme-substrate binding." Null: "pH has no significant effect on amylase activity."

Hard
In Criterion D evaluation, a student must compare their results to their hypothesis. Describe what "results support the hypothesis" vs "results partially support" means with an example.
Show Answer

Full support: data matches predicted direction AND magnitude. Partial support: direction correct but magnitude differs (e.g., predicted 50% increase, observed 20%). Must cite specific data values. Example: "Hypothesis predicted increase — results showed increase from 5 to 8 mL O₂/min (60%), supporting the direction but exceeding predicted 30%."

Hard
A hypothesis predicts a linear relationship between IV and DV. Data shows a curve. How should the student handle this in Criterion D?
Show Answer

State that the hypothesis is partially supported: the direction of initial increase matches, but the non-linear trend was not predicted. Suggest the mechanism is more complex (e.g., rate-limiting steps, enzyme saturation). Propose a new, revised hypothesis for future investigation.

Hard
Why is "because it is common knowledge" never acceptable as the scientific reasoning in an MYP hypothesis?
Show Answer

It cites no mechanism. MYP Criterion B requires reference to a specific scientific principle, process, or concept (e.g., collision theory, osmosis, enzyme kinetics) that causally connects the IV to the DV.

Hard
Compare these two hypotheses and explain which demonstrates higher-order scientific thinking: (A) "If temperature rises, enzyme activity increases because molecules move faster." (B) "If temperature rises from 10 to 60°C, catalase activity (O₂ mL/min) will increase to a peak around 37°C then decrease sharply, because above the optimum temperature hydrogen bonds in the enzyme's tertiary structure break, permanently altering the active site shape."
Show Answer

(B) is superior: specifies a range, names the enzyme and measurable DV, predicts the shape of the relationship (peak then fall), explains the molecular mechanism (hydrogen bond disruption, tertiary structure, active site). (A) is oversimplified and incomplete.

Hard
A student's experiment disproves their hypothesis. Write a model Criterion D response explaining what this means for their hypothesis and what they would do next.
Show Answer

"My hypothesis stated that increasing NaCl concentration would increase yeast respiration rate. However, data showed a decrease above 1 mol/L (from 45 to 12 bubbles/min), disproving the hypothesis. The high osmotic concentration likely caused plasmolysis of yeast cells, reducing enzyme activity. I would revise my hypothesis to predict an optimal salt concentration and investigate the mechanism of osmotic stress on cellular enzymes."

Set A Easy — Recall & Recognition

Define independent variable (IV).
Show Answer

The ONE variable deliberately changed by the experimenter to investigate its effect on the dependent variable. All other variables are kept constant.

Easy
Define dependent variable (DV).
Show Answer

The variable that is measured or observed in response to changes in the IV. It "depends on" what the experimenter does to the IV.

Easy
What are controlled variables (CVs)? Give two examples for a photosynthesis experiment.
Show Answer

Variables kept constant to ensure only the IV affects the DV. Examples: temperature (25°C), CO₂ concentration (0.04%), same plant species, same water volume.

Easy
What is a control group? How does it differ from a controlled variable?
Show Answer

A control group receives no treatment (IV = 0 or baseline) to provide a baseline for comparison. A controlled variable is any variable held constant throughout all trials including the control.

Easy
In an experiment testing how fertiliser concentration affects plant height, identify the IV, DV, and two CVs.
Show Answer

IV: fertiliser concentration (g/L). DV: plant height (cm) after a fixed period. CVs: same plant species, same soil volume, same watering amount, same light conditions, same temperature.

Easy
Why can you only change ONE independent variable in a fair test?
Show Answer

If two variables change simultaneously, you cannot determine which one caused the change in the DV. Changing one IV at a time allows valid cause-effect conclusions.

Easy
A student changes both temperature and pH in the same enzyme experiment. What is the problem?
Show Answer

This makes it a confounded experiment — any change in enzyme activity could be due to temperature, pH, or their interaction. You cannot attribute the result to either variable alone. Only one IV should change at a time.

Easy
Is time always a dependent variable? Explain with an example.
Show Answer

No. Time can be an IV (e.g., exposure duration) or a CV (e.g., all reactions run for exactly 5 minutes). It is a DV only when you measure how long something takes (e.g., time for magnesium to dissolve).

Easy

Set B Medium — Application & Analysis

A student tests how pH affects catalase activity. List FIVE controlled variables and explain why each must be held constant.
Show Answer

1) Temperature — affects enzyme kinetics independently. 2) Hydrogen peroxide concentration — substrate amount affects rate. 3) Enzyme (catalase) concentration — more enzyme = faster rate regardless of pH. 4) Volume of reaction mixture — affects total product amount. 5) Same potato disc size — ensures equal enzyme amount per trial.

Medium
Explain the role of a control group in an antibiotic experiment using agar plates.
Show Answer

A control plate has a disc soaked in solvent only (no antibiotic). This shows any inhibition zone is caused by the antibiotic, not the solvent. It also confirms bacteria are alive and growing normally, validating the experimental results.

Medium
In osmosis experiments, a student uses potato cylinders of different sizes. Which type of variable is this, and what problem does it cause?
Show Answer

This is an uncontrolled variable (should be a CV). Different sizes have different surface area-to-volume ratios, affecting the rate of osmosis. Results across concentrations become incomparable, reducing validity.

Medium
Design a variable table for an experiment testing how sugar concentration affects fermentation rate in yeast.
Show Answer

IV: glucose concentration (0/5/10/15/20 g/100mL). DV: CO₂ produced (mL/min, measured by gas syringe). CVs: yeast mass (2g), water temperature (35°C), total solution volume (50mL), time measured (10 min), same yeast strain.

Medium
What is an extraneous variable? Give an example that could affect a plant growth experiment.
Show Answer

Any variable not intentionally changed that could influence the DV. Example: draughts near the window affecting CO₂ availability; a student knocking over one pot causing irregular watering; different soil batches with varying nutrient content.

Medium
A student claims their experiment is "fair" because they used the same equipment. Is this sufficient? Explain.
Show Answer

No. Using the same equipment only controls for equipment-related variation. A fair test also requires the same conditions (temperature, time, concentration) for all trials and the same method applied identically. Equipment alone does not ensure all CVs are controlled.

Medium
Distinguish between a confounding variable and a controlled variable.
Show Answer

A controlled variable is one that the experimenter deliberately holds constant. A confounding variable is one that was not controlled and varies alongside the IV, potentially causing false relationships between IV and DV.

Medium
Why is a control group not the same as a "control experiment"?
Show Answer

A control group is one set of trials within the same experiment where the IV = 0/baseline. A "control experiment" sometimes loosely means the whole baseline experiment. MYP requires precision: the control group is a specific treatment group (e.g., 0 mg/L antibiotic) in a single designed experiment.

Medium

Set C Hard — MYP Criteria B & D

A student's data shows an unusual outlier in trial 2 of 5 for one IV value. Should they: (a) remove it, (b) keep it, (c) repeat the trial? Justify in the context of controlled variables.
Show Answer

(c) Repeat the trial. Check if a CV was accidentally altered (e.g., temperature spike, different substrate batch). If a specific CV breach is identified, discard and replace. If not, keep all data and report the outlier with reasoning in evaluation. Removing without justification is dishonest.

Hard
Evaluate this experimental design: "I tested 3 plants at each fertiliser level (0/5/10 g/L) but used a different plant pot for each." What variable control issue exists?
Show Answer

Different pots may have different soil volumes, drainage rates, or residual nutrients — all uncontrolled variables. Should use identical pots from the same batch, filled with the same mass of the same soil, prepared identically to ensure soil properties are a controlled variable.

Hard
A student says "I controlled temperature by doing all experiments on the same day." Critique this approach.
Show Answer

Room temperature varies throughout a day (morning cooler, afternoon warmer). "Same day" does not ensure constant temperature. A water bath set to a specific temperature (e.g., 25°C ± 0.5°C) is required. This is a systematic error — consistently uncontrolled CV affecting all trials.

Hard
Design a full variable analysis table for an osmosis experiment achieving MYP Criterion B 7–8 marks. Include IV (with range and units), DV (with method of measurement and units), and at least 5 CVs with values.
Show Answer

IV: NaCl concentration (0 / 0.5 / 1.0 / 1.5 / 2.0 mol/L). DV: % change in mass of potato cylinders (±0.01g balance, calculate [(final−initial)/initial]×100%). CVs: cylinder dimensions (3 cm length, 1 cm diameter, cut with cork borer and ruler); same potato variety; immersion time (30 min); solution volume (30 mL in each beaker); room temperature (25°C, monitored with thermometer).

Hard
Explain why a negative control and a positive control serve different purposes. Illustrate with an antibiotic experiment.
Show Answer

Negative control (disc in water only): shows bacteria grow normally — no natural inhibition zones. Positive control (known antibiotic like ampicillin): confirms bacteria are susceptible to antibiotics generally. Together they validate that any inhibition zone for the test antibiotic is real and meaningful.

Hard
In Criterion D, a student writes "my experiment was unfair because I could not control all variables." Improve this evaluation to achieve 7–8 marks.
Show Answer

Improvement: "A potential confounding variable was room temperature, which fluctuated between 21–24°C during trials (measured with a thermometer). Since enzyme activity is temperature-sensitive, this could have caused the larger-than-expected variation in trial 3 (reaction rate 15% higher). Future improvement: use a thermostatic water bath set to 25°C ± 0.5°C for all trials."

Hard
Why is having only one IV change at a time described as "the fundamental rule of experimental science"?
Show Answer

It is the only way to establish a causal (not merely correlational) relationship. If two variables change, any observed effect could result from either, their interaction, or confounding. The entire logic of controlled experimentation rests on isolating one variable at a time.

Hard
A student claims that using the same beaker throughout an experiment is a controlled variable. Is this correct? Explain.
Show Answer

Only partially. Using the same beaker controls for equipment differences, but may introduce contamination between trials (residual reagents). Proper CV control requires cleaning and rinsing beakers between trials, or using separate identical beakers. The key is that the equipment properties — not just equipment identity — are controlled.

Set A Easy — Recall & Recognition

Define qualitative data and give two examples from a biology experiment.
Show Answer

Qualitative data describes non-numerical observations. Examples: colour change of iodine from yellow-brown to blue-black; presence or absence of a precipitate; organism behaviour (swimming vs still).

Easy
Define quantitative data and give two examples.
Show Answer

Quantitative data is numerical and measurable. Examples: temperature in °C; plant height in cm; mass in g; reaction time in seconds; pH reading.

Easy
Why should units be placed in the column header of a data table, not next to each value?
Show Answer

It avoids repetition, improves readability, and follows scientific convention. Writing "cm" in every cell adds clutter; placing it once in the header (e.g., "Height / cm") is clear and efficient.

Easy
What is the minimum number of repeats required in MYP experiments to demonstrate reliability, and why?
Show Answer

Minimum 3 repeats per IV level. With 3 values you can identify outliers, calculate a mean, and assess whether variation is random or systematic. Fewer repeats make anomalies indistinguishable from real results.

Easy
What is the difference between accuracy and precision in data collection?
Show Answer

Accuracy: how close a measurement is to the true value. Precision: how consistent repeated measurements are with each other. Precise but inaccurate = clustered around wrong value. Accurate and precise = clustered around true value.

Easy
A student records temperature as "about 37 degrees." Identify TWO problems with this.
Show Answer

1) "About" is imprecise — exact value not recorded. 2) No unit symbol (should be °C). Scientific data must use precise values with correct SI units.

Easy
Describe how to set up a raw data table for an experiment testing 5 concentrations with 3 repeats each.
Show Answer

Table has: Column 1 = IV with unit (e.g., "Concentration / mol/L"); Columns 2–4 = Trial 1, Trial 2, Trial 3 with DV and unit in header; Column 5 = Mean (same unit). 5 rows for each concentration value.

Easy
Why is qualitative data alone insufficient for an MYP Criterion C investigation?
Show Answer

Criterion C requires processing data: calculating means, identifying trends, constructing graphs. Qualitative observations cannot be graphed or statistically processed, so quantitative measurements are essential for full marks.

Easy

Set B Medium — Application & Analysis

A student records enzyme activity as: Trial 1 = 12, Trial 2 = 13, Trial 3 = 25. Should they include Trial 3 in their mean? Justify.
Show Answer

Trial 3 is a likely outlier (nearly double the others). Check experimental notes for errors (e.g., wrong concentration). If an error is identified, exclude it and repeat. If not, report the mean with and without it, noting the anomaly in evaluation with possible causes.

Medium
Design a complete data table for measuring the effect of temperature (20/30/40/50/60°C) on enzyme activity (mL O₂/min, 3 repeats).
Show Answer

Headers: Temperature / °C | Trial 1 / mL O₂ min⁻¹ | Trial 2 / mL O₂ min⁻¹ | Trial 3 / mL O₂ min⁻¹ | Mean / mL O₂ min⁻¹. Five rows: 20, 30, 40, 50, 60. Values entered during experiment; mean calculated as (T1+T2+T3)/3.

Medium
Explain the difference between resolution and precision of a measuring instrument. Give examples.
Show Answer

Resolution: the smallest increment a device can display (e.g., ruler 1mm, digital balance 0.01g). Precision: the consistency of repeated readings with the same instrument. A ruler can be precise (same reading each time) but have low resolution (±0.5mm). For MYP, record to the instrument's resolution.

Medium
A student uses a colour change from blue to colourless to measure starch digestion. How could they make this qualitative observation quantitative?
Show Answer

Use a colorimeter to measure light absorbance (arbitrary units) at 580 nm — the absorbance of the iodine-starch complex decreases as starch is digested. Alternatively, measure time (seconds) for the colour to disappear as a proxy for rate.

Medium
Why should raw data never be altered, even if some values appear wrong?
Show Answer

Raw data is the unprocessed record of what was observed. Altering it is scientific misconduct. Anomalous values should be noted and justified in the evaluation, not erased. Original data must always be preserved.

Medium
How does increasing repeats from 3 to 5 per IV level affect the reliability of results?
Show Answer

More repeats reduce the influence of random errors on the mean, producing a more reliable average. With 5 repeats, one outlier has less effect on the mean (shifts it less). It also allows calculation of standard deviation to show variability.

Medium
A student records observations in a notebook as free text rather than a table. What are TWO problems with this approach?
Show Answer

1) Difficult to compare values across conditions and identify trends. 2) Easy to accidentally omit or duplicate readings. Tables ensure systematic, complete recording with labels and units visible for every entry.

Medium
What does "uncertainty" mean when recording a measurement, and how is it expressed?
Show Answer

Uncertainty reflects the range within which the true value lies, based on instrument resolution. Expressed as ± half the smallest division. Example: ruler with 1mm divisions → record as 34 ± 0.5 mm. Appears in column headers or as subscript notes in tables.

Medium

Set C Hard — MYP Criteria B & D

Evaluate this data table: "pH | Result." Identify all formatting errors and redesign it for Criterion C.
Show Answer

Errors: no units for pH (correct: pH is dimensionless but note that); "Result" is vague (no DV name or unit); no repeat columns; no mean column. Redesign: pH (no unit needed) | Reaction rate / mL O₂ min⁻¹ (Trial 1) | Trial 2 | Trial 3 | Mean. Units in headers, specific DV named.

Hard
A student collects data for 5 IV values but only 1 repeat for each. Explain the impact on Criterion B (design) AND Criterion D (evaluation) marks.
Show Answer

Criterion B: low marks — design does not ensure reliability; MYP requires ≥3 repeats. Criterion D: cannot assess reliability, cannot identify outliers, cannot calculate meaningful uncertainty. Both criteria penalise absence of repeats heavily.

Hard
Explain how a systematic error in data collection differs from random error, with a concrete example from a titration experiment.
Show Answer

Systematic: consistent offset in all readings — e.g., always reading the burette at the top of the meniscus instead of the bottom gives every titre value 0.2mL too high. Random: unpredictable — e.g., end-point colour varies due to slight differences in how fast the student adds drops; affects individual readings but averages out with more repeats.

Hard
Why is it insufficient to record data to fewer decimal places than the instrument allows? Give an example.
Show Answer

It discards information and misrepresents precision. Example: a digital balance reads 2.34 g; recording "2 g" loses 0.34 g of precision. MYP Criterion C requires data that accurately reflects instrument resolution — record to the last reliable digit the device displays.

Hard
A student uses a stopwatch to time a reaction. They also qualitatively describe "the solution became cloudy." How should they incorporate both types of data in a Criterion C table?
Show Answer

Primary quantitative table: Time to cloudiness / s, with repeats and mean. Secondary qualitative column (or separate table): "Observations" noting cloud intensity on a 0–3 scale (turning semi-quantitative) or simply as descriptive notes. Reference qualitative data in analysis to support interpretation of quantitative trends.

Hard
Design a processed data table (means and % change) from a raw osmosis dataset where potato mass increased for low concentrations and decreased for high ones.
Show Answer

Columns: NaCl / mol/L | Initial mass / g | Final mass / g | Change in mass / g | % change in mass. Row for each concentration. % change = [(final−initial)/initial] × 100%. Negative values indicate mass loss (water out); positive = mass gain (water in). This processed table is what gets graphed.

Hard
Explain why the mean of 3 repeats is more reliable than any single trial value.
Show Answer

Random errors in individual trials (over-readings and under-readings) partially cancel when averaged. The mean is a better estimate of the true value than any single measurement, because systematic errors affect all trials equally but random errors are reduced by averaging.

Hard
A student notices their Trial 1 values are consistently 15% higher than Trials 2 and 3 across all IV levels. What type of error is this, and what is its most likely cause?
Show Answer

This is a systematic error specific to Trial 1 — most likely the experimental conditions changed after the first trial (e.g., reagent warmed up, enzyme became more active, or a CV like temperature was not re-equilibrated between Trial 1 and the others). It is not random because it is consistent across all IV levels in one direction.

Graphing and Analysis

Set A Easy — Recall & Recognition

Which variable goes on the x-axis and which on the y-axis? State the rule.
Show Answer

IV on the x-axis (horizontal); DV on the y-axis (vertical). Memory aid: "DRY MIX" — Dependent on Y, Responding; Manipulated (Independent) on X.

Easy
When should you draw a line of best fit rather than connect points dot-to-dot?
Show Answer

Always for continuous data (e.g., temperature vs rate). A line of best fit shows the underlying trend, smoothing out random measurement error. Dot-to-dot implies every zigzag is real and meaningful, which it usually is not.

Easy
What must every axis of a scientific graph include?
Show Answer

1) Variable name; 2) Unit in brackets or after a slash (e.g., "Temperature / °C"); 3) Evenly spaced scale that starts at an appropriate value (not necessarily zero if data doesn't require it).

Easy
What is the difference between a line graph and a bar graph? When is each appropriate?
Show Answer

Line graph: IV is continuous/numerical (e.g., temperature, concentration). Bar graph: IV is categorical/discrete (e.g., species, type of fertiliser). Using a line for categorical data implies a false continuity between categories.

Easy
What does it mean to "describe a trend" in graph analysis?
Show Answer

State the direction of change (increases/decreases/stays constant), the shape (linear, exponential, peak then decline), and reference specific data values from the graph to support the description.

Easy
A student draws a graph with the y-axis starting at 50 instead of 0. What problem could this cause?
Show Answer

It can exaggerate the visual appearance of changes, making small differences look large. However, starting at 0 is not always required — if all values are between 50–100, a truncated axis is acceptable as long as it is clearly labelled. Must not be misleading.

Easy
What is an anomalous point, and how should it be shown on a graph?
Show Answer

A data point that falls far from the trend of other points (likely due to error). It should still be plotted, but the line of best fit should ignore it. Circle or annotate it on the graph and explain it in the evaluation.

Easy
State what the gradient (slope) of a line graph represents in a scientific context.
Show Answer

The rate of change of the DV per unit change in the IV. Example: gradient of a distance-time graph = speed (m/s). In an enzyme experiment: gradient of O₂ produced vs time = reaction rate (mL/min).

Easy

Set B Medium — Application & Analysis

A graph of temperature vs enzyme activity shows values: (10°C, 2 mL/min), (20°C, 5), (30°C, 9), (40°C, 12), (50°C, 4), (60°C, 1). Describe the trend using specific values.
Show Answer

"Enzyme activity increased from 2 mL/min at 10°C to a peak of 12 mL/min at 40°C, then decreased sharply to 1 mL/min at 60°C. The increase from 10–40°C was approximately linear (+3.3 mL/min per 10°C), while the decrease above 40°C was steeper, suggesting enzyme denaturation."

Medium
Explain why a best-fit curve is used instead of a straight line for enzyme activity vs temperature data.
Show Answer

The relationship is non-linear — activity rises then falls (bell-curve shape). A straight line cannot represent this relationship. A smooth curve through the general trend of points is more accurate and reflects the underlying biology (optimum temperature, denaturation).

Medium
How do you calculate the gradient of a line of best fit? Show the method step by step.
Show Answer

1) Choose two points on the LINE (not data points) that are far apart. 2) Gradient = (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁). 3) Include units: (y-unit)/(x-unit). Example: (14−2)/(30−10) = 12/20 = 0.6 mL·min⁻¹·°C⁻¹.

Medium
In osmosis data, the % change in mass goes from +15% at 0 mol/L to −20% at 2 mol/L. The line crosses zero at 0.8 mol/L. What does the zero-crossing represent?
Show Answer

The isotonic point — where the solution concentration equals the water potential of the potato cells. No net movement of water occurs; the cell neither gains nor loses mass. This is the point of osmotic equilibrium.

Medium
A student plots a scatter graph with a positive correlation. Explain what "positive correlation" means and give a biological example.
Show Answer

As the IV increases, the DV also increases. Example: as light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis (O₂ mL/min) increases — provided light is the limiting factor. Correlation does not prove causation without controlled experiment.

Medium
What information must a graph title include to be complete for MYP Criterion C?
Show Answer

Title must state: "The effect of [IV with unit] on [DV with unit]" — e.g., "The effect of NaCl concentration (mol/L) on the percentage change in mass (%) of potato cylinders." Axis labels with units, plus a descriptive title, are both required.

Medium
Explain the difference between interpolation and extrapolation when reading values from a graph.
Show Answer

Interpolation: reading a value within the range of data plotted — more reliable because it stays within tested conditions. Extrapolation: reading beyond the plotted data range — less reliable, because the trend may not continue (e.g., enzyme would not keep increasing beyond 40°C).

Medium
A student draws error bars on their graph. What do error bars represent, and what does it mean when error bars overlap?
Show Answer

Error bars show the range or standard deviation of repeat values, indicating variability/uncertainty. Overlapping error bars suggest the difference between two means may not be statistically significant — the variation could explain the apparent difference.

Medium

Set C Hard — MYP Criteria B & D

A student draws a line graph but connects the data points with straight lines. Critique this for Criterion C and explain what graph is more appropriate and why.
Show Answer

Straight lines between points imply each measurement is perfectly accurate with no error. Criterion C requires a smooth best-fit curve (or best-fit line for linear data) that represents the underlying trend. The straight-line approach also obscures the shape of the relationship (e.g., a peak is misleadingly rendered as a sharp angle).

Hard
A student's graph shows a clear positive trend in 4 of 5 IV values, but one point is far off the trend. How should the analysis address this?
Show Answer

Describe the overall positive trend with specific values. Then note: "The value at [X=...] of [DV=...] is anomalous — it deviates from the trend by [amount]. This may be due to [experimental error, e.g., contamination, incorrect concentration]. The line of best fit excludes this point." Reference it again in Criterion D evaluation.

Hard
Compare the scientific information conveyed by (A) a bar chart and (B) a scatter plot with best-fit line for the same enzyme data (temperature vs rate). Which is more appropriate and why?
Show Answer

(B) scatter plot is more appropriate. Temperature is continuous (not categorical), so a line showing the trend is more informative — it reveals the shape (optimum, denaturation), allows gradient calculation, and shows interpolated values. A bar chart implies each temperature is an isolated category and cannot show a continuous biological relationship.

Hard
Explain how to use a graph to calculate the rate of reaction at a specific time point on a curved progress curve (product vs time).
Show Answer

Draw a tangent to the curve at the desired time point (a straight line that just touches the curve at that point without crossing it). Calculate the gradient of the tangent: Δy/Δx = (change in product mL)/(change in time s). This gives the instantaneous rate at that moment.

Hard
A student's graph shows the DV decreasing as IV increases. Write a full Criterion C analysis paragraph referencing specific data values.
Show Answer

Example: "As NaCl concentration increased from 0 to 2 mol/L, the percentage change in potato mass decreased from +14% to −18%. The relationship was approximately linear (gradient ≈ −16% per mol/L, calculated from the best-fit line). This indicates that as osmotic concentration increases, greater water loss from cells occurs, supporting the hypothesis. The point at 1.5 mol/L (−12%) falls below the trend, possibly due to measurement error."

Hard
Why can two variables have a strong correlation (r = 0.95) but the IV does not cause the DV? Give a biological example.
Show Answer

Correlation only shows the variables change together; it does not prove causation. Both could be caused by a third variable. Example: shoe size and reading ability correlate in children because both increase with age — age is the confounding variable. Only a controlled experiment (changing one IV, all else constant) establishes causation.

Hard
A student's graph has a line of best fit that does not pass through the origin, even though theory predicts it should. Explain what this implies about their data.
Show Answer

A non-zero y-intercept suggests a systematic error: all measurements are offset by a constant amount (e.g., the balance was not zeroed, the stopwatch was started late, or background absorbance was not subtracted). This shifts the whole line but does not change its gradient/trend. Should be identified and discussed in Criterion D.

Hard
Describe what a "plateau" in a graph means in terms of limiting factors, using photosynthesis as an example.
Show Answer

A plateau indicates the DV stops increasing despite further IV increases — another factor is now limiting. In photosynthesis: as light intensity increases, O₂ production rises, but levels off when CO₂ concentration or temperature becomes limiting. The plateau shows the original IV is no longer the rate-determining factor.

Set A Easy — Recall & Recognition

Define systematic error and give one example.
Show Answer

A consistent, repeatable error that affects all measurements in the same direction by roughly the same amount. Example: a balance showing +0.5g on every reading because it was not zeroed — every mass measurement is 0.5g too high.

Easy
Define random error and give one example.
Show Answer

An unpredictable error that causes measurements to vary up and down around the true value. Example: slightly different reaction times when pressing a stopwatch each trial; parallax error when reading a meniscus from different angles each time.

Easy
Why is "my results were inaccurate because of human error" not an acceptable evaluation comment?
Show Answer

It is too vague. MYP Criterion D requires specific identification: which human action caused error, how it affected the DV (direction), how large the effect was, and a specific improvement to address it.

Easy
What is the difference between reliability and validity in an experiment?
Show Answer

Reliability: consistency of results — same results when repeated. Validity: whether the experiment actually measures what it claims to measure (no confounding variables; correct method for the DV). Can have reliable results that are not valid (e.g., consistently measuring the wrong thing).

Easy
A student suggests "I could improve my experiment by being more careful." Why is this insufficient for Criterion D?
Show Answer

It identifies no specific weakness and proposes no concrete change. A valid improvement must state: the specific error (e.g., "meniscus read incorrectly"), the method change (e.g., "use a digital burette"), and the expected outcome (e.g., "reduce titre uncertainty from ±0.5 mL to ±0.05 mL").

Easy
Can systematic errors be reduced by taking more repeats? Explain.
Show Answer

No. More repeats reduce random errors (which average out) but not systematic errors (which affect every measurement equally in the same direction). Systematic errors require a change in the method or instrument calibration.

Easy
What does it mean to say results "support" a hypothesis? What is the criterion?
Show Answer

Results support a hypothesis if the observed trend matches the predicted direction. For full support, the data should match both direction and approximate magnitude. Always reference specific data values when making this claim.

Easy
List the three main components of a high-scoring MYP Criterion D evaluation.
Show Answer

1) Compare results to hypothesis (supported/partially/not supported, with data values). 2) Identify specific weaknesses/errors (systematic or random, with effect on DV). 3) Propose specific, realistic improvements that would address each weakness.

Easy

Set B Medium — Application & Analysis

A student finds their enzyme activity results were consistently 20% higher than published literature values. Identify the type of error and suggest a specific cause.
Show Answer

Systematic error (consistent overestimate). Possible cause: enzyme concentration higher than stated (inaccurate dilution), or temperature slightly above 37°C (water bath not calibrated). All trials affected in the same direction.

Medium
Write a specific improvement for this weakness: "The cork borer may have cut potato cylinders at slightly different diameters."
Show Answer

"Use a single size-6 cork borer throughout and measure each cylinder's diameter with digital calipers before use. Exclude any cylinder deviating more than ±0.1 mm from the target. This reduces variation in surface area and enzyme content between cylinders, improving reliability."

Medium
Explain how the use of a water bath instead of room temperature reduces a specific type of error in enzyme experiments.
Show Answer

It eliminates temperature fluctuations (an extraneous variable/systematic error source). Room temperature varies ±3–4°C during an experiment; a thermostatically controlled water bath maintains ±0.5°C. Since enzyme activity is temperature-sensitive (~Q₁₀ = 2 per 10°C), uncontrolled temperature causes significant systematic error in rate measurements.

Medium
A student's results show high variability (large spread between repeats). Is this a random or systematic error, and how can it be addressed?
Show Answer

High variability between repeats = random error. Address by: increasing number of repeats (5 instead of 3), improving technique consistency (same person performs all measurements), using more precise equipment (digital vs analogue timer), and identifying and controlling sources of variation in the method.

Medium
Distinguish between a limitation and an improvement. Why must every improvement directly address a stated limitation?
Show Answer

A limitation identifies a specific weakness in the method or data. An improvement is a concrete change that would eliminate or reduce that specific weakness. They must be paired — an improvement that doesn't address the stated limitation is irrelevant and scores no marks in MYP Criterion D.

Medium
Explain why "I would do more repeats" is only partially valid as an improvement.
Show Answer

It addresses random error but not systematic error or design flaws. It must specify: how many more repeats (e.g., from 3 to 5), why (to reduce effect of outliers on mean), and should be accompanied by improvements targeting any systematic errors identified in the evaluation.

Medium
A student concludes "my experiment was reliable because I got the same result three times." Evaluate this claim.
Show Answer

Only partially valid. Three identical results suggests reliability (consistency), but if all three are affected by the same systematic error, they are reliably wrong. Reliability must be assessed alongside validity: are the results also measuring the correct thing in an unbiased way?

Medium
In a fermentation experiment, CO₂ was collected using a balloon over a conical flask. Identify ONE source of systematic error specific to this method and suggest an improvement.
Show Answer

Systematic error: CO₂ dissolves in the liquid before reaching the balloon, so collected volume underestimates actual CO₂ produced. Improvement: use an inverted measuring cylinder over a water trough (water displacement method) to collect gas more completely, or use a gas syringe with minimal dead volume.

Medium

Set C Hard — MYP Criteria B & D

Write a complete Criterion D evaluation paragraph for an enzyme experiment where results partially supported the hypothesis (activity peaked at 40°C rather than predicted 37°C).
Show Answer

"The hypothesis predicted peak enzyme activity at 37°C; results showed peak at 40°C (12 mL O₂/min vs 9 mL O₂/min at 37°C), providing partial support — direction was correct (activity increased then decreased) but the optimum temperature was higher than predicted. A possible systematic error: the water bath was calibrated to ±2°C, so actual temperature may have been 38–42°C. Improvement: use a precision water bath (±0.1°C) and confirm temperature with a calibrated thermometer at the flask, not the bath. This would allow more accurate determination of optimum temperature."

Hard
Explain why "my results do not support my hypothesis, so my experiment failed" is incorrect scientific reasoning.
Show Answer

A disconfirmed hypothesis is a valid scientific result. It eliminates a possible explanation and provides new information. The experiment "failed" only if the method was so flawed that no valid conclusions can be drawn. Failure to support the hypothesis can be equally valuable — it prompts revision of the hypothesis and deeper understanding of the mechanism.

Hard
A student identifies three weaknesses but all three improvements say "use better equipment." Why does this score poorly and how should it be improved?
Show Answer

"Better equipment" is vague and addresses all errors generically. Each improvement must be specific: name the exact equipment (e.g., "replace the 50mL measuring cylinder with a 10mL burette"), explain how it reduces the specific error (e.g., "reduces volume uncertainty from ±1mL to ±0.05mL"), and link to the specific weakness identified.

Hard
Distinguish between extending the investigation and improving it. Give an example of each for an osmosis experiment.
Show Answer

Improvement: change the current method to fix a flaw (e.g., "cut all potato cylinders using a calibrated die to ensure uniform size"). Extension: keep the method but explore a new variable (e.g., "test with different plant tissues — carrot, turnip — to compare osmotic water potentials"). Criterion D rewards both; they must be explicitly labelled to receive separate marks.

Hard
A student's osmosis data shows a strong linear trend (r = 0.98) but one data point at 1.0 mol/L is a large outlier. Write a model evaluation of this anomaly.
Show Answer

"The point at 1.0 mol/L (+2% change) deviates significantly from the trend (expected: −8%, based on extrapolation). This is likely due to an error in preparing the 1.0 mol/L solution (possible cross-contamination or dilution mistake), or the potato cylinder was not properly blotted dry, adding surface water to its mass. Improvement: prepare all solutions fresh for each trial using a calibrated volumetric flask, and standardise blotting procedure — 3 presses on filter paper for each cylinder."

Hard
How do you determine whether a weakness is "major" or "minor" when writing an evaluation? Why does the distinction matter for Criterion D marks?
Show Answer

A major weakness significantly affects the DV and/or makes the conclusion unreliable. A minor weakness has small, quantifiable impact. To distinguish: estimate the magnitude of the error (e.g., "temperature varied by ±3°C, which could change enzyme rate by ~6% based on Q₁₀"). Criterion D awards higher marks when students prioritise weaknesses by their impact on results, showing deeper analytical thinking.

Hard
A student evaluates: "My results support my hypothesis because the graph shows an increase." Why does this score only 1–2 on Criterion D and how should it be improved?
Show Answer

Score 1–2: no specific data values cited, no comparison with predicted values, no assessment of reliability or limitations. Improved: "Results support the hypothesis: activity increased from 2 mL/min at 10°C to 12 mL/min at 40°C, matching the predicted increase. However, the hypothesis predicted peak activity at 37°C; the actual peak was at 40°C, suggesting partial support. The spread of repeat values at 40°C (10–14 mL/min) indicates moderate random error from manual timing."

Hard
Explain the phrase "the conclusion is only valid within the range of data collected." What are the implications for extrapolation?
Show Answer

Conclusions apply to the tested range of IV values only. If you test 10–60°C, you cannot validly conclude what happens at 70°C — the trend may change (e.g., complete denaturation and zero activity). Extrapolation outside the data range is speculation, not a scientific conclusion, and must be clearly labelled as a prediction requiring further experimentation.
