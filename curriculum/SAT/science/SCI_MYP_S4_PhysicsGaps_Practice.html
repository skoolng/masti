SET A Easy – Wave Fundamentals

Define amplitude of a wave and state what physical quantity it determines.Easy
Show answer

Amplitude is the maximum displacement of a particle from its rest (equilibrium) position. It determines the energy carried by the wave — greater amplitude means more energy.

State the equation linking wave speed, frequency, and wavelength.Easy
Show answer

v = fλ, where v = wave speed (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m).

What is the period of a wave, and how is it related to frequency?Easy
Show answer

Period (T) is the time for one complete oscillation. T = 1/f; e.g., a 50 Hz wave has T = 0.02 s.

Distinguish between a transverse and a longitudinal wave with one example of each.Easy
Show answer

Transverse: particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of wave travel (e.g., light, a rope wave). Longitudinal: particles vibrate parallel to wave travel (e.g., sound).

A wave has a frequency of 200 Hz and a wavelength of 1.5 m. Calculate its speed.Easy
Show answer

v = fλ = 200 × 1.5 = 300 m/s.

What are the compressions and rarefactions in a longitudinal wave?Easy
Show answer

Compressions: regions where particles are pushed close together (high pressure). Rarefactions: regions where particles are spread apart (low pressure).

Do waves transfer matter or energy? Explain briefly.Easy
Show answer

Waves transfer energy, not matter. The particles of the medium oscillate about fixed positions; they do not travel with the wave.

On a wave diagram, identify where the crests and troughs are located.Easy
Show answer

Crests are the highest points (maximum positive displacement); troughs are the lowest points (maximum negative displacement) of the wave.

Define wavelength. In what unit is it measured?
Show Answer

Wavelength (λ) is the distance between two consecutive points in phase (e.g., crest to crest or trough to trough). Unit: metres (m), though nm or cm are used for small wavelengths.

Easy
A wave has period T = 0.25 s. Calculate its frequency.
Show Answer

f = 1/T = 1/0.25 = 4 Hz.

SET B Medium – Applying Wave Equations

Sound travels at 340 m/s in air. A note has a frequency of 440 Hz. Calculate its wavelength.Medium
Show answer

λ = v/f = 340/440 ≈ 0.77 m.

Explain why increasing the frequency of a wave (at constant speed) decreases its wavelength.Medium
Show answer

From v = fλ, if v is constant and f increases, λ must decrease proportionally to keep their product equal to v.

A wave has a period of 0.025 s. What is its frequency?Medium
Show answer

f = 1/T = 1/0.025 = 40 Hz.

Compare how energy is transmitted in a transverse versus a longitudinal wave, using the medium's particle motion.Medium
Show answer

In both types, energy passes from particle to particle via vibration. In transverse waves the vibration is sideways (e.g., rope), while in longitudinal waves particles push and pull each other along the direction of travel (e.g., spring).

A radio wave has a wavelength of 3 m. Given c = 3×10⁸ m/s, calculate its frequency.Medium
Show answer

f = v/λ = (3×10⁸)/3 = 1×10⁸ Hz (100 MHz).

Describe what happens to the amplitude and frequency of a wave as it travels from one medium to another (e.g., air to water).Medium
Show answer

The frequency stays the same (set by the source). The speed and wavelength change in the new medium; amplitude may also change due to reflection/transmission at the boundary.

Explain reflection and refraction in terms of wave behaviour.Medium
Show answer

Reflection: wave bounces off a surface; angle of incidence = angle of reflection. Refraction: wave changes speed at a boundary, causing a change in direction and wavelength (frequency unchanged).

A student doubles the frequency of a wave while keeping the wave speed constant. What happens to the period and wavelength?Medium
Show answer

Period halves (T = 1/f, so doubling f halves T). Wavelength also halves (λ = v/f; doubling f at constant v halves λ).

X-ray light has wavelength 0.1 nm. Calculate its frequency. (c = 3×10&sup8; m/s)
Show Answer

f = c/λ = 3×10&sup8; / 10¹&sup0; = 3×10¹&sup8; Hz.

SET C Hard – Analysis and Extended Response

Explain the principle of superposition and describe constructive and destructive interference.Hard
Show answer

When two waves overlap, the resultant displacement equals the algebraic sum of individual displacements. Constructive interference: waves in phase → amplitudes add (louder sound / brighter light). Destructive interference: waves out of phase by 180° → amplitudes cancel (quiet spot / dark fringe).

A seismic P-wave travels at 6,000 m/s and has a frequency of 2 Hz. Calculate its wavelength, then calculate the period.Hard
Show answer

λ = v/f = 6000/2 = 3000 m (3 km). T = 1/f = 1/2 = 0.5 s.

Explain why sound cannot travel through a vacuum but light can. Reference the wave type of each.Hard
Show answer

Sound is a mechanical (longitudinal) wave that requires a medium of particles to compress and rarefy. A vacuum has no particles, so sound cannot propagate. Light is an electromagnetic wave — self-sustaining oscillating electric and magnetic fields that require no medium.

Diffraction is most pronounced when the wavelength is comparable to the gap size. Use this to explain why low-frequency sounds bend around buildings but high-frequency (ultrasound) does not.Hard
Show answer

Low-frequency sound has long wavelengths (metres), comparable to building gaps (a few metres), causing pronounced diffraction — the wave spreads around corners. Ultrasound has very short wavelengths (millimetres), much smaller than building gaps, so diffraction is negligible and it travels in straight beams.

A student observes that two identical speakers produce a loud-soft-loud pattern across a room. Explain this using wave theory, naming the phenomenon.Hard
Show answer

This is interference (specifically a standing-wave-like pattern). At points where waves from both speakers arrive in phase, constructive interference creates loud regions. Where they arrive out of phase (path difference = half wavelength), destructive interference creates quiet (soft) spots.

Evaluate the statement: "A louder sound has a higher frequency." Is this correct? Justify using wave properties.Hard
Show answer

Incorrect. Loudness depends on amplitude — a larger amplitude means more energy per second, hence louder sound. Frequency determines pitch (how high or low the sound is). A loud bass note has high amplitude and low frequency; a quiet whistle has low amplitude and high frequency.

Explain why water waves slow down and bend when moving from deep to shallow water. Use v = fλ in your answer.Hard
Show answer

In shallow water, friction with the sea bed reduces wave speed (v decreases). Since the frequency is fixed by the source (f unchanged), from v = fλ the wavelength must decrease. The side of the wave entering shallow water first slows down first, causing the wavefront to bend (refract) towards the shallow region.

An ultrasound pulse is used in medical imaging. The pulse travels at 1,540 m/s in tissue and returns after 0.0002 s. Calculate the depth of the reflecting surface. If the frequency used is 5 MHz, what is the wavelength?Hard
Show answer

Distance = v × t = 1540 × 0.0002 = 0.308 m; depth = 0.308/2 = 0.154 m (15.4 cm). λ = v/f = 1540/(5×10⁶) = 3.08×10⁻⁴ m (0.308 mm).

A ship uses SONAR at 20 kHz (v = 1500 m/s in seawater). A pulse returns after 0.08 s. Find the depth of the seabed.
Show Answer

Distance = v×t = 1500×0.08 = 120 m. Depth = 120/2 = 60 m (pulse travels down and back).

Hard
Explain why wave energy is proportional to amplitude squared (I ∝ A²), not amplitude. Give a real-world consequence for sound waves.
Show Answer

Wave energy depends on the maximum kinetic energy of oscillating particles: KE ∝ v² ∝ (Aω)² ∝ A². Consequence: doubling a sound’s amplitude quadruples its intensity. On the decibel scale, intensity doubles every ~3 dB. A loudspeaker at full volume is not twice as loud as half volume — it is much more (quadrupled intensity).

SET B Medium – Properties and Applications

A gamma ray has a frequency of 3×10²⁰ Hz. Calculate its wavelength.Medium
Show answer

λ = c/f = (3×10⁸)/(3×10²⁰) = 1×10⁻¹² m (1 pm).

Explain what is meant by ionising radiation and identify which parts of the EM spectrum are ionising.Medium
Show answer

Ionising radiation has enough photon energy to remove electrons from atoms, creating ions. UV, X-rays, and gamma rays are ionising EM radiation.

Why can radio waves travel further around the Earth than visible light for communication purposes?Medium
Show answer

Long-wavelength radio waves diffract around buildings and hills, and can reflect off the ionosphere, enabling long-distance communication. Visible light has very short wavelengths and travels in straight lines with little diffraction.

Compare the wavelengths of radio waves and gamma rays. What does this tell you about their frequencies?Medium
Show answer

Radio waves have wavelengths of metres to kilometres; gamma rays have wavelengths smaller than 10⁻¹¹ m. Since all EM waves travel at the same speed, gamma rays have enormously higher frequencies than radio waves (f = c/λ).

Explain why UV radiation can cause sunburn but visible light cannot.Medium
Show answer

UV photons have higher energy than visible light photons. Above a threshold energy, photons can ionise molecules in skin cells, breaking chemical bonds and damaging DNA — causing sunburn and potentially skin cancer. Visible light photons lack sufficient energy to ionise skin molecules.

State the role of the ozone layer in filtering EM radiation.Medium
Show answer

The ozone layer absorbs most of the Sun's harmful UV radiation (especially UV-B and UV-C), preventing it from reaching Earth's surface and protecting living organisms from DNA damage.

A visible light wave has wavelength 600 nm. Calculate its frequency (c = 3×10⁸ m/s).Medium
Show answer

f = c/λ = (3×10⁸)/(600×10⁻⁹) = 5×10¹⁴ Hz.

How are gamma rays produced, and how do they differ from X-rays in origin?Medium
Show answer

Gamma rays are produced in nuclear decay (from the nucleus). X-rays are produced when fast electrons decelerate or when electrons fall between high-energy atomic shells. Both are high-frequency EM waves, but gamma rays originate from nuclei and generally have higher frequencies.

An X-ray has frequency 10¹&sup9; Hz. Calculate its wavelength and compare to a visible photon (λ = 500 nm). (c = 3×10&sup8; m/s)
Show Answer

λ(X-ray) = 3×10&sup8;/10¹&sup9; = 3×10⁻¹¹ m = 0.03 nm. Visible: 500 nm. Ratio = 500/0.03 ≈ 16,700× longer. X-rays are ~17,000× shorter wavelength than visible light.

Medium
Why can microwaves cook food from the inside but infrared only heats the surface?
Show Answer

Microwaves penetrate several centimetres into food, exciting water molecules throughout the volume. IR is absorbed at the surface (skin depth ~mm), heating outward in. Microwave cooking is faster and more uniform; IR (grill/oven) chars and browns the surface.

SET C Hard – Analysis and Evaluation

Discuss the benefits and risks of X-ray use in medicine. How do hospitals balance these?Hard
Show answer

Benefits: non-invasive diagnosis of fractures, tumours, lung disease. Risks: ionising radiation can damage DNA and increase cancer risk. Hospitals balance this by using minimum effective dose, lead shielding, limiting frequency of scans, and using alternatives (MRI, ultrasound) where possible.

Explain why gamma rays are used in cancer radiotherapy yet also cause cancer. Is this contradictory?Hard
Show answer

Not contradictory — gamma rays kill cells by ionising DNA. In radiotherapy, beams are focused precisely on tumour tissue from multiple angles, delivering lethal doses to cancer cells while minimising dose to surrounding healthy tissue. The same ionising property that can damage healthy cells is exploited to destroy cancerous ones in a controlled way.

The cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation is evidence for the Big Bang. Explain why it is in the microwave region, not visible light.Hard
Show answer

The CMB was emitted ~380,000 years after the Big Bang as hot plasma cooled to ~3000 K (red/infrared light). Over 13.8 billion years, the expansion of the universe has stretched these wavelengths enormously (cosmological redshift), shifting them from infrared/visible to the microwave region (~1 mm wavelength, ~160 GHz).

Evaluate the claim: "Mobile phones cause brain cancer because they emit electromagnetic radiation." Use your knowledge of the EM spectrum.Hard
Show answer

Mobile phones emit microwave/radio-frequency EM radiation — non-ionising radiation. This does not have enough energy to break chemical bonds or ionise DNA. Large-scale epidemiological studies (e.g., WHO's INTERPHONE study) have not shown a confirmed causal link between mobile phone use and brain cancer. The claim conflates all EM radiation with dangerous ionising radiation; this is scientifically inaccurate.

An astronomer detects light from a distant galaxy shifted towards longer wavelengths (redshift). Explain what this tells us and link it to the EM spectrum.Hard
Show answer

Redshift means the observed wavelength is longer than the emitted wavelength, indicating the galaxy is moving away from us (Doppler effect). The light has shifted towards the lower-frequency (red) end of the visible spectrum or into infrared. This is evidence that the universe is expanding.

Explain how a greenhouse on Earth relates to the greenhouse effect, using infrared radiation and the EM spectrum.Hard
Show answer

A greenhouse lets in short-wavelength visible light (passes through glass), which is absorbed by soil/plants and re-emitted as longer-wavelength infrared. Glass is opaque to IR, trapping heat inside. Similarly, greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄) in the atmosphere are transparent to incoming visible solar radiation but absorb outgoing IR from Earth's surface, trapping heat.

Compare the penetrating power of radio waves, X-rays, and gamma rays. Relate this to their frequencies and applications.Hard
Show answer

Penetrating power increases with frequency. Radio waves pass through walls/buildings (used in broadcasting). X-rays penetrate soft tissue but are absorbed by dense bone (used in medical imaging). Gamma rays penetrate most materials and require thick lead or concrete shielding (used in cancer therapy, sterilisation). Higher frequency → higher photon energy → greater penetration.

A spy satellite uses infrared cameras to detect heat signatures at night. Explain the physics principle that makes this possible.Hard
Show answer

All objects above absolute zero emit EM radiation. The peak wavelength depends on temperature (Wien's Law: λ_peak ∝ 1/T). At human body temperature (~310 K) or engine temperatures, peak emission is in the near/mid-infrared range. Infrared cameras detect this thermal emission, producing images even in darkness when no visible light is present.

Gamma rays from cobalt-60 (E = 1.17 MeV) are used in radiotherapy. Calculate the frequency. (h = 6.63×10⁻³&sup4; J·s; 1 eV = 1.6×10⁻¹&sup9; J)
Show Answer

E = 1.17×10&sup6;×1.6×10⁻¹&sup9; = 1.872×10⁻¹³ J. f = E/h = 1.872×10⁻¹³/6.63×10⁻³&sup4; = 2.82×10²&sup0; Hz.

Hard
Explain why radio telescopes are much larger than optical telescopes. Link to diffraction and angular resolution (θ ≈ λ/D).
Show Answer

Resolution θ ≈ λ/D. Radio waves have λ ∼ metres vs optical λ ∼ 500 nm. To achieve the same angular resolution as a 1 m optical telescope (θ = 5×10⁻&sup7; rad), a radio telescope at λ = 1 m needs D = 1/5×10⁻&sup7; = 2×10&sup6; m = 2000 km. This is why radio astronomers use arrays of widely-spaced dishes (VLBI) spanning continents to simulate an Earth-sized telescope.

SET A Recall & Comprehension (Easy)

State the principle of superposition of waves.
Show Answer

When two or more waves overlap, the resultant displacement equals the algebraic sum of individual displacements. After passing, each wave continues unchanged.

Easy
Define constructive interference and state the path difference condition.
Show Answer

Constructive interference: waves meet in phase (crest + crest), amplitudes add. Condition: path difference = nλ (n = 0,1,2,...).

Easy
Define destructive interference and state the path difference condition.
Show Answer

Destructive interference: waves meet out of phase (crest + trough), amplitudes cancel. Condition: path difference = (n + ½)λ.

Easy
What is diffraction?
Show Answer

Diffraction is the spreading of waves around obstacles or through gaps. Most significant when wavelength ≈ gap size.

Easy
Why can you hear someone around a corner but not see them?
Show Answer

Sound (λ ~ cm to m) diffracts around corners because λ ≈ gap size. Visible light (λ ~500 nm) is far smaller than doorways — negligible diffraction. Sound bends around corners; light does not.

Easy
What did Young’s double-slit experiment demonstrate?
Show Answer

The wave nature of light. Light through two slits creates alternating bright (constructive) and dark (destructive) fringes — impossible if light were only particles.

Easy
What is a diffraction grating and what is it used for?
Show Answer

Many equally spaced slits (thousands per mm). Produces sharp interference maxima at angles depending on wavelength. Used in spectroscopy to separate and measure wavelengths.

Easy
What happens to the diffraction pattern when a single slit is made narrower?
Show Answer

The central maximum becomes wider. Narrower slit → λ is a larger fraction of gap → more spreading → wider pattern.

Easy
Noise-cancelling headphones use destructive interference. Explain.
Show Answer

A microphone captures ambient noise. Electronics generate an inverted (180° phase-shifted) anti-noise. The anti-noise superposes destructively with incoming noise — cancelling it out.

Easy
State two conditions for a stable interference pattern to be observed.
Show Answer

1. Sources must be coherent (constant phase relationship, same frequency). 2. Amplitudes should be similar. In practice: use one source split into two paths.

Explain why AM radio (λ ~300 m) diffracts over hills but FM (λ ~3 m) does not easily.

Explain what coherence means and why it is essential for stable interference.
Show Answer

Coherence: constant phase relationship between sources. Incoherent sources have random phase fluctuations — interference pattern shifts faster than eye can see, appearing uniform. Young’s uses one source split into two — any phase change is common to both, preserving relative phase difference and a stable pattern.

Show that 3rd order of 480 nm coincides with 2nd order of 720 nm for any grating.
Show Answer

3rd order 480 nm: 3×480 = 1440 nm. 2nd order 720 nm: 2×720 = 1440 nm. Both give d sinθ = 1440 nm → same angle for any grating. Orders overlap.

Show Answer

Centre: all colours constructively interfere at zero path difference → white central fringe. Sides: each colour constructively interferes at different positions (longer λ further out) → coloured fringes, violet nearest centre, red furthest. Higher orders overlap and wash out.

Explain why electron microscopes resolve atomic-scale features that light microscopes cannot, using de Broglie wavelength (λ = h/mv).
Show Answer

Resolution limit ≈ λ. Light: λ ~400–700 nm. Electrons at 100 kV: λ ≈ 0.004 nm (~100,000× shorter). Diffraction limits resolution to ≈ λ, so electrons allow imaging of individual atoms — impossible with visible light.

Explain wave-particle duality shown by single-photon double-slit experiments.
Show Answer

Single photons sent one at a time still build up an interference pattern over many shots. Each registers as a dot but the cumulative pattern has dark and bright fringes. The photon “interferes with itself” — it passes through both slits as a probability wave. Observing which slit is used destroys the pattern. Central to quantum mechanics.

Show Answer

sinθ = nλ/(2d) = 0.15/(2×0.28) = 0.268. θ = 15.5°.

Hard
Explain why X-ray crystallography uses X-rays (not visible light) to determine crystal structures.
Show Answer

Crystal plane spacings ~0.1–1 nm match only X-ray wavelengths. Visible light (λ~500 nm) is too long to diffract between atomic planes. X-rays (0.01–10 nm) diffract from planes; measuring Bragg angles gives plane spacings d and thus 3D crystal structure.

Hard
A grating with 600 lines/mm is illuminated by 480 nm and 720 nm. Show the 3rd order of 480 nm coincides with 2nd order of 720 nm and find the angle.
Show Answer

d = 1.667×10⁻&sup6; m. sinθ for 3rd/480 = 3×480×10⁻&sup9;/1.667×10⁻&sup6; = 0.864. sinθ for 2nd/720 = 2×720×10⁻&sup9;/1.667×10⁻&sup6; = 0.864. Both θ = arcsin(0.864) = 59.9°.

Hard
Explain holography: how does a hologram record and reconstruct 3D information?
Show Answer

Recording: laser split into reference beam (direct to film) and object beam (reflects off subject). Their interference pattern (containing phase and amplitude) is recorded. Reconstruction: shine reference beam through developed film; diffraction recreates original wavefronts, producing a 3D image with parallax.

Hard
Design an experiment to measure laser wavelength using a diffraction grating. State measurements, formula, and two sources of uncertainty.
Show Answer

Equipment: laser, grating (known lines/mm), screen, ruler. Measure: distance D (grating to screen), position x of n=1 spot. Calculate: θ = arctan(x/D); λ = d sinθ/n. Uncertainties: (1) measuring x (±0.5 mm fringe width); (2) measuring D (±1 mm). Reduce by using higher orders (larger angles, smaller relative uncertainty).

Hard
Explain why a diffraction grating produces sharper spectral lines than a double slit, using the concept of resolving power.
Show Answer

With N slits, principal maxima sharpen by factor N (resolving power = nN). More slits create more destructive interference between maxima, narrowing bright lines. A 1000-line grating is 500× sharper than a double slit — enough to resolve the sodium doublet (589.0 and 589.6 nm, Δλ = 0.6 nm).

SET A Easy – Faraday and Lenz Basics

State Faraday's Law of electromagnetic induction in one sentence.Easy
Show answer

An EMF is induced in a conductor whenever there is a change in magnetic flux through it; the magnitude of the EMF is proportional to the rate of change of flux.

What is magnetic flux?Easy
Show answer

Magnetic flux is the product of the magnetic field strength and the area of the coil perpendicular to the field (Φ = BA). It represents the total number of magnetic field lines passing through a surface.

Does a stationary magnet inside a coil induce a current? Explain.Easy
Show answer

No. A current is induced only when there is a change in magnetic flux. A stationary magnet produces a constant flux — no change, no induced EMF.

Name three ways to increase the size of the induced EMF in a coil.Easy
Show answer

Move the magnet faster; use a stronger magnet; increase the number of turns in the coil.

State Lenz's Law.Easy
Show answer

The direction of the induced current is such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that caused it (the induced current creates a magnetic field opposing the original change).

In what year did Faraday discover electromagnetic induction?Easy
Show answer

1831.

If a north pole of a magnet is pushed into a coil, what pole does the near face of the coil become?Easy
Show answer

A north pole (by Lenz's Law, it repels the incoming north pole to oppose the increase in flux).

What happens to the induced current if the magnet is pulled out of the coil instead of pushed in?Easy
Show answer

The current reverses direction. Lenz's Law: now the coil tries to maintain the flux (attract the retreating magnet), so the near face becomes a south pole.

SET A Recall & Comprehension (Easy)

State Snell’s Law of refraction.
Show Answer

n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂, where n₁, n₂ are refractive indices of the two media and θ₁, θ₂ are angles of incidence and refraction measured from the normal.

Easy
Define refractive index (n) of a medium.
Show Answer

n = c/v, where c = speed of light in vacuum (3×10&sup8; m/s) and v = speed of light in the medium. n ≥ 1 always. Higher n means slower light and greater bending.

Easy
Give approximate refractive indices for air, water, and glass.
Show Answer

Air: n ≈ 1.0003 (≈1). Water: n ≈ 1.33. Glass: n ≈ 1.5. Diamond: n ≈ 2.42.

Easy
When light travels from air into glass, does it bend toward or away from the normal? Explain.
Show Answer

Toward the normal. Glass has a higher refractive index than air (n_glass > n_air). Light slows down in the denser medium and bends toward the normal (angle of refraction < angle of incidence).

Easy
What is the critical angle?
Show Answer

The critical angle θ_c is the angle of incidence (in the denser medium) at which the refracted ray travels exactly along the boundary (θ_r = 90°). If the angle of incidence exceeds θ_c, total internal reflection occurs.

Easy
State the formula for the critical angle.
Show Answer

sinθ_c = n₂/n₁, where n₁ > n₂ (light going from denser to less dense medium). For glass-air: sinθ_c = 1/n_glass.

Easy
What is total internal reflection (TIR)? State the two conditions required.
Show Answer

TIR: light is completely reflected back into the denser medium with no refracted ray. Conditions: (1) light must be travelling from a denser medium to a less dense medium (n₁ > n₂); (2) angle of incidence must exceed the critical angle (θ > θ_c).

Easy
How are optical fibres used in telecommunications?
Show Answer

Light signals are transmitted through glass fibres by TIR — light bounces along the core (high n) by repeatedly totally internally reflecting off the cladding (lower n). Data travels as light pulses at nearly the speed of light, with minimal loss over long distances. Undersea fibre cables carry internet traffic between continents.

Easy
Why does a diamond sparkle more than glass even though both use TIR?
Show Answer

Diamond has n = 2.42 vs glass n ≈ 1.5. Higher n → lower critical angle (sinθ_c = 1/2.42 = 0.413, θ_c = 24.4° vs glass ~42°). Light entering a diamond undergoes TIR much more easily (at smaller angles). The many facets are cut at angles that trap light inside and direct it back out toward the viewer — maximum sparkle.

Calculate the speed of light in glass (n = 1.5).
Show Answer

v = c/n = 3×10&sup8;/1.5 = 2×10&sup8; m/s.

Derive the formula for critical angle from Snell’s Law. Show that sinθ_c = n₂/n₁ for n₁ > n₂.
Show Answer

At critical angle, θ₂ = 90° (refracted ray along boundary). Snell’s: n₁sinθ_c = n₂sin90 = n₂×1. Therefore sinθ_c = n₂/n₁. For glass-air: sinθ_c = 1/n_glass. As n₁ → n₂, θ_c → 90° (less TIR). As n₁ >> n₂, θ_c → 0° (TIR at almost any angle).

Explain chromatic aberration in lenses using dispersion. How do camera manufacturers minimise it?
Show Answer

Mirages are caused by refraction in hot air. Explain how a mirage forms on a hot road surface using gradually changing refractive index.
Show Answer

Hot air near the road surface has lower density → lower n. Air becomes progressively cooler and denser higher up → gradually increasing n. Light from the sky travelling downward continuously refracts in this gradient, bending away from the normal (toward lower-n region). When the angle reaches the critical angle for the local n gradient, TIR occurs and light curves back upward. The observer sees sky light appearing to come from the road surface — like a reflection in water. This is a continuous refraction/TIR mirage, not a sharp boundary effect.

Rainbows: light enters a spherical raindrop, refracts, reflects internally, and refracts again on exit. The angle for red light is ~42° and violet is ~40° from the anti-solar point. Explain why red appears on the outside of a rainbow and violet on the inside.
Show Answer

Red light (n_red ≈ 1.331) refracts less (exits at 42° from anti-solar point); violet (n_violet ≈ 1.343) refracts more (exits at 40°). The “radius” of the rainbow (angle from anti-solar point) is larger for red than violet. Looking up at a rainbow, larger angle = higher in the sky = outside the arc. So red is on the outside (top), violet on the inside (bottom). A secondary rainbow has reversed colours (double internal reflection).

Explain why optical fibre communications use single-mode fibres (very thin core, ~9 μm) for long distances rather than multi-mode fibres (core ~50 μm).

Disadvantage: harder to couple light into (tiny core), more expensive connectors.
