SET A Foundational – Easy

What does temperature measure?
Show Answer

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. Higher temperature = faster-moving particles.

 Easy
What is heat?
Show Answer

Heat is thermal energy that is transferred from a hotter object to a cooler object. It is measured in joules (J).

 Easy
State the direction in which heat always flows naturally.
Show Answer

Heat always flows from a region of higher temperature (hotter object) to a region of lower temperature (cooler object).

 Easy
What unit is used to measure heat energy?
Show Answer

Heat energy is measured in joules (J).

 Easy
What two scales are commonly used to measure temperature?
Show Answer

Celsius (°C) and Kelvin (K) are the two most common scientific scales. Fahrenheit (°F) is used in everyday life in some countries.

 Easy
Convert 27 °C to Kelvin.
Show Answer

K = °C + 273 → 27 + 273 = 300 K

 Easy
A metal spoon is hotter than your hand. When you hold it, which way does heat flow?
Show Answer

Heat flows from the hot metal spoon into your cooler hand.

 Easy
Can two objects have the same temperature but different amounts of heat energy? Explain with an example.
Show Answer

Yes. A bath of water and a cup of water at 40 °C have the same temperature, but the bath contains far more heat energy because it has many more particles.

 Easy
What happens to the particles of a gas when its temperature increases?
Show Answer

The particles move faster and with greater kinetic energy; they collide more often and more forcefully.

 Easy
Is absolute zero (0 K) achievable in practice? What does it represent?
Show Answer

Absolute zero (0 K = −273 °C) represents the theoretical point where particles have minimum kinetic energy and no heat energy can be extracted. It is not achievable in practice.

 Easy

SET B Developing – Medium

Distinguish between heat and temperature using particle theory.
Show Answer

Temperature is the average kinetic energy per particle; heat is the total thermal energy transferred between objects. A large cold object can have more total heat energy than a small hot object.

 Medium
A thermometer reads 37 °C. What is this in Kelvin, and what does this temperature mean for a human?
Show Answer

37 + 273 = 310 K. This is normal human body temperature, indicating healthy metabolic activity.

 Medium
Why does a large swimming pool at 20 °C contain more heat energy than a small cup of boiling water at 100 °C?
Show Answer

Heat energy depends on both temperature and the amount (mass) of substance. The pool has an enormous number of particles; even at lower temperature their total kinetic energy far exceeds that of the small cup.

 Medium
Explain, using kinetic theory, why ice feels cold when you touch it.
Show Answer

The particles of ice have lower average kinetic energy than skin particles. Heat flows from your warm skin into the cold ice, removing energy from your hand — which your nerves detect as a cold sensation.

 Medium
A student says "cold flows from the ice into my drink." Correct this statement scientifically.
Show Answer

Cold does not flow. Heat flows from the warmer drink into the cooler ice, reducing the drink's temperature. There is no such thing as "cold flow."

 Medium
Convert −40 °C to Kelvin. What is significant about −40?
Show Answer

−40 + 273 = 233 K. −40 °C equals −40 °F — the only temperature where the Celsius and Fahrenheit scales give the same numerical value.

 Medium
If two identical iron balls are at 50 °C and 100 °C respectively, which has more heat energy and why?
Show Answer

The ball at 100 °C has more heat energy because its particles have higher average kinetic energy. Since the masses are equal, total thermal energy is greater at higher temperature.

 Medium
Why is it incorrect to say "a thermometer measures heat"?
Show Answer

A thermometer measures temperature (average kinetic energy per particle), not the total heat energy of an object. Two objects can have identical temperatures but very different amounts of heat energy.

 Medium
As a substance is heated, what happens to (a) its temperature, (b) its particle speed?
Show Answer

(a) Temperature rises. (b) Particles move faster — their average kinetic energy increases. In solids they vibrate more vigorously; in liquids and gases they translate faster.

 Medium
A metal rod has one end in a flame. Describe, using kinetic theory, how the heat moves along the rod.
Show Answer

Particles at the hot end gain kinetic energy and vibrate faster. They collide with neighbouring particles, transferring energy along the rod. This is the beginning of conduction.

SET C Extended – Hard (MYP Criteria B & D)

"Temperature and heat are the same thing." Evaluate this statement using particle theory and at least two examples. (MYP Criterion B)
Show Answer

This statement is incorrect. Temperature = average KE per particle; heat = total thermal energy transferred. Example 1: A burning match (high temp, tiny mass) has less total heat than a warm lake (lower temp, enormous mass). Example 2: Two identical glasses of water at 60 °C have the same temperature, but mixing them gives the same temperature — not double — showing temperature is intensive while heat content is extensive.

 Hard
Design an investigation to show that heat flows from hot to cold. State your independent variable, dependent variable, and one control variable.
Show Answer

IV: Temperature difference between two metal blocks. DV: Temperature change over time in each block (measured with thermometer). Control: Same mass of each block / same material / same surface contact area. Predicted outcome: the hotter block cools and the cooler block warms until they reach the same temperature.

 Hard
Explain why the Kelvin scale is considered more "scientific" than the Celsius scale.
Show Answer

The Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero — the lowest theoretically possible temperature where particles have minimum kinetic energy. There are no negative values, making it directly proportional to kinetic energy. This makes it essential for calculations in gas laws and thermodynamics.

 Hard
A student heats 500 g of water from 20 °C to 70 °C. Another student heats 200 g of water from 20 °C to 70 °C. Compare the heat energy needed and the temperature change in each case.
Show Answer

Both samples reach the same final temperature (70 °C), so their temperature change is identical (ΔT = 50 °C). However, using Q = mcΔT, the 500 g sample needs 2.5× more heat energy than the 200 g sample because it contains more particles.

 Hard
Using MYP Criterion D (reflection), discuss a real-world situation where confusing heat and temperature could have dangerous consequences.
Show Answer

In a foundry or kitchen: a small droplet of boiling oil (100 °C, tiny mass) has a high temperature but low total heat energy. A large vat of water at 60 °C (lower temperature) contains far more heat and could cause severe scalding over a larger body area. If workers focus only on temperature readings and ignore the mass/volume of hot material, they may underestimate burn risk from large volumes of moderately hot liquid.

 Hard
Describe how a clinical thermometer uses the principles of heat transfer to measure body temperature accurately.
Show Answer

The mercury/alcohol bulb is placed under the tongue where heat conducts from body tissues into the liquid in the bulb. Thermal expansion of the liquid rises up the calibrated tube. The constriction in the tube prevents the liquid from falling back when removed, allowing accurate reading. The thermometer reaches thermal equilibrium with body tissue, so its temperature equals body temperature.

 Hard
Compare the average kinetic energy of particles in a solid at 100 °C vs a gas at 100 °C. Are they different? Explain.
Show Answer

At the same temperature, both have the same average kinetic energy per particle. However, the gas particles translate freely through space, while solid particles are confined to vibrating about fixed positions. The type of motion differs, but temperature reflects only the average KE magnitude.

 Hard
Evaluate this claim: "Space has a temperature of −270 °C, so heat cannot be transferred through space." Is this scientifically accurate?
Show Answer

Partially incorrect. The background radiation temperature of space is about −270 °C (3 K), but heat can be transferred through space via radiation (infrared electromagnetic waves) — which require no medium. This is how the Sun's energy reaches Earth. Conduction and convection cannot occur in a vacuum.

 Hard
A thermometer reads the same temperature inside a freezer and inside a refrigerator. A student claims both have the same amount of cold. Critique this reasoning.
Show Answer

The reasoning is flawed on two levels: (1) "Cold" is not a measurable quantity — only heat is. (2) If the two compartments have different volumes/masses of air, they contain different total thermal energies despite identical temperatures. Temperature is an intensive property; total heat content is an extensive property.

 Hard
A scientist measures the temperature of a neutron star as 600,000 K. Convert to °C. What does this imply about the kinetic energy of its particles compared to a candle flame at ~1400 °C?
Show Answer

600,000 − 273 = 599,727 °C. Converting candle to K: 1400 + 273 = 1673 K. Ratio: 600,000 ÷ 1673 ≈ 359×. So neutron star particles have approximately 359 times greater average kinetic energy than candle flame particles. This means subatomic particles on the neutron star move at relativistic speeds.

SET A Foundational – Easy

What is thermal equilibrium?
Show Answer

Thermal equilibrium is reached when two objects in contact have the same temperature and no net heat flows between them.

 Easy
What happens to heat flow when two objects reach thermal equilibrium?
Show Answer

Net heat flow stops. Both objects are at the same temperature so there is no temperature difference to drive heat transfer.

 Easy
You drop an ice cube into a warm drink. What will eventually happen?
Show Answer

Heat flows from the warm drink into the ice, melting it and cooling the drink until both reach the same temperature — thermal equilibrium.

 Easy
State the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics in simple terms.
Show Answer

If object A is in thermal equilibrium with object B, and B is in thermal equilibrium with object C, then A and C are also in thermal equilibrium (i.e., all three are at the same temperature).

 Easy
When you leave a hot cup of tea on a table, it eventually cools to room temperature. What is this an example of?
Show Answer

This is an example of thermal equilibrium — the tea loses heat to the cooler surroundings until both reach the same temperature.

 Easy
Does ice water stay at 0 °C permanently? Explain.
Show Answer

No. Once all the ice melts, heat from the surroundings continues to warm the water until it reaches room temperature (thermal equilibrium with the environment).

 Easy
Name the instrument used to detect and measure thermal equilibrium between objects.
Show Answer

A thermometer. When the thermometer reading stops changing, the thermometer is in thermal equilibrium with the object.

 Easy
Why does the temperature of ice water stay at 0 °C while ice is still present?
Show Answer

The heat energy entering from the surroundings is used to melt the ice (latent heat) rather than raise the temperature, keeping it at 0 °C until all ice melts.

 Easy
What does it mean for a room to be "at thermal equilibrium"?
Show Answer

All objects in the room (furniture, air, walls) are at the same temperature and there is no net heat transfer between them.

 Easy
If a metal spoon and a wooden spoon have been lying in the same drawer for hours, are they at the same temperature?
Show Answer

Yes — both have been in contact with the same environment (the drawer) for a long time and have reached thermal equilibrium; they are at the same temperature (room temperature).

 Easy

SET B Developing – Medium

A metal spoon feels colder than a wooden spoon even though both are at room temperature. Explain this using thermal equilibrium and conduction.
Show Answer

Both spoons are at thermal equilibrium with the room (same temperature). However, metal conducts heat away from your hand much faster than wood. Heat flows rapidly from your warmer skin into the metal, making it feel cold. Wood transfers heat slowly, so your hand barely loses energy — it feels warmer.

 Medium
How does a clinical thermometer use the concept of thermal equilibrium to give an accurate reading?
Show Answer

When placed under the tongue, the thermometer bulb absorbs heat from the body until it reaches the same temperature — thermal equilibrium. At this point the mercury/alcohol level stabilises, giving an accurate body temperature reading.

 Medium
Explain why it takes longer to reach thermal equilibrium between a large lake and the atmosphere than between a small cup of water and the atmosphere.
Show Answer

The lake has far greater thermal mass (more particles). More heat energy must be transferred to raise or lower its temperature, so equilibration takes much longer despite the same temperature difference.

 Medium
A 90 °C metal block is placed in 20 °C water inside an insulated container. Predict the final equilibrium temperature qualitatively (without calculation). Will it be closer to 90 or 20? Explain.
Show Answer

The final temperature depends on the masses and specific heat capacities. If water mass ≫ block mass, it will be closer to 20 °C because water can absorb a lot of heat with little temperature rise. If mass of block ≫ water, closer to 90 °C. Either way, the final temperature is between 20 °C and 90 °C.

 Medium
Why is the concept of thermal equilibrium important when designing a cold storage facility?
Show Answer

Heat from outside constantly tries to reach thermal equilibrium with the cold interior. Good insulation slows heat flow, keeping the inside cold longer. Without insulation, equilibrium is reached quickly — the facility warms up and food spoils.

 Medium
Two beakers of water, one at 80 °C and one at 40 °C, are mixed. Using conservation of energy, what can you say about the final temperature?
Show Answer

Heat lost by hot water = heat gained by cold water (conservation of energy). If equal masses: T_final = (80+40)/2 = 60 °C. The final temperature lies between the two starting temperatures.

 Medium
Describe thermal equilibrium at the particle level.
Show Answer

At thermal equilibrium, particles of both objects have the same average kinetic energy. While individual collisions still transfer energy back and forth, the net rate of energy transfer is zero in each direction — a dynamic balance.

 Medium
Why do coastal cities have milder temperatures than inland cities at similar latitudes? Link your answer to thermal equilibrium.
Show Answer

The ocean has very high thermal mass and takes a long time to reach equilibrium with the atmosphere. It absorbs heat in summer (staying cooler) and releases heat in winter (staying warmer), moderating the climate compared to inland areas with less water.

 Medium
A student leaves a thermometer in a room for 5 minutes before reading it. Why is the wait important?
Show Answer

The thermometer needs time to reach thermal equilibrium with the room air. If read too quickly, the thermometer may still be at a different temperature (e.g., from a warmer pocket), giving an inaccurate reading.

 Medium
Explain why putting a hot object in a large amount of cold water barely changes the water temperature.
Show Answer

The heat from the object is distributed among a huge number of water particles. Since Q = mcΔT, a large mass means a tiny ΔT for the same Q. The object cools significantly while the water barely warms — equilibrium is established at close to the original water temperature.

 Medium

SET C Extended – Hard (MYP Criteria B & D)

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics underpins how thermometers work. Explain this connection in detail.
Show Answer

The Zeroth Law states: if A is in equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in equilibrium with C. A thermometer (B) is first calibrated in reference conditions (A = ice water, C = boiling water). When placed against an unknown object (A), the thermometer reaches equilibrium with it. Since the thermometer was calibrated to the same scale, its reading tells us the unknown object's temperature — using B as a mediator.

 Hard
A 200 g iron block at 100 °C is dropped into 500 g of water at 25 °C in an insulated container. Using Q = mcΔT, estimate the equilibrium temperature. (c_iron = 450 J/kg°C; c_water = 4200 J/kg°C)
Show Answer

Heat lost by iron = heat gained by water.
0.2 × 450 × (100 − T) = 0.5 × 4200 × (T − 25)
90(100 − T) = 2100(T − 25)
9000 − 90T = 2100T − 52500
61500 = 2190T
T ≈ 28.1 °C
The large water mass barely warms; the iron block cools dramatically.

 Hard
Thermal equilibrium is a "dynamic" process. Explain what this means at the molecular level.
Show Answer

At equilibrium, energy transfer between particles does not stop — collisions still occur and energy is still exchanged. However, the rate of energy transfer from A to B equals the rate from B to A, so there is no net change in temperature. The system is in dynamic, not static, balance.

 Hard
Design an investigation to determine the specific heat capacity of an unknown metal using thermal equilibrium. List apparatus, method steps, and formula.
Show Answer

Apparatus: Metal block, boiling water (100 °C), known mass of cool water, insulated calorimeter, thermometer, balance.
Method: (1) Measure mass of metal (m₁) and water (m₂). (2) Heat metal to 100 °C. (3) Drop metal into cool water; record initial water temp T₁ and final equilibrium temp T_f. (4) Apply: m₁c₁(100−T_f) = m₂×4200×(T_f−T₁). Solve for c₁.

 Hard
Why does the Earth's climate system never reach true thermal equilibrium with the Sun?
Show Answer

True equilibrium would require Earth to reach the Sun's surface temperature (~5500 °C). Instead, Earth absorbs solar radiation and re-emits infrared radiation to space. This creates a steady state (not equilibrium), where incoming and outgoing energy rates are balanced at Earth's much lower temperature (~15 °C average). Continuous solar input prevents true equilibrium.

 Hard
Critique the following experimental design: "To find equilibrium temperature, I'll mix equal volumes of water at 30 °C and 60 °C and expect 45 °C." What assumptions are made and when might it fail?
Show Answer

Assumption 1: Equal volumes = equal masses (valid only if density is the same — true for pure water). Assumption 2: No heat lost to surroundings (only valid in a perfect insulated system). Assumption 3: Same specific heat capacity (valid for pure water). It fails if: containers absorb heat, liquids differ (e.g., mixing water and oil), or volumes differ. Formula: T_f = (m₁T₁ + m₂T₂)/(m₁+m₂).

 Hard
A patient has a fever of 39.5 °C. A nurse places an ice pack on the forehead. Using thermal equilibrium, explain the heat transfer and why it reduces fever.
Show Answer

The ice pack (0 °C) is far below body temperature (39.5 °C). Heat flows from the hot skin into the ice pack. As the ice melts, it absorbs latent heat without rising in temperature, sustaining a large temperature gradient. Heat is continuously drawn from the blood vessels beneath the skin, reducing the local temperature and helping lower systemic fever.

 Hard
Explain why equilibrium temperature of mixed substances cannot exceed the temperature of the hotter substance or be below the colder, using the First Law of Thermodynamics.
Show Answer

The First Law states energy is conserved. The hotter substance can only lose energy (it cannot gain energy from a colder body). The colder substance can only gain energy. So the hotter cools down and the colder warms up — they meet somewhere in between. A final temperature outside this range would violate energy conservation.

 Hard
How does thermal equilibrium relate to the process of cooking food? Give two specific examples.
Show Answer

Example 1: Boiling an egg — heat from boiling water (100 °C) conducts through the shell, heating the egg white and yolk. Cooking is complete when the egg proteins denature at their required temperature. Example 2: Roasting in an oven — hot air reaches equilibrium with the food surface (convection + radiation); heat then conducts inward. Chefs use internal temperature probes to confirm equilibrium temperature of the meat's core (~75 °C for safety).

 Hard
(MYP Criterion D) Reflect on what would happen to life on Earth if thermal equilibrium could be reached instantaneously. Would this be beneficial or harmful?
Show Answer

Instantaneous equilibrium would be catastrophic: day–night temperature differences would vanish instantly (no time for gradual adaptation), weather systems driven by temperature gradients would collapse (no wind, rain, ocean currents), and climate zones would disappear. Life depends on gradients — convection currents, food webs, respiration all depend on energy flowing from high to low. Without this gradient, there would be no energy available to drive biological or atmospheric processes.

SET A Foundational – Easy

Define conduction in terms of particle vibration.
Show Answer

Conduction is the transfer of heat through a material by particle vibrations. Particles at the hot end vibrate faster and pass energy to neighbouring particles through collisions, without the particles themselves moving from place to place.

 Easy
In which states of matter does conduction work best?
Show Answer

Conduction works best in solids, where particles are closely packed. It also occurs in liquids and gases but is much less efficient because particles are further apart.

 Easy
Name two metals that are excellent conductors of heat.
Show Answer

Silver (best conductor) and copper are excellent conductors. Aluminium is also a good conductor.

 Easy
Name two materials that are poor conductors (insulators) of heat.
Show Answer

Wood and plastic (polystyrene) are poor conductors/insulators. Air, glass wool, and cork are also good insulators.

 Easy
Why is a wooden spoon used for stirring hot soup rather than a metal spoon?
Show Answer

Wood is a poor conductor of heat. It does not transfer heat quickly from the hot soup to your hand, so it stays safe to hold. A metal spoon would conduct heat rapidly and burn your hand.

 Easy
Does conduction require a medium (material) to transfer heat?
Show Answer

Yes. Conduction requires direct contact between particles and therefore requires a material medium. It cannot occur in a vacuum.

 Easy
What is the role of free electrons in metal conduction?
Show Answer

Metals have free (delocalised) electrons that can move rapidly through the lattice, carrying kinetic energy from hotter to cooler regions much faster than vibration alone. This makes metals far better conductors than non-metals.

 Easy
Why does a tile floor feel colder than a carpeted floor at the same temperature?
Show Answer

Tiles are good conductors — they draw heat away from your feet quickly, making them feel cold. Carpet is a poor conductor (insulator) — it transfers heat slowly, so your feet stay warm.

 Easy
Sketch/describe the direction of conduction in a metal rod heated at one end.
Show Answer

Heat conducts from the hot end (where particles vibrate most) progressively toward the cool end, as each layer of particles passes energy to the next. The entire rod eventually becomes uniformly hot.

 Easy
Give one everyday example of conduction being useful and one where it is undesirable.
Show Answer

Useful: Copper base of a frying pan conducts heat evenly for cooking. Undesirable: Metal handles of a pot conduct heat to your hand causing burns.

 Easy

SET B Developing – Medium

Explain why conduction is faster in metals than in non-metals using free electron theory.
Show Answer

In metals, conduction occurs via two mechanisms: (1) particle vibration (lattice vibration) and (2) free electron diffusion. Free electrons gain kinetic energy at the hot end and rapidly migrate through the metal, colliding with cooler lattice ions and transferring energy. Non-metals only have lattice vibration, which is much slower.

 Medium
Why is conduction in gases very poor compared to solids?
Show Answer

Gas particles are far apart and collide infrequently. The large gaps between particles mean little opportunity to transfer energy by collision. The mean free path (distance between collisions) is long, making energy transfer slow and inefficient.

 Medium
A saucepan is made of copper with a plastic handle. Explain the material choice for each part using conduction principles.
Show Answer

Copper has high thermal conductivity (free electrons) — it conducts heat efficiently from the flame to the food. The plastic handle is an insulator — it does not conduct heat from the pan to your hand, making it safe to hold.

 Medium
Compare the rate of conduction through a thin metal rod vs a thick metal rod of the same material and length.
Show Answer

The thicker rod conducts heat faster because it has a greater cross-sectional area — more particles are in contact across each layer, allowing more simultaneous energy transfer. Heat conduction rate is directly proportional to cross-sectional area (Fourier's Law).

 Medium
Why is double-glazed glass used in windows in cold countries? How does it reduce conduction?
Show Answer

Double glazing has two panes of glass with a layer of trapped air (or inert gas) between them. Air is a very poor conductor. The trapped air layer creates a thermal barrier that significantly reduces heat conduction from warm indoor air to cold outdoor air, improving insulation.

 Medium
Why does holding a metal bar feel colder than holding a wooden bar of the same size at the same temperature?
Show Answer

Both are at room temperature, but metal conducts heat away from your hand much faster than wood. The high thermal conductivity of metal draws body heat rapidly, which your nerves register as a cold sensation. Wood conducts heat so slowly that your hand stays warm.

 Medium
How does the thickness of a material affect the rate of heat conduction?
Show Answer

Thicker materials slow conduction — the temperature gradient (temperature difference per unit length) decreases. Heat has to travel further through more material. Rate of conduction ∝ 1/thickness (inversely proportional to length).

 Medium
Explain why loft insulation (e.g., glass wool) in a house works, even though glass itself conducts heat.
Show Answer

Glass wool traps millions of tiny air pockets between the glass fibres. Air is the real insulator here — it has very low thermal conductivity. The glass fibres are so thin and separated that the overall conductivity of the material is very low. Heat conduction through the loft is greatly reduced.

 Medium
A student says "metals are always hotter than plastics because metals conduct more heat." Is this correct? Explain.
Show Answer

Incorrect. Metals and plastics at the same temperature are at the same temperature — neither is "hotter." The difference is that metals feel hotter or colder because they conduct heat to/from your hand faster. The metal is not at a different temperature; it just transfers heat more rapidly.

 Medium
Why is it important for engineers to know the thermal conductivity of materials when designing buildings?
Show Answer

Materials with low thermal conductivity (insulation) in walls and roofs prevent heat loss in winter and heat gain in summer, reducing energy costs. Materials with high conductivity are chosen for radiators and heat exchangers. Wrong material selection leads to high energy bills and uncomfortable buildings.

 Medium

SET C Extended – Hard (MYP Criteria B & D)

Fourier's Law states that heat flow rate Q/t = kA(ΔT/L). Define each variable and explain what it tells engineers.
Show Answer

k = thermal conductivity (W/m·K) — material property; A = cross-sectional area (m²) — bigger area → more heat; ΔT = temperature difference (K or °C) — bigger gradient → more heat; L = thickness (m) — thicker material → less heat. Engineers use this to calculate insulation thickness, predict heat loss through walls, and design heat exchangers for required performance.

 Hard
A 1 cm thick copper plate (k = 400 W/m·K) and a 1 cm thick glass plate (k = 1 W/m·K) have the same area and the same temperature difference across them. Compare their heat conduction rates.
Show Answer

Using Q/t = kAΔT/L, with A, ΔT, L identical: ratio = k_copper/k_glass = 400/1 = 400. Copper conducts heat 400 times faster than glass under identical conditions. This explains why copper is used in heat sinks and glass/ceramics in thermal insulation.

 Hard
Design an experiment to rank four materials (copper, wood, glass, plastic) by thermal conductivity. Include controls, measurements, and how you would draw conclusions.
Show Answer

Method: Cut 4 rods of equal length, diameter and shape. Heat one end of each simultaneously in boiling water. Attach wax pellets at equal intervals along each rod. Record time for each wax pellet to melt. Controls: Same length, diameter, initial temperature, heating duration. Analysis: Material where wax melts furthest fastest = best conductor. Rank by distance melted in given time.

 Hard
A space suit must protect an astronaut from both extreme cold (shadow, −150 °C) and extreme heat (sunlight, +120 °C). How would conduction principles guide the suit material design?
Show Answer

The suit needs very low thermal conductivity to prevent rapid conduction either way. Multiple layers of insulating materials (e.g., aerogel, vacuum gaps) are used. Each layer traps "dead air" or creates a vacuum to minimise conduction. The suit also uses reflective outer layers (to deal with radiation) but the structural design against conduction uses multiple low-k barriers to slow heat exchange in both directions.

 Hard
Compare conduction in an ionic crystal (like salt) with conduction in a metal. Which is faster and why?
Show Answer

Metals conduct far faster. In ionic crystals, heat is transferred only through lattice vibrations (phonons) — positive and negative ions vibrate but electrons are tightly bound. In metals, free electrons carry thermal energy at much higher speeds. Copper's k ≈ 400 W/m·K vs salt's k ≈ 6 W/m·K — metals are ~65× better conductors.

 Hard
Explain why diamond has the highest thermal conductivity of any natural material (≈2000 W/m·K) despite being a non-metal.
Show Answer

Diamond's exceptionally rigid, symmetrical crystal lattice of covalently bonded carbon atoms conducts phonons (lattice vibrations) with minimal scattering. The strong C–C bonds and perfect lattice symmetry allow vibrations to propagate with extreme efficiency. Since all electrons are covalently bonded, the phonon mechanism alone achieves higher conductivity than most metals.

 Hard
A building has a wall made of three layers: brick (k=0.8), insulation foam (k=0.04), and plaster (k=0.5). Explain how these "thermal resistances" combine to reduce heat loss. (Qualitative answer.)
Show Answer

Each layer adds its own thermal resistance (R = L/k). Layers in series add their resistances. The foam layer, with k = 0.04 (very low), dominates — even if thin, it contributes much more resistance than brick or plaster. Total heat loss = ΔT / total_R. Multiple low-conductivity layers compound the insulating effect, dramatically reducing heat flow through the wall.

 Hard
Why do engineers use fins (extended surfaces) on heat sinks in electronic devices? Connect to conduction and surface area.
Show Answer

Fins increase the surface area available for heat transfer. Since Q/t ∝ A (Fourier's Law), more area means more heat can be conducted from the chip into the fin metal, and then convected into surrounding air. Without fins, heat would build up and damage the chip. Fins are made of high-k metals (aluminium/copper) to maximise internal conduction to the fin tips.

 Hard
Two students argue: Student A says "thicker insulation is always better." Student B says "there is a point of diminishing returns." Who is correct? Explain with reference to Fourier's Law.
Show Answer

Student B is correct. From Q/t = kAΔT/L, doubling L halves heat flow — the first layer of insulation has the greatest impact. As L increases further, the marginal reduction in heat flow decreases. Also, very thick insulation has cost, weight, and space penalties. Engineers optimise to the point where additional thickness provides negligible benefit — the "cost-benefit knee" of the curve.

 Hard
(MYP Criterion D) Reflect on the ethical implications of poor thermal insulation in low-income housing. How does physics knowledge of conduction connect to social justice?
Show Answer

Poor insulation leads to high heat loss in winter → higher heating bills → fuel poverty. Low-income families spend a disproportionate fraction of income on energy. Cold homes cause respiratory illnesses, mould growth, and mental health issues. Physics knowledge (Fourier's Law, R-values) is used by governments to set building standards and energy-efficiency schemes. Access to thermally efficient housing is therefore a social justice issue — understanding conduction science enables policy interventions (insulation grants, building codes) that protect vulnerable communities.

SET A Foundational – Easy

Name three good conductors of heat used in everyday cooking.
Show Answer

Copper (pots/pans), aluminium (baking trays), and stainless steel (cookware) are good conductors used in cooking because they transfer heat quickly and evenly to food.

 Easy
Name three insulators used in the kitchen.
Show Answer

Wooden spoon handles, silicone oven gloves, and plastic pot handles are insulators that prevent burns during cooking.

 Easy
Why do good conductors feel cold to the touch even when at room temperature?
Show Answer

Good conductors quickly draw heat away from your warm hand. The rapid heat loss from your skin makes the material feel cold, even though it is at the same room temperature as insulators.

 Easy
Why is fiberglass used as insulation in walls and attics?
Show Answer

Fiberglass traps air in tiny pockets between its fibres. Air is a very poor conductor, so the fiberglass-air combination greatly reduces heat flow through walls and ceilings.

 Easy
Which is a better conductor — silver or iron? How do you know?
Show Answer

Silver is a better conductor. Its thermal conductivity is ~429 W/m·K versus iron's ~80 W/m·K. Silver's large number of free electrons makes it the best naturally occurring thermal conductor.

 Easy
Why is air used as an insulator in many products?
Show Answer

Air has very low thermal conductivity (~0.025 W/m·K) because its particles are widely spaced and rarely collide. Trapped (still) air prevents convection currents and provides excellent insulation.

 Easy
Why is it dangerous to touch a metal car roof on a very hot sunny day?
Show Answer

Metal is a good conductor — it has absorbed solar radiation and its high conductivity means heat transfers rapidly from the hot metal surface to your skin, causing burns quickly.

 Easy
List four poor conductors (insulators) found in a typical home.
Show Answer

Wood (floors/furniture), rubber (window seals/gloves), polystyrene foam (insulation panels), and wool (carpets/blankets) are all poor conductors found in a home.

 Easy
Why do animals in cold climates have thick fur?
Show Answer

Fur traps air next to the skin. Air is a poor conductor, so it forms an insulating layer that slows heat loss from the animal's warm body to the cold environment.

 Easy
Why are baking trays made from metal rather than plastic?
Show Answer

Metal is a good conductor — it transfers heat from the oven quickly and evenly to the food. Plastic is an insulator and would not transmit enough heat; also plastic would melt at oven temperatures.

SET B Developing – Medium

Explain why free electrons make metals such good thermal conductors compared to non-metals.
Show Answer

In metals, valence electrons are not bound to individual atoms — they form a "sea" of free electrons. When heat is applied, these electrons gain kinetic energy and move rapidly throughout the metal, transferring energy far faster than slow phonon (lattice vibration) conduction alone. Non-metals lack free electrons and rely only on slow lattice vibrations.

 Medium
A sleeping bag uses both feathers and an air-trapping design. Explain how each contributes to insulation.
Show Answer

Feathers (down) are excellent at trapping pockets of still air. The irregular feather structure creates millions of tiny air pockets. Still air is a poor conductor; convection cannot occur in tiny pockets. The sleeping bag thus creates a thick, low-conductivity barrier that dramatically slows heat loss from the body.

 Medium
A student tests thermal conductivity by placing identical wax lumps on the ends of metal, plastic, and glass rods, all heated at the centre. The wax on the metal melts first. Explain this result.
Show Answer

Metal has the highest thermal conductivity — it transfers heat from the centre to the ends fastest, melting the wax soonest. Plastic and glass have far lower conductivities; heat reaches their ends slowly, so the wax stays solid longer.

 Medium
Why are good electrical conductors also usually good thermal conductors?
Show Answer

Both electrical and thermal conductivity in metals are due to free electrons. The same electrons that carry electric charge also carry thermal energy. The Wiedemann–Franz Law formalises this link: electrical conductivity ∝ thermal conductivity for most metals at a given temperature.

 Medium
Explain why birds fluff up their feathers in cold weather.
Show Answer

Fluffing feathers traps more air between them, increasing the thickness of the insulating air layer around the body. Since air is a poor conductor, more trapped air = more insulation = less heat lost to the cold environment. The bird's body heat is retained more effectively.

 Medium
Compare the thermal conductivity of water (~0.6 W/m·K) to air (~0.025 W/m·K) and explain why a person cools down much faster in water than in air at the same temperature.
Show Answer

Water conducts heat ~24× faster than air. When submerged, heat flows from the warm body into the surrounding water much faster than into still air. This is why hypothermia occurs far more rapidly in cold water than cold air at the same temperature.

 Medium
Why does a thermos flask use a vacuum between its walls instead of just air?
Show Answer

Even still air conducts a small amount of heat (k ≈ 0.025 W/m·K). A vacuum has no particles at all, so conduction and convection are impossible (no medium for particle-to-particle transfer). The vacuum provides far better insulation than any gas.

 Medium
Explain why aluminium foil placed under a carpet makes a room warmer.
Show Answer

Aluminium foil reflects infrared radiation, reducing heat loss downward through the floor. Combined with the carpet (insulator above), the combined effect reduces both radiant and conductive heat losses. The shiny surface also limits radiation emission.

 Medium
Why do we use expanded polystyrene (foam) cups for hot drinks rather than metal cups?
Show Answer

Expanded polystyrene is ~98% trapped air by volume — it is an excellent insulator. It slows heat conduction from the hot drink to your hand and slows heat loss from the drink to the environment. A metal cup would conduct heat rapidly — burning your hand and cooling the drink quickly.

 Medium
A house has single-pane windows (glass 4 mm thick) and single-brick walls (230 mm thick). Which loses more heat and why? (k_glass ≈ 1.0 W/m·K; k_brick ≈ 0.7 W/m·K)
Show Answer

Using Q/t ∝ k/L: glass = 1.0/0.004 = 250; brick = 0.7/0.23 = 3.0. Glass conducts heat ~83× faster per unit area than the brick wall. Windows are the primary heat loss path despite smaller area — hence double/triple glazing is the biggest energy improvement in buildings.

SET C Extended – Hard (MYP Criteria B & D)

Aerogel has a thermal conductivity of 0.015 W/m·K — lower than still air (0.025 W/m·K). How is this possible?
Show Answer

Aerogel is a silica nanofoam — ~99.8% air by volume, but the pores are smaller than the mean free path of air molecules (~70 nm). Air molecules cannot move freely between pores (they collide with pore walls before colliding with each other), so convection and normal conduction through the gas phase are suppressed. The silica skeleton is so sparse that solid conduction is negligible. The result: lower effective conductivity than free air.

 Hard
A manufacturer claims their new "nano-insulation" panel is better than conventional foam at 1/10th the thickness. What physical property must it have and what challenges might this create in practice?
Show Answer

It must have a thermal conductivity at most 1/10th that of conventional foam (e.g., if foam k=0.04, nano-panel k ≤ 0.004 W/m·K). Challenges: (1) Such low k materials (aerogels, vacuum panels) are fragile and expensive. (2) Thin panels are susceptible to damage compromising the insulation. (3) Sealing edges is critical — "thermal bridging" at joints could dominate total heat loss. (4) Moisture absorption can degrade performance.

 Hard
Compare and contrast the thermal insulation mechanisms in (a) a double-glazed window, (b) a polar bear's fur, and (c) a vacuum flask.
Show Answer

(a) Double glazing: trapped still air/gas between panes; prevents convection currents; air's low k blocks conduction. (b) Polar bear fur: hollow transparent hairs trap air; fibre structure prevents convection; may also channel UV to dark skin for warming. (c) Vacuum flask: vacuum eliminates conduction and convection; silvered walls reflect IR radiation. All three exploit poor conductors (air or vacuum) but differ in whether they also address radiation.

 Hard
Explain why stainless steel is preferred over pure iron for cookware despite copper being the best conductor.
Show Answer

Copper (k≈400) is expensive, heavy, reacts with acidic foods (forming toxic compounds), and requires polishing. Stainless steel (k≈15) is less conductive but is corrosion-resistant, food-safe, durable, and cheaper. Compromise: high-end cookware uses a copper/aluminium core bonded to a stainless steel exterior — combining conductivity with chemical safety. This illustrates that material selection involves multiple criteria beyond thermal conductivity alone.

 Hard
A wet suit keeps a diver warm in cold water. Explain the physics — why does a thin neoprene layer work better than bare skin, even though neoprene conducts more than air?
Show Answer

Neoprene (k≈0.05) is foam rubber with trapped gas bubbles. Crucially, it prevents the main mechanism of heat loss in water: convection. Cold water constantly circulating away body-warmed water accelerates heat loss dramatically. The wetsuit creates a thin water layer trapped against the skin that warms up and acts as additional insulation. The neoprene slows conduction through this boundary layer, keeping the diver warm despite being in water 24× more conductive than air.

 Hard
Explain "thermal bridging" and why it is a critical concern in building insulation design.
Show Answer

A thermal bridge is a component with significantly higher thermal conductivity than surrounding insulation — e.g., a metal bolt through a wall, a concrete floor slab at the wall junction. Heat "short-circuits" through the bridge, bypassing insulation. Even a small metal bridge can transfer more heat than a large insulated panel area. Engineers use thermal break materials (low-k plastic strips) at bridges, and model buildings with infrared cameras to identify bridge locations — cold spots visible outside = thermal bridges.

 Hard
Why does a metal feel colder than wood at the same temperature? Describe both the physics and the biology of this perception.
Show Answer

Physics: Metal's high k (e.g., 200 W/m·K) draws heat from skin rapidly; wood's low k (0.1 W/m·K) barely does. The rate of heat leaving the skin is proportional to k. Biology: Thermoreceptors in the skin detect heat flux (rate of heat loss), not absolute temperature. Rapid heat loss → cold signal; slow loss → warm signal. The brain interprets high heat-loss rate as "cold surface" even when the object is at identical temperature to a wood surface. This is why stone/tile floors feel cold and wooden floors feel warm.

 Hard
Data: k_silver=429, k_copper=401, k_gold=318, k_aluminium=237, k_iron=80 (all W/m·K). Rank these metals and explain the trend using electron theory.
Show Answer

Rank: Silver > Copper > Gold > Aluminium > Iron. The trend broadly follows the density and mobility of free electrons. Silver has the highest electron mobility of all metals — electrons scatter least from lattice impurities. Copper is nearly equal. Iron's more complex electron structure (d-electrons, magnetic domains) increases electron scattering, reducing conductivity. The correlation between electrical and thermal conductivity (Wiedemann–Franz) holds well across this series.

 Hard
Evaluate whether "natural" insulators (wool, feathers, wood) are truly superior to synthetic insulators (polystyrene, aerogel) for real-world applications. Consider at least three criteria.
Show Answer

Thermal performance: Aerogel (k=0.015) far outperforms wool (k=0.04) or feathers (k=0.03). Sustainability: Natural materials are renewable and biodegradable; synthetic often use fossil fuels. Cost: Natural materials are cheap and widely available; aerogel is expensive. Durability: Wool absorbs moisture (losing insulation); polystyrene is moisture-resistant. Conclusion: Neither is universally superior — application context determines best choice. Buildings may use mineral wool (balance of cost/performance/sustainability); space suits use aerogel (performance critical).

 Hard
(MYP Criterion D) In many developing countries, homes are built with metal roofs which make interiors extremely hot. Discuss the physics of why this happens and propose a low-cost improvement using principles of conduction and insulation.
Show Answer

Metal roofs (k≈50 W/m·K) absorb solar radiation (radiation) rapidly and conduct heat into the interior effectively. No insulation means heat enters unchecked — interiors can reach 50–60 °C. Low-cost improvement 1: Paint roof white/silver — reduces solar absorption (radiation). Improvement 2: Create an air gap (false ceiling of cardboard/palm leaves) — trapped still air insulates conductively. Improvement 3: Layer of sand/soil on roof — high thermal mass slows heat entry. These changes use basic conduction principles, are affordable locally, and can dramatically improve living conditions in hot climates.

SET A Foundational – Easy

Define convection in liquids.
Show Answer

Convection in liquids is the transfer of heat by the movement of the liquid itself. Warm liquid expands, becomes less dense, and rises; cool liquid is denser and sinks, creating a convection current.

 Easy
What happens to a liquid when it is heated?
Show Answer

When heated, liquid particles gain kinetic energy, move faster, spread further apart, and the liquid expands. It becomes less dense than the surrounding cooler liquid.

 Easy
Why does warm water rise and cold water sink?
Show Answer

Warm water is less dense (particles more spread out), so it is more buoyant and rises. Cold water is denser (particles closer together) and sinks. This density difference drives convection currents.

 Easy
Describe a convection current in a pot of water being heated on a stove.
Show Answer

Water at the bottom is heated → becomes less dense → rises to surface → cools at surface → becomes denser → sinks back down → is reheated at the bottom. This cycle continues as a circular convection current, gradually heating all the water.

 Easy
Can convection occur in a solid? Explain why or why not.
Show Answer

No. Convection requires particles to move from place to place, carrying heat with them. In solids, particles are fixed in a lattice and can only vibrate — they cannot flow. Therefore convection cannot occur in solids.

 Easy
Why does a kettle heat water more efficiently than a solid would? (Hint: think about convection.)
Show Answer

In a kettle, the heating element heats water at the bottom. Hot water rises and cooler water sinks to replace it. This convection current continually brings cool water to the heater and distributes hot water throughout, heating the entire kettle much faster than conduction alone would.

 Easy
Where is the heating element placed in most electric kettles, and why?
Show Answer

At the bottom. Heating at the bottom ensures the hottest, least dense water rises immediately, and cooler denser water sinks to replace it — maximising convection currents and heating efficiency.

 Easy
Name two practical systems that use convection in liquids to transfer heat.
Show Answer

(1) Hot water central heating systems (water heated by boiler, rises through pipes to radiators). (2) Car engine cooling systems (coolant heated by engine, rises and flows to radiator to cool).

 Easy
Why does stirring a hot liquid make it cool faster?
Show Answer

Stirring disrupts the natural convection pattern and forces mixing, bringing hot liquid from the interior to the surface where it can lose heat to the air by evaporation and convection. This accelerates cooling.

 Easy
At what temperature does water reach maximum density?
Show Answer

Water reaches its maximum density at 4 °C. Below and above this temperature, water is less dense. This anomalous behaviour is important in lakes freezing — ice forms on the surface, not the bottom.

 Easy

SET B Developing – Medium

In a hot water storage cylinder, the immersion heater is placed at the bottom. Explain step-by-step how the entire tank of water is heated by convection.
Show Answer

1. Heater warms water at the bottom → particles gain KE, expand, become less dense. 2. Less-dense warm water rises. 3. Cooler, denser water at the top descends to the heater. 4. A convection current circulates throughout the tank. 5. Gradually all water heats up. 6. Process continues until thermostat cuts heater off at target temperature.

 Medium
Why would a freezer compartment placed at the bottom of a refrigerator be inefficient? Where is it better placed and why?
Show Answer

Cold air is denser than warm air and sinks. A freezer at the bottom would keep the bottom cold but allow warm air to stay at the top — wasting energy. Placing the freezer at the top ensures cold air sinks throughout the entire cabinet, cooling all contents efficiently through convection.

 Medium
Explain why oceans carry heat from equatorial to polar regions. What type of heat transfer is this?
Show Answer

Warm equatorial water is heated by the Sun, expands (less dense), and flows as surface currents toward the poles. At the poles it cools, becomes denser, and sinks. Cold deep water flows back toward the equator as deep ocean currents. This is large-scale convection — the "ocean conveyor belt" — which distributes vast amounts of thermal energy globally, moderating climate.

 Medium
A dye is dropped into still cold water. When a heat source is placed at the bottom, the dye moves in a circular pattern. Explain this observation.
Show Answer

The dye traces the convection current: heated water at the bottom rises, carrying the dye upward. At the cooler top, the dye-laden water spreads outward, cools, sinks at the sides, and returns to the bottom. The circular dye path visualises the convection cell.

 Medium
Describe how Earth's mantle convects, similar to liquid convection. What effect does this have at the surface?
Show Answer

The mantle (semi-molten rock) is heated from the core → hot rock becomes less dense → rises as plumes → spreads laterally at the base of the crust → cools and sinks back. These convection cells drag tectonic plates, causing continental drift, earthquakes, and volcanic activity at plate boundaries.

 Medium
Why does water in a tall, narrow container take longer to reach boiling point than in a wide, shallow container when heated from the bottom (same volume, same heater)?
Show Answer

In a wide shallow container, the convection current travels a shorter path from bottom to top — hot water reaches the surface quickly and heat is distributed faster. In a tall narrow container, hot water must travel further, and the convection loop is longer; heat distribution is slower.

 Medium
Explain why a hot water radiator (central heating) should be placed on a cold wall/floor, not a warm interior wall.
Show Answer

Placing the radiator on a cold wall/near the floor maximises the temperature gradient between radiator and cold air, driving stronger convection currents. Cold air sinking from the cold wall/floor is immediately heated by the radiator and rises, creating a vigorous convection current that circulates warm air throughout the room efficiently.

 Medium
Why does warming water in a microwave (from the top via microwave energy) sometimes result in uneven heating, unlike a kettle?
Show Answer

Microwaves heat water non-uniformly — energy is absorbed at different depths depending on frequency and water composition. More importantly, if heating from the top, hot water (less dense) stays at the top; cold water below stays cold — convection does not establish because convection needs the heat source at the bottom to drive upward flow. This causes hot spots at the top and cold water at the bottom.

 Medium
Why do lakes in temperate climates "turn over" in autumn and spring? Link to convection and water density.
Show Answer

In summer, warm surface water (less dense) floats on cold deep water — stratification prevents mixing. In autumn, surface cools to 4 °C (maximum density) → sinks → displaces warmer deep water → this rises → lake "turns over," mixing nutrients. In spring, ice melts, surface reaches 4 °C again → sinks → second turnover. These are density-driven convection events critical to lake ecology.

 Medium
Explain why water-based cooling systems are used in car engines rather than air cooling, for high-performance vehicles.
Show Answer

Water has a much higher specific heat capacity (~4200 J/kg°C vs air ~1000 J/kg°C) and higher density, allowing it to absorb and carry away far more heat per unit volume than air. Water also conducts heat better into the coolant channels. High-performance engines generate heat faster than air cooling can remove — water cooling is essential to prevent engine damage.

SET C Extended – Hard (MYP Criteria B & D)

Explain the anomalous expansion of water and how it affects aquatic life in winter. What would happen to fish if water behaved like most liquids?
Show Answer

Most liquids contract as they cool — water also contracts from 100 °C to 4 °C, but between 4 °C and 0 °C it expands as hydrogen bonds form a crystalline lattice. So water at 0 °C (ice) is less dense than at 4 °C. In winter: surface water cools to 4 °C (densest) → sinks. Then surface cools below 4 °C → becomes less dense → stays on top → forms ice. Ice insulates the water below, keeping it above 0 °C. Fish survive in liquid water beneath. If water contracted to 0 °C like most liquids, ice would sink, lakes would freeze solid from the bottom up, killing all aquatic life.

 Hard
Compare the Rayleigh-Bénard convection pattern (cells) with the convection in a simple heated beaker. What determines cell size?
Show Answer

Rayleigh-Bénard convection occurs in a horizontal fluid layer heated from below: at a critical temperature gradient (Rayleigh number > ~1708), organised hexagonal convection cells form. In a beaker, convection is irregular and turbulent. Cell size is determined by fluid depth, viscosity, and thermal diffusivity. Deep/low-viscosity fluids form larger, faster cells. This pattern is observed in Earth's mantle, solar convection zone, and even pot of soup.

 Hard
A student claims: "Convection only occurs when you actively heat a liquid." Critique this using examples of natural convection in nature.
Show Answer

Incorrect. Natural convection occurs whenever a density difference exists, regardless of an active heater. Examples: (1) Ocean thermohaline circulation — driven by temperature and salinity differences. (2) Mantle convection — driven by Earth's internal heat (radioactive decay). (3) Groundwater circulation — heated by geothermal energy. (4) Blood circulation in living organisms is assisted by natural thermal convection. No "active heater" is needed — any heat source creating a density gradient suffices.

 Hard
Design an experiment to demonstrate convection in water using potassium permanganate crystals. Include hypothesis, method, expected result, and one source of error.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: When heated from below, KMnO₄ dye will move in a circular convection current. Method: Fill a beaker with cold water, place one crystal at the bottom, heat gently below the crystal with a Bunsen burner. Observe dye movement. Expected result: Dye rises above the heat source, spreads at the surface, sinks at the sides, returns to the bottom — showing a circular current. Source of error: Crystal may dissolve unevenly, or multiple currents may form if heating is uneven, making the pattern unclear.

 Hard
Explain how thermohaline circulation (the "ocean conveyor belt") regulates European climate. What would happen if it collapsed?
Show Answer

Warm surface water (e.g., Gulf Stream) carries heat from tropics to NW Europe, keeping it ~10 °C warmer than equivalent latitudes. At the North Atlantic, water cools, becomes saltier (sea ice excludes salt), sinks, forming North Atlantic Deep Water. This drives the global conveyor. Collapse (possible from Arctic freshwater influx diluting salinity): NW Europe could cool dramatically (5–10 °C within decades), disrupting agriculture, ecosystems, and storm patterns — a potential climate tipping point linked to global warming.

 Hard
A student heats water in a sealed insulated container (calorimeter). Does convection still occur? Does it still transfer heat? Explain.
Show Answer

Convection still occurs within the water (internal convection currents distribute heat within the liquid), but it does not transfer heat out of the insulated container — insulation blocks conduction and the sealed container prevents evaporative cooling. The internal convection merely homogenises the temperature within the calorimeter, it does not add or remove heat from the system. This is why calorimeters give accurate Q measurements — all heat stays inside.

 Hard
Explain why adding salt to water changes its convection behaviour. How does this relate to ocean dynamics?
Show Answer

Salt increases water density. Saltier water sinks even at higher temperatures — in oceans this "thermohaline" effect means both temperature and salinity drive vertical mixing. In the deep ocean, cold salty water (e.g., Antarctic Bottom Water) is denser than warm fresh water, and sinks to great depths. This double driver (temperature + salinity) creates more complex convection patterns than pure freshwater, and is critical in regulating deep ocean circulation and global heat distribution.

 Hard
During a solar eclipse, the sudden temperature drop can briefly reverse convection currents in surface ocean water. Predict what would happen to sea surface temperature and explain why.
Show Answer

The sudden cooling of the surface layer (loss of solar heating) increases surface water density → surface water sinks rapidly → upwelling of slightly warmer deep water replaces it. Net effect: sea surface temperature may briefly increase as warmer subsurface water is brought up. This is a temporary reversal of the normal stratification — observed as a few tenths of a degree warming in SST during totality before normal patterns re-establish as sunlight returns.

 Hard
A nuclear reactor uses water as both coolant and moderator. Explain why convection in the coolant is critical to reactor safety.
Show Answer

Nuclear fission generates immense heat in the fuel rods. Coolant water flowing around the rods removes heat by convection (forced and natural), preventing fuel meltdown. If coolant flow stops (loss-of-coolant accident), heat cannot be removed → fuel temperature rises → zirconium cladding oxidises → hydrogen explosion risk → radioactive release. Natural convection (passive cooling) is a safety backup: emergency core cooling uses the fact that hot water rises naturally to circulate coolant even without pumps, as in some passive-safety reactor designs.

 Hard
(MYP Criterion D) Climate change is disrupting ocean convection patterns. Reflect on the potential consequences for global weather, food security, and human migration.
Show Answer

Warming oceans reduce density differences → weaker convection currents → weakened thermohaline circulation. Consequences: (1) NW Europe cools while tropics warm — agricultural disruption. (2) Monsoons shift — billions dependent on monsoon rainfall face food insecurity. (3) Reduced upwelling → less nutrient mixing → decline in marine fisheries → threatens protein supply for ~3 billion people. (4) Rising seas + weather pattern shifts → mass migration from low-lying and drought-affected regions. The physics of a simple density-driven current has cascading social, ecological, and political consequences — illustrating how physical science is inseparable from human welfare.

SET A Foundational – Easy

What is a sea breeze and when does it occur?
Show Answer

A sea breeze occurs during the day. Land heats up faster than the sea → air over land becomes warm and less dense → rises. Cooler, denser air from over the sea flows inland to replace it. This cool onshore wind is the sea breeze.

 Easy
What is a land breeze and when does it occur?
Show Answer

A land breeze occurs at night. Land cools faster than the sea → air over land becomes cooler and denser → sinks. The sea remains relatively warm → air over sea rises. Cooler air from land flows out to sea. This offshore wind is the land breeze.

 Easy
Why do hot air balloons rise?
Show Answer

The burner heats the air inside the balloon envelope. Hot air is less dense than the cooler surrounding air. The buoyancy force exceeds the weight of the balloon, so it rises — a direct application of convection principles.

 Easy
Why does warm air rise in the atmosphere?
Show Answer

Warm air expands (particles move faster and spread out), becoming less dense than surrounding cooler air. The denser cold air sinks and displaces the warm air upward — this is buoyancy-driven convection.

 Easy
Name two real-world examples of atmospheric convection at a large scale.
Show Answer

(1) Global wind belts (trade winds, westerlies) — driven by unequal heating of equatorial vs polar regions. (2) Thunderstorm development — rapid updrafts of warm moist air creating cumulonimbus clouds.

 Easy
What causes wind?
Show Answer

Wind is caused by unequal heating of Earth's surface, creating areas of low pressure (warm, rising air) and high pressure (cool, sinking air). Air flows from high to low pressure — this air movement is wind, driven by atmospheric convection.

 Easy
Why do glider pilots seek out "thermals"?
Show Answer

Thermals are columns of warm rising air created by convection over sun-heated ground. Glider pilots circle within thermals to gain altitude without using an engine, using the convective uplift to stay airborne and travel long distances.

 Easy
Why does smoke from a chimney rise straight up on a calm, hot day?
Show Answer

Hot gases from the fire are less dense than surrounding cooler air. Buoyancy carries them straight upward as a convection column. On a calm day, there is no wind to deflect the rising column sideways.

 Easy
In which direction does cooler, denser air move — upward or downward?
Show Answer

Downward. Cooler, denser air is heavier and sinks under gravity, creating downdrafts and high-pressure zones.

 Easy
How does convection in the atmosphere help distribute heat around the Earth?
Show Answer

The equator receives more solar heating → hot air rises at the equator, flows toward poles at high altitude, cools and sinks at higher latitudes, and returns to the equator along the surface as trade winds. This global convection cycle (Hadley cells and other circulation cells) redistributes heat from the hot tropics toward the cooler poles.

 Easy

SET B Developing – Medium

Explain why coastal areas experience sea breezes in the day and land breezes at night, referring to the different heat capacities of land and sea.
Show Answer

Land has a much lower specific heat capacity than water. Daytime: land heats quickly → hot air rises → cool sea air rushes in (sea breeze). Night: land cools quickly → sea (slow to cool) is now relatively warm → air rises over sea → cool land air blows out to sea (land breeze). The switch in breeze direction follows the reversal in which surface is warmer.

 Medium
Explain how a thunderstorm develops using the concept of convection.
Show Answer

1. Sun heats ground → surface air warms and rises rapidly (strong thermal). 2. Warm moist air cools as it rises → water vapour condenses → cumulus cloud grows. 3. Condensation releases latent heat → further accelerates upward convection. 4. Tall cumulonimbus cloud forms → ice crystals collide generating static charge. 5. Lightning and heavy rain result. The entire process is driven by an intense convection cell powered by surface heating.

 Medium
Describe the three main atmospheric circulation cells (Hadley, Ferrel, Polar) and explain how they are driven by convection.
Show Answer

Hadley cell (0–30°): Hot equatorial air rises, flows poleward, cools at ~30° and sinks (creating subtropical deserts), returns to equator as trade winds. Ferrel cell (30–60°): Driven by interaction with Hadley and Polar cells; surface westerlies flow poleward. Polar cell (60–90°): Cold polar air sinks, flows equatorward, meets warm air at polar front. All three are driven by the temperature difference between equator and poles, with Coriolis effect deflecting winds.

 Medium
Why are deserts found at around 30° North and South latitude? Link to atmospheric convection.
Show Answer

At 30° latitude, dry air that rose at the equator (Hadley cell) has cooled and descends. Descending air warms adiabatically (compresses), lowering relative humidity and preventing cloud formation and rain. The persistent high pressure from sinking dry air creates the world's major subtropical deserts (Sahara, Arabian, Sonoran, Australian).

 Medium
How does a valley breeze differ from a mountain breeze? Describe the daily cycle.
Show Answer

Day (valley breeze): Sun heats valley slopes → air rises up the slopes → air from the valley floor flows upslope to replace it. Night (mountain breeze): Slopes cool rapidly by radiation → cold dense air sinks down into the valley → a cool breeze flows downslope. This daily reversal is a small-scale version of sea/land breeze, driven by differential heating of slope vs valley air.

 Medium
Why are windmills and wind turbines often more effective near coastlines or mountain passes?
Show Answer

Define specific heat capacity.
Show Answer

Specific heat capacity (c) is the amount of heat energy (in joules) needed to raise the temperature of 1 kilogram of a substance by 1 °C (or 1 K). Unit: J/kg°C or J/kg·K.

Explain at a molecular level why water has an unusually high specific heat capacity compared to other liquids.
Show Answer

Water molecules form strong hydrogen bonds (H–O–H…O) that require energy to disrupt. When heat is added, much of it goes into breaking/distorting hydrogen bonds rather than increasing translational speed (temperature). Water also has three modes of molecular motion (translation, rotation, vibration), all of which absorb energy. Liquids without hydrogen bonding (e.g., ethanol, oils) have far fewer intermolecular bonds to break — more energy goes directly into translational kinetic energy → faster temperature rise → lower apparent SHC. Water's c = 4200 J/kg°C; ethanol = 2440 J/kg°C; mercury = 140 J/kg°C.
