What is the pH range for acids, neutral substances, and bases?Easy
Show Answer

Acids: pH < 7. Neutral: pH = 7. Bases: pH > 7.

What does an acid do to litmus paper?Easy
Show AnswerAcids turn blue litmus paper red.

What does a base do to litmus paper?Easy
Show AnswerBases turn red litmus paper blue.

Name three common acids with their chemical formulas.Easy
Show AnswerHydrochloric acid (HCl), sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), nitric acid (HNO₃). Also: acetic acid (CH₃COOH), carbonic acid (H₂CO₃).

Name three common bases with their chemical formulas.Easy
Show AnswerSodium hydroxide (NaOH), potassium hydroxide (KOH), calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂). Also: ammonia solution (NH₃).

What ion do acids produce in water?Easy
Show AnswerHydrogen ions / hydronium ions (H⁺ or H₃O⁺).

What ion do bases produce in water?Easy
Show AnswerHydroxide ions (OH⁻).

What is an indicator? Name two common indicators.Easy
Show AnswerA substance that changes colour depending on the pH of a solution. Examples: litmus (red in acid, blue in base), universal indicator (range of colours across pH scale).

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Distinguish between a strong acid and a weak acid with examples.Medium
Show AnswerStrong acid: completely dissociates in water, producing high [H⁺] — e.g., HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃. Weak acid: partially dissociates, equilibrium exists — e.g., acetic acid (CH₃COOH), carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). Same concentration of strong acid has lower pH than weak acid.

Explain the Arrhenius and Brønsted-Lowry definitions of acids and bases.Medium
Show AnswerArrhenius: acid produces H⁺ in water; base produces OH⁻. Brønsted-Lowry (more general): acid is a proton (H⁺) donor; base is a proton acceptor. Brønsted-Lowry includes reactions not in water (e.g., NH₃ + HCl → NH₄Cl — NH₃ accepts H⁺ but produces no OH⁻).

Explain why pure water is neutral and has a pH of 7.Medium
Show AnswerPure water auto-ionises slightly: H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻. At 25°C, [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ mol/L. pH = −log[H⁺] = −log(10⁻⁷) = 7. Equal concentrations of H⁺ and OH⁻ means neither acidic nor basic.

Why does diluting an acid with water reduce its acidity?Medium
Show AnswerAdding water increases the volume of solution, spreading the same number of H⁺ ions over a larger volume. Concentration of H⁺ decreases → pH increases → solution becomes less acidic. The number of H⁺ ions does not change, only their concentration.

Describe how universal indicator can determine the approximate pH of a solution.Medium
Show AnswerUniversal indicator is a mixture of several indicators that show different colours at different pH values (red at pH 1–3, orange/yellow at 4–6, green at 7, blue at 8–10, violet at 11–14). Comparing the colour of the solution to a pH chart gives an approximate pH value.

Explain how bee stings and wasp stings differ in chemistry, and how each can be treated.Medium
Show AnswerBee stings: acidic (formic acid, methanoic acid) — treat with a mild base (baking soda/NaHCO₃). Wasp stings: alkaline — treat with a mild acid (vinegar/dilute acetic acid). Neutralisation relieves pain in both cases.

What is the difference between an alkali and a base?Medium
Show AnswerA base is any substance that reacts with acids to form salt and water (may be insoluble — e.g., CuO). An alkali is a base that is soluble in water (dissolves to give OH⁻ ions) — e.g., NaOH, KOH. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.

Explain why rainwater is naturally slightly acidic (pH ~5.6) even without pollution.Medium
Show AnswerCO₂ from the atmosphere dissolves in rainwater: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. This natural carbonic acid gives rainwater a pH of ~5.6. This is distinct from acid rain (pH 4–5) caused by industrial SO₂ and NO₂ pollution.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Critically compare the Arrhenius, Brønsted-Lowry, and Lewis definitions of acids and bases, explaining why each more general definition was needed.Hard
Show AnswerArrhenius: limited to aqueous solutions. Brønsted-Lowry: extended to any proton transfer (e.g., NH₃ + HCl in gas phase). Lewis (most general): acid = electron pair acceptor; base = electron pair donor — covers reactions with no proton transfer (e.g., BF₃ + NH₃ → BF₃·NH₃; BF₃ is a Lewis acid). Each definition expands to cover reactions the previous one could not explain.

Explain the concept of buffer solutions and their biological importance, using blood pH as an example.Hard
Show AnswerA buffer resists pH change when small amounts of acid or base are added — contains a weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid). Blood pH is maintained at 7.35–7.45 by the carbonate-bicarbonate buffer: H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Adding H⁺: HCO₃⁻ absorbs it. Adding OH⁻: H₂CO₃ donates H⁺ to neutralise it. Deviation outside pH 7.0–7.8 is fatal — respiratory/metabolic acidosis or alkalosis.

Discuss how the pH scale is logarithmic and what this means for comparing acidic solutions.Hard
Show AnswerpH = −log₁₀[H⁺]. Each unit change in pH = 10-fold change in [H⁺]. pH 3 has 10× more H⁺ than pH 4, and 100× more than pH 5. A stomach acid at pH 1 is 1,000× more acidic than black coffee at pH 4. This logarithmic nature means small pH changes represent enormous chemical differences — important in biological systems where enzymes are pH-sensitive.

Analyse how acid-base indicators work at the molecular level, using phenolphthalein as an example.Hard
Show AnswerPhenolphthalein (HIn) is itself a weak acid. In acidic solution, it exists predominantly as HIn (colourless); in basic solution, it loses H⁺ to become In⁻ (pink). The colour change is due to structural changes in the conjugated double-bond system of In⁻ that absorbs visible light differently. pH of transition depends on the Ka of the indicator.

Evaluate the dangers of mixing household acids (e.g., toilet bowl cleaners, HCl) with household bases (e.g., bleach, NaOCl) accidentally.Hard
Show AnswerHCl + NaOCl → NaCl + HOCl; in excess acid: HOCl + HCl → Cl₂↑ + H₂O. Chlorine gas is a toxic, corrosive respiratory irritant. Similar reactions occur between acidic cleaners and ammonia-based cleaners (produce toxic chloramines). These represent serious household chemical hazards — reinforcing that acid-base chemistry has life-and-death practical implications.

Discuss how ocean acidification (a global acid-base chemistry problem) threatens marine ecosystems.Hard
Show AnswerCO₂ dissolves in ocean: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1 (26% more acidic). Lower pH reduces CO₃²⁻ availability — corals, molluscs, and foraminifera cannot build CaCO₃ shells/skeletons: CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻. Coral bleaching and shell dissolution threaten 25% of marine species and the fisheries supporting 3 billion people's protein needs.

Critically evaluate the statement: "Neutralisation always produces a neutral (pH 7) solution."Hard
Show AnswerFalse. Mixing equivalent moles of acid and base produces a salt, but the salt's pH depends on hydrolysis. Strong acid + strong base → neutral salt (e.g., NaCl, pH 7). Weak acid + strong base → basic salt (e.g., sodium acetate, pH > 7; CH₃COO⁻ hydrolyses: CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻). Strong acid + weak base → acidic salt (e.g., NH₄Cl, pH < 7). "Neutralisation" means the acid-base reaction occurs; the resulting pH depends on the strength of both participants.

Analyse the role of gastric acid (HCl) in digestion and explain the chemistry behind antacid treatments.Hard
Show AnswerStomach HCl (pH 1.5–3.5): denatures proteins (unfolding enables pepsin access), activates pepsinogen → pepsin (protein digestion), kills pathogens. Antacids: contain bases that neutralise excess HCl. Examples: CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate — Tums): CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. Mg(OH)₂ (milk of magnesia): Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O. NaHCO₃ (sodium bicarbonate): NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ (CO₂ released causes burping).

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Write the general word equation for neutralisation.Easy
Show AnswerAcid + Base → Salt + Water

Write the net ionic equation for any neutralisation reaction.Easy
Show AnswerH⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)

Write the balanced equation for HCl + NaOH.Easy
Show AnswerHCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

Is neutralisation an exothermic or endothermic reaction? How do you know?Easy
Show AnswerExothermic — the temperature of the solution rises when an acid and base are mixed. The formation of H₂O from H⁺ and OH⁻ releases energy (~57 kJ/mol).

What is an antacid? Name two examples.Easy
Show AnswerA medicine (base) that neutralises excess stomach acid (HCl). Examples: Mg(OH)₂ (milk of magnesia), CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate), NaHCO₃ (sodium bicarbonate).

What is titration?Easy
Show AnswerA laboratory technique to determine the concentration of an acid (or base) by carefully adding a base (or acid) of known concentration until neutralisation is complete, indicated by an indicator colour change.

Name the salt formed when H₂SO₄ reacts with KOH.Easy
Show AnswerPotassium sulphate (K₂SO₄). H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O.

Explain why neutralisation is important in treating indigestion (excess stomach acid).Easy
Show AnswerIndigestion is caused by excess HCl in the stomach. Antacids contain bases (Mg(OH)₂, CaCO₃) that neutralise HCl: Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O, reducing acidity and relieving pain.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain what happens during a titration at the molecular level, step by step.Medium
Show AnswerBase added to acid: OH⁻ from base reacts with H⁺ in acid forming H₂O; [H⁺] decreases; pH rises. Before equivalence point: excess H⁺ remains, solution acidic. At equivalence point: H⁺ and OH⁻ completely consumed; indicator changes colour (e.g., phenolphthalein turns pink). After equivalence point: excess OH⁻ makes solution basic.

A 25 mL of 0.1 mol/L HCl is titrated with 0.1 mol/L NaOH. Calculate the volume of NaOH needed for complete neutralisation.Medium
Show AnswerMoles HCl = 0.025 L × 0.1 mol/L = 0.0025 mol. HCl:NaOH = 1:1 → 0.0025 mol NaOH needed. Volume NaOH = 0.0025/0.1 = 0.025 L = 25 mL.

Explain why farmers add calcium hydroxide (lime) to acidic soil.Medium
Show AnswerAcidic soils have pH too low for most crops. Ca(OH)₂ (lime) is a base that neutralises soil acids: Ca(OH)₂ + 2H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + 2H₂O. This raises soil pH toward neutral (~6.5–7), optimising nutrient availability and enzyme activity for plant roots.

Write balanced equations for the neutralisation of H₂SO₄ with: (a) NaOH, (b) Ca(OH)₂.Medium
Show Answer(a) H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O. (b) H₂SO₄ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaSO₄ + 2H₂O.

Explain the difference between the equivalence point and the end point in a titration.Medium
Show AnswerEquivalence point: the exact stoichiometric point where moles of acid equal moles of base (H⁺ = OH⁻). End point: the observed colour change of the indicator. These ideally coincide but may differ slightly if the indicator is not chosen correctly (its pKa should be near the expected pH at equivalence).

Why does CaCO₃ work as an antacid but NaOH is NOT used?Medium
Show AnswerCaCO₃ is a mild, insoluble base — reacts slowly and specifically with HCl without raising pH excessively: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. NaOH is a strong alkali — dissolves rapidly, too corrosive for ingestion, would dangerously raise pH, and could cause chemical burns to the oesophagus.

Explain how neutralisation is used industrially to treat acidic factory effluents before releasing them into rivers.Medium
Show AnswerAcidic waste water (from metal pickling, pharmaceutical production) is treated with lime (Ca(OH)₂) or sodium carbonate in settling tanks. Neutralisation raises pH to acceptable levels (6.5–8.5 for discharge); metal ions may precipitate as hydroxides. Ensures river ecosystems are not harmed by pH extremes or dissolved metals.

Describe the process of acid-base titration and explain why the burette and indicator are both necessary.Medium
Show AnswerBurette: allows precise, controlled addition of titrant with accurate volume measurement (to ±0.05 mL). Indicator: provides a visual signal of when the equivalence point is reached. Without the burette, volumes cannot be measured precisely; without the indicator, there is no way to determine when neutralisation is complete. Both are essential for accurate quantitative results.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Explain why the pH curve for a strong acid–strong base titration differs from a weak acid–strong base titration near the equivalence point.Hard
Show AnswerStrong acid–strong base (HCl vs NaOH): sharp pH jump at equivalence point (pH 7); simple H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O, no buffering. Weak acid–strong base (CH₃COOH vs NaOH): before equivalence point, buffer region (weak acid + conjugate base) resists pH change; equivalence point pH > 7 (conjugate base hydrolyses: CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻). pH jump is less steep — requires careful indicator selection (phenolphthalein appropriate; methyl orange not).

Discuss the chemistry of acid mine drainage (AMD) and explain how neutralisation can be used to remediate it.Hard
Show AnswerAMD: pyrite (FeS₂) in mine tailings oxidises in air/water: 4FeS₂ + 15O₂ + 14H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃ + 8H₂SO₄. Resulting water has pH 2–4, rich in dissolved metals (Fe, Zn, As). Remediation: add CaCO₃ or Ca(OH)₂ to neutralise H₂SO₄ → Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ (gypsum); raise pH causes metal hydroxide precipitation: Fe³⁺ + 3OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₃↓. Active treatment is ongoing cost; passive treatment (limestone beds, constructed wetlands) is lower-cost long-term solution.

Evaluate the use of sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃) as an antacid — advantages and disadvantages compared to CaCO₃.Hard
Show AnswerNaHCO₃: fast-acting (soluble), immediate relief; NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ (causes burping — CO₂ release). Disadvantages: CO₂ distension; Na⁺ load problematic for hypertensive or heart failure patients; if absorbed, can cause metabolic alkalosis; only short-acting. CaCO₃: slower (less soluble), longer-acting; provides Ca²⁺ supplementation; no Na⁺ risk; can cause constipation; occasional hypercalcaemia risk with high dose. CaCO₃ is generally preferred for chronic use.

Analyse the role of neutralisation in the Solvay process for producing sodium carbonate (washing soda).Hard
Show AnswerThe Solvay process involves: NaCl(aq) + NH₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → NaHCO₃↓ + NH₄Cl (NaHCO₃ precipitates). Heating: 2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ (decomposition). NH₃ recovery: 2NH₄Cl + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCl₂ + 2NH₃ + 2H₂O (neutralisation of NH₄⁺ salt with lime recovers NH₃ for reuse). Neutralisation is integral to the NH₃ recycling step — key to the process's economic viability.

Critically evaluate whether the enthalpy of neutralisation (−57 kJ/mol for strong acid/strong base) varies for weak acid/base reactions, and explain why.Hard
Show AnswerStrong acid + strong base: −57 kJ/mol, corresponding to H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O for fully dissociated ions. Weak acid + strong base: less exothermic (e.g., CH₃COOH + NaOH ≈ −55.8 kJ/mol) because some energy must be used to dissociate the weak acid before neutralisation (endothermic ionisation step partially offsets the exothermic water formation). Weak acid + weak base: even less exothermic — both ionisation steps absorb energy. The enthalpy of neutralisation reflects the net of ionisation energy and H₂O bond formation energy.

Discuss how back-titration technique extends neutralisation chemistry to solid samples that dissolve slowly.Hard
Show AnswerBack-titration: add excess acid of known amount to the solid sample; allow complete reaction; titrate remaining (unreacted) acid with standard base to find how much acid reacted with the sample. Example: determining purity of antacid tablet — add excess HCl to tablet, then titrate remaining HCl with NaOH. Moles of HCl that reacted = moles reacted with antacid → calculate purity. Used when direct titration is impractical due to slow dissolution, poor indicator response, or coloured solutions.

Evaluate how neutralisation chemistry is applied in the treatment of soil affected by acid rain in agricultural contexts.Hard
Show AnswerAcid rain (pH 4–5, H₂SO₄/HNO₃) leaches calcium, magnesium, and potassium from soil; mobilises toxic Al³⁺ which damages plant roots. Liming (CaCO₃ or Ca(OH)₂) neutralises soil acids, raises pH, replaces leached Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺, and precipitates Al³⁺ as Al(OH)₃. Dose must be calculated — overliming creates alkaline soil (pH > 8) releasing toxic Mo, reducing Fe/Mn availability. Liming must be repeated as acid deposition continues — treatment of symptom rather than cause (requires reducing SO₂/NOₓ emissions).

Analyse how proton transfer in neutralisation relates to the concept of conjugate acid-base pairs, using the reaction of ammonia with hydrochloric acid as an example.Hard
Show AnswerNH₃ + HCl → NH₄⁺ + Cl⁻. Brønsted-Lowry: HCl (acid) donates H⁺ to NH₃ (base). Conjugate pairs: HCl/Cl⁻ (HCl loses H⁺ → conjugate base Cl⁻); NH₃/NH₄⁺ (NH₃ gains H⁺ → conjugate acid NH₄⁺). The strength of the conjugate base (Cl⁻, very weak) explains why the forward reaction goes almost to completion — stronger acids have weaker conjugate bases. This framework explains why NH₄Cl solution is mildly acidic (NH₄⁺ is a weak acid, donates H⁺ back to water).

What gas is produced when an acid reacts with a metal? How do you test for it?Easy
Show AnswerHydrogen gas. Test: a lit splint placed at the mouth of the test tube produces a squeaky "pop" sound.

What are the products when an acid reacts with a metal oxide (base)?Easy

Write the equation for CuO + H₂SO₄.Easy

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why concentrated H₂SO₄ reacts differently with zinc compared to dilute H₂SO₄.Medium
Show AnswerDilute H₂SO₄ + Zn: Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂ (H⁺ ions oxidise Zn, releasing H₂). Concentrated H₂SO₄ + Zn: Zn + 2H₂SO₄(conc) → ZnSO₄ + SO₂↑ + 2H₂O. Concentrated H₂SO₄ is an oxidising acid — SO₄²⁻ is reduced to SO₂ instead of H⁺ being reduced to H₂. No hydrogen gas is produced.

Explain why the reaction of CaCO₃ with H₂SO₄ slows down and stops even with excess acid present.Medium
Show AnswerCaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂. Calcium sulphate (CaSO₄) is sparingly soluble — it forms an insoluble white crust around the CaCO₃ particles, blocking further acid contact. The reaction stops (passivation). This is why HCl (producing soluble CaCl₂) is used to dissolve limestone, not H₂SO₄.

Write the equation for the reaction of NaHCO₃ with HCl and explain a practical application.Medium
Show AnswerNaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑. Application: baking soda (NaHCO₃) in baked goods reacts with acidic components (buttermilk, vinegar), releasing CO₂ that makes dough rise. Also used as antacid (reacts with stomach HCl).

Explain why hydrogen gas is produced when metals react with acids, but not with metal oxides.Medium
Show AnswerWith metals: H⁺ from acid accepts electrons from the metal: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻; 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ (displacement reaction; hydrogen displaced from acid). With metal oxides: the oxide anion (O²⁻) reacts with H⁺ to form water — no electron transfer to H⁺; no H₂ produced.

Why does magnesium react more vigorously than iron with dilute HCl? Explain using reactivity.Medium
Show AnswerMg is higher than Fe in the reactivity series — greater tendency to lose electrons. Mg reacts rapidly: Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂ (fast, vigorous bubbling). Fe reacts slowly: Fe + 2HCl → FeCl₂ + H₂ (slow, gentle bubbling). More reactive metal = faster rate of electron loss = faster rate of H₂ production.

Describe the test for carbon dioxide gas produced when acid reacts with a carbonate.Medium
Show AnswerBubble the gas through limewater (Ca(OH)₂ solution). If CO₂ is present, limewater turns milky (white precipitate): Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O. If excess CO₂ is passed, the precipitate dissolves: CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca(HCO₃)₂ (soluble).

Explain why copper does not react with dilute HCl but iron does.Medium
Show AnswerCopper is below hydrogen in the reactivity series — insufficient tendency to lose electrons to H⁺ ions; Cu cannot displace hydrogen from acid. Iron is above hydrogen — greater tendency to lose electrons, allowing H⁺ to be reduced to H₂ while Fe is oxidised to Fe²⁺.

Write and explain the reaction between H₃PO₄ and Ca(OH)₂ — what salt is formed and what is its importance?Medium
Show Answer2H₃PO₄ + 3Ca(OH)₂ → Ca₃(PO₄)₂ + 6H₂O. Salt: calcium phosphate (Ca₃(PO₄)₂). Importance: component of bones and teeth (hydroxyapatite). Also, triple superphosphate fertiliser is made by reacting phosphate rock (Ca₃(PO₄)₂) with H₃PO₄ — the resulting soluble calcium dihydrogen phosphate (Ca(H₂PO₄)₂) is directly available to plants.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Analyse why concentrated nitric acid and concentrated sulphuric acid (a mixture called aqua regia when HNO₃ is mixed with HCl instead) can dissolve gold, even though dilute acids cannot.Hard
Show AnswerDilute acids dissolve metals by H⁺ accepting electrons — gold's reduction potential (+1.52 V) is too high for H⁺ (0 V). Aqua regia (3:1 HCl:HNO₃): HNO₃ provides NO₃⁻ as strong oxidising agent (reduction potential +0.96 V) while Cl⁻ complexes Au³⁺ as AuCl₄⁻, removing it from equilibrium and driving the reaction: Au + HNO₃ + 4HCl → HAuCl₄ + NO↑ + 2H₂O. The combination of strong oxidation AND complexation makes it thermodynamically and kinetically feasible.

Discuss the industrial production of HCl by the Mannheim process and explain how the reaction of acids with metals is reversed industrially.Hard
Show AnswerMannheim process: NaCl + H₂SO₄ → NaHSO₄ + HCl↑ (at 150°C); NaHSO₄ + NaCl → Na₂SO₄ + HCl↑ (at 540°C). This is a displacement — H₂SO₄ displaces HCl from its salt. It "reverses" the typical acid + metal narrative by using a stronger acid (H₂SO₄) to displace a weaker acid's anion (Cl⁻) from its salt. Na₂SO₄ (salt cake) is a useful byproduct for glass manufacture.

Critically assess the reaction of metals with acids as a laboratory source of hydrogen gas — advantages and limitations.Hard
Show AnswerAdvantages: simple apparatus; cheap reagents (Zn + dilute H₂SO₄); room temperature; controllable rate. Limitations: H₂ produced contains acid vapour (need drying with CaCl₂/conc H₂SO₄); cannot use conc H₂SO₄ or HNO₃ (oxidising acids — SO₂/NO₂ produced instead); Cu/Ag/Au do not react (limited to reactive metals); scale-up is energy-intensive and impractical. Industrial H₂ comes from steam reforming of methane: CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂ (much more economical).

Explain the reaction of non-metallic oxides (acidic oxides) with bases, and contrast with metal oxides reacting with acids.Hard
Show AnswerMetal oxides: basic oxides — react with acids: CuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂O. Non-metal oxides: acidic oxides — react with bases: CO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O; SO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaSO₃ + H₂O. The pattern: acidic oxides (non-metals) neutralised by bases; basic oxides (metals) neutralised by acids. Some oxides are amphoteric (e.g., Al₂O₃, ZnO) — react with both acids and bases.

Analyse the chemistry of acid etching of copper PCBs (printed circuit boards) and explain why FeCl₃ is preferred over HCl as the etching acid.Hard
Show AnswerPCB etching: removes unprotected copper to reveal circuit pattern. HCl + H₂O₂ system: slow, unstable, H₂O₂ decomposes. FeCl₃ system: FeCl₃ + Cu → FeCl₂ + CuCl₂ (iron displaces copper — not acid-metal, but redox displacement). FeCl₃ is preferred: fast, controlled, room temperature, no H₂ gas produced, FeCl₂/CuCl₂ solution can be treated to recover copper and regenerate FeCl₃ (electrolysis: FeCl₂ + Cl₂ → FeCl₃). More sustainable and safer than HCl-based etching.

Discuss how the reaction of acids with carbonates is exploited to test for carbonate minerals in geology (fizz test), including its limitations.Hard
Show AnswerGeologists apply dilute HCl to rock samples: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂ (effervescence confirms carbonate). Identifies limestone, chalk, marble, and calcite veins in igneous/metamorphic rocks. Limitations: reaction with dolomite (CaMg(CO₃)₂) is slow with cold dilute acid (needs hot conc HCl); distinguishing between CaCO₃ and MgCO₃ requires further testing; other carbonates (FeCO₃ — siderite) react but less vigorously; the test is qualitative, not quantitative.

Evaluate the environmental impact of acid reacting with carbonate rocks in landscapes (karst geomorphology), linking to the carbon cycle.Hard
Show AnswerNaturally acidic rainwater (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃): CaCO₃ + H₂CO₃ → Ca(HCO₃)₂ (soluble) — dissolves limestone, creating caves, sinkholes, and karst landscapes (e.g., Meghalaya, Guilin, Dolomites). This weathering is a major component of the long-term carbon cycle — pulling CO₂ from atmosphere. Ca(HCO₃)₂ carried to oceans is precipitated as CaCO₃ by marine organisms. Over geological time, this silicate weathering thermostat has regulated atmospheric CO₂ and prevented runaway warming.

Critically evaluate whether the reaction of acids with metals is a viable industrial method for producing hydrogen for the emerging hydrogen economy.Hard
Show AnswerNot viable at scale. Reasons: metal consumption is expensive (zinc costs ~$2,500/tonne; produces H₂ at $50+/kg vs. ~$1–4/kg for steam reforming); acids are consumed (cost and safety); metal salt waste (ZnSO₄, FeSO₄) needs disposal; low energy efficiency. Green hydrogen via electrolysis powered by renewables (~$2–5/kg, declining rapidly) is the viable alternative. Acid-metal reactions remain useful only in laboratory demonstrations, emergency hydrogen generation, and niche chemical synthesis contexts.

What is the chemical name and formula for table salt?Easy

What is the chemical name and formula for baking soda?Easy
Show AnswerSodium hydrogen carbonate (sodium bicarbonate), NaHCO₃.

What is washing soda and what is its formula?Easy
Show AnswerSodium carbonate decahydrate, Na₂CO₃·10H₂O. Used for softening hard water and as a cleaning agent.

What is lime water and what is its formula?Easy

What is plaster of Paris and what is its formula?Easy
Show AnswerCalcium sulphate hemihydrate: CaSO₄·½H₂O. Used in plaster casts (medical) and construction.

What is limestone and what is its formula?Easy
Show AnswerCalcium carbonate, CaCO₃. A naturally occurring salt; used in cement, glass, and steel making.

Name the salt formed from H₂SO₄ + Mg(OH)₂.Easy

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain the role of NaHCO₃ (baking soda) in cooking, relating to its chemistry.Medium
Show AnswerNaHCO₃ reacts with acidic ingredients (buttermilk, lemon juice, vinegar) or is thermally decomposed: 2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ (above 60°C). CO₂ bubbles create pockets in dough, making baked goods rise and become light/fluffy. Baking powder also contains a dry acid (cream of tartar) to ensure CO₂ release without requiring an acidic ingredient in the recipe.

Explain why CaSO₄·½H₂O (plaster of Paris) sets hard when water is added.Medium
Show AnswerCaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O (gypsum). The setting is a hydration reaction — hemihydrate absorbs water, recrystallising to gypsum. Interlocking crystals of gypsum form a rigid structure. The reaction is slightly exothermic (warmth felt when cast sets). This rigid structure makes it ideal for moulds, orthopaedic casts, and dental impressions.

Explain the water-softening role of Na₂CO₃ (washing soda).Medium
Show AnswerHard water contains Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions. Washing soda precipitates them as insoluble carbonates: Ca²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → CaCO₃↓; Mg²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → MgCO₃↓. Removing Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ prevents them from reacting with soap (scum formation) and scale deposition in pipes/boilers. Na₂CO₃ is cheap and effective for large-scale water treatment.

Explain the difference between anhydrous and hydrated salts, using CuSO₄ as an example.Medium
Show AnswerHydrated salt: contains water molecules in its crystal structure as water of crystallisation. CuSO₄·5H₂O (blue) — five H₂O per formula unit in the lattice, stabilising the crystal. Anhydrous: no water of crystallisation. CuSO₄ (white) — water removed by gentle heating. Adding water to anhydrous CuSO₄ restores blue colour — used to test for presence of water.

How is NaCl obtained industrially from seawater? Describe the process.Medium
Show AnswerSolar evaporation: seawater led into shallow pans; sun and wind evaporate water, increasing salt concentration; NaCl crystallises when its solubility limit is exceeded; harvested, washed, and dried. Alternative: underground salt mining (rock salt) and solution mining (injecting water into salt deposits, pumping out brine). India is the world's third-largest salt producer.

Name the acid and base that produce each salt: (a) ZnSO₄, (b) NH₄Cl, (c) Ca(NO₃)₂.Medium
Show Answer(a) ZnSO₄: H₂SO₄ + Zn(OH)₂ (or ZnO or Zn). (b) NH₄Cl: HCl + NH₃. (c) Ca(NO₃)₂: HNO₃ + Ca(OH)₂.

Explain why "normal" salts, acidic salts, and basic salts are different, with one example of each.Medium
Show AnswerNormal salt: all H⁺ of acid replaced — NaCl (from HCl + NaOH). Acidic salt: only some H⁺ replaced (from diprotic/polyprotic acid) — NaHSO₄, NaHCO₃ (from H₂SO₄/H₂CO₃ partially neutralised). Basic salt: some OH⁻ of base retained — Pb(OH)Cl (basic lead chloride). Acidic salts still contain ionisable H⁺.

Describe the industrial importance of NaCl beyond its use as table salt.Medium
Show AnswerNaCl is the raw material for the chlor-alkali industry (NaOH, Cl₂, H₂ — all from brine electrolysis). Used to manufacture PVC (from Cl₂), bleach (NaOCl), and many other chlorine-based chemicals. Preserving food (salting/curing). De-icing roads in cold countries. Manufacturing sodium carbonate (Solvay process), soda ash, and glass.

Discuss the industrial importance of CaCO₃ (limestone) across three different industries, with chemical equations.Hard
Show Answer1. Cement: CaCO₃ heated → CaO + CO₂; CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (belite) — forms cement clinker. 2. Steel: limestone added to blast furnace removes silica impurities: CaCO₃ → CaO; CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (slag — floats on molten iron). 3. Glass: Na₂CO₃ + CaCO₃ + SiO₂ heated → sodium calcium silicate (glass). Also: water treatment (raises pH), paper, plastics filler, CO₂ source for food industry.

Analyse the chemistry of kidney stones (mostly CaC₂O₄, calcium oxalate) and explain how dietary changes can prevent them.Hard
Show AnswerKidney stones form when [Ca²⁺][C₂O₄²⁻] exceeds the solubility product (Ksp) of CaC₂O₄ (Ksp = 2.34×10⁻⁹). Risk factors: high oxalate diet (spinach, nuts, chocolate); dehydration (concentrates urine); high Ca²⁺ or low urinary citrate. Prevention: high water intake (dilutes ions); reduce dietary oxalate; citrate supplementation (citrate complexes Ca²⁺, reducing free Ca²⁺ available to form stones); thiazide diuretics reduce urinary Ca²⁺.

Evaluate the environmental consequences of large-scale salt mining and evaporation operations.Hard
Show AnswerSolar salt pans: create unique hypersaline habitats (flamingos use them) but can displace coastal mangroves and wetlands. Saline brine discharge harms freshwater ecosystems. Underground salt mining: can cause subsidence (surface collapse) above caverns. Solution mining creates underground cavities used for natural gas storage (risk of brine leaks). Energy consumption for industrial salt drying/processing contributes to CO₂ emissions. Salt dust from salt flats in windy areas causes local air pollution and soil salinisation.

Explain the chemistry of chalk, limestone, and marble as different forms of CaCO₃ and why they behave differently in acid.Hard
Show AnswerAll three are CaCO₃ but differ in crystal structure and purity: chalk is biogenic (microorganism shells), porous, soft (low metamorphic grade). Limestone is sedimentary, denser, harder. Marble is metamorphic (recrystallised under heat/pressure), very dense, pure, crystalline — interlocking calcite crystals. All react: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. Chalk reacts fastest (high surface area, porous). Marble reacts slowest (dense, low surface area). Same chemistry, different kinetics.

Discuss how the chloride, sulphate, and carbonate salts of calcium differ in solubility and explain the practical consequences of these differences.Hard
Show AnswerCaCl₂: very soluble (745 g/L) — used as road de-icer, food additive, desiccant. CaSO₄: sparingly soluble (2 g/L) — forms scale in boilers and pipelines; used as plaster of Paris/gypsum. CaCO₃: practically insoluble (0.013 g/L) — forms stalactites, kidney stones; serves as antacid. Solubility determines where each salt is found in nature, how it is manufactured, and what problems (scale) or benefits (antacid, de-icer) it provides in practice.

Critically evaluate the use of aluminium potassium sulphate (alum, KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O) in water purification, explaining the chemistry involved.Hard
Show AnswerAlum dissolves in water: Al³⁺ hydrolyses → Al(OH)₃ colloid (positively charged). Suspended fine clay/silt particles and bacteria (negatively charged) are attracted to the Al(OH)₃ floc — coagulation and flocculation. The floc settles (sedimentation), removing turbidity and bacteria. Remaining clarified water is filtered and disinfected. Alum is cheap (~$150/tonne), effective, and widely used in developing countries. Concerns: residual Al³⁺ in drinking water (neurotoxic? — controversial evidence); sludge disposal. Alternative coagulants: FeSO₄, FeCl₃, polyaluminium chloride (PAC).

Analyse how the concept of common ion effect applies to the solubility of salts, with a practical example from water chemistry.Hard
Show AnswerCommon ion effect: adding a salt with an ion in common with a sparingly soluble salt depresses the solubility of the latter (Le Chatelier's principle). Example: CaSO₄ is sparingly soluble; adding Na₂SO₄ increases [SO₄²⁻], shifting CaSO₄ ⇌ Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ equilibrium left → more CaSO₄ precipitates. In boiler water, high sulphate levels from feed water accelerate CaSO₄ scale formation. In drinking water softening: adding CO₃²⁻ (soda ash) when [Ca²⁺] is already present exceeds Ksp of CaCO₃, precipitating calcium carbonate and softening the water.

Critically evaluate Na₂CO₃ (soda ash) as one of the most industrially important salts, discussing its production, uses, and environmental impact.Hard
Show AnswerProduction: Solvay process (NaCl + CaCO₃ + NH₃ → Na₂CO₃); also mined as trona (Wyoming, USA; Natron Lake, Africa). Uses: glass (50% of demand), detergents, chemicals, water softening, pulp and paper, food (pH adjustment). Environmental impact: Solvay produces CaCl₂ waste (limited uses); ammonia recovery reduces emissions but some loss. Mining trona is more sustainable. Global production ~60 Mt/year; CO₂ emitted in calcination step (~0.4 tonne CO₂/tonne Na₂CO₃. Low-carbon production routes (using CO₂ from flue gas directly) under development.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

What is the chlor-alkali process?Easy
Show AnswerElectrolysis of concentrated sodium chloride solution (brine) to produce three industrially valuable products: sodium hydroxide (NaOH), chlorine gas (Cl₂), and hydrogen gas (H₂).

Write the equation for the electrolysis of brine.Easy
Show Answer2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g) + H₂(g)

At which electrode is chlorine gas produced, and what is the half-reaction?Easy
Show AnswerAnode (positive electrode). Half-reaction: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ (oxidation).

At which electrode is hydrogen gas produced, and what is the half-reaction?Easy
Show AnswerCathode (negative electrode). Half-reaction: 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻ (reduction).

Name three uses of chlorine (Cl₂) produced in the chlor-alkali process.Easy
Show AnswerWater purification/disinfection, bleach manufacture (NaOCl), PVC production (CH₂=CHCl), manufacture of pesticides and solvents.

Name three uses of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) produced in the chlor-alkali process.Easy
Show AnswerSoap/detergent manufacture, paper (kraft process), aluminium production, drain cleaner, textile (mercerising cotton), food processing (olives, pretzels).

Name two uses of hydrogen gas produced in the chlor-alkali process.Easy
Show AnswerManufacture of ammonia (Haber process), hydrogenation of oils (margarine production), fuel for electricity generation (fuel cells), HCl synthesis.

Why must the three products of brine electrolysis be kept separate?Easy
Show AnswerCl₂ + NaOH → NaOCl + NaCl + H₂O (bleach forms if they mix). H₂ + Cl₂ is explosive. Products must be separated during production: membrane cells or diaphragm cells physically prevent mixing.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why electrolysis of brine produces Cl₂ at the anode rather than O₂ (which would be expected from water oxidation).Medium
Show AnswerThermodynamically, O₂ should be preferred (lower electrode potential). However, in concentrated brine, Cl⁻ ion concentration is very high — Cl⁻ is preferentially oxidised due to overpotential effects and high local concentration. In dilute NaCl solutions, O₂ is produced instead of Cl₂. Concentration is the key variable.

Describe the membrane cell used in modern chlor-alkali plants and explain the role of the ion-exchange membrane.Medium
Show AnswerThe membrane cell has an anode compartment (brine fed in; Cl₂ produced at anode) and cathode compartment (pure water fed in; H₂ and OH⁻ produced at cathode). The Nafion ion-exchange membrane: allows Na⁺ to pass from anode to cathode side (forming NaOH) but prevents Cl⁻ from migrating to cathode side, keeping products pure. Replaced older diaphragm and mercury cells (environmental hazard).

Explain the environmental problems associated with the older mercury cell process for chlor-alkali production.Medium
Show AnswerMercury cell: uses Hg as cathode — Na-Hg amalgam forms, then reacts with water to give NaOH + Hg. Problem: mercury is highly toxic; industrial accidents and chronic leakage contaminated rivers and food chains (Minamata disease, Japan — 2,000+ deaths). EU phased out mercury cells by 2017; membrane cells replaced them, eliminating Hg pollution entirely.

Calculate the volume of Cl₂ produced at STP when 1 mol of NaCl is electrolysed (assume complete reaction).Medium
Show AnswerFrom equation: 2NaCl → Cl₂ + 2NaOH + H₂ (simplified). 2 mol NaCl → 1 mol Cl₂. 1 mol NaCl → 0.5 mol Cl₂. Volume at STP (22.4 L/mol) = 0.5 × 22.4 = 11.2 L of Cl₂.

Explain why the chlor-alkali process is classified as an electrolytic decomposition reaction.Medium
Show AnswerNaCl (dissolved in water) is decomposed into its constituent elements/compounds using electrical energy — one reactant (brine) breaks down into multiple products (NaOH, Cl₂, H₂). This fits the definition of electrolytic decomposition: decomposition driven by electricity rather than heat or light.

Explain how chlorine from the chlor-alkali process is used to make bleach (sodium hypochlorite) and write the equation.Medium
Show AnswerCl₂(g) + 2NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + NaOCl(aq) + H₂O(l). Sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) is the active ingredient in household bleach. NaOCl is an oxidising agent that kills bacteria by oxidising cellular proteins and DNA. The Cl₂ and NaOH from adjacent chlor-alkali production streams are reacted together on site.

Describe two environmental and safety challenges in operating a large chlor-alkali plant.Medium
Show Answer1. Chlorine gas hazard: Cl₂ is toxic (used as chemical weapon in WWI); leaks can be fatal. Requires rigorous containment, monitoring, and emergency response. 2. Energy consumption: electrolysis is highly energy-intensive (~3,000 kWh/tonne Cl₂). Carbon footprint is significant unless powered by renewables. Also: disposal of chlorinated waste streams; NaOH corrosivity; H₂ explosion risk.

Explain how NaOH from the chlor-alkali process is used in the manufacture of soap.Medium
Show AnswerSaponification: fat (triglyceride) + NaOH → 3 fatty acid sodium salts (soap) + glycerol. Fat + 3NaOH → 3 RCOO⁻Na⁺ + C₃H₅(OH)₃. Soap molecules have a long hydrophobic hydrocarbon tail and a hydrophilic COO⁻Na⁺ head — they surround oil/grease in water, forming micelles that can be rinsed away. NaOH gives hard (bar) soap; KOH gives soft (liquid) soap.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate the chlor-alkali process as an example of sustainable industrial chemistry — what makes it efficient, and what are its limitations?Hard
Show AnswerEfficient: three valuable products (NaOH, Cl₂, H₂) from one cheap raw material (brine); co-product integration reduces waste; H₂ can be used on-site as fuel (reducing net energy cost). Limitations: very high energy consumption (~3,000 kWh/tonne Cl₂); currently depends on grid electricity (often fossil-fuel-based); Cl₂ is hazardous to transport; balanced production of NaOH and Cl₂ may not match market demand — overproduction of one limits the other. Transitioning to renewable electricity would make it nearly carbon-neutral.

Discuss the role of the chlor-alkali industry in enabling modern water disinfection globally, including the chemistry and public health impact.Hard
Show AnswerChlorination of drinking water (using Cl₂ or NaOCl) kills pathogens: Cl₂ + H₂O → HOCl + HCl; HOCl oxidises bacterial cell membranes and DNA at pH 6–7.5 (>10× more effective than OCl⁻). Chlorination eliminated waterborne diseases (cholera, typhoid, dysentery) in the 20th century — saving an estimated 177 million lives/year globally. WHO considers water chlorination one of the greatest public health achievements. Concern: trihalomethanes (THMs) form when Cl₂ reacts with organic matter (carcinogens at high doses) — managed by reducing organic load before chlorination or switching to chloramines/UV.

Analyse how overproduction of chlorine (relative to NaOH demand) has driven the development of chlorine chemistry derivatives, including PVC and HCl.Hard
Show AnswerChlor-alkali always produces equimolar Cl₂ and NaOH, but market demand fluctuates independently. Excess Cl₂: used to manufacture VCM (vinyl chloride monomer): CH₂=CH₂ + Cl₂ → CH₂Cl-CH₂Cl; → HCl + CH₂=CHCl (VCM) → PVC polymer (world's third-most-produced plastic). Also: HCl synthesis (H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl), chlorinated solvents, pesticides, pharmaceuticals. This downstream chlorine chemistry ensures near-zero waste — excess Cl₂ converted to high-value products rather than stored/destroyed.

Critically assess the environmental impact of PVC (polyvinyl chloride) production and disposal, tracing back to the chlor-alkali process.Hard
Show AnswerPVC production: energy-intensive; releases dioxins during manufacture; vinyl chloride monomer (VCM) is a Group 1 carcinogen. Use: PVC is durable, cheap, versatile (pipes, window frames, cables, flooring). Disposal: incineration releases HCl and dioxins; landfill leaches plasticisers (phthalates, endocrine disruptors); recycling is difficult (produces toxic pyrolysis products). Mechanical recycling fraction is low (<30%). More sustainable alternatives: biobased PVC, chlorine-free substitutes (polyethylene pipes). Life cycle assessment shows PVC has among the highest environmental burden of common plastics.

Discuss how the chlor-alkali process is being decarbonised through the use of oxygen-depolarised cathodes (ODCs) and renewable electricity.Hard
Show AnswerConventional cathode: 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻. ODC replaces H₂ evolution with O₂ reduction: O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻ (zero H₂ produced but saves ~30% energy vs conventional). If powered by renewable electricity, the process produces NaOH and Cl₂ with near-zero direct CO₂ emissions. BASF, Bayer, Covestro, and Thyssenkrupp have piloted ODC technology. Challenge: ODC membranes are expensive and the H₂ co-product is lost (valuable for other decarbonisation uses). Long-term path: fully renewable-powered membrane cells with ODC for maximum efficiency.

Analyse why the balanced production of NaOH and Cl₂ in the chlor-alkali process creates an interdependence between the alkali and chlorine chemical industries.Hard
Show AnswerFrom stoichiometry: 2NaCl → 2NaOH + Cl₂ + H₂ — exactly 2 mol NaOH per mol Cl₂ always. If NaOH demand exceeds Cl₂ demand (e.g., paper mill boom), operators must either overproduce Cl₂ (store/destroy at cost) or limit NaOH production. If Cl₂ demand exceeds NaOH demand (e.g., PVC boom), NaOH accumulates. This chlor-alkali balance is tracked globally; prices of NaOH and Cl₂ are inversely correlated (when Cl₂ price rises, NaOH price tends to fall because producers must run plants to meet Cl₂ demand, generating excess NaOH). It creates a unique economic interdependence in the chemicals market.

Evaluate whether the chlor-alkali process should be considered a "circular economy" model or a linear model, citing specific examples of product linkages.Hard
Show AnswerPartially circular: H₂ co-product used as fuel on site or for Haber process (NH₃ → fertiliser); Cl₂ used for HCl synthesis (H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl) which can later be oxidised back to Cl₂ (Sumitomo/Bayer HCl recycling); waste heat recovered from exothermic chlorination steps. Linear aspects: NaCl consumed from mines (not regenerated); HCl from downstream reactions often emitted or disposed. PVC end-of-life is poorly circular (low recycling rates). A fully circular model would close the Cl loop (recover Cl from spent organochlorines) and use renewable energy — partially achieved in advanced facilities but not yet systemic.

Critically evaluate the statement: "The chlor-alkali process is the single most important electrochemical industrial process in the world."Hard
Show AnswerStrong claim, well-supported: NaOH and Cl₂ are raw materials for hundreds of downstream products (PVC, bleach, pharmaceuticals, textiles, paper, aluminium, disinfection, detergents). Global production: ~80 Mt Cl₂/year; ~80 Mt NaOH/year. Touching ~55% of all industrial chemistry by product tree. Alternative claimants: aluminium smelting (Hall-Héroult — also electrochemical), copper refining, steel (blast furnace, though not electrochemical). The chlor-alkali process's breadth of influence across consumer goods, sanitation, and materials sectors makes it a compelling candidate for the most impactful industrial electrochemical process.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Define acid rain and state its pH range.Easy
Show AnswerPrecipitation (rain, snow, fog) with pH below 5.6 due to dissolved sulphuric acid and nitric acid from industrial emissions. Typical pH: 4–5.

Name the two main pollutant gases that cause acid rain.Easy
Show AnswerSulphur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen dioxide (NO₂).

Write the chemical equation showing how SO₂ forms sulphuric acid in the atmosphere.Easy
Show Answer2SO₂ + O₂ → 2SO₃; SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄

Write the equation showing how NO₂ forms nitric acid in the atmosphere.Easy
Show Answer4NO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4HNO₃

Name three sources of SO₂ pollution.Easy
Show AnswerCoal-fired power stations, smelting of sulphide ores, petroleum refining, volcanic eruptions (natural).

State three harmful effects of acid rain.Easy
Show AnswerDamages forests (leaches soil minerals, directly damages leaves), acidifies lakes and rivers (kills aquatic life), corrodes buildings and statues (especially limestone/marble), harms human health (respiratory problems).

Name one natural and one artificial source of NOₓ gases.Easy
Show AnswerNatural: lightning (N₂ + O₂ → 2NO at high temperatures). Artificial: combustion in car engines and power stations (high-temperature N₂ + O₂ → NO → NO₂).

State two solutions to reduce acid rain.Easy
Show AnswerFlue gas desulphurisation (scrubbing SO₂ from power station emissions); catalytic converters in vehicles (convert NOₓ/CO to N₂/CO₂); switching to renewable/nuclear energy; using low-sulphur fuels.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain the chemistry of flue gas desulphurisation (FGD) used in power stations to reduce SO₂ emissions.Medium
Show AnswerWet scrubbing: flue gases pass through a slurry of CaCO₃ or Ca(OH)₂: CaCO₃ + SO₂ → CaSO₃ + CO₂; CaSO₃ + ½O₂ → CaSO₄ (gypsum). Gypsum is a useful byproduct (used in plasterboard). Removes up to 99% of SO₂. UK/Germany dramatically reduced acid rain after FGD was mandated in the 1990s.

Explain how acid rain damages limestone and marble statues, writing the chemical equation.Medium
Show AnswerCaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂. Also: CaCO₃ + 2HNO₃ → Ca(NO₃)₂ + H₂O + CO₂. The calcium sulphate/nitrate formed is more soluble and weaker than CaCO₃ — it washes away, eroding fine details. The Taj Mahal has suffered significant surface erosion; Greek Parthenon statues have been moved indoors.

Describe how acid rain acidifies lakes and the effect on aquatic life.Medium
Show AnswerAcid rain (H₂SO₄, HNO₃) falls directly into lakes or leaches through acidified soils into waterways. pH of lake water drops below 5 → fish eggs fail to hatch; gill damage in fish and amphibians; Al³⁺ mobilised from soil (toxic to gill membranes); invertebrate prey species eliminated; food chains collapse. Scandinavian lakes lost most fish populations in 1970s-80s from Central European industrial SO₂ emissions.

Explain how catalytic converters in cars reduce NOₓ and CO emissions, relating to the chemistry of acid rain formation.Medium
Show AnswerThree-way catalytic converter (Pt/Pd/Rh catalyst): (1) 2CO + 2NO → 2CO₂ + N₂. (2) 2C₃H₆ + 9O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O. (3) unburnt hydrocarbons oxidised. Reducing NOₓ to N₂ prevents it from reacting with atmospheric O₂ and water to form HNO₃ — directly reducing the NOₓ contribution to acid rain. Catalysts work only when hot and are poisoned by lead (reason for unleaded petrol).

Explain why acid rain is a "transboundary" pollution problem and give a real-world example.Medium
Show AnswerSO₂ and NOₓ can be carried hundreds or thousands of km by wind before being washed down as acid rain — in a different country from the emitter. Example: UK power station emissions (1960s-80s) were a major source of acid rain over Scandinavia, acidifying Swedish lakes. This raised international tensions and led to international agreements (CLRTAP, 1979). Polluters and victims are different nations — raising justice and governance challenges.

Describe the effect of acid rain on forests, explaining the chain of chemical and biological effects.Medium
Show AnswerAcid rain: (1) direct leaf damage — H⁺ leaches Mg²⁺ from chlorophyll, causing yellowing/death. (2) Soil acidification: leaches Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ nutrients; mobilises toxic Al³⁺ (damages root membranes, inhibiting water/nutrient uptake). (3) Disrupts mycorrhizal fungi — reducing nutrient supply. (4) Trees weakened → more susceptible to frost, drought, and pests. Black Forest (Germany) — "Waldsterben" (forest death) in 1980s: 25% of trees damaged. Soil liming partially counteracts effects.

Calculate the pH of rainwater where [H⁺] = 2.5 × 10⁻⁵ mol/L and classify it.Medium
Show AnswerpH = −log(2.5 × 10⁻⁵) = −(log 2.5 + log 10⁻⁵) = −(0.4 − 5) = −(−4.6) = 4.6. This is acid rain (pH < 5.6).

Explain why liming lakes (adding CaCO₃) is only a short-term solution to acid rain damage.Medium
Show AnswerLiming neutralises lake acidity: CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂ — temporarily raises pH, allowing fish repopulation. However, if acid rain continues falling, the lake acidifies again — repeated liming is needed. It treats the symptom (acidic lake) not the cause (SO₂/NOₓ emissions). Long-term solution: reduce emissions at source (FGD, catalytic converters, renewable energy).

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Critically assess whether the "success story" of acid rain reduction in Europe/North America offers lessons applicable to India's growing acid rain problem.Hard
Show AnswerEurope/North America: SO₂ emissions fell ~80% since 1980 through FGD mandates, fuel switching, and catalytic converters. Scandinavian lakes partially recovered. India: SO₂ emissions growing with coal capacity expansion — India now the world's largest SO₂ emitter (surpassed China in 2018, some estimates). Lessons applicable: FGD technology is proven; strict emission standards work; cost of health damage far exceeds compliance costs. Barriers: enforcement capacity, coal industry lobbying, electricity pricing. India's 2015 SO₂ standards (stricter) were delayed; implementation remains the challenge, not technology.

Analyse how the chemistry of acid rain links to ozone depletion, climate change, and biodiversity loss as interconnected environmental problems.Hard
Show AnswerAcid rain: SO₂/NOₓ from fossil fuels. NOₓ also depletes stratospheric O₃ (NOₓ + O₃ → NO₂ + O₂). SO₂ aerosols cool climate (offset ~0.5°C of warming) — creating a paradox where cutting SO₂ for acid rain relief may accelerate warming. Acid rain reduces forest carbon sinks → more CO₂ in atmosphere → climate change. Climate change alters precipitation patterns, affecting acid rain deposition geography. These interactions mean treating acid rain in isolation misses systemic consequences.

Evaluate the economic case for strict emission controls on SO₂ and NOₓ, comparing compliance costs with health and environmental damage costs.Hard
Show AnswerCost-benefit analyses (US EPA, European Commission) consistently show health/environmental benefits of SO₂/NOₓ reduction are 10–30× larger than compliance costs. US Clean Air Act Amendments (1990): compliance cost ~$65B over 20 years; health benefits ~$2 trillion (avoided premature deaths, healthcare savings, reduced crop damage). Economic externalities of pollution (PM₂.₅ formation from SO₂, acid damage to infrastructure, fishery losses) are rarely borne by polluters — classic market failure justifying regulation.

Discuss how dry deposition of SO₂ and NOₓ differs from wet deposition (acid rain) in its environmental impact and measurement challenges.Hard
Show AnswerWet deposition: SO₂/NOₓ dissolve in cloud water → H₂SO₄/HNO₃ → acid rain. Measured by rain gauges and rain chemistry sampling. Dry deposition: gaseous SO₂/NO₂ or particulate sulphates/nitrates settle directly onto surfaces (plants, soil, water) without rain. Harder to measure (requires micrometeorological techniques). Often >50% of total acid deposition in semi-arid or low-rainfall regions. Dry-deposited SO₂ on leaf surfaces converts to sulphuric acid when wetted — direct foliar damage. Models for critical load calculations must include both deposition pathways.

Explain how biological sulphur cycling naturally produces SO₂ and compare its contribution to acid rain with industrial emissions.Hard
Show AnswerBiological: marine phytoplankton (especially coccolithophores) produce DMSP (dimethylsulphoniopropionate) → DMS (dimethyl sulphide) → oxidised in atmosphere to SO₂/sulphate aerosols. This natural sulphur cycle contributes ~25 Tg S/year globally. Industrial: ~100 Tg S/year (4× natural). Natural SO₂ contributes to background acidity of rainwater (pH 5.0–5.6) — already below neutral. Industrial emissions push pH to 4–5 in affected regions. Without human emissions, natural acid rain would have minimal ecosystem impact; the industrial excess is the problem.

Discuss the chemistry and environmental impact of secondary organic aerosols (SOAs) formed from NOₓ and VOC interactions in photochemical smog, and their relationship to acid rain.Hard
Show AnswerPhotochemical smog: NOₓ + UV light → NO + O•; O• + O₂ → O₃ (tropospheric). O₃ + VOCs → SOAs (fine particulate matter, including nitrate and sulphate aerosols, organic acids). SOAs contribute to acid deposition both as dry particles and as cloud condensation nuclei (CCN) — increasing cloud acidity. PM₂.₅ from SOAs penetrates deep into lungs (cardiovascular/respiratory disease). Unlike primary acid rain (H₂SO₄/HNO₃ in rain), SOAs form in complex non-linear reactions that make them harder to predict and regulate. Delhi and Mumbai regularly exceed WHO PM₂.₅ limits by 5–10× — linked to vehicle NOₓ and crop stubble burning VOCs.

Evaluate the role of cap-and-trade systems (like the US Acid Rain Program, 1990) vs direct regulation in controlling SO₂ emissions.Hard
Show AnswerUS Acid Rain Program: assigned tradeable SO₂ allowances to power stations. Stations below cap could sell allowances to those above — creating financial incentive for early abatement. Result: US SO₂ emissions fell 67% by 2010 at 50% lower cost than predicted (early movers found cheaper abatement methods than expected). Advantages over direct regulation: economic efficiency, flexibility, stimulates innovation. Criticisms: "right to pollute"; geographic "hot spots" (facilities buying allowances still emit heavily in disadvantaged communities); administrative complexity. Most economists favour cap-and-trade; environmental justice advocates prefer direct emission limits in sensitive areas. Both approaches have demonstrably reduced acid rain.

Critically analyse why acid rain is now considered a "solved" problem in Europe but remains a serious threat in South and East Asia, and assess the policy lessons for India.Hard
Show AnswerEurope "solved": CLRTAP treaty frameworks, binding emission ceilings, FGD mandates, coal phase-out, successful advocacy (high public pressure after forest death scandals). Asia: China's SO₂ emissions peaked ~2006, now declining rapidly (fastest environmental regulation in history). India: rising coal use; SO₂ emissions growing; FGD retrofits on coal plants legally mandated from 2017 but compliance remains low (<30% by 2022). Policy lessons: (1) strict enforceable standards work — China proved rapid change possible with political will; (2) economic incentives (FGD tenders, renewable auctions) accelerate adoption; (3) strong ambient air quality monitoring creates public accountability; (4) health co-benefits (PM₂.₅ reduction) can align air quality and acid rain policy.
