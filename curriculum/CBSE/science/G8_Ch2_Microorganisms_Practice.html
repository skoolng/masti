What are microorganisms?Easy
Show Answer

Microorganisms (microbes) are living organisms too small to be seen with the naked eye; they can only be observed under a microscope.

Name the five main types of microorganisms.Easy
Show Answer

Bacteria, Viruses, Fungi, Protozoa, and Algae are the five main types of microorganisms.

Which type of microorganism is responsible for making curd?Easy
Show Answer

Lactobacillus bacteria convert milk into curd by fermenting lactose to lactic acid.

Name one disease caused by a protozoan.Easy
Show Answer

Malaria is caused by Plasmodium, a protozoan transmitted by female Anopheles mosquitoes.

What instrument is used to observe microorganisms?Easy
Show Answer

A microscope is used to observe microorganisms that are invisible to the naked eye.

Give one example of a microorganism found in extreme environments (extremophile).Easy
Show Answer

Thermophilic bacteria (e.g., Thermus aquaticus) live in boiling hot springs at temperatures above 70°C.

Are all microorganisms harmful? Justify your answer briefly.Easy
Show Answer

No. Many microorganisms are beneficial — they help in food production (yeast, Lactobacillus), fix nitrogen (Rhizobium), decompose waste, and are used in medicines.

Name one algae that is used as a food supplement.Easy
Show Answer

Spirulina is a blue-green alga (cyanobacterium) rich in protein and used as a nutritional supplement.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain why microorganisms are found almost everywhere on Earth.Medium
Show Answer

Microorganisms are extremely small, reproduce rapidly, tolerate a vast range of temperature, pH, and salinity conditions, can survive as dormant spores, and require minimal nutrients — making them successful in almost every habitat on Earth.

Distinguish between unicellular and multicellular microorganisms with one example each.Medium
Show Answer

Unicellular: single-celled organisms where one cell performs all life functions (e.g., Amoeba, bacteria). Multicellular fungi like mushrooms are larger organisms but moulds (e.g., Rhizopus) are technically multicellular yet microscopic in their early growth stages.

How do algae differ from bacteria in terms of cell structure?Medium
Show Answer

Algae are eukaryotes — they have a membrane-bound nucleus, chloroplasts, and organised organelles. Bacteria are prokaryotes with no nucleus or membrane-bound organelles.

Explain the ecological role of protozoa in aquatic food chains.Medium
Show Answer

Protozoa consume bacteria, algae, and organic particles, controlling their populations. They are in turn eaten by small invertebrates, transferring energy up the food chain and recycling nutrients in aquatic ecosystems.

Why are fungi classified separately from bacteria despite both being microorganisms?Medium
Show Answer

Fungi are eukaryotes with cell walls made of chitin, obtain food by absorption (decomposers), and can be unicellular (yeast) or multicellular (moulds). Bacteria are prokaryotes with cell walls of peptidoglycan — fundamentally different in structure and evolutionary origin.

Compare the habitats of two extremophile microorganisms with specific environments.Medium
Show Answer

Thermophiles (e.g., Pyrolobus fumarii) live in hydrothermal vents at 113°C. Halophiles (e.g., Halobacterium) live in extremely salty environments like the Dead Sea (salt concentration 10× ocean). Both adapted their cell chemistry to their extreme environments.

How does the size of microorganisms relate to their ability to cause disease?Medium
Show Answer

Their microscopic size allows them to enter cells, penetrate tissues, spread through air droplets, and colonise surfaces undetected. Smallness enables billions to thrive in tiny volumes (a teaspoon of soil holds ~1 billion bacteria), making them formidable pathogens.

Describe how algae contribute to the oxygen supply on Earth.Medium
Show Answer

Phytoplankton (microscopic algae) perform photosynthesis in oceans and freshwater, producing approximately 50% of Earth's oxygen. They also form the base of aquatic food chains and absorb significant amounts of atmospheric CO₂.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate why the discovery of microorganisms by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek changed the course of biology and medicine.Hard
Show Answer

Before Leeuwenhoek (1670s), disease was attributed to bad air (miasma) or supernatural causes. His microscope revealed an invisible living world, establishing the "germ theory" foundation. This led Pasteur and Koch to prove microbes cause disease, enabling antiseptic surgery, vaccines, and modern medicine.

Analyse why viruses are not classified as living organisms by most biologists, despite replicating and evolving.Hard
Show Answer

Viruses lack cells, cannot metabolise independently, have no ribosomes, cannot reproduce without a host, and do not maintain homeostasis. They are obligate parasites at the molecular level — biological entities but not independently living organisms by classical definitions. Their evolution without metabolism challenges both living and non-living classifications.

Design an investigation to compare the diversity of microorganisms in soil from a forest versus soil from a polluted urban area.Hard
Show Answer

Collect 10g soil samples from each site. Serial dilute in sterile water (10⁻³, 10⁻⁵, 10⁻⁷). Plate on nutrient agar, Sabouraud agar (fungi), and selective media. Incubate at 28°C for 48–72 hours. Count colony-forming units, identify morphology types. Compare diversity indices. Control variables: sample volume, incubation conditions, same operator technique.

Critically assess the claim that "microorganisms are more important to Earth's ecosystems than all visible organisms combined."Hard
Show Answer

Supported: microbes drive all biogeochemical cycles (nitrogen, carbon, sulphur), produce 50% of global oxygen, decompose all organic matter, form the base of food chains, and constitute ~70% of Earth's total biomass. Without microbes, nutrient cycles collapse and all complex life dies. The claim is strongly supported by evidence.

Using systems thinking, explain how the disappearance of decomposer microorganisms would affect an entire forest ecosystem.Hard
Show Answer

Dead organic matter accumulates undecomposed → nutrients locked in biomass → soil nutrient depletion → plant growth fails → herbivores starve → predators collapse → entire food web collapses. CO₂ and nitrogen cycles halt, altering global climate. The forest ecosystem would cease to function within decades.

Evaluate the ethical implications of creating synthetic microorganisms in laboratories (synthetic biology).Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: designed microbes could clean oil spills, produce biofuels, deliver targeted medicines. Risks: accidental release of engineered organisms with unknown ecological effects; biosecurity risk (bioweapons); irreversibility if organisms spread and evolve; equity concerns about who controls these powerful technologies. Strong international governance is needed.

Propose how microorganisms could be harnessed to address plastic pollution, and identify the current limitations of this approach.Hard
Show Answer

Ideonella sakaiensis bacteria can break down PET plastic using PETase enzyme. Engineered strains could be deployed in plastic-rich environments. Limitations: slow degradation rate (months per thin film), cannot handle all plastic types, risk of unintended ecological impacts from introducing engineered organisms, and industrial scale-up challenges remain unsolved.

Compare how Whittaker's 5-kingdom and Woese's 3-domain systems treat microorganisms differently and explain why the 3-domain system is more scientifically accurate.Hard
Show Answer

Whittaker groups all bacteria in Kingdom Monera (one group). Woese's 3-domain system separates Bacteria and Archaea as fundamentally distinct domains based on ribosomal RNA sequences — Archaea are more closely related to Eukaryotes than to Bacteria. This reflects actual evolutionary history better than morphological grouping alone.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Are bacteria prokaryotes or eukaryotes? What does this mean?Easy
Show Answer

Bacteria are prokaryotes — they lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Their DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm.

Name the four shapes of bacteria with their scientific terms.Easy
Show Answer

Cocci (spherical), Bacilli (rod-shaped), Spirilli (spiral/helical), and Vibrio (comma-shaped).

How do bacteria reproduce?Easy
Show Answer

Bacteria reproduce by binary fission — one cell divides into two identical daughter cells. Under ideal conditions this can happen every 20 minutes.

Name the bacterium that converts milk to curd and state its function.Easy
Show Answer

Lactobacillus acidophilus ferments lactose (milk sugar) into lactic acid, which coagulates milk proteins, forming curd.

What is Rhizobium and where is it found?Easy
Show Answer

Rhizobium is a nitrogen-fixing bacterium found in root nodules of leguminous plants (peas, beans, lentils). It converts atmospheric N₂ into ammonia usable by plants.

What is a bacterial cell wall made of?Easy
Show Answer

Bacterial cell walls are made of peptidoglycan (murein), a unique polymer of sugars and amino acids not found in eukaryotes.

Name one bacterium that causes a disease and state the disease it causes.Easy
Show Answer

Mycobacterium tuberculosis causes tuberculosis (TB), a serious lung infection spread through airborne droplets.

What is the function of flagella in bacteria?Easy
Show Answer

Flagella are whip-like appendages that rotate to propel bacteria through liquid environments, helping them move towards nutrients or away from harmful substances.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain how binary fission allows bacteria to grow to enormous numbers rapidly.Medium
Show Answer

Binary fission doubles the population every 20 minutes. Starting from 1 bacterium: after 1 hour = 8; after 10 hours = over 1 billion. This exponential growth means a tiny contamination can become a dangerous infection within hours under warm, moist conditions.

How does the nitrogen fixation by Rhizobium benefit both the bacterium and the plant (mutualism)?Medium
Show Answer

The plant provides sugars (energy) from photosynthesis to Rhizobium. In return, Rhizobium fixes atmospheric N₂ into ammonia, supplying the plant with nitrogen for protein synthesis. Both organisms benefit — a true mutualistic relationship.

Compare Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria and explain the significance in antibiotic treatment.Medium
Show Answer

Gram-positive: thick peptidoglycan layer, stains purple, e.g., Staphylococcus. Gram-negative: thin peptidoglycan plus outer lipopolysaccharide membrane, stains pink, e.g., E. coli. The extra outer membrane in Gram-negative bacteria blocks many antibiotics, making them harder to treat.

Why does refrigerating food slow down bacterial spoilage?Medium
Show Answer

Cold temperatures slow bacterial metabolic processes and reduce enzyme activity. At 4°C, most bacteria grow 50–100× more slowly than at 37°C. This extends food shelf life by reducing the rate of bacterial multiplication and spoilage enzyme activity.

Explain why Lactobacillus in curd is beneficial for human digestive health.Medium
Show Answer

Lactobacillus colonises the gut, competing with harmful bacteria for space and nutrients. It produces lactic acid (lowering gut pH, inhibiting pathogens), produces vitamins (B12), and strengthens the gut immune lining.

Describe how bacteria cause food spoilage and the signs you would observe.Medium
Show Answer

Bacteria secrete enzymes that break down food proteins and carbohydrates, producing foul-smelling compounds (amines, sulphur compounds). Signs: sour or rotten smell, slimy texture, colour changes, gas bubbles in sealed containers.

How do bacterial endospores help bacteria survive harsh conditions?Medium
Show Answer

Endospores are dormant, thick-walled structures formed when conditions are unfavourable (drought, heat, chemicals). They are extremely resistant to boiling, UV radiation, and disinfectants. When conditions improve, spores germinate back into active bacteria.

A student leaves milk at room temperature for 8 hours. Predict what happens and explain the biochemistry.Medium
Show Answer

Lactobacillus (already present in milk) multiplies rapidly at room temperature. It ferments lactose → lactic acid. pH drops from ~6.7 to ~4.5, causing casein proteins to coagulate (curdle). The milk sours and forms a semi-solid curd texture.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Analyse how horizontal gene transfer (conjugation) in bacteria differs from typical inheritance and why it is medically significant.Hard
Show Answer

Conjugation transfers DNA (plasmids) directly between live bacteria — not parent to offspring, but between unrelated bacteria. This allows antibiotic resistance genes to spread rapidly across species, explaining why resistance emerges quickly in hospital environments even among bacteria not previously exposed to antibiotics.

Evaluate the role of Rhizobium in sustainable agriculture and whether it can replace synthetic nitrogen fertilisers entirely.Hard
Show Answer

Rhizobium fixes 50–200 kg N/ha/year — sufficient for legumes but inadequate for high-yield cereals requiring 150–200 kg N/ha. Mixed farming (legume–cereal rotation) with biofertiliser inoculants reduces synthetic N need by 30–50%. Full replacement is biologically impossible at current yield targets without accepting lower production.

Design an experiment to investigate the effect of temperature on the growth rate of E. coli bacteria.Hard
Show Answer

Inoculate identical E. coli cultures in nutrient broth. Incubate at 10°C, 20°C, 37°C, 45°C, and 55°C. Measure optical density (turbidity) every 2 hours using a colorimeter as a proxy for cell count. Plot growth curves. Control: identical starting concentration, same broth composition, same volume. Repeat 3 times. Expected: peak growth at 37°C (optimal), growth arrest at extremes.

Critically assess the CRISPR-Cas9 system (originally discovered in bacteria) and its implications for genetic medicine.Hard
Show Answer

CRISPR is a bacterial immune system that can cut specific DNA sequences. In medicine it enables precise gene editing to correct genetic diseases (sickle cell, cystic fibrosis). Benefits: high precision, cheap, fast. Risks: off-target edits, germline editing in embryos raises ethical concerns about designer babies, and equitable access remains unresolved.

Evaluate how the human gut microbiome (predominantly bacteria) influences mental health, challenging the traditional view of bacteria as merely digestive aids.Hard
Show Answer

Gut bacteria produce neurotransmitter precursors (serotonin, GABA) and communicate with the brain via the vagus nerve (gut-brain axis). Dysbiosis (disrupted microbiome) correlates with depression, anxiety, and autism spectrum conditions in clinical studies. Probiotic interventions show some mood improvement. This expands bacterial roles beyond digestion into neurological health.

Analyse the implications of bacterial quorum sensing for our understanding of bacterial behaviour and infection control.Hard
Show Answer

Quorum sensing allows bacteria to "count" their population using chemical signals. Above a threshold density they coordinate behaviours (biofilm formation, toxin release, virulence). Disrupting quorum sensing could be an antibiotic-free strategy to prevent infection — bacteria cannot coordinate attacks, reducing virulence without selective pressure for resistance.

Propose how engineered nitrogen-fixing bacteria could be introduced into non-legume crops and discuss the associated risks.Hard
Show Answer

Proposal: insert nif genes (nitrogen fixation) into rhizosphere bacteria naturally colonising wheat roots, or engineer symbiosis signals in wheat to attract Rhizobium. Risks: engineered bacteria could spread to wild ecosystems, disrupt existing nitrogen cycles, reduce diversity of soil bacteria, and create unpredictable ecological effects. Regulatory containment would be essential.

Using the concept of natural selection, explain how pasteurisation and modern food safety practices act as selective pressure on bacteria.Hard
Show Answer

Pasteurisation kills heat-sensitive bacteria (most pathogens). Any bacteria with heat-resistant proteins survive and reproduce — selecting for thermotolerant strains over generations. Similarly, excessive antibiotic use in food production selects resistant strains. Food safety practices, while protective, inadvertently apply selective pressure that can drive evolution of more resistant bacteria.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

Are viruses living or non-living? Give one reason for each side.Easy
Show Answer

Non-living outside a host: no metabolism, no cellular structure. Living inside a host: they use host machinery to replicate and evolve over generations. Viruses are on the boundary between living and non-living.

What are the two main structural components of a virus?Easy
Show Answer

A virus consists of a protein coat (capsid) and genetic material (either DNA or RNA) inside it. Some viruses also have a lipid envelope around the capsid.

Name three diseases caused by viruses.Easy
Show Answer

Influenza (flu), HIV/AIDS, and COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2) are diseases caused by viruses.

Why can antibiotics not treat viral infections?Easy
Show Answer

Antibiotics target bacterial structures (cell wall, ribosomes). Viruses have no cell wall and use the host's own ribosomes, so antibiotics have no target in viral infections.

What does "host-specific" mean in the context of viruses?Easy
Show Answer

Host-specific means a virus can only infect specific types of cells or organisms whose surface receptors match the virus's attachment proteins. The common cold virus infects human respiratory cells but not plant cells.

Name the virus that causes AIDS and state what it attacks in the immune system.Easy
Show Answer

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) causes AIDS. It specifically attacks and destroys CD4+ T-helper lymphocytes, weakening the immune system.

What is a capsid?Easy
Show Answer

A capsid is the protein shell surrounding a virus's genetic material. It protects the viral DNA/RNA and helps the virus attach to and enter host cells.

How does a virus differ from a bacterium in size?Easy
Show Answer

Viruses (20–300 nm) are 10–100 times smaller than bacteria (1–10 µm). Viruses cannot be seen with a light microscope; an electron microscope is needed.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain the lytic cycle of viral replication step by step.Medium
Show Answer

1. Attachment: virus binds to specific receptor on host cell. 2. Injection: viral DNA/RNA enters host. 3. Replication: host ribosomes and enzymes copy viral genes. 4. Assembly: new virus particles assembled. 5. Lysis: host cell bursts, releasing hundreds of new viruses.

How does the influenza virus mutate rapidly, requiring new vaccines each year?Medium
Show Answer

Influenza uses RNA as genetic material. RNA polymerase has no proofreading ability, so copying errors (mutations) accumulate rapidly. Changes in surface proteins (haemagglutinin/neuraminidase) mean immune memory from last year's vaccine no longer recognises the new strain.

Compare the replication of DNA viruses and RNA viruses in terms of mutation rate.Medium
Show Answer

DNA viruses: lower mutation rate because DNA polymerase proofreads errors; more stable genome (e.g., herpes, poxvirus). RNA viruses: high mutation rate because RNA polymerase lacks proofreading; evolve rapidly (e.g., influenza, HIV, coronaviruses).

Explain how COVID-19's spike protein enables it to infect human cells.Medium
Show Answer

SARS-CoV-2's spike protein binds to the ACE2 receptor on human respiratory and other cells. This triggers membrane fusion, allowing the virus to inject its RNA into the cell. Vaccines train the immune system to recognise and block this spike protein.

Why is HIV particularly dangerous compared to most other viruses?Medium
Show Answer

HIV uses the lysogenic cycle — it integrates its DNA into the host cell's chromosomes permanently. It specifically destroys T-helper cells, dismantling the entire immune response. Without treatment, the immune system collapses, leaving the body defenceless against normally harmless infections (opportunistic infections).

How do antiviral drugs differ from antibiotics in their mechanism of action?Medium
Show Answer

Antiviral drugs target virus-specific processes: blocking viral enzymes (reverse transcriptase in HIV), preventing viral capsid assembly, or blocking receptor binding. They must be precisely targeted to avoid damaging host cell processes. Antibiotics target bacterial structures completely absent in human cells.

Describe what happens during the lysogenic cycle and explain why it is more dangerous than the lytic cycle.Medium
Show Answer

In the lysogenic cycle, viral DNA integrates into the host chromosome as a provirus and replicates silently with every cell division. It can persist for years undetected. Triggered by stress, it switches to lytic mode, causing sudden massive infection. HIV and herpes use this strategy, making them incurable.

A new respiratory virus emerges. Suggest the key information scientists need to determine before developing a vaccine.Medium
Show Answer

Scientists need: viral genome sequence (DNA or RNA), surface protein structure (antigen target), replication mechanism, mutation rate, mode of transmission, animal reservoir (if any), and which immune response (antibody vs T-cell) best controls the virus. This determines vaccine platform choice.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the mRNA vaccine technology used against COVID-19 and whether it represents a paradigm shift in vaccinology.Hard
Show Answer

mRNA vaccines instruct cells to produce viral spike protein temporarily, training immunity without any viral material. Paradigm shift: platforms can be designed in days once genome is sequenced; no live virus manufacturing; no risk of infection from vaccine. Limitation: cold chain requirements (−70°C for Pfizer), short mRNA stability. Represents a genuine platform revolution with cancer vaccine applications already in trials.

Analyse how zoonotic virus spillover (animal-to-human transmission) has driven the most dangerous pandemic threats in human history.Hard
Show Answer

HIV originated from chimpanzees (SIVcpz); Ebola from bats; COVID-19 linked to bat coronaviruses. Deforestation, wildlife trade, and livestock-human interfaces increase spillover probability. Human immune systems lack prior immunity to novel animal viruses, enabling rapid spread before adaptive immunity develops. Surveillance at animal-human interfaces is critical prevention.

Design an experiment to test whether a new antiviral compound can prevent influenza virus replication in cell culture.Hard
Show Answer

Grow human lung epithelial cells in identical culture dishes. Infect all with identical influenza dose. Treat experimental wells with antiviral compound at 3 concentrations; control wells receive vehicle only. After 48 hours, measure viral load via plaque assay (count viral plaques on indicator cells). Compare viral concentrations. Include positive control (oseltamivir) to validate the assay.

Critically evaluate the concept of herd immunity as a pandemic management strategy, using COVID-19 as a case study.Hard
Show Answer

Herd immunity requires 60–90% population immunity (via infection or vaccination) to interrupt transmission. For COVID-19 (R₀ ~3–5), ~75% immunity needed. Natural infection herd immunity causes preventable deaths and long COVID. Vaccine herd immunity is ethically superior. Variant emergence (Omicron) with immune escape complicates whether immunity thresholds can be maintained long-term.

Analyse the ethical dimensions of human challenge trials (deliberately infecting healthy volunteers with a virus to test vaccines).Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: drastically faster vaccine testing (weeks not years), fewer subjects needed, controlled conditions. Ethical concerns: volunteers accept substantial personal risk, especially if severe outcomes occur; risk of harm to irreplaceable participants; informed consent sufficiency when risks are unknown; equity (typically recruited from lower socioeconomic groups accepting payment). Medical ethics demands robust oversight and compensation frameworks.

Evaluate the evidence for and against the hypothesis that viruses played a role in the origin of complex life (eukaryogenesis).Hard
Show Answer

Evidence for: ~8% of human genome is endogenous viral elements (integrated retroviral DNA); syncytins (viral proteins) are now essential for placenta formation; nuclear pore complex may have viral origins. Evidence against: insufficient to confirm causation; eukaryote complexity likely emerged through multiple processes (endosymbiosis, cell fusion). Viruses likely contributed but are not the sole driver of eukaryotic evolution.

Propose a global governance framework to prevent the next pandemic based on lessons from COVID-19.Hard
Show Answer

Framework: 1) Funded WHO pandemic surveillance network with mandatory reporting. 2) International pathogen-sharing treaty (COVAX model) for equitable vaccine access. 3) Wildlife-livestock interface monitoring. 4) Pre-positioned vaccine platform technology (mRNA) for rapid deployment. 5) Pandemic treaty with binding obligations and financial penalties for non-compliance. Key lesson: equity is not altruism — unvaccinated populations allow variants to emerge globally.

Using your knowledge of natural selection, predict how HIV might evolve in a population where antiretroviral treatment is universally available.Hard
Show Answer

Universal ART creates selective pressure for drug-resistant HIV strains. Mutations in reverse transcriptase or protease genes conferring resistance would be selected. However, effective ART that drives viral loads to undetectable levels also prevents transmission (Undetectable = Untransmittable). If adherence is high, drug-resistant strains have no selective advantage as they cannot spread. Evolutionary outcome depends on the balance between treatment coverage and adherence quality.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What is fermentation? Name one product made by fermentation.Easy
Show Answer

Fermentation is the process by which microorganisms convert sugars into simpler compounds (alcohol, acids) in the absence of oxygen. Example: yeast converts glucose into alcohol and CO₂ in bread making.

How does yeast help bread to rise?Easy
Show Answer

Yeast ferments sugars in the dough, producing CO₂ gas. The gas gets trapped in the elastic gluten network, causing the dough to expand (rise).

Name two antibiotics and the microorganisms that produce them.Easy
Show Answer

Penicillin is produced by Penicillium notatum fungus. Streptomycin is produced by Streptomyces griseus bacteria.

What role do decomposer microorganisms play in ecosystems?Easy
Show Answer

Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down dead organic matter into simple inorganic nutrients, recycling carbon, nitrogen, and minerals back into the soil for plants to use.

Name one vaccine and the disease it prevents.Easy
Show Answer

The polio vaccine (Salk/Sabin) prevents poliomyelitis, a viral disease that can cause permanent paralysis.

How is insulin produced using microorganisms today?Easy
Show Answer

The human insulin gene is inserted into E. coli bacteria using genetic engineering. The bacteria produce human insulin protein, which is extracted and purified for diabetes treatment.

What is the role of microorganisms in the nitrogen cycle?Easy
Show Answer

Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium, Azotobacter) convert N₂ to ammonia. Nitrifying bacteria convert ammonia to nitrates. Denitrifying bacteria return nitrogen to the atmosphere. Together they cycle nitrogen between living organisms and the atmosphere.

Name one fermented food from India and the microorganism involved.Easy
Show Answer

Idli/Dosa batter is fermented by Leuconostoc mesenteroides and Lactobacillus bacteria, which produce lactic acid and CO₂, giving the characteristic taste and texture.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain how Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin and why this discovery was so significant.Medium
Show Answer

Fleming noticed in 1928 that a Penicillium mould had contaminated a Staphylococcus plate and killed the bacteria around it. He isolated the active substance. Before antibiotics, bacterial infections like pneumonia were often fatal. Penicillin transformed medicine, reducing mortality from infections dramatically.

How does vaccination train the immune system without causing disease?Medium
Show Answer

Vaccines introduce weakened/inactivated pathogens or their specific proteins (antigens). The immune system mounts a response and creates memory B-cells and T-cells. On real infection, memory cells respond rapidly, clearing the pathogen before disease develops.

Compare alcohol fermentation (yeast) and lactic acid fermentation (bacteria) as processes.Medium
Show Answer

Both are anaerobic. Yeast: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ (used in beer, wine, bread). Lactic acid bacteria: glucose → lactic acid only, no gas (used in curd, cheese, idli). The end products differ because different enzymes are used in the final step of the pathway.

Explain how bioremediation uses microorganisms to clean up oil spills.Medium
Show Answer

Hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria (e.g., Alcanivorax) metabolise petroleum compounds as a carbon source, converting them to CO₂ and water. They are stimulated by adding nitrogen/phosphorus fertilisers (biostimulation) or inoculated directly (bioaugmentation) to accelerate natural degradation.

Describe how microorganisms are used in wastewater treatment.Medium
Show Answer

Aerobic bacteria break down organic waste in aeration tanks. Anaerobic bacteria in digesters break down sludge, producing biogas. Nitrifying bacteria remove ammonia. The result is clean water and nutrient-rich sludge (biosolids) usable as fertiliser.

How do probiotics differ from regular food microorganisms in terms of health benefit?Medium
Show Answer

Probiotics are live microorganisms specifically selected and dose-controlled for documented health effects (e.g., restoring gut flora after antibiotics, reducing diarrhoea duration). Regular fermented food microbes also have benefits but are not standardised for specific therapeutic purposes.

Explain the process of cheese-making and the role of bacteria at each stage.Medium
Show Answer

Starter bacteria (Lactococcus) acidify milk with lactic acid → rennet (enzyme) curdles proteins → curds separated from whey → additional bacteria or moulds added for ripening. Different species (Propionibacterium for Swiss cheese holes, Penicillium roqueforti for blue cheese) create distinct flavours and textures.

How does Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) function as a biological insecticide?Medium
Show Answer

Bt bacteria produce crystal proteins (Cry toxins) that are harmless to humans but toxic to specific insect larvae. When caterpillars eat Bt-sprayed crops, the Cry toxins activate in the alkaline insect gut and bind to gut receptors, creating pores that destroy the gut lining, killing the insect within days.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the potential of microbial fuel cells to generate clean electricity from wastewater while simultaneously treating it.Hard
Show Answer

Microbial fuel cells (MFCs) use exoelectrogenic bacteria (Geobacter) that oxidise organic waste and transfer electrons to an electrode, generating electricity. Current power output is too low (milliwatts) for commercial use, but coupled with wastewater treatment this represents a self-powered treatment system. Scalability and electrode cost remain barriers to deployment.

Analyse how the loss of decomposer diversity in agricultural soils has affected nutrient cycling and what restoration strategies are available.Hard
Show Answer

Heavy pesticide use, monocultures, and synthetic fertilisers have reduced soil microbial diversity by 40–60% in intensive farming systems. This impairs nutrient cycling, reduces natural decomposition rates, and increases dependence on synthetic inputs. Restoration: cover cropping, reducing tillage, adding compost, biochar application, and avoiding prophylactic fungicide use to allow mycorrhizal networks to re-establish.

Design an investigation to test whether daily consumption of probiotic yoghurt reduces the duration of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea.Hard
Show Answer

Double-blind RCT: 100 patients prescribed a standard antibiotic course randomised to probiotic yoghurt (defined CFU/day) or placebo yoghurt (heat-killed bacteria). Same antibiotic, same dose. Record daily stool consistency/frequency. Primary outcome: duration of loose stools. Secondary: gut microbiome diversity (16S rRNA sequencing at baseline, end of antibiotics, 2 weeks post). Analyse using Mann-Whitney U test.

Critically assess whether synthetic biology (engineering microorganisms to produce drugs, fuels, and materials) is the most important biotechnology development of the 21st century.Hard
Show Answer

Evidence for: engineered yeast now produces artemisinin (malaria drug), insulin, spider silk proteins, and bio-jet fuel. Transformative potential for medicine and sustainability. Counter: CRISPR gene therapy, AI-driven drug discovery, and organ-on-chip platforms offer equally transformative potential. Synthetic biology is transformative but shares the "most important" claim with several other technologies. Its democratisation (biohackers with DNA printers) also introduces novel biosecurity risks.

Evaluate the role of the human microbiome in immunity, and discuss what happens when it is disrupted by antibiotics.Hard
Show Answer

The gut microbiome trains immune cells to distinguish self from pathogen, produces competitive exclusion against pathogens, maintains gut barrier integrity, and generates short-chain fatty acids that regulate inflammation. Broad-spectrum antibiotics kill commensal bacteria alongside pathogens. Consequences: Clostridioides difficile overgrowth, increased susceptibility to infections, altered immune development (if antibiotics given in infancy), and long-term allergy/autoimmune disease risk.

Using economic and ecological arguments, justify why preserving microbial biodiversity in soil is as important as preserving charismatic megafauna (tigers, elephants).Hard
Show Answer

Soil microbes drive all terrestrial nutrient cycles, sequester 1.5 Gt carbon annually, decompose organic matter, suppress plant pathogens, and support crop productivity worth trillions of dollars. Loss of microbial diversity would cause ecosystem collapse far more certainly than megafauna extinction. Megafauna extinction is highly visible but functionally less critical than the invisible microbial foundation. Both deserve equivalent conservation priority.

Analyse how the discovery that microorganisms produce natural antibiotics changes our understanding of competition in microbial ecosystems.Hard
Show Answer

Antibiotics are microbial weapons in soil competition — Streptomyces and Penicillium evolved them to suppress competing microbes, not to treat human infections. This reveals soil as a chemical warfare zone. It explains why antibiotic resistance genes predate clinical antibiotics (found in 30,000-year-old permafrost bacteria). Understanding soil competition ecology may guide discovery of new antibiotic classes from unexplored soil microbiomes.

Propose how microorganisms could be used to address three major global challenges simultaneously: food, medicine, and environment.Hard
Show Answer

Food: engineered nitrogen-fixing microbes for non-legume crops reduce synthetic fertiliser by 30–50% while maintaining yields. Explain how cholera causes dehydration so rapidly that it can kill within hours.Medium
Show Answer

Vibrio cholerae produces cholera toxin that hijacks intestinal cells' cAMP signalling, causing massive secretion of water and electrolytes (sodium, chloride, potassium) into the intestine. This produces "rice water" diarrhoea at rates of 10–20 litres/day, causing shock and death from dehydration if untreated.

How does the Salmonella bacterium cause food poisoning, and what are the symptoms?Medium
Show Answer

Salmonella enters intestinal cells, triggering inflammation and disrupting fluid balance. Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, watery diarrhoea, and fever — beginning 6–48 hours after consuming contaminated food. Most cases resolve in 4–7 days; severe cases require hospitalisation.

Show Answer

Malaria transmission requires the Anopheles vector. Treating patients cures them but does not prevent new infections. Eliminating the mosquito vector breaks the transmission cycle. Measures: insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor spraying, larval habitat drainage. Without vector control, new cases continuously emerge even if treatment is available.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What is antibiotic resistance?Easy
Show Answer

Antibiotic resistance is when bacteria evolve the ability to survive and reproduce despite being exposed to antibiotics that would previously have killed them.

What does MRSA stand for and why is it dangerous?Easy
Show Answer

MRSA = Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus. It is dangerous because it resists most common antibiotics including methicillin, penicillin, and related drugs, making infections very difficult to treat.

Why should you complete the full course of antibiotics even if you feel better?Easy
Show Answer

Stopping early kills only the weakest bacteria. Hardier bacteria survive and multiply, becoming the next generation — which may be antibiotic resistant. Completing the course eliminates all bacteria, preventing resistance development.

Name one mechanism by which bacteria become resistant to antibiotics.Easy
Show Answer

Bacteria can produce enzymes (like beta-lactamase) that break down antibiotics before they can act on the cell. Other mechanisms include modified drug targets or increased drug efflux pumps.

Why is it wrong to take antibiotics for a cold or flu?Easy
Show Answer

Cold and flu are caused by viruses. Antibiotics kill bacteria only, not viruses. Taking antibiotics unnecessarily kills beneficial gut bacteria and selects for antibiotic-resistant bacteria without any benefit.

What is MDR-TB?Easy
Show Answer

MDR-TB = Multi-Drug Resistant Tuberculosis. It is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis strains resistant to at least isoniazid and rifampicin — the two most important TB antibiotics.

How does natural selection explain why bacteria become resistant to antibiotics?Easy
Show Answer

Random mutations create bacteria with slight resistance. Antibiotic exposure kills sensitive bacteria, allowing resistant ones to survive and reproduce (survival of the fittest). Repeated exposure selects increasingly resistant strains.

Name two practices that contribute to antibiotic resistance in India.Easy
Show Answer

1. Over-the-counter antibiotic sales without prescription. 2. Overuse of antibiotics in livestock farming (as growth promoters) are two major practices driving antibiotic resistance in India.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain how antibiotic resistance evolves in a bacterial population over multiple antibiotic courses.Medium
Show Answer

Before antibiotic: large population, mostly sensitive (S) with rare resistant (R) mutants. After course 1: most S die; R survive and reproduce → mostly resistant population. After course 2: same antibiotic kills fewer; increasingly resistant strains dominate. After repeated exposure: population is now largely resistant — natural selection has driven the evolution of resistance.

Compare MRSA infections acquired in hospitals versus community-acquired MRSA. Why do hospital infections tend to be more severe?Medium
Show Answer

Hospital MRSA (HA-MRSA): affects immunocompromised patients, often involves invasive devices (catheters, surgical wounds), access to more resistant strains. Community MRSA (CA-MRSA): typically healthier hosts, skin/soft tissue infections. Hospital patients have weaker immune responses and more entry points for bacteria, making infections more severe and harder to treat.

How does antibiotic use in livestock farming contribute to human antibiotic resistance?Medium
Show Answer

Livestock are given antibiotics as growth promoters in sub-therapeutic doses — ideal conditions for selecting resistant bacteria. Resistant bacteria contaminate meat during slaughter, enter the human gut when meat is undercooked, and transfer resistance genes to human gut bacteria. Resistant strains also spread to farms, water, and food crops via animal waste runoff.

Describe three mechanisms bacteria use to resist antibiotics.Medium
Show Answer

1. Enzyme production: beta-lactamase destroys penicillin's active ring. 2. Target modification: altered penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) no longer bind the antibiotic (MRSA mechanism). 3. Efflux pumps: membrane proteins actively pump antibiotics out of the bacterial cell before they can act.

A patient with MDR-TB needs treatment. What challenges does the doctor face compared to regular TB?Medium
Show Answer

MDR-TB requires second-line antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, injectable drugs) that are 10–100× more expensive, have more severe side effects, and require 18–24 months treatment (vs 6 months for regular TB). Treatment adherence is harder, cure rates are lower (~60%), and XDR-TB may be untreatable with available drugs.

Explain why antibiotic resistance is described as a "global commons tragedy" — a problem each individual creates but everyone suffers from.Medium
Show Answer

When any individual takes antibiotics unnecessarily, they contribute marginally to resistance. The collective effect of billions of unnecessary antibiotic courses erodes the effectiveness of antibiotics for everyone. But each individual bears little personal cost from their one action. No individual has sufficient incentive to change without collective governance — a classic tragedy of the commons.

How could phage therapy offer an alternative to antibiotics for resistant bacterial infections?Medium
Show Answer

Bacteriophages are viruses that specifically infect and kill bacteria. Unlike broad-spectrum antibiotics, phages can be tailored to target specific resistant strains. Bacteria that develop phage resistance often lose their antibiotic resistance simultaneously (co-evolutionary trade-off). Phage therapy is being revived for MRSA and MDR-TB cases where antibiotics fail.

Suggest three policy-level interventions to reduce antibiotic resistance in India.Medium
Show Answer

1. Enforce prescription-only antibiotic sales (currently poorly regulated). 2. Ban routine antibiotic use in livestock as growth promoters. 3. Fund antibiotic stewardship programmes in hospitals training doctors to use antibiotics appropriately, with antibiogram-guided prescribing.
