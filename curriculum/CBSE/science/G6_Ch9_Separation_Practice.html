Set A Foundation Questions Easy

What is a mixture?
Show Answer

A mixture is a combination of two or more substances that are NOT chemically bonded. Each substance keeps its own properties and can be separated by physical means.

Easy
Name two examples of mixtures found in your kitchen.
Show Answer

Examples: salt and pepper mixed together; rice and lentils; sugar in tea; flour and baking powder mixed for baking.

Easy
What is a pure substance? Give one example.
Show Answer

A pure substance contains only one type of particle throughout. Examples: pure water (H₂O), pure gold, pure table salt (NaCl), pure oxygen gas.

Easy
Is air a pure substance or a mixture? Explain.
Show Answer

Air is a mixture. It contains nitrogen (~78%), oxygen (~21%), argon, CO₂, and water vapour — not chemically bonded together.

Easy
True or False: In a mixture, substances are chemically bonded to each other.
Show Answer

False. In a mixture substances are physically combined but NOT chemically bonded. They can be separated by physical methods.

Easy
Can the ratio of substances in a mixture vary? Give an example.
Show Answer

Yes — mixtures have variable composition. Lemonade can have more or less sugar and still be a mixture of sugar and water.

Easy
Which is a pure substance? (a) Seawater (b) Sand (c) Distilled water (d) Soil
Show Answer

(c) Distilled water — contains only H₂O molecules. The others are all mixtures.

Easy
When you mix iron filings and sulfur powder, do they keep their individual properties?
Show Answer

Yes. The iron stays magnetic and the sulfur keeps its yellow colour — each substance retains its own properties in a mixture.

Easy
State one difference between a mixture and a compound.
Show Answer

In a mixture, substances are physically combined and can be separated by physical means. In a compound, elements are chemically bonded in fixed ratios and only chemical reactions can separate them.

Easy
Give three examples each of homogeneous and heterogeneous mixtures.
Show Answer

Homogeneous (uniform): salt water, sugar solution, air. Heterogeneous (not uniform): sand and water, salad, soil.

Easy

Set B Applying Concepts Medium

Explain: "Components of a mixture retain their physical properties." Give an example.
Show Answer

Each substance keeps its own properties. Iron filings mixed with sand are still attracted to a magnet — the iron does not lose its magnetic property just because it is mixed.

Medium
A student mixes blue sand and white salt. Is this homogeneous or heterogeneous? How can you tell?
Show Answer

Heterogeneous — you can clearly see two different coloured components. The mixture is not uniform throughout.

Medium
Why is bronze (copper + tin) classified as a mixture, not a compound?
Show Answer

Bronze is a mixture (alloy) because: the ratio of copper to tin can vary; the metals are not chemically bonded; and they can be separated by physical means such as melting and density separation.

Medium
Classify each: (a) Copper wire (b) Muddy water (c) Oxygen from a cylinder (d) Vinegar
Show Answer

(a) Pure substance – pure copper. (b) Mixture – mud + water. (c) Pure substance – only O₂. (d) Mixture – acetic acid dissolved in water.

Medium
Iron and sulfur are heated strongly and form a new substance with different properties. Why is this no longer a mixture?
Show Answer

Iron and sulfur have reacted chemically to form iron sulfide (FeS) — a compound. The product has different properties (not magnetic, different colour). A chemical bond formed, so it is no longer a mixture.

Medium
List three physical properties that can be used to separate components of a mixture.
Show Answer

1. Particle size (sieving). 2. Magnetic properties (magnetic separation). 3. Boiling point (distillation). Others: density, solubility.

Medium
Is blood homogeneous or heterogeneous? Justify your answer.
Show Answer

Heterogeneous — under a microscope you can see separate components: red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, and plasma. Components are not uniformly distributed at microscopic level.

Medium
Why can mixtures be separated by physical methods, but compounds cannot?
Show Answer

Mixtures are held by weak physical forces — components keep individual properties, so physical techniques (using size, density, magnetism) can pull them apart. Compounds have chemical bonds that require chemical reactions to break.

Medium
Brass can be 70% copper/30% zinc or 60% copper/40% zinc. What does this tell you about mixtures?
Show Answer

Mixtures have variable composition — the ratio of components can vary. Both are still brass (a mixture) but with different proportions. This contrasts with compounds which have fixed ratios.

Medium
A student says "pure orange juice is a pure substance." Is this correct? Explain.
Show Answer

No. In science, "pure" means one type of particle. Orange juice contains water, sugars, citric acid, vitamins, and minerals — it is a mixture. "Pure" in everyday language (unprocessed) differs from the scientific definition.

Set C MYP Inquiry & Reasoning Hard

Design an investigation to distinguish a pure substance from a mixture using melting point data. What results would you expect?
Show Answer

Method: heat each sample slowly and record temperature vs. time. Pure substance: melts at a sharp, fixed temperature (flat plateau on graph). Mixture: melts over a range of temperatures (no flat plateau). Impurities disrupt the crystal lattice, lowering and broadening the melting point.

Hard
Evaluate: "Separating a mixture is always easy and always possible." To what extent do you agree?
Show Answer

Partially agree. Separation is always theoretically possible (no chemical bonds). However, it is NOT always easy — some mixtures (fine suspensions, azeotropic liquids) require complex multi-step techniques. Cost, time, and practicality vary greatly.

Hard
Compare particle arrangements in (a) a pure solid element, (b) a homogeneous mixture, and (c) a heterogeneous mixture.
Show Answer

(a) Regular, close-packed identical particles in a lattice. (b) Solute particles randomly distributed among solvent particles — uniform at any scale. (c) Clusters of different particles visible — composition varies from region to region.

Hard
MYP Criterion D: Seawater is a mixture. Explain the scientific basis and describe TWO pieces of evidence you could gather to support it.
Show Answer

Scientific basis: Seawater contains water and dissolved salts not chemically bonded; composition varies by location. Evidence: (1) Evaporate a sample — white salt residue shows two separate substances. (2) Measure boiling point — seawater boils above 100°C, indicating dissolved substances raise the boiling point.

Hard
A geologist finds a rock sample. Explain how they would determine whether it is a pure mineral or a mixture of minerals, and why this matters.
Show Answer

Tests: (1) Examine under microscope — pure mineral shows single crystal type. (2) X-ray diffraction — pure mineral gives single pattern. (3) Check physical properties — consistent throughout for pure mineral. Importance: pure minerals have predictable industrial properties; mixtures (ores) need separation to extract useful components.

Hard
Explain why "variable composition" defines mixtures. Provide a real-world example.
Show Answer

Variable composition means mixtures can contain different proportions and still be the same type. Example: steel — low-carbon steel has <0.3% carbon; high-carbon steel has 0.6–1.0% carbon. Both are steel (iron + carbon mixture) with different properties. This contrasts with water (H₂O) which always has exactly 2H:1O.

Hard
A mixture contains: A (boiling point 78°C), B (boiling point 100°C), C (boiling point 197°C). Design a separation strategy explaining the science at each step.
Show Answer

Step 1: Heat to 78°C — A boils off, condense and collect. Step 2: Continue to 100°C — B distils over, condense. C remains as residue. Principle: each liquid has a unique boiling point; heating selectively converts the lowest-boiling component to vapour first (fractional distillation).

Hard
MYP Criterion B: A water treatment plant receives water with dissolved salts, suspended mud, and floating oil. What type of mixture is this and which properties would you exploit to clean it?
Show Answer

Complex heterogeneous + homogeneous mixture. (1) Oil — immiscible, less dense → separating funnel. (2) Suspended mud — insoluble particles → filtration (particle size). (3) Dissolved salts → distillation or reverse osmosis. Multi-step needed because no single method handles all three components.

Hard
Why do scientists need the concept of "pure substances" when almost everything around us is a mixture?
Show Answer

Pure substances serve as reference standards with fixed, reproducible properties (water boils at exactly 100°C at 1 atm). In medicine, pure drugs have predictable doses. In electronics, pure silicon performs consistently. Without purity, science cannot establish reliable baselines or quality control.

Hard
Extended response: A pharma company must purify paracetamol from a reaction mixture containing paracetamol, unreacted chemicals, and solvent. Outline the conceptual steps, linking each to mixture properties.
Show Answer

Step 1 – Filtration: remove insoluble solids (particle size difference). Step 2 – Extraction: use a solvent in which paracetamol is more soluble than impurities (differential solubility). Step 3 – Evaporation/Crystallisation: remove solvent by heating; paracetamol crystallises (boiling point difference). Step 4 – Recrystallisation: dissolve in hot solvent, cool slowly for purer crystals (temperature-dependent solubility). Step 5 – Drying: remove remaining solvent. Each step exploits a physical property difference without breaking chemical bonds in paracetamol.

Set A Foundation Questions Easy

Define solute, solvent, and solution.
Show Answer

Solute: substance that dissolves (smaller amount). Solvent: substance that dissolves the solute (larger amount). Solution: homogeneous mixture formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent.

Easy
In salt water, identify the solute and solvent.
Show Answer

Solute = salt (NaCl). Solvent = water (H₂O).

Easy
Why can you not see solute particles in a true solution?
Show Answer

Solute particles are broken down to individual ions or molecules (less than 1 nm) — too small to scatter light or be seen, even under a microscope.

Easy
Is a solution homogeneous or heterogeneous? Explain.
Show Answer

Homogeneous — the same composition and properties throughout. Any sample taken from anywhere in the solution has the same concentration.

Easy
Name three common solvents used in everyday life.
Show Answer

1. Water. 2. Ethanol/alcohol (perfumes, medicines). 3. Acetone (nail polish remover). Others: petrol, turpentine.

Easy
What is air? Identify the main solvent and one solute in air.
Show Answer

Air is a gaseous solution (homogeneous gaseous mixture). Main solvent = nitrogen (~78%). Solutes include oxygen (~21%), argon, CO₂.

Easy
What does it mean when a solution is "saturated"?
Show Answer

A saturated solution holds the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at that temperature. Adding more solute will leave it undissolved.

Easy
Give two examples of solutions NOT made with water as the solvent.
Show Answer

1. Iodine in alcohol (tincture of iodine — ethanol solvent). 2. Paint dissolved in turpentine.

Easy
True or False: A solution can be filtered to separate solute from solvent.
Show Answer

False. Dissolved particles are too small to be caught by filter paper — they pass straight through. Filtration only removes insoluble particles.

Easy
Why is water called the "universal solvent"?
Show Answer

Water can dissolve more substances than any other liquid. Its polar molecules attract and surround ions and polar molecules, making it excellent at dissolving salts, sugars, acids, and many other compounds.

Easy

Set B Applying Concepts Medium

Compare a solution, a suspension, and a colloid. Give one example of each.
Show Answer

Solution: homogeneous, particles <1 nm, transparent, does not settle. Example: salt water. Suspension: heterogeneous, particles >1000 nm, cloudy, settles on standing. Example: muddy water. Colloid: particles 1–1000 nm, appears uniform, does not settle easily. Example: milk.

Medium
How does temperature affect solubility of most solid solutes in water?
Show Answer

Solubility increases with temperature for most solids. More sugar dissolves in hot water than cold water — higher temperature provides more energy to break apart the crystal lattice.

Medium
Explain why sugar dissolves faster when stirred. What property does this relate to?
Show Answer

Stirring brings fresh solvent molecules to the solute surface, replacing the saturated layer. This affects the rate of dissolution (how quickly), NOT the solubility (total amount). Stirring does not change the saturation point.

Medium
20 g of salt is dissolved in 100 mL water (solubility of NaCl ≈ 36 g/100 mL). If you try to dissolve another 40 g, what happens?
Show Answer

Remaining capacity = 36 − 20 = 16 g more can dissolve. Only 16 g of the extra 40 g dissolves. The remaining 24 g stays as undissolved solid at the bottom.

Medium
Explain why solubility of gases in water decreases as temperature increases. Give a real-world consequence.
Show Answer

Higher temperature gives gas molecules more kinetic energy to escape from solution. Consequence: warm river water holds less dissolved oxygen, threatening fish survival — this is why thermal pollution from power plants harms aquatic ecosystems.

Medium
A tea bag is placed in hot water. Identify solute(s), solvent, and explain whether tea is a true solution or suspension.
Show Answer

Solvent: hot water. Solutes: tannins, caffeine, flavour compounds. Tea is mostly a true solution for dissolved compounds, but may also contain colloidal particles giving it colour — closer to a colloid for some components.

Medium
Describe the particle-level process when NaCl dissolves in water.
Show Answer

Polar water molecules are attracted to Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions at the crystal surface. Water molecules surround each ion (hydration) and pull them away from the lattice. Na⁺ is surrounded by oxygen ends of water; Cl⁻ by hydrogen ends. Eventually all ions are dispersed uniformly.

Medium
Explain "like dissolves like" and give one polar example and one non-polar example.
Show Answer

Polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes; non-polar solvents dissolve non-polar solutes. Polar: sugar (polar) dissolves in water (polar). Non-polar: wax (non-polar) dissolves in petrol/hexane (non-polar) but not in water.

Medium
How would you prepare a 10% (w/v) salt solution? Show your working.
Show Answer

10% w/v = 10 g per 100 mL solution. Weigh 10 g NaCl. Dissolve in ~80 mL distilled water. Transfer to 100 mL volumetric flask. Add distilled water to the 100 mL mark. Mix thoroughly.

Medium
What happens to a saturated solution when cooled? Why?
Show Answer

Solubility decreases on cooling. The solution can no longer hold all dissolved solute, so excess crystallises out — this is crystallisation. Solvent molecules have less energy to keep solute particles separated.

Medium

Set C MYP Inquiry & Reasoning Hard

Design an experiment to determine the solubility curve of KNO₃ from 20°C to 80°C. Include variables, method, expected results.
Show Answer

Independent variable: temperature (20–80°C in 10°C steps). Dependent variable: max mass of KNO₃ dissolving in 100 mL water. Controlled: volume of water, stirring method, pressure. Method: at each temperature, add KNO₃ until no more dissolves; record mass. Expected: solubility increases sharply (from ~31 g at 20°C to ~169 g at 80°C) giving an upward-curving solubility graph.

Hard
A supersaturated sodium acetate solution is disturbed by adding a small crystal. Describe and explain what happens using particle theory.
Show Answer

Rapid crystallisation occurs. The small crystal provides a nucleation site — a template for dissolved ions to attach to and form a crystal lattice. Once started, crystallisation propagates rapidly as dissolved particles align onto the growing crystal, releasing energy (exothermic). The solution contains more solute than the equilibrium amount.

Hard
The Dead Sea has 34% salinity vs. normal seawater at 3.5%. Explain consequences on (a) floating objects, (b) aquatic life, (c) salt extraction methods.
Show Answer

(a) Higher salinity = greater density → greater upthrust → humans float more easily. (b) High salt concentration causes osmotic stress — water moves out of organisms' cells by osmosis, dehydrating and killing them. (c) Simple evaporation is efficient: high concentration means less water removal needed to reach saturation. Large evaporation ponds are used commercially.

Hard
Explain why increasing surface area of a solute increases dissolution rate but NOT the total amount that dissolves.
Show Answer

Larger surface area exposes more solute particles to solvent simultaneously — dissolution is faster. However, solubility (total amount) is determined by thermodynamic equilibrium — the balance between dissolving energy and crystal lattice energy — which is independent of surface area. Once saturation is reached, no more dissolves regardless.

Hard
MYP Criterion D: Evaluate — "All solutions are liquids." Provide evidence for and against.
Show Answer

Against (statement is incorrect): Solutions exist in all three states. Gaseous solutions: air. Solid solutions: alloys — brass (Cu+Zn), steel (Fe+C). For (partial): Most familiar everyday solutions are liquid. Conclusion: Solutions are homogeneous mixtures in any state of matter.

Hard
A scientist needs to separate a dissolved dye from water. Simple filtration fails. Explain why, and suggest TWO alternatives with scientific basis.
Show Answer

Filtration fails — dissolved dye is molecular-scale, passes through filter pores. Alternatives: (1) Distillation — heat the solution; water evaporates, dye remains (boiling point difference). (2) Chromatography — dye molecules adsorb differently from water based on polarity, separating by travel distance. (3) Reverse osmosis — force water through a membrane under pressure; large dye molecules cannot pass.

Hard
Compare the Tyndall effect in solutions versus colloids. How can this be used as a practical test to distinguish them?
Show Answer

Tyndall effect: colloidal particles (1–1000 nm) scatter light — beam is visible from the side. True solutions: particles too small (<1 nm) — beam is invisible. Practical test: shine a torch through sample in a darkened room. Visible light beam = colloid. No beam = true solution.

Hard
A carbonated drink goes flat faster when warm. Explain using gas solubility and Henry's Law.
Show Answer

Henry's Law: gas solubility is proportional to partial pressure above the liquid. When opened, pressure drops → CO₂ solubility decreases → CO₂ escapes as bubbles. Temperature also matters: at higher temperature, CO₂ molecules have more kinetic energy and escape more easily from the liquid. Therefore warm drink loses CO₂ faster.

Hard
Extended response: Explain how solutions are applied in the human body with TWO specific examples including what dissolves in what and why it matters.
Show Answer

Example 1 – Blood plasma: water (solvent) dissolves glucose, proteins, hormones, CO₂, ions. These are transported throughout the body. Dehydration (too concentrated) or overhydration (too dilute) disrupts cell function. Example 2 – Gastric acid: HCl dissolved in water (pH 1–3). H⁺ ions activate digestive enzymes (pepsin) and kill bacteria. The acidic solution denatures proteins, beginning digestion. In both cases, dissolution in water is essential to life.

Hard
Critique this experiment: A student dissolves 5 g sugar in 50 mL water and concludes "solubility of sugar = 5 g per 50 mL." What is wrong and how would you improve it?
Show Answer

Problems: (1) Only tested whether 5 g dissolves — this is NOT the maximum (solubility limit). Solubility = maximum amount dissolvable. (2) Temperature not controlled/stated. (3) No repetition. Improved: Keep temperature constant (e.g., 25°C). Add sugar in small increments (0.5 g) to 50 mL water. Record when no more dissolves. Repeat 3 times and average. Report as "X g per 100 mL water at 25°C."

Set A Foundation Questions Easy

What is sieving and what property of substances does it exploit?
Show Answer

Sieving is a method of separating a mixture of solids with different particle sizes. It exploits the difference in particle size — smaller particles pass through the holes (mesh) of a sieve, while larger particles are retained.

Easy
Name two household examples where sieving is used.
Show Answer

1. Sifting flour to remove lumps or husks. 2. Using a tea strainer to separate tea leaves from liquid. 3. Draining cooked pasta through a colander.

Easy
In a mixture of rice and sand, which component passes through a fine sieve? Which stays behind?
Show Answer

Sand (smaller particles) passes through the fine sieve. Rice (larger particles) stays behind (is retained by the sieve).

Easy
True or False: Sieving can separate dissolved salt from water.
Show Answer

False. Dissolved salt particles are molecular-sized (too small for any sieve). Sieving only works for mixtures where components have visible size differences.

Easy
What determines whether a particle passes through a sieve or not?
Show Answer

The size of the particle relative to the size of the holes (mesh size) in the sieve. Particles smaller than the mesh holes pass through; particles larger than the holes are retained.

Easy
Name one industrial application of sieving.
Show Answer

Construction sites use sieving/screening to sort gravel and sand by size for different uses (e.g., fine sand for plastering, coarse gravel for concrete). Also: flour mills, coal sorting, pharmaceutical powder grading.

Easy
What type of mixture is suitable for separation by sieving?
Show Answer

A mixture of solid particles of different sizes. Both components must be solids (or one is a liquid used as medium), and there must be a significant size difference between them.

Easy
A cook sieves flour before baking. What is removed by the sieve?
Show Answer

Large lumps of flour, husks, bran particles, or any foreign objects (e.g., insect parts, grit) that are larger than the fine mesh holes are removed. Fine flour particles pass through.

Easy
Is sieving a chemical or physical method of separation? Explain.
Show Answer

Physical method. No new substances are created and no chemical bonds are broken or formed. The process only sorts particles by size.

Easy
Give the name of the material retained by the sieve and the material that passes through.
Show Answer

Material retained = residue (on the sieve). Material that passes through = filtrate or siftings (that which is sifted through). In food context: the retained material is often called "bran" or "coarse fraction."

Easy

Set B Applying Concepts Medium

A construction worker wants to separate a mixture of large gravel, small gravel, and sand. Describe how they would use sieving to sort these into three fractions.
Show Answer

Use two sieves with different mesh sizes. Sieve 1 (large mesh): pour mixture through — large gravel retained on top, small gravel + sand pass through. Sieve 2 (fine mesh): pour the mixture through — small gravel retained, sand passes through. Result: three separate fractions obtained.

Medium
Explain why sieving is effective for separating rice from small pebbles but NOT effective for separating salt from sugar.
Show Answer

Rice and pebbles have a very different particle size — a suitable sieve mesh can distinguish them easily. Salt and sugar crystals are similar in size — no practical sieve can distinguish them. Sieving requires a sufficient SIZE DIFFERENCE between components.

Medium
A scientist wants to separate a powder into particles of specific sizes. Describe how they would use a series of sieves with decreasing mesh sizes.
Show Answer

Stack sieves from largest mesh (top) to smallest mesh (bottom) with a collection pan at the bottom. Pour powder through the stack. Each sieve retains particles larger than its mesh. Result: different size fractions collected at each level — this is called "sieve analysis" or "particle size distribution."

Medium
In traditional Indian farming, grain is separated from sand using sieving. Explain the process and the property being exploited.
Show Answer

A sieve with mesh size between grain and sand particle sizes is used. The sand (smaller particles) falls through the mesh while the grain (larger particles) is retained. The property exploited is particle size difference.

Medium
How would you choose the correct mesh size for a particular sieving task? Give a specific example.
Show Answer

The mesh size must be: (1) smaller than the particles you want to retain, and (2) larger than the particles you want to pass through. Example: to separate chickpeas (5 mm diameter) from coarse sand (1 mm) — choose a mesh with ~2–3 mm holes. Chickpeas are retained; sand passes through.

Medium
What are the limitations of sieving as a separation technique? Name three.
Show Answer

1. Only works for solid particle mixtures (cannot separate dissolved substances). 2. Requires a significant size difference between components. 3. Very fine particles may clog the mesh (blinding the sieve). 4. Cannot separate solids with similar sizes. 5. Not useful if components are similar in size but different in density.

Medium
A mining company extracts ore containing large valuable rocks and waste fine powder. How would sieving help in their processing?
Show Answer

Sieving separates the coarse ore pieces (retained on sieve) from the fine waste powder (passes through). This concentrates the valuable mineral in one fraction and reduces the volume that needs further processing — saving time and energy in subsequent steps.

Medium
Why do bakers sieve flour even when there are no visible lumps? What benefit does sieving provide beyond removing large particles?
Show Answer

Sieving aerates the flour — it introduces air between flour particles. This makes the flour lighter and helps it mix more evenly with other ingredients, resulting in a lighter, more uniform baked product. It also removes fine clumps not visible to the naked eye.

Medium
Compare sieving with filtration. When would you choose sieving over filtration?
Show Answer

Sieving: separates dry solid mixtures by particle size using a mesh; typically for larger particle differences; done in air. Filtration: separates insoluble solids from liquids using filter paper. Choose sieving over filtration when both components are solid (not in liquid medium) and have different particle sizes. Filtration is needed when one component is a liquid.

Medium
Design a simple experiment to test whether a kitchen sieve can effectively separate a mixture of rice and table salt.
Show Answer

Method: Measure equal masses of rice and table salt; mix thoroughly. Weigh the mixture (total mass). Pass through kitchen sieve over clean paper. Collect material that passes through and material retained. Weigh each fraction separately. Examine each fraction (look/taste carefully). Expected result: salt passes through (smaller particles), rice is retained. Check that masses add up to original total mass (conservation of mass).

MYP Criterion B: A pharmaceutical company manufactures tablet granules of three different sizes: 0.5 mm, 1.5 mm, and 3 mm. Design a sieve-based quality control system to verify granule size distribution and ensure correct granules go into each product.
Show Answer

Use a series of three sieves: 2 mm mesh, 1 mm mesh, 0.25 mm mesh, stacked from top to bottom with a collection pan. Pour sample through. 3 mm granules retained on 2 mm sieve; 1.5 mm granules retained on 1 mm sieve; 0.5 mm granules retained on 0.25 mm sieve; dust/fines collected in pan. Weigh each fraction. Quality check: verify mass percentage in each fraction matches specification. Out-of-specification batches are rejected or reprocessed.

Hard
Evaluate: "Sieving is a simple, low-tech process with no real application in modern industry." To what extent is this true?
Show Answer

This is largely false. Sieving (industrial screening/fractionation) is used extensively: food processing (flour grading, coffee bean sorting), construction (aggregate sizing for concrete), mining (ore classification), pharmaceuticals (powder particle size control), recycling (sorting materials by size). Modern sieves use vibration, ultrasound, and automated systems — far from low-tech. The principle is simple but the applications are highly sophisticated and economically important.

Hard
A mixture of glass beads has an unknown size distribution. Design a sieve analysis experiment to determine the percentage mass of particles in each size range. Include a results table and graph type.
Show Answer

Equipment: sieves of mesh sizes 4 mm, 2 mm, 1 mm, 0.5 mm, 0.25 mm; balance; collection pan. Method: weigh total sample (e.g., 100 g). Stack sieves (largest on top). Shake mechanically for 10 minutes. Weigh each fraction. Results table: | Sieve size (mm) | Mass retained (g) | % mass |. Calculate % = (mass retained / total) × 100. Graph: histogram of % mass vs. sieve size (particle size distribution curve). This is standard "sieve analysis" used in civil engineering (ASTM standards).

Hard
In the context of soil science, explain how sieve analysis is used to classify soil texture (clay, silt, sand, gravel) and why this classification matters for farming.
Show Answer

Soil texture is determined by particle size distribution. Sieve analysis separates: gravel (>2 mm), coarse sand (0.2–2 mm), fine sand (0.02–0.2 mm). Clay and silt (<0.02 mm) require sedimentation analysis. Farming importance: sandy soils (large particles) drain quickly — need frequent irrigation, little waterlogging. Clay soils (small particles) retain water and nutrients but may waterlog. Loam (balanced mix) is ideal for most crops. Farmers choose crops, irrigation, and fertiliser strategies based on soil texture.

Hard
Extended response (MYP Criterion D): A food inspector is analysing wheat flour samples for adulteration with chalk powder (CaCO₃). Both are white powders. Explain whether sieving alone can detect the adulteration, and if not, what combination of methods would work. Justify each step scientifically.
Show Answer

Sieving alone: may not be sufficient if chalk and flour particles are similar in size. However, chalk particles (ground CaCO₃) are often denser and may have different size distribution — sieving could partially separate them but would not definitively confirm identity. Better approach: (1) Add dilute HCl — chalk fizzes (CO₂ produced) but flour does not — confirmatory chemical test. (2) Burn a sample — flour chars/burns; chalk remains as white powder. (3) Microscopy — chalk shows distinct crystal shapes vs. flour starch granules. (4) Density separation — chalk (density ~2.7 g/cm³) is denser than flour (~0.5 g/cm³ bulk density). Scientific conclusion: sieving is a preliminary physical separation tool, but chemical tests are needed for positive identification of adulteration.

Hard
Explain the concept of "effective mesh size" in sieves and why particles smaller than the nominal mesh size may still be retained.
Show Answer

Nominal mesh size is the theoretical opening size. Effective mesh size may differ due to: (1) Particle shape — elongated particles may pass through at one orientation but not another; (2) Particle agglomeration — fine particles clump together, effectively increasing their size; (3) Mesh distortion — wear and use deform the mesh openings; (4) Static charge — fine particles may electrostatically stick to the mesh. Consequence: sieve analysis gives approximate, not exact, size distributions. In practice, sieves are regularly calibrated and particles are assumed to be spherical for standardised analysis.

Hard
How does the principle of sieving appear in natural geological processes? Describe one natural "sieve" process.
Show Answer

Natural sieve process — river sorting (fluvial sorting): as river water flows, larger/heavier particles (pebbles, gravel) settle first in fast-moving sections; smaller particles (sand) settle in slower sections; finest particles (silt, clay) are carried furthest before settling. The varying current speed acts as a natural sieve, separating particles by size over distance. This creates stratified sediment deposits with predictable size distribution — geologists use this to reconstruct ancient river systems.

Hard
A student claims: "The smaller the mesh size, the better the sieve." Critically evaluate this claim.
Show Answer

Incorrect — mesh size must match the specific task. A very small mesh: retains almost all particles (not useful if you want separation); clogs easily; is fragile and expensive. A very large mesh: allows most particles through (no effective separation). "Better" depends on the size difference between components to be separated. Explain the scientific principle behind winnowing using the concepts of mass, gravity, and air resistance.
Show Answer

When the mixture is tossed into air, gravity pulls both components downward. Air resistance acts upward on both. For the lighter component (chaff): air resistance is comparable to or greater than its weight — wind carries it laterally. For the heavier component (grain): weight greatly exceeds air resistance — it falls nearly straight down. The net result is spatial separation of the two components.

Using the terminal velocity concept, explain mathematically why a grain falls faster than chaff in still air. (Hint: terminal velocity depends on mass, drag coefficient, and cross-sectional area.)
Show Answer

Terminal velocity: Vt = √(2mg / ρ·A·Cd) where m=mass, g=gravity, ρ=air density, A=cross-sectional area, Cd=drag coefficient. For grain: larger m, similar A to chaff → higher Vt → grain falls faster and less affected by horizontal wind. For chaff: smaller m, similar or larger A → lower Vt → chaff is suspended longer and blown sideways. In a horizontal wind, chaff's lower Vt means it spends more time in the air → more lateral displacement. Grain's high Vt means rapid descent → minimal lateral displacement.

What is filtration and what type of mixture does it separate?

Set A Foundation Questions Easy

What is evaporation and what type of mixture does it help separate?
Show Answer

Evaporation is the process of heating a solution so that the solvent (liquid) turns into vapour and escapes, leaving behind the dissolved solid (solute). It separates a dissolved solid from its solvent.

Easy
Name two real-world examples where evaporation is used as a separation technique.
Show Answer

1. Salt production from seawater — water evaporates in shallow ponds (salt pans), leaving salt crystals. 2. Making sugar — sugarcane juice is evaporated to concentrate and crystallise sugar. 3. Drying clothes — water evaporates from wet fabric.

Easy
What happens to the solute when a solution is evaporated?
Show Answer

The solute (dissolved substance) remains behind as the solvent evaporates. As the solution becomes more concentrated, eventually the solute crystallises out as a solid residue.

Easy
Why does salty water from the sea become saltier as it evaporates in shallow salt pans?
Show Answer

As water evaporates from the salt pan, the amount of water decreases but the amount of salt stays the same. The same amount of salt is now dissolved in less water — the concentration increases. Eventually the solution becomes saturated and salt crystallises out.

Easy
True or False: After evaporation, you can collect the pure solvent back.
Show Answer

False — in simple evaporation, the vapour escapes into the air and is lost. If you want to collect the solvent as well, you need distillation (which condenses and collects the vapour).

Easy
What energy source is used in salt pans to evaporate seawater?
Show Answer

Solar energy (heat from the Sun) and wind evaporate the water naturally from the shallow salt pans. This makes salt production very low-cost and sustainable.

Easy
Name the type of dish used in lab evaporation experiments.
Show Answer

Evaporating basin (evaporating dish) — a shallow, wide porcelain dish that allows maximum surface area for rapid evaporation when heated over a Bunsen burner or hot plate.

Easy
Can evaporation remove dissolved gas from water? Give an example.
Show Answer

Yes. Dissolved gases (such as CO₂ in sparkling water, or dissolved oxygen in boiled water) evaporate when water is heated. Boiling water drives off dissolved CO₂ and O₂.

Easy
Is evaporation the same as boiling? Explain one key difference.
Show Answer

No. Evaporation occurs at the surface of a liquid at any temperature (below boiling point). Boiling occurs throughout the liquid at its specific boiling point when vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure. Evaporation is slower; boiling is rapid.

Easy
What is crystallisation and how does it relate to evaporation?
Show Answer

Crystallisation is the process where a dissolved solid forms solid crystals as the solution becomes concentrated (or is cooled). Evaporation concentrates the solution by removing solvent, which triggers crystallisation. Slow evaporation gives larger, purer crystals.

Easy

Set B Applying Concepts Medium

Compare simple evaporation and crystallisation for recovering salt from seawater. When would you prefer crystallisation over simple evaporation?
Show Answer

Simple evaporation: heat strongly until all water gone — quick but may give impure product (other dissolved substances also remain). Crystallisation: heat until concentrated, then cool slowly — allows pure crystals to form selectively. Prefer crystallisation when: purity is important; you want well-formed large crystals (e.g., for scientific or culinary purposes).

Medium
Explain why evaporation is not suitable for separating a mixture of alcohol and water. What method would you use instead?
Show Answer

Both alcohol and water are liquids and both evaporate — you would not recover pure alcohol (some water vapour would also evaporate). If you want to separate two volatile liquids with different boiling points (alcohol: 78°C, water: 100°C) you should use distillation, which condensed and collects the vapour of the more volatile component.

Medium
Why should you NOT evaporate salt solution to complete dryness in a single strong heating? What can go wrong?
Show Answer

Heating to complete dryness: (1) Risk of splattering — last drops of solution spit and spray crystals out. (2) Over-heating may decompose or char the solid. (3) Steam burns the person nearby. Better practice: evaporate until a concentrated syrup, then allow to cool and crystallise slowly, or remove from heat when crystals first appear.

Medium
In India, sea salt is produced by solar evaporation in salt pans. Describe the process step by step.
Show Answer

Step 1: Seawater is pumped into a series of large, shallow ponds. Step 2: Sun and wind evaporate the water over days to weeks. Step 3: As water evaporates, concentration increases; less soluble salts (gypsum, CaSO₄) crystallise first and are removed. Step 4: More water evaporates until NaCl (table salt) crystallises. Step 5: Salt crystals are scraped, collected, washed, and dried. Step 6: Further refining removes remaining impurities.

Medium
How does the rate of evaporation affect the size of crystals formed? Explain with reference to particle theory.
Show Answer

Slow evaporation: solute particles have time to arrange themselves into an ordered crystal lattice slowly → large, regular crystals. Rapid evaporation: solute particles crystallise out quickly before they can form ordered structures → small, irregular crystals. Particle theory: crystal growth requires particles to find their correct positions in the lattice; slow evaporation gives enough time; fast evaporation does not.

Medium
Explain how evaporation is used to concentrate fruit juice. What physical and chemical changes occur?
Show Answer

Physical: water is removed by heating (or vacuum evaporation at lower temperature). The juice becomes more viscous and concentrated. Physical changes: water content decreases, sugar concentration increases, density increases. Chemical changes at high temperature: some vitamins (C) may be destroyed; Maillard reaction may cause browning; flavour compounds may change. Modern low-temperature vacuum evaporation minimises chemical changes while efficiently removing water.

Medium
A student evaporates a copper sulfate solution and notices the crystals are blue. After heating strongly, the crystals turn white. Explain this observation.
Show Answer

Blue crystals: copper sulfate pentahydrate (CuSO₄·5H₂O) — each crystal contains 5 water molecules of crystallisation. Heating: water of crystallisation is driven off → anhydrous copper sulfate (CuSO₄) which is white. This demonstrates that the blue colour in these crystals comes from the water molecules coordinated around Cu²⁺ ions.

Medium
How does the large surface area of an evaporating basin improve the efficiency of evaporation?
Show Answer

Evaporation occurs at the surface of a liquid — molecules at the surface escape into the vapour phase. Larger surface area → more molecules at the surface at any time → more molecules can escape per unit time → faster evaporation. Also allows air circulation to carry away water vapour, preventing re-absorption.

Medium
Why is vacuum evaporation used in the food industry rather than normal evaporation for concentrating milk?
Show Answer

Vacuum evaporation lowers the pressure above the liquid, which lowers the boiling point of water (e.g., at 0.2 atm, water boils at ~60°C). Advantages: (1) Milk is concentrated at lower temperature → proteins, vitamins, and flavours are not destroyed by high heat. (2) Energy efficient. (3) Better quality product (preserves nutrients and taste). Normal evaporation at 100°C would cook the milk, changing its taste and destroying vitamins.

Medium
Distinguish between evaporation and transpiration. How does transpiration relate to the water cycle?
Show Answer

Evaporation: water turning to vapour from surfaces of water bodies (lakes, oceans, puddles) — purely physical. Transpiration: water vapour released through tiny pores (stomata) on leaves of plants — biological process. Both contribute to the water cycle: evapotranspiration (combined term) moves water from land into the atmosphere as water vapour, which condenses to form clouds and falls as precipitation (rain/snow), replenishing water bodies.

Extended response: Compare the energy requirements and efficiency of solar evaporation in salt pans versus modern industrial evaporation methods. Which is more sustainable and why?
Show Answer

Solar evaporation: uses FREE solar energy; zero fossil fuel; zero carbon emissions during operation; very low cost; but: very slow (weeks to months), large land area needed, only works in sunny/dry climates, quality control difficult, seasonal limitations. Hard
Explain the concept of "water of crystallisation" using copper sulfate as an example. How does this relate to the reversibility of crystallisation?
Show Answer

Water of crystallisation: water molecules that are incorporated into the crystal structure as part of the crystalline lattice (not just surface water). CuSO₄·5H₂O: five water molecules per formula unit are bonded to the Cu²⁺ ion, giving the characteristic blue colour and octahedral structure. Heating drives off water of crystallisation → white anhydrous CuSO₄. Adding water back → blue hydrated crystals reform. This shows reversibility: CuSO₄·5H₂O ⇌ CuSO₄ + 5H₂O. The process is reversible — a key property of physical (not chemical) change. Application: anhydrous CuSO₄ is used as a chemical test for water (turns blue on contact).

Hard
MYP Criterion D: Evaluate evaporation as a method for providing fresh water in arid regions. Include scientific principles, advantages, limitations, and one improvement.
Show Answer

Method: solar stills — plastic sheets over saline water; sun evaporates water; vapour condenses on cool plastic; pure condensate drips into collection. Scientific principle: water evaporates preferentially (salts and impurities are non-volatile); condensation on cool surface collects pure water. Advantages: uses solar energy; no chemicals; produces pure water; simple construction. Limitations: very low yield (only 0.5–3 L/m²/day); large area needed for significant output; plastic degrades over time; not effective in cold/cloudy climates. Improvement: multi-stage solar desalination — heated vapour condenses in a cooled chamber, condensation heat pre-heats the next stage of incoming seawater → "multi-effect" uses each unit of heat multiple times, increasing yield by 3–5×.

Hard
Explain the industrial process of "spray drying" as an advanced form of evaporation. Give two products manufactured this way.
Show Answer

Spray drying: the solution is atomised into tiny droplets using a nozzle and sprayed into a chamber of hot gas (180–300°C). Because droplets are tiny (very large surface area:volume ratio), water evaporates almost instantaneously (milliseconds) — leaving dry powder. The short contact time with heat prevents thermal degradation of heat-sensitive materials. Products: (1) Instant milk powder — milk solution spray-dried to fine powder. (2) Instant coffee — brewed coffee spray-dried. (3) Pharmaceuticals — drug solutions spray-dried to stable powders. Principle same as basic evaporation: remove liquid solvent to leave solid solute; but vastly accelerated by maximising surface area.

Hard
Critically evaluate: "Evaporation is always the best way to get salt from seawater." To what extent is this true?
Show Answer

True in many contexts: solar evaporation is the cheapest and most widespread method for salt production globally; it produces 30% of world salt supply. Not always best: (1) In cloudy/cold climates (Northern Europe) — insufficient solar energy; mining rock salt is more efficient. (2) When high-purity salt is needed — solar salt contains gypsum, magnesium, and other impurities requiring further refining. (3) When fresh water is the priority product — desalination (reverse osmosis or distillation) is more efficient for producing water. (4) When specific minerals are wanted — electrodialysis can selectively extract specific ions. Conclusion: evaporation is often best for scale and cost but context determines optimal method.

Hard
How does perspiration (sweating) in humans function as a biological application of evaporation? Explain the thermodynamics involved.
Show Answer

Perspiration: sweat glands secrete a dilute salt solution onto skin surface. As water evaporates from skin: it absorbs latent heat of vaporisation (~2,260 J per gram of water) from the skin surface. This energy comes from the body's heat — the skin cools as heat is transferred to evaporating water molecules. Thermodynamics: evaporation is endothermic at the surface — it requires energy (latent heat) to break hydrogen bonds holding water molecules in liquid. This energy is taken from the skin, reducing skin temperature. Even 1 g of sweat evaporating removes 2,260 J (0.54 kcal) of heat — very efficient cooling mechanism. Humidity matters: in humid air, evaporation rate decreases (air already saturated) → less cooling → why humid heat feels worse than dry heat.

Hard
Describe the difference between simple evaporation and evaporation under reduced pressure (vacuum). How does Raoult's Law explain why reduction in pressure lowers the boiling point?
Show Answer

Simple evaporation at atmospheric pressure: water boils at 100°C; solute must withstand this temperature. Vacuum evaporation: reducing pressure (e.g., to 0.2 atm) lowers boiling point of water (to ~60°C). Raoult's Law: the vapour pressure of a pure solvent is lowered when solute is added (P = x·P°, where x = mole fraction). However, reducing external pressure: boiling occurs when vapour pressure of liquid = external pressure. Lowering external pressure to 0.2 atm means liquid needs less kinetic energy to overcome it → lower temperature sufficient for evaporation/boiling. Application: vacuum evaporation used for heat-sensitive biologics, pharmaceuticals, milk, juices — preserving nutritional and structural integrity.

Hard
IB MYP Criterion B: Formulate a hypothesis and design an experiment to test whether adding salt to water affects its evaporation rate. Predict and explain the expected result using particle theory.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: "Adding salt to water will decrease the rate of evaporation, because dissolved Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions attract water molecules and reduce their tendency to escape the liquid surface." Variables: Independent: salt concentration (0 g, 5 g, 10 g, 20 g, 35 g per 100 mL water). Dependent: mass loss per minute at constant temperature. Controlled: temperature (30°C), volume (100 mL), container size, humidity, air movement. Method: weigh samples every 5 min for 30 min; calculate rate. Particle explanation: dissolved ions form hydration shells — each Na⁺ and Cl⁻ binds several water molecules strongly. These bound water molecules have much less tendency to escape to the vapour phase, lowering the vapour pressure of the solution (Raoult's Law). Lower vapour pressure = slower evaporation. Expected result: rate decreases with increasing salt concentration.

Hard
Extended response: Connect the water cycle to the separation technique of evaporation. Explain how nature performs a form of distillation continuously. What does this produce and why is it important for life on Earth?
Show Answer

Nature's distillation cycle: (1) Solar energy heats ocean/lake water. (2) Evaporation: water molecules with sufficient kinetic energy escape the surface as vapour — leaving behind dissolved salts, minerals, and most impurities. (3) Water vapour rises into atmosphere — cools at altitude. (4) Condensation: vapour condenses around dust particles forming clouds — liquid water droplets. (5) Precipitation: rain and snow fall — delivering naturally distilled (essentially pure) water to land. This is natural distillation: solar energy drives evaporation of saline ocean water; condensation on cooling produces pure fresh water; gravity delivers it. Importance: produces all fresh water on Earth — rivers, lakes, groundwater, glaciers that sustain all land life. Without solar-powered evaporation, ocean salinity would prevent life from colonising land. The water cycle has produced and maintained the freshwater supply that enabled the evolution of terrestrial life for ~400 million years.

Set A Foundation Questions Easy

What is distillation and what property does it exploit to separate substances?
Show Answer

Distillation is a separation method that heats a liquid mixture until the more volatile component evaporates, then cools and condenses the vapour to collect it as a pure liquid. It exploits differences in boiling points between liquids.

Easy
What is the difference between evaporation and distillation?
Show Answer

Evaporation: the vapour is allowed to escape into the air — the liquid solvent is lost and the solid solute is collected. Distillation: the vapour is collected and condensed back to liquid — both components can be recovered separately.

Easy
Name the apparatus through which the vapour passes and is cooled during distillation.
Show Answer

The condenser (Liebig condenser) — a long glass tube surrounded by a jacket of cold water. The vapour passes through the inner tube and is cooled by water in the outer jacket, condensing back to liquid.

Easy
Give two examples of products made or purified using distillation.
Show Answer

1. Fresh water from seawater (desalination by distillation). 2. Alcoholic spirits (whisky, vodka — alcohol distilled from fermented mixture). 3. Perfume — fragrant oils distilled from flowers. 4. Petroleum refining — crude oil fractionally distilled to give petrol, diesel, kerosene.

Easy
Why is simple distillation not used to separate two liquids with very similar boiling points?
Show Answer

If boiling points are similar (less than ~25°C apart), both liquids evaporate at similar temperatures and the vapour is a mixture of both — distillation gives an impure product. Fractional distillation (with a fractionating column) is needed instead.

Easy
Water boils at 100°C and ethanol boils at 78°C. When a water-ethanol mixture is heated, which component evaporates first?
Show Answer

Ethanol evaporates first because it has the lower boiling point (78°C). At temperatures between 78°C and 100°C, ethanol boils off while water remains mostly as liquid.

Easy
What is a thermometer used for during distillation?
Show Answer

To monitor the temperature of the vapour rising from the flask. When the temperature stabilises at a specific value (e.g., 78°C for ethanol), it confirms that only that particular component is being collected. A change in temperature signals a different component starting to distil.

Easy
What is the distillate?
Show Answer

The distillate is the liquid collected after the vapour is condensed in the condenser. It is the purified (or separated) component that was vaporised from the original mixture.

Easy
True or False: Distillation can separate salt from seawater and collect fresh water.
Show Answer

True. Water is heated from seawater → water vapour rises (salt cannot evaporate — it is ionic and non-volatile) → vapour is condensed back to pure liquid water. Salt remains in the flask.

Easy
Why is the cold water in the condenser jacket run in the opposite direction to the vapour flow?
Show Answer

Running cold water counter-current to the vapour ensures the coolest water meets the hottest incoming vapour first (at the top of the condenser), providing maximum temperature difference and efficient cooling throughout the condenser length. This gives better and more complete condensation.

Easy

Set B Applying Concepts Medium

Describe, step by step, the laboratory distillation of salt water to obtain pure water.
Show Answer

1. Pour salt water into round-bottom flask. Add boiling chips (to prevent bumping). 2. Attach thermometer (bulb at side arm junction). 3. Connect condenser — cold water running through jacket. 4. Connect collection flask at condenser outlet. 5. Heat flask slowly. 6. Water vapour rises at ~100°C. 7. Vapour condenses in condenser to liquid water. 8. Pure water (distillate) collects in collection flask. 9. Salt remains in round-bottom flask as residue.

Medium
Explain how fractional distillation separates crude oil into different fractions. Why is this process important?
Show Answer

Crude oil (mixture of hydrocarbons with different boiling points) is heated at the bottom of a fractionating tower. As vapour rises through the column, it cools. Compounds condense at different levels: heaviest (highest boiling point — bitumen) at bottom; progressively lighter fractions (diesel, kerosene, petrol/gasoline) condense at higher levels; lightest (petroleum gas) exits at top. Importance: crude oil is the raw material for fuels (petrol, diesel, jet fuel), plastics, medicines, lubricants — fractional distillation is the first step in converting raw crude to useful products.

Medium
What is steam distillation and why is it used in perfume production?
Show Answer

Steam distillation: steam is passed through plant material (petals, herbs). The steam carries fragrant essential oil vapour. The mixture of steam + essential oil vapour is condensed — giving a layer of essential oil floating on water. Used in perfumes because: essential oils have boiling points above 200°C (would decompose if heated directly). Steam distillation brings them over at ~100°C (with the steam), preventing thermal decomposition and preserving delicate fragrance molecules.

Medium
Why is fractional distillation more effective than simple distillation for separating liquids with similar boiling points?
Show Answer

A fractionating column provides many "theoretical plates" (stages) — each acts as a mini-distillation. Vapour rising through the column repeatedly condenses and re-evaporates. Each cycle enriches the vapour in the more volatile component. After many cycles in the column, the vapour reaching the top is essentially pure more-volatile component. Simple distillation provides only one stage — insufficient to separate components with close boiling points.

Medium
Why is distillation used for desalination of seawater in some countries? What are its advantages and disadvantages compared to reverse osmosis?
Show Answer

Distillation advantages: produces very pure water; can use waste heat from power plants (multi-stage flash); reliable and proven technology. Disadvantages: very high energy consumption (~10 kWh/m³ vs. ~3–5 kWh/m³ for reverse osmosis); requires infrastructure for heating and condensing. Reverse osmosis: lower energy, lower capital cost per unit, more suitable for large-scale freshwater production. Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, UAE) use both — distillation where cheap hydrocarbon energy is available; RO where energy cost is critical.

Medium
A student collects distillate at 95°C instead of the expected 78°C for ethanol. What does this mean about the purity of the distillate?
Show Answer

The distillate collected at 95°C is NOT pure ethanol. Pure ethanol distils at 78°C. At 95°C, the vapour is a mixture of ethanol and water vapours — indicating that the ethanol is nearly exhausted from the flask or that the separation was incomplete. The student needs to discard this fraction and should have monitored temperature more carefully, only collecting the fraction when temperature was stable at 78°C.

Medium
What are "boiling chips" (anti-bumping granules) and why are they used in distillation?
Show Answer

Boiling chips are small, porous ceramic or glass granules added to a liquid before heating. They provide nucleation sites for bubble formation, ensuring smooth, continuous boiling rather than sudden violent "bumping" (superheating followed by explosive boiling). Bumping can shatter glassware, cause hot liquid to splash, and give inaccurate temperature readings. Boiling chips prevent all these problems.

Medium
Explain the difference between the "still pot" and the "distillate" in a distillation experiment in terms of composition.
Show Answer

Still pot (round-bottom flask — what remains in the flask): becomes enriched in the higher-boiling-point component as the lower-boiling-point component evaporates away. At end of distillation it contains the less volatile component. Distillate (what is collected): enriched in the lower-boiling-point (more volatile) component. If separation is complete, distillate is pure low-boiling component; still pot residue is pure high-boiling component.

Medium
Describe two industrial applications of distillation other than petroleum refining.
Show Answer

1. Alcoholic spirits production: fermented liquids (beer/wine) distilled to concentrate ethanol — whisky, vodka, rum, brandy. 2. Chemical manufacturing: many industrial chemicals (acetone, acetic acid, benzene) are purified by distillation. 3. Production of liquid gases: air is liquefied and fractionally distilled to separate N₂ (boiling point −196°C), O₂ (−183°C), and Ar (−186°C). 4. Pharmaceutical solvent recovery: solvents from drug manufacturing are recovered and reused by distillation.

Medium
What is an azeotrope and why does it cause problems in distillation? Give one example.
Show Answer

An azeotrope is a mixture that boils at a constant temperature (like a pure substance) and cannot be further separated by normal distillation — both components evaporate together in the same ratio. Example: ethanol-water azeotrope (96.5% ethanol / 3.5% water) boils at 78.1°C — the same temperature as pure ethanol. Simple distillation cannot produce ethanol purer than 96.5%. To get 100% ethanol (absolute alcohol), special methods are needed (azeotropic distillation with a third component, molecular sieves, or membrane separation).

Set C MYP Inquiry & Reasoning Hard

Explain the thermodynamic basis for distillation using the concept of vapour pressure and boiling point. Why does the component with higher vapour pressure at a given temperature distil first?
Show Answer

Vapour pressure: at any temperature, molecules at the liquid surface escape to vapour phase — creating vapour pressure. Higher vapour pressure = more molecules escaping = more volatile. Boiling point: temperature at which vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure (101.3 kPa). Component with higher vapour pressure at a given temperature has a lower boiling point. In a mixture, when heated, the component with higher vapour pressure (lower boiling point) reaches atmospheric pressure first — it preferentially evaporates and distils over first. The distillate is richer in the more volatile component. Raoult's Law quantifies this: partial pressure Pi = xi · Pi° (mole fraction × pure vapour pressure).

MYP Criterion D: Evaluate the environmental impact of using distillation for large-scale seawater desalination compared to alternative fresh water strategies.
Show Answer

Distillation desalination impacts: (1) High energy consumption (fossil fuel based in Gulf states) → CO₂ emissions and climate change. (2) Hot brine discharge into sea → thermal pollution, increased salinity, kills marine life near discharge. (3) Chemical additives (anti-scaling, anti-corrosion) can pollute coastal waters. (4) Large infrastructure with high maintenance. Alternatives: Rainwater harvesting: zero energy, zero emissions — but rainfall-dependent. Conservation: reduces demand — most sustainable. Reverse osmosis: lower energy, but still uses electricity, produces brine waste. Greywater recycling: treats used water — very efficient. Evaluation: distillation is appropriate where other options are unavailable and cheap energy exists, but its environmental costs are significant. Long-term solution requires combining water conservation, renewable-energy-powered desalination, and demand management.

Hard
Explain what happens at the molecular level when ethanol vapour is condensed back to liquid in the condenser. Include energy changes.
Show Answer

In vapour phase: ethanol molecules move randomly at high speed, far apart, with minimal intermolecular forces. As they contact the cool condenser walls: (1) Molecules slow down (lose kinetic energy → transferred as heat to condenser wall and cooling water). (2) When molecules slow enough, intermolecular forces (hydrogen bonds and van der Waals) between ethanol molecules become significant. (3) Molecules begin clustering and forming the liquid state. (4) Latent heat of condensation is released (exothermic — reverse of vaporisation). Energy: latent heat of condensation of ethanol ≈ 841 kJ/kg is transferred to the cooling water. This is why the cooling water must flow continuously — to remove this heat and keep condenser cool.

Hard
Compare simple distillation, fractional distillation, and steam distillation. For each, state the mixture type, principle, and one industrial application.
Show Answer

Simple distillation: mixture type — liquid-dissolved solid OR two liquids with boiling points >25°C apart. Principle — heat to boiling point of more volatile component; condense vapour. Application — desalination; alcohol from beer. Fractional distillation: mixture type — liquids with similar boiling points (<25°C apart). Principle — fractionating column provides multiple condensation-evaporation cycles; enriches vapour in more volatile component. Application — petroleum refining; separation of liquid air. Steam distillation: mixture type — heat-sensitive volatile oils mixed with high-boiling-point impurities. Principle — steam carries volatile oil over at ~100°C (far below oil's actual boiling point). Application — essential oil and perfume extraction; flavour production.

Extended response: How is fractional distillation of liquid air used to produce industrial gases? Trace the separation from air to individual gases with temperatures and applications.
Show Answer

Step 1: Air filtered to remove particles, CO₂, and water vapour. Step 2: Air compressed to ~200 atm and cooled — it liquefies. Step 3: Liquid air (LN₂ + LO₂ + LAr + traces of other gases) fed to fractionating column. Step 4: As liquid air warms and rises through column: N₂ (boiling point −196°C) vaporises first and exits at top of column. Ar (−186°C) collected at middle section. O₂ (−183°C) remains as liquid at bottom, then collected. Applications: N₂ — food preservation (inert atmosphere packaging), liquid coolant (MRI machines, cryosurgery), production of ammonia. O₂ — hospitals (medical oxygen), steel making, rocket propellant, water treatment. Ar — inert gas for welding, LED and fluorescent lights, industrial furnaces. Separation exploits the 10–13°C boiling point differences between these gases.

IB MYP Criterion B: A researcher needs to purify a biological compound (boiling point 156°C) from a water-based fermentation broth. The compound decomposes above 120°C. Design a suitable distillation strategy.
Show Answer

Problem: compound boiling point (156°C) exceeds its decomposition temperature (120°C). Cannot distil at atmospheric pressure. Solution: Vacuum distillation — reduce pressure to lower boiling point below 120°C. At ~0.05 atm (approx.), boiling points are reduced by ~60–80°C → compound boils at ~80–100°C, below decomposition temperature. Alternative: steam distillation — use steam to carry compound over with water at ~100°C (if compound is steam-volatile). Alternative 2: short-path distillation — highly reduced pressure, very short path from flask to condenser minimises decomposition time. Validation: monitor distillate temperature, check product identity by spectroscopy, check for decomposition products.

Hard
Explain why the composition of vapour above an ideal solution differs from the composition of the liquid, and how this is exploited in distillation.
Show Answer

Raoult's Law (ideal solution): partial pressure of component A = mole fraction of A in liquid × vapour pressure of pure A. Since the more volatile component (A) has a higher pure vapour pressure, its mole fraction in the vapour is HIGHER than in the liquid. This enrichment of vapour in the more volatile component is exactly what distillation exploits. Example: 50% ethanol-water liquid → vapour above it is ~75% ethanol (due to ethanol's higher vapour pressure). Each distillation step enriches the vapour further. Fractional distillation uses many such steps (theoretical plates) to achieve nearly complete separation.

Hard
Critique this statement: "Distillation always produces pure substances." To what extent is this true?
Show Answer

Partially true. Distillation can produce very pure substances under ideal conditions. Explain the physics of ferromagnetism at the atomic level. Why are only iron, nickel, and cobalt strongly magnetic?
Show Answer

Ferromagnetism arises from quantum mechanical alignment of electron spins in adjacent atoms. In Fe, Ni, and Co, unpaired electrons in the d-orbital create magnetic moments. More importantly, these atoms form magnetic domains — regions where millions of electron magnetic moments spontaneously align parallel due to quantum exchange interactions. When an external field is applied, domains aligned with the field grow; others shrink → net large-scale magnetic moment. Most elements lack the right combination of unpaired d-electrons AND exchange interaction strength for domain formation → not ferromagnetic. Below the Curie temperature (770°C for Fe), domains are stable; above it, thermal energy destroys domain order → iron becomes non-magnetic.

Set A Foundation Questions Easy

What is chromatography and what type of mixture does it separate?
Show Answer

Chromatography is a separation technique that separates mixtures of dissolved substances based on how far they travel through an absorbent medium (stationary phase) when carried by a solvent (mobile phase). It is used for mixtures of different coloured dyes, inks, pigments, and dissolved compounds.

Easy
In paper chromatography, what is the stationary phase and what is the mobile phase?
Show Answer

Stationary phase: the paper (and water absorbed in the paper fibres). Mobile phase: the solvent (e.g., water, ethanol) that moves up the paper carrying dissolved substances with it.

Easy
What is the Rf value? Write the formula for calculating it.
Show Answer

Rf (retention factor) = distance travelled by the substance ÷ distance travelled by the solvent front. It is a value between 0 and 1 (or 0% and 100%) that is characteristic for each substance under the same conditions.

Easy
A substance travels 4 cm and the solvent front travels 8 cm. Calculate the Rf value.
Show Answer

Rf = distance substance / distance solvent front = 4 / 8 = 0.5. The Rf value is 0.5.

Easy
Why does a mixture of dyes separate into different spots on chromatography paper?
Show Answer

Different dye molecules have different attractions to the paper (stationary phase) and different solubilities in the solvent (mobile phase). Those more attracted to paper move slowly; those more soluble in solvent move faster. This gives different travel distances and separates the dyes into distinct spots.

Easy
True or False: Pure substances produce only one spot on a chromatogram.
Show Answer

True. A pure substance contains only one component — it produces a single, compact spot. If multiple spots appear, the substance is a mixture of several components.

Show Answer

1. Separating the different coloured inks in a felt-tip pen or food dye. 2. Identifying dyes used in textiles. 3. Testing sweets/food colouring to identify permitted dyes. 4. In forensic science — analysing ink from a forged document.

Easy
Why must the baseline (where samples are spotted) be above the solvent level in chromatography?
Show Answer

If the baseline is submerged in solvent, the sample spots would dissolve directly into the solvent and be washed away before chromatographic separation can occur. The solvent must travel UP through the spots by capillary action, not through them by direct contact.

Easy
What property of a substance determines how far it travels in chromatography?
Show Answer

The relative affinity (attraction) of the substance for the stationary phase (paper) versus the mobile phase (solvent). Substances more soluble in the solvent travel further; substances more attracted to the paper travel less far.

Show Answer

Pencil (graphite) is insoluble in most solvents and does not dissolve or travel with the solvent. Pen ink contains dissolved dyes that would travel with the solvent and create interfering spots, making results difficult to interpret.

How is chromatography used in DNA sequencing? Briefly explain gel electrophoresis as a form of chromatography applied to genetic material.
