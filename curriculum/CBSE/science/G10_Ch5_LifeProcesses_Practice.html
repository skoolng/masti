Photosynthesis

Set A Recall & Definitions

Write the balanced chemical equation for photosynthesis and identify every reactant and product. Easy
Show answer

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Reactants: carbon dioxide (from air via stomata), water (absorbed by roots), light energy (sunlight). Products: glucose (stored as starch) and oxygen (released as by-product).

Where exactly in the plant cell does photosynthesis take place, and which pigment absorbs light? Easy
Show answer

Photosynthesis occurs in the chloroplasts. The green pigment chlorophyll (mainly chlorophyll a and b) absorbs red and blue wavelengths of light and reflects green, which is why leaves appear green.

Distinguish between the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent (dark) reactions in terms of location, inputs, and outputs. Medium
Show answer

Light reactions occur on the thylakoid membranes; they use light to split water (photolysis), producing ATP, NADPH, and O₂. Dark reactions (Calvin cycle) occur in the stroma; they use ATP and NADPH to fix CO₂ into glucose (no direct light needed).

Name three external factors that limit the rate of photosynthesis and briefly explain how each acts as a limiting factor. Medium
Show answer

1. Light intensity – more photons increase rate until saturation. 2. CO₂ concentration – more CO₂ increases Calvin cycle rate. 3. Temperature – enzymes work faster up to ~35 °C; above this, enzymes denature and rate falls.

Explain why variegated (green-and-white) leaves are used to demonstrate that chlorophyll is essential for photosynthesis in a starch test. Medium
Show answer

White parts of the leaf lack chlorophyll. After de-staining and adding iodine solution, only the green areas turn blue-black (starch present), while white areas remain orange-brown. This acts as a built-in control showing starch is made only where chlorophyll is present.

A farmer increases CO₂ concentration in a greenhouse from 0.04% to 0.1% but sees no further increase in crop yield. Suggest two reasons with reference to limiting factors. Hard
Show answer

1. Light intensity may now be the limiting factor – even with excess CO₂, the light reactions cannot produce enough ATP/NADPH. 2. Temperature may be limiting – enzymes of the Calvin cycle may already be at their optimum; or water availability may be insufficient.

Describe how the structure of a leaf is adapted for efficient photosynthesis. (Give at least four structural features.) Hard
Show answer

1. Broad flat lamina → large surface area for light capture. 2. Thin blade → short diffusion path for CO₂. 3. Transparent upper epidermis → light reaches palisade cells. 4. Palisade mesophyll packed with chloroplasts → maximum light absorption. 5. Spongy mesophyll with air spaces → efficient gas exchange. 6. Stomata → entry of CO₂ and exit of O₂. 7. Leaf veins (xylem + phloem) → water supply and sugar transport.

Explain the significance of photosynthesis at the ecosystem level, including its role in the oxygen cycle and carbon cycle. Hard
Show answer

Photosynthesis is the primary source of atmospheric O₂ (via photolysis of water) and the main mechanism by which CO₂ is fixed into organic compounds, linking the inorganic carbon pool to the food chain. It forms the base of almost all food webs, making it essential for sustaining all aerobic life and regulating Earth's climate.

Set B Application & Problem-Solving

A student places an aquatic plant (Elodea) under different light intensities and counts oxygen bubbles per minute. At 500 lux the count is 20 bubbles/min; at 1000 lux it is 38; at 1500 lux it is 40. What conclusion can be drawn? Medium
Show answer

Between 500 and 1000 lux, photosynthesis increases almost proportionally. However between 1000 and 1500 lux the rate barely increases (40 vs 38), indicating that light is no longer the limiting factor – another factor such as CO₂ concentration or temperature is now limiting the rate.

Why do leaves of a plant placed in the dark not produce starch even when CO₂ and water are available? Easy
Show answer

Without light, the light-dependent reactions cannot occur. No ATP or NADPH is generated, so the Calvin cycle cannot fix CO₂ into glucose. Without glucose production there is no starch synthesis regardless of CO₂ and water availability.

How does the opening and closing of stomata affect the rate of photosynthesis? Include the role of guard cells. Medium
Show answer

Guard cells surround each stoma. In light, guard cells absorb water by osmosis (K⁺ ions move in → osmotic pressure rises), becoming turgid and opening the stoma. Open stomata allow CO₂ in, maintaining high CO₂ concentration for the Calvin cycle. In drought, stomata close to prevent water loss but this restricts CO₂ entry and slows photosynthesis.

Predict what happens to the rate of photosynthesis if a plant is exposed to only green light. Explain. Medium
Show answer

The rate drops dramatically. Chlorophyll reflects green light rather than absorbing it, so very little light energy is captured for the light reactions. Consequently, ATP and NADPH production falls, and the Calvin cycle slows, greatly reducing glucose and oxygen production.

A plant's stomata close during the afternoon even though light is plentiful. Explain this phenomenon and its effect on photosynthesis. Hard
Show answer

When daytime temperatures are very high, the plant undergoes water stress – guard cells lose turgor and stomata close to conserve water. This restricts CO₂ entry; CO₂ concentration inside the leaf falls, and the Calvin cycle is limited. The plant may switch to photorespiration under low CO₂/high O₂ conditions, further reducing net photosynthesis.

Explain how photosynthesis and cellular respiration are complementary processes in terms of gas exchange and energy flow. Hard
Show answer

Photosynthesis uses CO₂ + H₂O + light → glucose + O₂, storing energy in chemical bonds. Respiration breaks glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP, releasing that stored energy. The products of each serve as the reactants of the other, creating a cycle. During the day, photosynthesis often exceeds respiration (net O₂ released); at night only respiration occurs (net CO₂ released).

Describe an experiment to show that CO₂ is necessary for photosynthesis, naming all materials and expected results. Hard
Show answer

Set up two identical potted plants. In Experiment: enclose leaves in a transparent bag containing KOH (which absorbs CO₂). Control: leaves enclosed in a bag without KOH. Place both in bright light for 6 hours. Test leaves with iodine solution after destaining. Expected: Experiment leaves → no blue-black (no starch, CO₂ absent). Control leaves → blue-black (starch present, CO₂ available). This demonstrates CO₂ is essential.

The compensation point of a plant is reached when photosynthesis equals respiration. What would you observe visually about gas exchange at the compensation point? Hard
Show answer

At the compensation point, the rate of CO₂ uptake by photosynthesis exactly equals CO₂ production by respiration, and O₂ production equals O₂ consumption. There is no net exchange of gases with the environment – no bubbles in aquatic plants, no net CO₂ gain or loss in air. Internally the plant is still actively running both processes.

Set C Higher-Order & IB MYP

Using an action spectrum and absorption spectrum, explain which wavelengths are most effective for photosynthesis and why. Hard
Show answer

The absorption spectrum shows chlorophyll absorbs mainly red (~680 nm) and blue (~450 nm) light. The action spectrum shows the highest photosynthetic rates at the same red and blue wavelengths. This correlation demonstrates that the wavelengths most absorbed by chlorophyll drive the most photosynthesis. Green light (~550 nm) is poorly absorbed (reflected), so it drives minimal photosynthesis.

Evaluate the claim: "Plants only photosynthesise; they do not respire." Use evidence from NCERT content. Medium
Show answer

The claim is false. Plants respire continuously (24 hours) in all living cells to produce ATP for growth and maintenance. During the day, photosynthesis exceeds respiration, masking respiratory CO₂ release. At night, only respiration occurs and CO₂ is released. Mitochondria in plant cells carry out respiration just as in animal cells.

Assess the environmental and food-security significance of improving photosynthetic efficiency in crop plants through biotechnology. Hard
Show answer

Higher photosynthetic efficiency means more CO₂ fixed per unit area, increasing crop yields without expanding farmland – important for feeding a growing population. It could reduce need for irrigation and fertilisers. Environmentally, more efficient carbon fixation could help offset rising atmospheric CO₂. However, risks include unknown ecological effects of transgenic crops and reduced biodiversity if monocultures are promoted.

Design an investigation (IB MYP Criterion B) to test the effect of temperature on the rate of photosynthesis in Elodea. State the IV, DV, CVs, hypothesis, and method outline. Hard
Show answer

IV: Temperature of water (10, 20, 30, 40, 50 °C). DV: Number of O₂ bubbles per minute. CVs: Light intensity, CO₂ concentration (add NaHCO₃), same plant species and length, same duration. Hypothesis: Rate increases up to ~35 °C (enzyme optimum) then decreases as enzymes denature. Method: Place Elodea in beaker of NaHCO₃ solution at each temperature; illuminate with constant lamp; count bubbles in 3 minutes; repeat 3 times; calculate mean.

Compare C3 and C4 photosynthesis pathways (not mandatory in NCERT but appears in olympiad/MYP extension). Why are C4 plants like sugarcane more efficient in hot sunny conditions? Hard
Show answer

C3 plants fix CO₂ directly in mesophyll via RuBisCO; at high temperatures and low CO₂, photorespiration wastes energy. C4 plants pre-concentrate CO₂ using PEP carboxylase in mesophyll cells into 4-carbon acids, then pump it to bundle sheath cells around the vascular bundle where the Calvin cycle runs at high CO₂ concentration. This suppresses photorespiration, making C4 plants more efficient in hot, bright conditions with limited water.

Explain the photolysis of water: write the equation, state where it occurs, and describe its importance for life on Earth. Hard
Show answer

2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂ (occurs on thylakoid membranes in light reactions). Importance: (1) Provides electrons to replace those lost by chlorophyll → sustains light reactions. (2) H⁺ ions contribute to chemiosmosis → ATP synthesis. (3) O₂ released → source of nearly all atmospheric oxygen that supports aerobic respiration in organisms worldwide.

A student claims that plants kept in constant darkness will eventually die even if given glucose solution. Evaluate this claim. Medium
Show answer

Partly valid. With external glucose, the plant can respire for energy and survive short-term. However, long-term: (1) lack of light prevents synthesis of chlorophyll and other light-regulated compounds; (2) stomata remain largely closed, limiting gas exchange; (3) no light-regulated growth hormones are produced; (4) root tissues in darkness cannot photosynthesise anyway; (5) the plant would eventually etiolate and die. So while glucose delays death, it does not fully sustain the plant.

How does the IB MYP Criterion D (Reflecting on the impacts of science) apply to large-scale deforestation's effect on global photosynthesis rates and climate? Hard
Show answer

Deforestation removes vast numbers of photosynthesising trees → less CO₂ is fixed annually → atmospheric CO₂ rises → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming. Additionally, burning cleared vegetation releases stored carbon. Biodiversity loss disrupts ecosystem services. Socially, indigenous communities and water cycles are affected. Scientists, policymakers, and communities must collaborate; solutions include reforestation, sustainable agriculture, and reducing fossil fuel use.

Heterotrophic Nutrition & Human Digestion

Set A Recall & Definitions

Define heterotrophic nutrition and give two examples of organisms that are heterotrophs. Easy
Show answer

Heterotrophic nutrition is the mode of nutrition in which organisms cannot synthesise their own organic food and must obtain it by consuming other organisms or organic matter. Examples: humans (holozoic), Rhizopus fungus (saprotrophic).

List the main organs of the human alimentary canal in order from mouth to anus. Easy
Show answer

Mouth → oesophagus → stomach → small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum) → large intestine (caecum, colon, rectum) → anus.

State the enzyme present in saliva, its substrate, and the product of its action. Easy
Show answer

Enzyme: Salivary amylase (ptyalin). Substrate: Starch. Product: Maltose (a disaccharide). The reaction begins in the mouth and requires neutral to slightly alkaline pH.

Explain the role of HCl in the stomach and why the stomach doesn't digest itself. Medium
Show answer

HCl (i) creates low pH (~2) that denatures proteins for easier enzyme access, (ii) activates pepsinogen → pepsin, and (iii) kills most ingested bacteria. The stomach is protected by a thick mucus layer secreted by goblet cells, which neutralises acid at the stomach wall surface. Ulcers occur when this mucus barrier is breached.

Describe the role of bile in digestion. Where is bile produced, stored, and where does it act? Medium
Show answer

Bile is produced in the liver, stored in the gall bladder, and released into the duodenum. Bile salts emulsify fats – breaking large fat globules into smaller droplets (emulsification), increasing surface area for lipase to act on. Bile also neutralises stomach acid in the duodenum to create the alkaline pH needed for intestinal enzymes.

How are villi and microvilli (brush border) adapted for absorption in the small intestine? Name at least four adaptations. Hard
Show answer

1. Finger-like villi greatly increase surface area for absorption. 2. Each villus has a network of capillaries for absorption of glucose and amino acids directly into blood. 3. Each villus has a central lacteal for absorption of fatty acids and glycerol (as chylomicrons). 4. Microvilli (brush border) on epithelial cells further multiply surface area. 5. Single-cell-thick epithelium → short diffusion distance. 6. Rich blood supply maintains concentration gradient.

List the pancreatic enzymes secreted into the duodenum and the macromolecules each digests. Medium
Show answer

1. Pancreatic amylase → starch → maltose. 2. Trypsin and chymotrypsin (proteases) → proteins → peptides/amino acids. 3. Lipase → fats (after bile emulsification) → fatty acids + glycerol. 4. Nucleases → DNA/RNA → nucleotides. The pancreatic juice also contains sodium bicarbonate to neutralise stomach acid.

Distinguish between mechanical digestion and chemical digestion with one example each. Easy
Show answer

Mechanical digestion physically breaks food into smaller pieces without changing chemical composition (e.g., chewing/mastication in the mouth by teeth). Chemical digestion uses enzymes to break covalent bonds in macromolecules, changing them into simpler molecules (e.g., pepsin breaking proteins into peptides in the stomach).

Set B Application & Problem-Solving

A person has their gall bladder removed. How would this affect fat digestion, and what dietary advice would a doctor give? Medium
Show answer

Without the gall bladder, bile trickles continuously from the liver to the duodenum rather than being released in concentrated bursts after a meal. This means fat emulsification is less efficient, particularly after high-fat meals. The doctor would advise a low-fat diet, smaller more frequent meals, and possibly supplementing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) which depend on fat absorption.

Why is the pH of the stomach important, and what happens if a patient takes antacids excessively? Medium
Show answer

The stomach's low pH (~2) is essential for pepsin activity and killing bacteria. Excessive antacids raise gastric pH, inactivating pepsin and reducing protein digestion efficiency. It may also promote bacterial overgrowth in the stomach. However, short-term antacid use for heartburn is safe.

Trace the journey of a starch molecule from the mouth to absorption in the small intestine, naming every enzyme involved. Medium
Show answer

Mouth: salivary amylase → starch → maltose. Stomach: no starch digestion (amylase inactivated by acid). Duodenum: pancreatic amylase → remaining starch → maltose. Small intestine brush border: maltase → maltose → glucose + glucose. Glucose is absorbed by active transport into capillaries of villi → portal vein → liver.

A student eats a meal high in protein. Describe what happens to the protein from the time it enters the stomach until its building blocks are absorbed. Medium
Show answer

Stomach: HCl denatures protein, activates pepsinogen → pepsin; pepsin partially hydrolyses proteins into polypeptides. Duodenum: pancreatic trypsin and chymotrypsin further digest polypeptides into shorter peptides. Small intestine brush border: peptidases break peptides into individual amino acids. Amino acids absorbed by active transport into capillaries → portal vein → liver.

What is the role of the large intestine? Why is it NOT primarily a site of digestion? Easy
Show answer

The large intestine mainly absorbs water and mineral salts from undigested food matter, converting liquid chyme into semi-solid faeces. It also houses gut bacteria that ferment remaining carbohydrates and produce some vitamins (B₁₂, K). Most chemical digestion is complete by the time material reaches the large intestine, so it is primarily an absorption and compaction organ.

Explain why cellulose cannot be digested by humans even though it is a carbohydrate. What is its nutritional role? Medium
Show answer

Cellulose consists of glucose units linked by β-1,4 glycosidic bonds. Humans lack cellulase enzyme to break these bonds (only amylase, which breaks α-1,4 bonds). Despite not being digested, cellulose (dietary fibre) is important: it adds bulk to faeces, stimulates peristalsis, prevents constipation, and reduces risk of bowel cancer and cardiovascular disease.

How do peristaltic movements help in digestion and absorption? Describe the muscular mechanism involved. Hard
Show answer

Peristalsis is the wave-like contraction and relaxation of circular and longitudinal smooth muscles in the gut wall. Circular muscles behind the food bolus contract, narrowing that section; longitudinal muscles ahead relax, widening it → food moves forward. This churns food, mixes it with enzymes, and propels it along the alimentary canal, ensuring continuous exposure of food to digestive surfaces and ultimately moving waste to the rectum.

Differentiate between holozoic, saprotrophic, and parasitic nutrition with one organism example for each. Easy
Show answer

Holozoic: organism ingests solid food, digests internally, absorbs nutrients (e.g., humans). Saprotrophic: organism secretes enzymes onto dead organic matter, absorbs soluble products externally (e.g., Rhizopus/bread mould). Parasitic: organism lives on/in a host, feeds at host's expense, causing harm (e.g., Plasmodium – malaria parasite).

Set C Higher-Order & IB MYP

Explain how the gut microbiome (bacteria in the large intestine) contributes to human health and what disrupts it. Hard
Show answer

Gut bacteria ferment dietary fibre into short-chain fatty acids (energy for colon cells), synthesise vitamin K and B vitamins, stimulate immune development, and compete with pathogens. Disruption occurs through antibiotics (kill beneficial bacteria), poor diet (low fibre), stress, or infection → dysbiosis can lead to IBD, obesity, and reduced immunity. Probiotics and prebiotic-rich foods (fibre) help restore balance.

Compare the digestion of carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids in terms of enzymes, location, and final absorbed products. Hard
Show answer

Carbohydrates: amylase (mouth+duodenum) + brush-border disaccharidases → monosaccharides (glucose) → absorbed into capillaries. Proteins: pepsin (stomach) + trypsin/chymotrypsin (duodenum) + peptidases (brush border) → amino acids → capillaries. Lipids: lipase (duodenum, after bile emulsification) → fatty acids + glycerol → re-esterified into triglycerides in epithelial cells → chylomicrons → lacteals → lymph.

Apply IB MYP Criterion D: Evaluate the social and economic impact of malnutrition and poor sanitation on digestive health in developing countries. Hard
Show answer

Protein-energy malnutrition (kwashiorkor/marasmus) impairs enzyme production, reducing digestive efficiency, and stunts growth. Poor sanitation allows pathogens like cholera (disrupts water absorption in large intestine) and intestinal worms to thrive. Economic cost: reduced productivity, high healthcare expenditure, and impaired cognitive development in children. Solutions include clean water access, nutrition programmes, deworming campaigns, and improved sanitation infrastructure.

Explain the hormonal regulation of digestion: name two gastrointestinal hormones, where they are secreted, what stimulates their release, and their effects. Hard
Show answer

1. Gastrin: secreted by G-cells in stomach wall when food enters; stimulates gastric acid (HCl) secretion and pepsinogen release. 2. Secretin: secreted by S-cells in duodenum when acidic chyme enters; stimulates pancreas to release bicarbonate-rich juice to neutralise acid. (Cholecystokinin is bonus: released by duodenal I-cells when fat/protein enters; stimulates gall bladder contraction and pancreatic enzyme release.)

A person is diagnosed with lactose intolerance. Explain the biochemical cause and the symptoms that result after consuming milk. Medium
Show answer

Lactose intolerance results from deficiency of the brush-border enzyme lactase (β-galactosidase). Without lactase, lactose (milk sugar) is not broken into glucose + galactose and cannot be absorbed. Undigested lactose passes into the large intestine where gut bacteria ferment it, producing gases (H₂, CO₂, methane) and organic acids. Symptoms: bloating, flatulence, abdominal cramps, and osmotic diarrhoea.

Design an experiment to test whether temperature affects the activity of salivary amylase on starch, stating hypothesis, variables, and expected results. Hard
Show answer

Hypothesis: Salivary amylase activity is highest at body temperature (~37 °C) and decreases at lower or higher temperatures. IV: Temperature (0, 20, 37, 50, 70 °C). DV: Time for starch to disappear (tested with iodine every 2 min until no blue-black). CVs: Same concentration of amylase and starch, same volume, same pH (buffer). Expected: Fastest digestion at 37 °C; slow at 0 °C (low kinetic energy); no digestion at 70 °C (enzyme denatured).

Explain how the liver's role goes beyond bile production in the context of nutrient processing after absorption. Hard
Show answer

After absorption, the hepatic portal vein carries nutrients to the liver, which: (1) regulates blood glucose – excess glucose converted to glycogen (glycogenesis) or fat; (2) deaminates excess amino acids → urea (urea cycle) excreted in urine; (3) detoxifies alcohol and drugs; (4) synthesises plasma proteins (albumin, fibrinogen, clotting factors); (5) stores fat-soluble vitamins and iron; (6) converts fatty acids to ketone bodies during fasting. The liver is central to metabolic homeostasis.

Evaluate the claim that "digestion is complete in the stomach." Use evidence to support or refute this. Medium
Show answer

Claim is false. The stomach only partially digests proteins (pepsin) and performs mechanical churning (peristalsis). Most chemical digestion occurs in the small intestine: pancreatic amylase (starch), lipase (fats after bile emulsification), and proteases (proteins), plus brush-border enzymes. Absorption also occurs almost entirely in the small intestine (nutrients) and large intestine (water). The stomach is just one stage in a multi-organ digestive process.

Aerobic & Anaerobic Respiration

Set A Recall & Definitions

Write the balanced equation for aerobic cellular respiration and state where in the cell it primarily occurs. Easy
Show answer

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP (energy). Aerobic respiration occurs primarily in the mitochondria; glycolysis (first stage) occurs in the cytoplasm.

What is the difference between aerobic and anaerobic respiration in terms of oxygen use, products, and ATP yield? Easy
Show answer

Aerobic: uses O₂; complete oxidation of glucose; products CO₂ + H₂O; high ATP yield (~38 ATP per glucose). Anaerobic: no O₂; partial oxidation; in humans → lactic acid (2 ATP); in yeast → ethanol + CO₂ (2 ATP). Aerobic is far more efficient.

Why does anaerobic respiration in human muscle cells cause a "burning" sensation during intense exercise? Easy
Show answer

During intense exercise, oxygen supply cannot meet demand. Muscles switch to anaerobic respiration: glucose → lactic acid + 2 ATP. Lactic acid accumulates in muscle cells, lowering pH (making cytoplasm acidic), which interferes with enzyme and muscle protein function, causing the painful burning sensation and eventual fatigue/cramp.

Name the three main stages of aerobic respiration and state where each occurs in the cell. Medium
Show answer

1. Glycolysis – cytoplasm: glucose (6C) → 2 pyruvate (3C) + 2 ATP + 2 NADH. 2. Krebs (Citric Acid) Cycle – mitochondrial matrix: pyruvate fully oxidised, CO₂ released, NADH/FADH₂ generated, 2 ATP. 3. Electron Transport Chain (oxidative phosphorylation) – inner mitochondrial membrane: NADH/FADH₂ electrons drive proton gradient → ~34 ATP; O₂ acts as final electron acceptor → H₂O.

What is the "oxygen debt" (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption) and how does the body repay it? Medium
Show answer

Oxygen debt is the extra oxygen required after exercise to oxidise the accumulated lactic acid. After exercise stops, lactic acid is transported in blood to the liver, where it is converted back to pyruvate (or glucose via gluconeogenesis) using extra O₂. This is why breathing remains elevated after exercise ends – to supply the additional oxygen needed.

Describe the role of mitochondria in aerobic respiration, referring to the inner membrane and matrix. Hard
Show answer

The mitochondrial matrix contains enzymes for the Krebs cycle and pyruvate decarboxylation; CO₂ is released here. The highly folded inner membrane (cristae) houses the electron transport chain (ETC) proteins and ATP synthase. Electrons from NADH/FADH₂ pass down the ETC, pumping H⁺ into the intermembrane space, creating a proton gradient. H⁺ flows back through ATP synthase → ATP synthesised (chemiosmosis). O₂ accepts final electrons → H₂O.

Give two practical applications of anaerobic respiration by yeast in food technology. Easy
Show answer

1. Bread making: yeast ferments sugars in dough → CO₂ causes dough to rise (leaven); alcohol evaporates during baking. 2. Alcohol production (brewing/wine making): yeast ferments fruit sugars → ethanol + CO₂; CO₂ is collected or released, ethanol is the desired product in beer and wine.

Explain why athletes "warm up" before vigorous exercise in terms of respiration and enzyme activity. Medium
Show answer

Warming up raises body and muscle temperature, which increases the kinetic energy of enzyme molecules (respiratory enzymes, myosin ATPase). This increases the rate of aerobic respiration, producing more ATP for muscle contraction. It also increases heart rate and blood flow, delivering more O₂ and glucose, and makes muscles and joints more flexible, reducing injury risk.

Set B Application & Problem-Solving

A marathon runner collapses at the end of a race with severe muscle cramps. Explain the biochemical reasons and suggest treatment. Medium
Show answer

During prolonged intense running, O₂ delivery falls short of demand → anaerobic respiration → lactic acid accumulates → acidosis → muscle cramp and pain. Treatment: rest, slow deep breathing to increase O₂ intake (helps convert lactic acid to pyruvate aerobically), gentle stretching, hydration, and isotonic drinks to replace lost electrolytes.

Why do we breathe faster and more deeply during exercise? Connect this to cellular respiration. Easy
Show answer

Exercise increases ATP demand in muscles; more aerobic respiration occurs → more O₂ consumed and more CO₂ produced. Rising CO₂ (detected by chemoreceptors in the medulla) triggers faster, deeper breathing to (1) bring more O₂ into the blood for mitochondria and (2) expel CO₂ to prevent blood acidification.

Yeast is placed in a sealed container with sugar solution. Initially bubbles of CO₂ are produced rapidly, then slow down. Explain. Medium
Show answer

Initially, yeast carries out anaerobic fermentation: glucose → ethanol + CO₂. As fermentation continues: (1) glucose concentration decreases → less substrate available; (2) ethanol accumulates → becomes toxic to yeast at ~12–15% → inhibits yeast enzymes and kills yeast cells. Both factors reduce CO₂ production over time.

Calculate the number of ATP molecules produced when 2 molecules of glucose are completely oxidised aerobically (assume 38 ATP per glucose). How does this compare to anaerobic? Medium
Show answer

Aerobic: 2 × 38 = 76 ATP molecules. Anaerobic: 2 × 2 = 4 ATP molecules. Aerobic respiration is 76/4 = 19 times more efficient. This demonstrates why aerobic respiration is the preferred pathway when oxygen is available – it extracts far more usable energy from the same amount of glucose.

Explain why seeds germinate faster at 25 °C than at 5 °C or 45 °C in terms of respiration and enzyme activity. Hard
Show answer

At 5 °C, enzyme molecules (including respiratory enzymes) have low kinetic energy; few collisions with substrate → slow aerobic respiration → insufficient ATP for rapid cell division and growth. At 25 °C, enzymes work near optimum → maximum respiration rate → sufficient ATP for germination. At 45 °C, respiratory enzymes begin to denature (lose 3D shape) → reduced ATP production → slow or failed germination.

Why do mitochondria have a highly folded inner membrane (cristae)? What would happen to ATP production if the cristae were flattened? Hard
Show answer

Cristae greatly increase the surface area of the inner membrane, accommodating more ETC protein complexes and ATP synthase molecules, thus maximising the rate of oxidative phosphorylation and ATP production. If cristae were flattened, surface area would decrease dramatically → fewer ETC complexes → less proton pumping → smaller proton gradient → much less ATP synthesised → cell energy shortage.

A patient has a genetic defect that reduces the number of functional mitochondria in heart muscle cells. Predict the consequences. Medium
Show answer

Fewer mitochondria → reduced aerobic respiration capacity → insufficient ATP for continuous, powerful heart contractions. The heart relies almost entirely on aerobic respiration (unlike skeletal muscle which can use anaerobic). Consequences: weakened heart muscle (cardiomyopathy), reduced cardiac output, fatigue at minimal exertion, inability to respond to increased demand (e.g., exercise), and potentially fatal heart failure.

A biscuit label states it contains 20 g of carbohydrate per serving. Trace the metabolic fate of this glucose from ingestion to ATP production. Hard
Show answer

Digestion: starch → glucose (amylase + disaccharidases). Absorption: glucose → portal blood → liver. Liver regulates blood glucose; excess → glycogen. In cells: glucose enters glycolysis (cytoplasm) → 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP + 2 NADH. Pyruvate → acetyl-CoA → Krebs cycle (matrix) → CO₂ + NADH + FADH₂ + 2 ATP. ETC (inner membrane) uses NADH/FADH₂ → ~34 ATP via chemiosmosis. Total ≈ 38 ATP per glucose molecule. O₂ consumed; CO₂ and H₂O released.

Set C Higher-Order & IB MYP

Evaluate the efficiency of aerobic vs anaerobic respiration from an evolutionary perspective: why would anaerobic pathways be preserved? Hard
Show answer

Anaerobic respiration evolved first when Earth's atmosphere lacked oxygen. Though inefficient (2 ATP), it provides rapid ATP for short intense bursts – advantageous for "fight-or-flight" responses, sprinting, or when O₂ is transiently insufficient. Aerobic respiration (38 ATP) is efficient but slower to activate. Both pathways are preserved because each confers adaptive advantages: anaerobic for speed, aerobic for sustained energy. The evolutionary history (anaerobic → aerobic) is also reflected in glycolysis preceding the Krebs cycle.

How does cyanide poisoning cause death at the cellular respiration level? Name the specific target. Hard
Show answer

Cyanide (CN⁻) binds to and irreversibly inhibits cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV) of the electron transport chain. This blocks the final step of electron transfer to O₂, halting the ETC. No proton gradient is created → ATP synthase stops → no ATP produced via oxidative phosphorylation (accounts for ~34/38 ATP). Cells switch to anaerobic (only 2 ATP) → massive energy deficit → cell death, particularly in oxygen-demanding tissues like heart and brain → rapid death.

Compare mitochondria and chloroplasts structurally and functionally. What evidence supports their endosymbiotic origin? Hard
Show answer

Both have double membranes and their own circular DNA, replicate by binary fission, and have 70S ribosomes (like prokaryotes). Mitochondria: inner membrane folded into cristae; house ETC for aerobic respiration; produce ATP from chemical energy. Chloroplasts: inner membrane forms thylakoids and grana; house photosystems for photosynthesis; convert light to chemical energy. Endosymbiotic evidence: prokaryote-sized, binary fission, own DNA/ribosomes, inner membrane composition similar to certain bacteria.

IB MYP Criterion C: Explain how data from an experiment measuring CO₂ output at different temperatures could be processed to find the Q₁₀ value of respiration. Hard
Show answer

Q₁₀ = (Rate at T+10°C) / (Rate at T°C). Measure CO₂ output (cm³/min) at e.g. 20 °C and 30 °C using a respirometer. If rate doubles from 20→30 °C, Q₁₀ = 2.0 (typical for enzyme-controlled reactions). Draw and label the four chambers of the human heart. State which side pumps oxygenated vs deoxygenated blood. Easy

Explain why the left ventricle has a thicker muscular wall than the right ventricle. Easy
Show answer

The left ventricle pumps blood at high pressure through the entire systemic circulation (body), which is a much longer and higher-resistance circuit than the pulmonary circulation. A thicker muscular wall generates greater contractile force to maintain the high pressure needed to push blood to distant organs. The right ventricle only needs to pump blood the short distance to the nearby lungs at lower pressure.

What is double circulation? Why is it advantageous compared to single circulation found in fish? Easy
Show answer

Double circulation means blood passes through the heart twice per complete circuit: pulmonary (heart → lungs → heart) and systemic (heart → body → heart). Advantage: oxygenated and deoxygenated blood are completely separated, ensuring high-pressure delivery of fully oxygenated blood to tissues. Fish have single circulation (one pump), so blood loses pressure in gills before reaching body → less efficient delivery to organs.

Explain the role of haemoglobin in oxygen and carbon dioxide transport in blood. Medium
Show answer

What is the cardiac cycle? Define systole and diastole and describe what happens in each phase. Hard
Show answer

Ventricular systole: ventricles contract → high pressure closes AV valves (prevents backflow) → semilunar valves open → blood ejected into aorta/pulmonary artery. Define excretion. Why is excretion necessary for life? Name two excretory products in humans. Easy

Name the functional unit of the kidney. Describe its three main processes in urine formation. Easy
Show answer

Functional unit: nephron. Three processes: 1. Glomerular filtration – high pressure forces small molecules (water, glucose, urea, ions) from glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule (proteins and cells remain in blood). 2. Tubular secretion – additional waste materials (excess H⁺, drugs, K⁺) actively secreted from blood into tubule. Remaining fluid = urine.

State the role of ADH (antidiuretic hormone) in regulating water balance. What stimulates its release? Easy
Show answer

ADH acts on the collecting duct of the nephron, making it more permeable to water → more water is reabsorbed into blood → smaller volume of concentrated urine produced. In well-hydrated state: less ADH → less water reabsorption → dilute, larger volume of urine.

How is urea formed in the body? Name the organ responsible and the metabolic process involved. Medium

Urea is formed in the liver via the urea cycle (ornithine cycle). When amino acids are in excess or used for energy, they undergo deamination: the amino group (–NH₂) is removed → forms ammonia (NH₃), which is highly toxic. Ammonia is immediately converted to less toxic urea: 2NH₃ + CO₂ → CO(NH₂)₂ + H₂O. Urea is released into the blood and filtered out by the kidneys into urine.

Insulin: stimulates liver and muscle to take up glucose and convert to glycogen (glycogenesis); promotes glucose uptake by all body cells for respiration; reduces blood glucose back to normal. If blood glucose falls too low, alpha cells release glucagon → stimulates liver to convert glycogen back to glucose (glycogenolysis) → blood glucose rises.

What is the difference between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes mellitus in terms of cause and treatment? Easy

Type 1: autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells → no insulin produced. Treatment: daily insulin injections (cannot take orally – insulin is a protein, digested). Describe how the body responds to overheating. Name at least three physiological mechanisms. Medium
Show answer

Hypothalamus detects rising core temperature. Responses: 1. Vasodilation of skin arterioles → more blood to skin → heat lost by radiation and conduction. 2. Sweating: sweat glands secrete sweat → evaporation of water from skin surface → latent heat of vaporisation cools skin. 3. Reduced metabolic rate. 4. Increased breathing rate (some heat lost in exhaled air). 5. Behavioural: seeking shade, removing clothing, drinking cold water.

Explain what happens in the body during hypoglycaemia (dangerously low blood glucose). Why is this immediately dangerous? Medium
Show answer

Blood glucose below ~3.9 mmol/L → cells (especially neurons) cannot take up adequate glucose for respiration → insufficient ATP for brain function. Symptoms: dizziness, confusion, shakiness, sweating, rapid heartbeat, unconsciousness, seizures. The brain cannot use fatty acids for fuel and has minimal glycogen stores; it depends almost entirely on blood glucose. Severe hypoglycaemia causes brain damage or death within minutes if untreated. Emergency: oral glucose or IV glucose injection to rapidly restore blood glucose.

What is the role of the hypothalamus in homeostasis? Name three functions it controls. Medium
Show answer

The hypothalamus is the primary homeostatic control centre in the brain. It acts as a comparator (compares actual values to set points) and coordinates nervous and endocrine responses. Three functions: 1. Thermoregulation – detects blood temperature and triggers heat loss/conservation responses. 2. Osmoregulation – detects blood osmolarity → signals posterior pituitary to release/inhibit ADH. 3. Hunger/satiety regulation – detects glucose, leptin levels → controls appetite. (Also: controls circadian rhythms, stress responses via adrenal axis.)

A diabetic patient forgets to take their insulin and eats a large meal. Trace the metabolic consequences in their blood and cells over the next 2 hours. Medium
Show answer

Carbohydrates digested → blood glucose rises sharply. Without insulin: cells cannot take up glucose (no GLUT4 activation in muscle/fat) → hyperglycaemia persists. Cells "starve" despite high blood glucose. Body switches to lipid catabolism → fatty acids oxidised → ketone bodies produced (ketoacidosis risk). Liver cannot store excess glucose as glycogen efficiently → continued hyperglycaemia. Kidneys: renal threshold exceeded → glycosuria → osmotic diuresis → dehydration. Over hours: fatigue, thirst, frequent urination, possible diabetic ketoacidosis if severe.

Why do marathon runners sometimes experience hyponatraemia (low blood sodium) even though they drink a lot of water? How does this affect homeostasis? Medium
Show answer

Endurance athletes lose Na⁺ in large amounts through sweat. Drinking large volumes of plain water dilutes blood Na⁺ further → hyponatraemia (serum Na⁺ < 135 mmol/L). Low blood Na⁺ → reduced osmolarity → cells take in water by osmosis → cell swelling. Brain cells swell in a rigid skull → cerebral oedema → headache, confusion, seizures, coma. Prevention: isotonic sports drinks (contain Na⁺ + K⁺) rather than plain water during prolonged exercise to replace both water and electrolytes and maintain osmotic homeostasis.

Explain what happens to blood pH during vigorous exercise and how the body compensates to maintain homeostasis. Medium
Show answer

Vigorous exercise → more CO₂ from aerobic respiration + lactic acid from anaerobic respiration → blood pH falls (acidosis). Compensation: 1. Respiratory: medulla detects ↑CO₂/↓pH → hyperventilation → more CO₂ exhaled → blood pH rises (fast). 2. Blood buffers: bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) binds H⁺ to form H₂CO₃ → CO₂ + H₂O (immediate). 3. Renal: kidneys excrete more H⁺ in urine and reabsorb more HCO₃⁻ (slower, hours). These three systems work together to maintain blood pH within 7.35–7.45.

Describe what would happen to body temperature regulation in a person with damaged sweat glands (anhidrosis) in a 40 °C environment. Hard
Show answer

Without functioning sweat glands, the main mechanism for evaporative cooling is lost. In 40 °C heat, body temperature rises rapidly (hyperthermia). Vasodilation still occurs (more blood to skin → some convective heat loss) but insufficient to compensate for extreme heat. Body temperature continues to rise → heat stroke (T > 40°C) → enzymes denature → multiple organ dysfunction → brain damage → death if not rapidly cooled (cool water immersion, fans, IV fluids). Anhidrosis is a serious medical emergency in hot environments.

Explain how feedback from adipose tissue (leptin) is involved in long-term body weight homeostasis. Hard
Show answer

Adipose (fat) cells secrete leptin in proportion to fat mass. Leptin acts on the hypothalamus: high leptin → suppresses appetite (reduces hunger signals), increases metabolic rate → body weight decreases toward set point. Low leptin (fat loss) → hypothalamus increases hunger and reduces metabolism → weight regained. In obesity: some individuals develop leptin resistance (hypothalamus does not respond to high leptin) → appetite is not suppressed despite excess fat → continued overeating. This is an example of long-term homeostatic regulation beyond minute-to-minute glucose control.

Compare the speed and duration of nervous vs endocrine responses in homeostasis, with one example of each. Medium
Show answer

Nervous system: very fast (milliseconds via electrical impulses); short-lived; localised response. Example: immediate vasoconstriction/vasodilation of skin arterioles in response to temperature change (controlled via sympathetic nerves). Endocrine system: slower (seconds to hours for hormones to travel in blood); longer-lasting; widespread/systemic response. Example: insulin secreted over minutes after a meal; acts on liver, muscle, and fat cells over 1–2 hours to normalise blood glucose. Most homeostatic regulation involves both systems working together (neuroendocrine coordination).

Explain the dangers of fever (pyrexia) and why a moderate fever may actually be beneficial to the immune system. Medium
Show answer

Fever (>38 °C) is caused by pyrogens (bacterial toxins, interleukins) resetting the hypothalamic thermostat upward. Moderate fever benefits: higher temperature inhibits bacterial/viral replication (they prefer cooler temperatures); enhances white blood cell activity and antibody production; speeds up metabolic reactions of immune cells. Dangers: very high fever (>40 °C) → enzyme denaturation → cell damage, brain damage, febrile seizures in children, death. Paracetamol/ibuprofen lower fever by inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis (which reset the hypothalamic set point).

How does the osmoregulation role of the kidney differ in a person living in a desert vs a person in a humid tropical climate? Hard
Show answer

Desert person: high ambient temperature + dry air → heavy water loss in sweat + respiratory moisture → raised blood osmolarity → high ADH levels → maximum water reabsorption in collecting duct → very small volume of highly concentrated urine (up to 1200 mOsm/L). High salt intake from diet must also be managed. Tropical (humid) person: lower sweat evaporation (air already saturated); moderate water loss; normal to lower blood osmolarity → moderate ADH → moderate water reabsorption → normal urine volume. Both maintain blood osmolarity ~285–295 mOsm/L through the kidney's regulatory precision.

Evaluate the claim that "diabetes is a disease of poor lifestyle choices." Consider genetic, social, and environmental factors in your answer. Hard
Show answer

Partially valid for Type 2: obesity, physical inactivity, and high-sugar diet increase risk significantly → lifestyle factors are real contributors. However: Type 1 is entirely autoimmune (no lifestyle cause); genetic susceptibility (HLA genes) strongly influences Type 2 risk; food environment (ultra-processed food availability, food deserts, marketing) shapes food choices; economic factors (healthy food is expensive; exercise requires time and safety); stress and sleep deprivation alter glucose metabolism. Explain the physiological basis of hypothermia and hyperthermia, comparing the risk each poses to enzyme function. Hard
Show answer

Hypothermia (core T < 35 °C): enzyme reaction rates decrease (reduced kinetic energy) → slowed metabolism → heart arrhythmias, reduced cardiac output, impaired neural function → coma → death if T < ~25 °C. Enzymes are not denatured (3D shape preserved) so warming can restore function – explains why hypothermic drowning victims can sometimes be resuscitated after prolonged submersion. Hyperthermia (T > 40 °C): enzymes begin to denature (hydrogen bonds in tertiary structure break) → irreversible loss of function → multi-organ failure. Hyperthermia is generally more immediately dangerous because the damage is irreversible.

Using a systems thinking approach (IB MYP), explain how the endocrine, nervous, and excretory systems all contribute to maintaining water homeostasis simultaneously. Hard
Show answer

Nervous: hypothalamic osmoreceptors detect blood osmolarity change → trigger thirst sensation (behavioural response – drinking). Endocrine: hypothalamus signals posterior pituitary → ADH released → acts on kidney collecting duct. Excretory: kidney collecting duct increases water reabsorption (if ADH high) → concentrated urine → water retained in blood. All three systems act simultaneously and synergistically: nervous triggers fast behavioural response; endocrine fine-tunes kidney function over minutes-hours; kidney executes the actual water excretion/retention. Failure of any one system (hypothalamic damage, pituitary tumour, kidney failure) disrupts the whole integrated homeostatic mechanism.

Compare positive and negative feedback loops in biological systems. Give one example of each, including one where positive feedback is essential for survival. Hard
Show answer

Negative feedback: deviation from set point → response that corrects the deviation → stability restored (e.g., insulin lowers blood glucose; ADH regulates water; shivering raises temperature). Positive feedback: deviation from set point → response that amplifies the deviation → change accelerates (generally destabilising, but sometimes essential). Example of essential positive feedback: childbirth – oxytocin causes uterine contractions → head presses on cervix → more oxytocin released → stronger contractions → accelerates labour until birth → positive feedback loop broken by delivery. Blood clotting cascade is another example (amplification ensures rapid clot). Most homeostasis uses negative feedback; positive feedback is used where a rapid, self-amplifying response to completion is needed.

Explain why the continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) devices used by diabetics are an application of homeostatic principles, and how they mimic biological feedback systems. Hard
Show answer

CGM devices contain a glucose sensor (electrode, often enzymatic) implanted subcutaneously that continuously measures interstitial glucose and transmits data wirelessly to a device/smartphone. This mimics biological receptors (pancreatic beta cells). The CGM alarms when glucose is too high or low (mimicking the comparator/set-point function). In closed-loop systems (artificial pancreas): the CGM data feeds to an insulin pump algorithm → automatically delivers insulin or reduces delivery. This mimics effector response (pancreatic insulin release). The entire system is an engineered negative feedback loop, directly modelled on the body's own glucose homeostasis but implemented externally as assistive technology for patients whose natural feedback system is impaired.

How do neonates (newborn babies) maintain temperature homeostasis differently from adults? What is brown adipose tissue (BAT)? Hard
Show answer

Neonates cannot shiver effectively (underdeveloped motor coordination) and have poor insulation (little subcutaneous fat, high surface area to volume ratio). They rely heavily on brown adipose tissue (BAT) thermogenesis: BAT cells contain numerous mitochondria rich in uncoupling protein-1 (UCP-1/thermogenin). UCP-1 short-circuits the ETC proton gradient → energy released as heat rather than ATP. BAT is located around the neck, between shoulder blades, and around organs. This non-shivering thermogenesis allows rapid heat production. Adults retain small amounts of BAT; research targets BAT activation for obesity treatment (burns fat without exercise).

Analyse how the IB MYP concept of "interconnected systems" applies to a heatwave affecting multiple body systems simultaneously. Hard
Show answer

A prolonged heatwave stresses all homeostatic systems simultaneously: Thermoregulation: sweating → dehydration → hyperthermia risk if sweat can't keep pace. Osmoregulation: water loss → raised blood osmolarity → ADH rises → kidneys conserve water; if dehydration severe → kidney damage (acute kidney injury). Cardiovascular: vasodilation of skin arterioles → reduced blood pressure → heart must increase rate to maintain perfusion; dehydration reduces blood volume → preload falls → cardiac output drops. Neural: hyperthermia → confusion (brain temperature rises); hyponatraemia (if rehydrated with plain water) → cerebral oedema. Glucose regulation: heat stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline) raise blood glucose → challenges insulin regulation. All systems interact: addressing one (e.g., drinking water) helps others. IB MYP systems thinking: vulnerability of elderly and young (impaired thermoregulation) highlights social dimensions of climate change impacts.

Evaluate the role of melatonin in circadian rhythm regulation as a form of temporal homeostasis. What disrupts it and what are the consequences? Hard
Show answer

Melatonin is produced by the pineal gland (controlled by the hypothalamic suprachiasmatic nucleus – SCN). In darkness, SCN signals pineal to increase melatonin secretion → promotes sleep onset and maintains circadian rhythm (body's ~24-hour internal clock). Melatonin can be seen as "temporal homeostasis" – maintaining the body's internal time consistent with the external light-dark cycle. Disruptors: artificial blue light (screens) at night suppresses melatonin even in darkness; shift work; jet lag; irregular sleep schedules. Consequences: disrupted circadian rhythm → sleep disorders, impaired immune function, increased metabolic disease risk (obesity, Type 2 diabetes), depression, and (long-term) increased cancer risk (shift workers have elevated breast/prostate cancer rates). Melatonin supplements and blue-light-blocking glasses are used as interventions.
