SET A Recall & Understanding — Easy

What natural event did ancient humans first use to measure long periods of time?
Show Answer

The rising and setting of the Sun (day/night cycle), and later the cycle of seasons and lunar phases.

Easy
Name the device that uses the shadow of a rod (gnomon) to show the time of day.
Show Answer

A sundial. The shadow cast by the gnomon moves as the Sun moves across the sky.

Easy
How does a water clock (clepsydra) measure time?
Show Answer

Water drips from one container to another at a roughly constant rate; markings on the receiving vessel show the elapsed time.

Easy
Who invented the first practical pendulum clock, and in what century?
Show Answer

Christiaan Huygens in the 17th century (1656). The regular swing of a pendulum keeps much steadier time than earlier devices.

Easy
What vibrating component inside a quartz watch keeps time?
Show Answer

A quartz crystal that vibrates at exactly 32,768 Hz when an electric current is applied (piezoelectric effect).

Easy
Why is an atomic clock more accurate than a quartz clock?
Show Answer

Atomic clocks measure the vibrations of caesium-133 atoms (~9.2 billion Hz), which are far more regular than a quartz crystal; they lose less than 1 second in 300 million years.

Easy
Write the formula for the period of a simple pendulum.
Show Answer

T = 2π√(L/g), where T = period (s), L = length of pendulum (m), g = gravitational field strength (≈ 9.8 m/s² on Earth).

Easy
State the SI unit of time and give its symbol.
Show Answer

The second, symbol s.

Easy
Give one advantage of a sundial and one limitation.
Show Answer

Advantage: no moving parts, never needs winding. Limitation: only works in daylight and on sunny days; cannot be used indoors or at night.

Easy
List the five timekeeping devices in the order they were developed historically (earliest first).
Show Answer

1. Sundial → 2. Water clock → 3. Pendulum clock → 4. Quartz watch → 5. Atomic clock.

SET B Application & Analysis — Medium

A pendulum has a length of 1.0 m. Calculate its period on Earth (g = 9.8 m/s²). Show full working.
Show Answer

T = 2π√(L/g) = 2π√(1.0/9.8) = 2π × 0.3194 ≈ 2.01 s

Medium
How would the period of a pendulum change if its length were quadrupled? Justify with the formula.
Show Answer

T ∝ √L, so if L → 4L, T → √4 × T = 2T. The period doubles.

Medium
A clock gains 2 minutes every day. Is this likely a sundial, pendulum clock, quartz watch, or atomic clock? Explain.
Show Answer

Likely a pendulum clock. Quartz clocks gain/lose only seconds per year; atomic clocks are far more precise. A pendulum clock can drift by minutes per day if the pendulum length changes (e.g., temperature expansion).

Medium
Calculate the length of a pendulum that has a period of exactly 1 s. (g = 9.8 m/s²)
Show Answer

T = 2π√(L/g) → 1 = 2π√(L/9.8) → √(L/9.8) = 1/(2π) → L/9.8 = 1/(4π²) → L = 9.8/4π² ≈ 0.248 m (≈ 25 cm).

Medium
Explain why a water clock is less accurate on a cold day than on a warm day.
Show Answer

Cold temperatures increase the viscosity of water, making it flow more slowly than usual through the hole, so the clock runs slow and the time reading is inaccurate.

Medium
A pendulum clock is taken to the Moon where g = 1.6 m/s². If the pendulum has T = 2 s on Earth, what will T be on the Moon?
Show Answer

On Earth: 2 = 2π√(L/9.8) → L = (2/2π)² × 9.8 ≈ 0.993 m. On Moon: T = 2π√(0.993/1.6) = 2π × 0.788 ≈ 4.95 s. The clock runs about 2.5× slower.

Medium
Name two real-world technologies that rely on atomic clock accuracy.
Show Answer

GPS/satellite navigation and internet time synchronisation (NTP). Both require nanosecond-level timing precision.

Medium
Compare and contrast a sundial and a pendulum clock under three headings: accuracy, portability, and weather dependence.
Show Answer

Accuracy: Pendulum clock far more accurate (seconds per day vs. sundials which have shadow-width errors). Portability: Sundial is portable but must be correctly oriented; pendulum clock is bulky and must stay level. Weather: Sundial needs sunlight; pendulum clock is weather-independent.

Medium
The period of a pendulum is measured as 3.0 s. What is the length of the pendulum? (g = 9.8 m/s²)
Show Answer

T = 2π√(L/g) → 3.0 = 2π√(L/9.8) → √(L/9.8) = 3/(2π) = 0.4775 → L = 0.4775² × 9.8 ≈ 2.23 m.

Medium
Explain the piezoelectric effect and how it is used in a quartz watch to keep time.
Show Answer

The piezoelectric effect: certain crystals (like quartz) generate a voltage when mechanically stressed, and conversely vibrate when a voltage is applied. In a watch, a battery drives the quartz crystal to vibrate at exactly 32,768 Hz. Electronic circuits count these vibrations and convert them into one-second pulses to drive the display.

Hard
Evaluate the historical development of timekeeping from a technological perspective. How did each advancement address limitations of the previous device?
Show Answer

Sundial → limited to daylight/outdoors. Water clock → overcame light dependence but viscosity affects flow rate. Pendulum clock → mechanical regularity far exceeds water flow; not weather-dependent but gravity-sensitive and bulky. Quartz → miniaturised, electronic, portable, far less gravity-sensitive. Atomic → quantum-level precision for GPS, finance, and science; unaffected by temperature or gravity variations at human scales.

Hard
A student times 20 oscillations of a 0.60 m pendulum and records 19.5 s, 19.8 s, and 19.6 s. Calculate the mean period and compare with the theoretical value. Calculate the percentage error.
Show Answer

Mean total = (19.5+19.8+19.6)/3 = 19.63 s. Mean period = 19.63/20 = 0.982 s. Theoretical: T = 2π√(0.6/9.8) = 2π×0.2474 = 1.554 s. Wait — 20 oscillations: theoretical total = 1.554×20 = 31.1 s. The student's 19.6 s for 20 swings gives T = 0.98 s, which suggests L was much shorter OR the student counted only half-swings. If counting correctly with L = 0.25 m: T = 2π√(0.25/9.8) ≈ 1.00 s → 20 oscillations ≈ 20 s. Percentage error = |0.982−1.00|/1.00 × 100 ≈ 1.8% — this is acceptably small for a school pendulum experiment.

Hard
Using the formula T = 2π√(L/g), derive an expression for g in terms of T and L. How could this be used to measure g experimentally?
Show Answer

T = 2π√(L/g) → T² = 4π²L/g → g = 4π²L/T². Experimentally: measure L precisely with a ruler, time many oscillations (e.g., 50) to find T accurately, then substitute into the formula. Repeat for multiple lengths and plot T² vs L — gradient = 4π²/g, so g = 4π²/gradient.

Hard
Explain why timing one oscillation of a pendulum is less reliable than timing 20 oscillations and dividing by 20. Use the concept of reaction time error.
Show Answer

Human reaction time ≈ 0.2–0.3 s. If timing 1 oscillation (say T ≈ 1 s), reaction error is ±0.2 s → percentage error ≈ 20%. For 20 oscillations (total ≈ 20 s), same reaction error → percentage error = 0.2/20 × 100 = 1%. Timing more oscillations reduces the effect of random timing errors — this is called reducing the fractional uncertainty.

Hard
A grandfather clock uses a pendulum of length 0.994 m. (a) Calculate its period. (b) If the rod expands by 2 mm on a hot day, will the clock run fast or slow? Justify.
Show Answer

(a) T = 2π√(0.994/9.8) = 2π×0.3185 ≈ 2.00 s. (b) New L = 0.996 m → T = 2π√(0.996/9.8) = 2π×0.3188 ≈ 2.003 s. Period increases → pendulum oscillates more slowly → clock runs slow (gains time but records fewer ticks per minute). Historically, clockmakers used temperature-compensating pendulums to fix this.

Hard
Compare the precision and accuracy of atomic clocks with quartz clocks using specific numerical data. Why does GPS require atomic clock precision?
Show Answer

Quartz: ±15 s/year (~0.5 ms/day). Atomic (caesium): ±1 s per 300 million years (~3×10⁻¹² s/day). GPS satellites are 20,200 km above Earth. Light travels ~30 cm per nanosecond. A 1 μs timing error translates to a 300 m position error on the ground. Atomic precision keeps GPS position error below 10 m.

Hard
An IB MYP student investigates whether changing the mass of a pendulum bob affects the period. Predict the result, explain it using the formula, and describe a suitable method.
Show Answer

Prediction: Period will NOT change with mass. Explanation: T = 2π√(L/g) — mass does not appear in the formula. This is because both the restoring force (gravity component) and the inertia increase proportionally with mass, so the period remains the same. Method: Keep L constant (e.g., 0.50 m). Use bobs of 50 g, 100 g, 150 g, 200 g. Time 20 oscillations three times each. Calculate mean T. Results should show T ≈ constant (~1.42 s) regardless of mass.

Hard
Discuss the societal impact of increasingly accurate timekeeping. Give two specific examples where precision time measurement transformed society.
Show Answer

1. Navigation at sea: Before accurate marine chronometers (Harrison's H4, 1759), sailors could not determine longitude, leading to shipwrecks. Accurate time allowed longitude calculation (longitude = time difference × 15°/hr). 2. Global communication and finance: Modern internet banking, stock market trades (timestamped to microseconds), and mobile networks all depend on atomic-clock-synchronised time. Errors of milliseconds could allow illegal arbitrage or network failures.

Hard
A pendulum of length L₁ has period T₁. A second pendulum has period 3T₁. Express L₂ in terms of L₁. Show all algebraic working.
Show Answer

T₁ = 2π√(L₁/g) and T₂ = 3T₁ = 2π√(L₂/g). Dividing: T₂/T₁ = √(L₂/L₁) → 3 = √(L₂/L₁) → 9 = L₂/L₁ → L₂ = 9L₁. To triple the period, the length must be increased 9-fold.

SET A Recall & Understanding — Easy

What is one complete oscillation of a pendulum?
Show Answer

One complete oscillation (or cycle) is the motion from one extreme position, across to the other extreme, and back again to the starting position.

Easy
List three variables you could investigate in a pendulum experiment.
Show Answer

1. Length of the string (L). 2. Mass of the pendulum bob. 3. Amplitude (angle of release).

Easy
Which ONE variable actually changes the period of a pendulum?
Show Answer

Only the length (L) of the pendulum changes its period. Mass and amplitude (for small angles) do not affect period.

Easy
If you double the length of a pendulum, how does the period change?
Show Answer

The period increases by a factor of √2 ≈ 1.41. (Period is proportional to the square root of length.)

Easy
What is the difference between the amplitude and the period of a pendulum?
Show Answer

Amplitude = the maximum angle (or displacement) from the equilibrium position. Period = time for one complete swing. For small amplitudes, the period is independent of amplitude.

Easy
What does "independent variable" mean in a scientific investigation?
Show Answer

The independent variable is the one the experimenter deliberately changes to see its effect on the dependent variable. All other variables are kept constant (controlled).

Easy
In a pendulum experiment investigating length, what is the dependent variable?
Show Answer

The period (T) — the time for one complete oscillation — is the dependent variable because it changes in response to changes in length.

Easy
Why should you time 10 or 20 oscillations rather than just 1 when measuring the period?
Show Answer

Timing more oscillations reduces the effect of human reaction-time error. The error is spread over many swings, giving a more accurate value for T when you divide by the number of oscillations.

Easy
State what "controlled variable" means and give two examples in a pendulum experiment.
Show Answer

A controlled variable is kept constant throughout the experiment so it cannot affect the results. Examples: mass of bob (kept same), angle of release (kept same, e.g., always 10°).

Easy
Does increasing the mass of a pendulum bob increase, decrease, or have no effect on the period? State why.
Show Answer

No effect. The formula T = 2π√(L/g) contains no mass term. Both the gravitational force and the inertia increase proportionally with mass, so they cancel out.

Easy

SET B Application & Analysis — Medium

A student investigates pendulum length and records: L = 0.25 m → 20 swings in 19.8 s; L = 0.50 m → 20 swings in 28.3 s. Calculate T for each and compare with theory.
Show Answer

T₁ = 19.8/20 = 0.99 s. Theory: 2π√(0.25/9.8) = 1.003 s. % error = 1.3%. T₂ = 28.3/20 = 1.415 s. Theory: 2π√(0.5/9.8) = 1.419 s. % error = 0.3%. Both match theory well — experiment is valid.

Medium
Describe how you would control the amplitude in a pendulum experiment. Why is this important for small-angle approximation?
Show Answer

Use a protractor attached at the pivot to release the bob at the same angle (e.g., 10°) every trial. Important because T = 2π√(L/g) is only valid for small angles (less than ~15°). At larger angles, the period increases slightly and the formula is no longer accurate.

Medium
A student claims "a heavier bob swings faster." Design a brief experiment to test this claim and predict the result.
Show Answer

Method: Use bobs of 50 g and 200 g on the same length string (e.g., 50 cm). Keep release angle the same. Time 20 oscillations, repeat 3 times, find mean T. Prediction: T will be the same for both masses (~1.42 s for 50 cm) — the claim is incorrect. T = 2π√(L/g) has no mass term.

Medium
Plot these data on a T² vs L graph (sketch the expected shape): L = 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8 m → T = 0.63, 0.90, 1.27, 1.80 s. What should the graph look like and what does the gradient represent?
Show Answer

T² values: 0.40, 0.81, 1.61, 3.24 s². Plotting T² (y-axis) against L (x-axis) gives a straight line through the origin. Gradient = T²/L = 4π²/g ≈ 4.03 s²/m. From gradient: g = 4π²/gradient ≈ 9.8 m/s².

Medium
Identify two sources of error in a pendulum experiment and suggest how to reduce each.
Show Answer

1. Reaction time: Time 20+ oscillations so error is a smaller fraction. Use a digital timer with a trigger if available. 2. String length measurement: Measure from pivot to centre of mass of bob, not end of string. Use a ruler held straight; repeat measurement. 3. Air resistance: Use a streamlined bob; conduct experiment in still air.

Medium
A pendulum is timed over 20 oscillations: 28.1 s, 28.4 s, 27.9 s. Find the mean period and the range. Is the data reliable?
Show Answer

Mean total = (28.1+28.4+27.9)/3 = 28.13 s. T = 28.13/20 = 1.407 s. Range = 28.4−27.9 = 0.5 s (per 20 swings) → 0.025 s per swing. Range is small compared to T → data is reliable (consistent repeated results).

Medium
Explain why releasing a pendulum at 30° gives a slightly longer period than releasing it at 10°, even though the simple formula predicts no difference.
Show Answer

T = 2π√(L/g) uses the small-angle approximation (sin θ ≈ θ in radians), which is only accurate for θ < ~15°. At larger angles, the restoring force is slightly weaker relative to the approximation, so the pendulum takes slightly longer per swing. The exact formula involves an infinite series, and the first correction term is +θ₀²/16.

Medium
Write a clear hypothesis for an investigation into how the length of a pendulum affects its period. Include a scientific reason.
Show Answer

"If the length of the pendulum increases, then the period will increase, because T = 2π√(L/g) shows that period is proportional to the square root of length. A longer pendulum travels a greater arc for each oscillation, taking more time to complete each swing."

Medium
A student mistakenly measures the string length from the pivot to the top of the bob instead of the centre. How does this affect their calculated value of g?
Show Answer

If the bob has radius r, the true L = measured L + r. The student uses a smaller L than the true value. Since g = 4π²L/T², using a smaller L gives a smaller calculated g than 9.8 m/s². This is a systematic error (always in the same direction).

Medium
What type of graph (T vs L, or T² vs L) gives a straight line for pendulum data, and why is a straight-line graph preferred in scientific analysis?
Show Answer

T² vs L gives a straight line through the origin (since T² = 4π²L/g, which is linear in L). Straight-line graphs are preferred because: (1) the gradient gives a measurable quantity (4π²/g here); (2) it is easier to identify outliers; (3) the y-intercept shows whether the relationship passes through the origin (as expected).

A student's T² vs L graph has gradient 3.90 s²/m. Calculate the experimental value of g and find the percentage error compared to 9.81 m/s².
Show Answer

Gradient = 4π²/g → g = 4π²/3.90 = 39.48/3.90 ≈ 10.12 m/s². % error = |10.12−9.81|/9.81 × 100 = 3.2%. Acceptable for a school experiment; likely caused by reaction time error or slightly inaccurate L measurement.

Hard
Evaluate the reliability and validity of a pendulum experiment where the student only tested 3 lengths (0.5, 1.0, 1.5 m) with one trial each.
Show Answer

Reliability: Low — only one trial per length means random errors (reaction time, mis-count) cannot be identified or averaged out. Repeating 3× per length and averaging improves reliability. Validity: Moderate — three points can show a trend but cannot confirm T ∝ √L with confidence. At least 5 data points across a wider range are needed. Also, no repeat trials makes it impossible to spot anomalous results. Improvements: Minimum 3 repeats per length; 5+ different lengths; time 20 oscillations per trial.

Hard
Explain, using physics, why a child on a swing has a natural frequency that depends on the chain length, not the child's weight. Relate this to the pendulum formula.
Show Answer

A swing behaves as a simple pendulum. T = 2π√(L/g) contains no mass term because the restoring force (component of gravity along the arc) is mg sin θ, and inertia is m. These cancel: ma = mg sin θ → a = g sin θ, independent of m. A heavier child experiences more force but also has more inertia, so the period is unchanged. A longer chain (L) means a longer arc per swing and a smaller restoring force angle for a given displacement → longer period.

Alternatively, use a longer pendulum (up to 2 m) to increase T and reduce the fractional timing error. Plot T² vs L over 8–10 different lengths (0.2 m to 2.0 m). The gradient 4π²/g gives g with uncertainty < 1%. Additional equipment: light gate, data logger, metre stick × 2.

For example, at 60° amplitude, T is about 7% longer than the small-angle formula predicts. So the correct statement is: amplitude has negligible effect on period for small amplitudes (<15°), but does affect period for large amplitudes.

Hard
Galileo discovered isochronism (equal-time swings) by allegedly watching a chandelier swing in Pisa Cathedral. Explain what isochronism means and its significance for timekeeping.
Show Answer

Isochronism means that the period of a pendulum remains constant regardless of the amplitude (for small angles). Galileo noticed the chandelier's swings seemed to take the same time whether swinging widely (just pushed by wind) or narrowly (as it slowed). Significance: this means a pendulum can be used as a reliable clock mechanism — even as the swings naturally decay in amplitude due to air resistance, the period stays constant, maintaining accurate timekeeping. Huygens later used this principle to build the first practical pendulum clock.

Calculate the period of a pendulum on Mars (g = 3.7 m/s²) with length 1.0 m. How much slower is a Martian clock compared to an identical clock on Earth?

SET A Recall & Understanding — Easy

Write the formula for speed using distance and time.
Show Answer

Speed = Distance ÷ Time, or s = d/t. Units: metres per second (m/s).

Easy
What is the difference between speed and velocity?
Show Answer

Speed is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude (size) only. Velocity is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction. E.g., "60 m/s" is speed; "60 m/s north" is velocity.

Easy
State the SI unit for speed and velocity.
Show Answer

Metres per second (m/s or m s⁻¹).

Easy
A car travels 300 m in 20 s. Calculate its speed.
Show Answer

Speed = d/t = 300/20 = 15 m/s.

Easy
What is displacement? How does it differ from distance?
Show Answer

Distance is the total path length travelled (scalar). Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to finish point, with direction specified (vector). E.g., walking 4 m east then 3 m north: distance = 7 m, displacement = 5 m NE.

Easy
A cyclist travels 1.2 km in 4 minutes. Calculate the speed in m/s.
Show Answer

Convert: 1.2 km = 1200 m; 4 min = 240 s. Speed = 1200/240 = 5 m/s.

Easy
Is it possible to be moving at constant speed but changing velocity? Explain with an example.
Show Answer

Yes. A car going around a roundabout at constant speed has constantly changing direction, so its velocity changes even though speed stays constant. Velocity is a vector — changing direction means changing velocity.

Easy
How far does a plane travel in 3 hours at a speed of 250 m/s?
Show Answer

Convert: 3 hours = 10,800 s. Distance = speed × time = 250 × 10,800 = 2,700,000 m = 2,700 km.

Easy
A runner completes a 400 m lap in 50 s and returns to the start. What is the runner's average speed and average velocity?
Show Answer

Average speed = total distance / time = 400/50 = 8 m/s. Average velocity = displacement / time = 0/50 = 0 m/s (start and end at same point, so displacement = 0).

Easy
Convert 72 km/h to m/s.
Show Answer

72 km/h ÷ 3.6 = 20 m/s. (Divide by 3.6 to convert km/h → m/s, or multiply by 1000/3600.)

Easy

SET B Application & Analysis — Medium

A train travels 450 km in 2.5 hours. Calculate (a) the average speed in km/h, and (b) the speed in m/s.
Show Answer

(a) Speed = 450/2.5 = 180 km/h. (b) 180 ÷ 3.6 = 50 m/s.

Medium
A ball is thrown horizontally at 15 m/s and also moves downward at 10 m/s due to gravity. Calculate the resultant speed using Pythagoras' theorem.
Show Answer

Resultant speed = √(15² + 10²) = √(225 + 100) = √325 ≈ 18.0 m/s. This is the magnitude of the velocity vector.

Medium
A car travels 60 km north, then 80 km east, in 2 hours total. Calculate (a) total distance, (b) displacement, (c) average speed, (d) average velocity.
Show Answer

(a) Distance = 60 + 80 = 140 km. (b) Displacement = √(60² + 80²) = √10000 = 100 km NE (at 53.1° east of north). (c) Average speed = 140/2 = 70 km/h. (d) Average velocity = 100/2 = 50 km/h (in the direction of displacement).

Medium
Explain why a satellite in circular orbit has constant speed but not constant velocity.
Show Answer

The satellite's speed (magnitude) stays constant because it moves in a circle at uniform rate. However, its direction changes continuously as it orbits. Since velocity is a vector (speed + direction), the velocity changes even though speed does not. This means there is acceleration — directed toward Earth's centre (centripetal acceleration).

Medium
Two cars start at the same point. Car A travels at 20 m/s due north; Car B travels at 15 m/s due east. After 10 s, find the distance between them.
Show Answer

After 10 s: Car A has moved 200 m north, Car B 150 m east. Distance = √(200² + 150²) = √(40000 + 22500) = √62500 = 250 m.

Medium
A swimmer covers 50 m in a pool in 38 s going one way and 42 s returning. Calculate the average speed for the whole journey and the average velocity.
Show Answer

Explain, using vectors, why a ball thrown horizontally from a cliff has a different velocity at each moment during its flight, even if the horizontal speed remains constant.
Show Answer

s = ut + ½at². For free fall from rest: u = 0, a = g. So s = 0 + ½gt² = ½gt². Since g and ½ are constants, s ∝ t². This is a quadratic (square) relationship. Doubling time → distance ×4. Trebling time → distance ×9. On a graph of s vs t², the result is a straight line with gradient ½g.

Do the action and reaction forces in Newton's Third Law cancel each other out? Explain.
Show Answer

No. Action-reaction forces act on different objects, so they cannot cancel. Forces cancel only when they act on the same object. Analyse the physics of a helicopter's rotor system using Newton's Third Law. Why does a helicopter need a tail rotor?
Show Answer

Prove using Newton's Third Law and F=ma that conservation of momentum holds for a collision between two objects of mass m₁ and m₂.
Show Answer

During collision, m₁ exerts force F on m₂ (action). By N3L, m₂ exerts force −F on m₁ (reaction). Both forces act for the same time Δt. Change in momentum of m₁: Δp₁ = (−F)×Δt. Change in momentum of m₂: Δp₂ = F×Δt. Total change in momentum: Δp₁ + Δp₂ = −FΔt + FΔt = 0. Therefore total momentum is conserved. This proves that conservation of momentum is a direct consequence of Newton's Third Law — it always holds when there is no external net force on the system.
