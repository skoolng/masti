SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Why should you never taste a laboratory acid to identify it?Medium
Show Answer

Laboratory acids such as H₂SO₄ and HCl are highly corrosive and can cause severe chemical burns to the mouth, throat, and digestive system. Tasting is never a safe identification method in a lab.

Explain why acidic solutions conduct electricity.Medium
Show Answer

Acids dissociate in water to produce free ions (H⁺ and an anion). These free-moving ions carry electric charge, allowing the solution to conduct electricity.

Describe what you would observe when zinc is added to dilute sulphuric acid.Medium
Show Answer

Bubbles of colourless hydrogen gas are produced, the zinc gradually dissolves, and the solution becomes warm. The reaction produces zinc sulphate solution.

Compare strong acids with weak acids giving one example of each.Medium
Show Answer

A strong acid (e.g., HCl) fully dissociates in water releasing all its H⁺ ions. A weak acid (e.g., acetic acid in vinegar) only partially dissociates, so fewer H⁺ ions are present. Strong acids are more corrosive at the same concentration.

Why does sulphuric acid have a lower pH than acetic acid at the same concentration?Medium
Show Answer

Sulphuric acid is a strong acid that fully dissociates, producing more H⁺ ions per mole. Acetic acid is a weak acid and only partially dissociates, producing fewer H⁺ ions, so its pH is higher (less acidic).

How would you test that the gas produced when an acid reacts with a metal is hydrogen?Medium
Show Answer

List three physical properties that help identify an acidic solution.Medium
Show Answer

1. Sour taste (safe examples only). 2. Turns blue litmus paper red. 3. pH value below 7 on a pH meter or with universal indicator. 4. Conducts electricity.

Why is HCl considered a mineral acid while citric acid is an organic acid?Medium
Show Answer

HCl contains no carbon and is derived from inorganic (mineral) sources, making it a mineral acid. Citric acid contains carbon and is produced by living organisms (fruits), classifying it as an organic acid.

Design an experiment to determine whether an unknown household liquid is acidic. State hypothesis, method, expected results, and a safety precaution. (MYP Criterion B/D)Hard

Hypothesis: If the liquid is acidic, it will turn blue litmus red and show a pH below 7.
Method: (1) Place a few drops on blue litmus paper. (2) Test with universal indicator solution and compare to the colour chart. (3) Measure pH with a calibrated pH meter.

Safety: Wear gloves and goggles; never taste unknown substances.

Evaluate the reliability of using taste as a method to identify acids in a school laboratory. Suggest a more reliable and safe alternative.Hard
Show Answer

Taste is unreliable and extremely dangerous in a lab context. Many laboratory acids (H₂SO₄, HCl) are highly corrosive and would cause chemical burns. The method cannot distinguish between different acids or give quantitative data. A reliable alternative is using a calibrated pH meter, which gives precise numerical data, is non-destructive to the sample, and poses no safety risk when used correctly.

Analyse why concentrated sulphuric acid is more hazardous than dilute sulphuric acid. Relate your answer to ion concentration and corrosiveness.Hard
Show Answer

Concentration determines the number of H⁺ ions per unit volume. Concentrated H₂SO₄ has far more H⁺ ions, making the pH extremely low (near 0). It is also a dehydrating agent that can char organic material (skin, paper) by removing water molecules. It releases significant heat on contact with water, causing thermal burns in addition to chemical burns. Dilute H₂SO₄ still has corrosive H⁺ ions but at a much lower concentration, making it less immediately dangerous.

Compare the roles of H⁺ ions in: (a) the sour taste of vinegar and (b) the reaction of HCl with zinc. What does this suggest about the general reactivity of acids?Hard
Show Answer

(a) H⁺ ions stimulate sour-taste receptors on the tongue in safe, dilute organic acids like acetic acid. (b) H⁺ ions oxidise zinc metal, displacing zinc ions into solution and releasing H₂ gas: Zn + 2H⁺ → Zn²⁺ + H₂. This suggests that H⁺ ions are the chemically active species in acids responsible for both sensory effects and chemical reactivity, supporting the Arrhenius definition that acids are H⁺ donors.

A student claims: "All sour-tasting substances are strong acids." Evaluate this claim using evidence.Hard
Show Answer

The claim is incorrect. Sourness indicates acidity (presence of H⁺ ions) but does not indicate strength. Lemon juice and vinegar taste sour but are weak acids (citric acid pH ~2.2, acetic acid pH ~2.9 at typical concentrations) that only partially dissociate. Strong acids like HCl and H₂SO₄ are not sour in the culinary sense because they are corrosive and cannot safely be tasted. Acid strength depends on degree of dissociation in water, not taste intensity. Therefore taste is not a valid measure of acid strength.

Explain, using particle theory, why diluting an acid increases its pH value.Hard
Show Answer

When water is added to an acid, the total number of H⁺ ions stays the same but they are now spread through a larger volume of solution. The concentration of H⁺ ions (moles per litre) decreases. Since pH = −log[H⁺], a lower H⁺ concentration gives a higher (less negative) log value, so pH increases. For example, diluting HCl ten-fold increases pH by 1 unit.

Why does the reaction rate between magnesium and hydrochloric acid decrease over time? Use collision theory in your answer.Hard
Show Answer

As the reaction proceeds, H⁺ ions are consumed, so the concentration of HCl decreases. According to collision theory, fewer reactant particles per unit volume means fewer successful collisions per second between H⁺ ions and the Mg surface. The rate of collision — and hence reaction rate — falls. Also, the surface area of Mg decreases as it dissolves, further reducing collisions.

Predict what would happen if you added excess magnesium to a fixed volume of dilute HCl. Explain in terms of limiting reagent.Hard
Show Answer

Bubbling (H₂ production) would continue until all the HCl is consumed. At that point the acid (limiting reagent) is fully used up and the reaction stops. The remaining excess Mg would remain unreacted in the solution. The solution would become neutral (MgCl₂ solution) rather than acidic.

Evaluate whether stomach acid (HCl, pH ~2) is more or less acidic than black coffee (pH ~5). Calculate how many times more acidic. (MYP Criterion D)Hard
Show Answer

Stomach acid at pH 2 is more acidic than black coffee at pH 5. The pH scale is logarithmic: each unit represents a 10× difference in H⁺ concentration. The difference is 5 − 2 = 3 units, so stomach acid has 10³ = 1000 times higher H⁺ ion concentration than black coffee.

A scientist measures the pH of river water upstream and downstream of a factory discharge point and finds values of 7.2 and 4.8 respectively. Analyse what this suggests and propose one way to investigate the cause. (MYP Criterion B)Hard
Show Answer

The downstream pH of 4.8 is significantly acidic compared to the near-neutral 7.2 upstream, strongly suggesting that the factory is discharging acidic waste (possibly containing H₂SO₄ or HCl). The drop of 2.4 pH units indicates roughly 250× more H⁺ ions downstream. Investigation: Collect water samples at multiple points downstream at different distances and times; test for specific acid anions (SO₄²⁻, Cl⁻) using chemical tests; compare with factory discharge records and legal effluent standards.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain the difference between a base and an alkali using examples.Medium
Show Answer

A base is any substance that can accept protons or neutralise an acid to form a salt and water. An alkali is a specific type of base that is soluble in water and produces OH⁻ ions. Example of a base that is not an alkali: CuO (insoluble). Example of an alkali: NaOH (soluble, forms Na⁺ and OH⁻ in water).

Why does soap feel slippery? Relate to the chemistry of bases.Medium
Show Answer

Soap is made from a base (NaOH) reacting with fats. The resulting sodium salts of fatty acids are mildly alkaline in solution. The OH⁻ ions and the saponified fat molecules react with and break down the oils on the skin surface, creating a slippery emulsion.

Describe what happens when you add sodium hydroxide to water. Write an equation.Medium
Show Answer

NaOH dissolves exothermically in water, fully dissociating into Na⁺ and OH⁻ ions. The solution becomes hot.
NaOH(s) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

Compare NaOH and NH₃ as bases. Which is stronger and why?Medium
Show Answer

NaOH is a strong base — it fully dissociates in water, producing a high concentration of OH⁻ ions. Ammonia (NH₃) is a weak base — it only partially reacts with water (NH₃ + H₂O ⇋ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻), producing fewer OH⁻ ions at the same concentration. Therefore NaOH has a higher pH at equivalent concentrations.

Give three practical uses of alkalis in everyday life.Medium
Show Answer

1. NaOH in drain cleaners (dissolves grease and hair). 2. Ca(OH)₂ (lime) to neutralise acidic soil in farming. 3. Mg(OH)₂ in antacids to neutralise excess stomach acid. 4. NaHCO₃ in baking as a leavening agent.

Why is calcium hydroxide spread on agricultural fields?Medium
Show Answer

Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) is alkaline. It is added to neutralise overly acidic soil, raising the pH to an optimal range (6–7) for most crops. This improves nutrient availability and encourages beneficial soil bacteria.

Explain why swallowing a strong base like NaOH is dangerous.Medium
Show Answer

Strong bases like NaOH are highly corrosive. The OH⁻ ions break down proteins and fats in tissue (saponification), causing chemical burns to the mouth, oesophagus, and stomach lining. This is called alkaline corrosion and can be life-threatening.

What happens to the pH of water when NaOH is added? Explain using ions.Medium
Show Answer

The pH increases above 7. NaOH dissociates to give OH⁻ ions. These ions reduce the concentration of H⁺ ions in water (via H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O), shifting the equilibrium and making the solution basic.

Name two metal hydroxides that are bases but not alkalis and explain why.Medium
Show Answer

Copper(II) hydroxide Cu(OH)₂ and iron(III) hydroxide Fe(OH)₃ are bases because they can neutralise acids, but they are insoluble in water so they cannot form alkaline solutions and are therefore not alkalis.

How would you distinguish experimentally between an acidic and a basic solution using only red and blue litmus paper?Medium
Show Answer

Test with both litmus papers. If blue litmus turns red and red litmus stays red → acidic. If red litmus turns blue and blue litmus stays blue → basic. If neither changes colour → neutral.

SET C MYP Analysis & Evaluation — Hard

Evaluate the statement: "All alkalis are dangerous and should not be used at home." Use evidence to support or refute this claim.Hard
Show Answer

The claim is an overgeneralisation. Concentrated NaOH drain cleaners are dangerous due to corrosive OH⁻ ions. However, many household alkalis are safe when dilute: baking soda (NaHCO₃, pH ~8.3) is used in cooking and as a gentle antacid; milk of magnesia (Mg(OH)₂) is an approved medicine. The danger depends on concentration and specific substance, not simply being an alkali. Risk-benefit analysis must consider dilution, intended use, and safety precautions.

Explain why ammonia is classified as a weak base but can still cause damage to the respiratory system.Hard
Show Answer

NH₃ is a weak base because it only partially ionises in water (NH₃ + H₂O ⇋ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻), producing a relatively low concentration of OH⁻ ions compared to NaOH. However, NH₃ is a volatile gas with a pungent odour. When inhaled, it dissolves in the moist mucous membranes of the respiratory tract, forming ammonium hydroxide which irritates and burns the delicate tissue lining. High concentrations can cause pulmonary oedema. So chemical strength and physiological harm are distinct — even weak bases can cause harm through other mechanisms.

Design an investigation to determine whether a given insoluble metal oxide is a base. Include hypothesis, method, variables, and expected result. (MYP Criterion B)Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: If the metal oxide is a base, it will neutralise an acid to form a salt and water, raising the pH of an acidic solution.
Method: Add a known mass of the oxide to a measured volume of dilute HCl. Stir. Monitor pH with a pH meter before and after. Filter off any excess solid.
Variables: IV: presence/amount of metal oxide. DV: pH of solution. CV: volume and concentration of HCl, temperature, stirring time.
Expected: If the oxide is a base, pH will increase from acidic toward neutral as H⁺ ions are consumed in the reaction: MO + 2HCl → MCl₂ + H₂O.

Analyse why the OH⁻ ion is considered the active species in base reactions, similar to how H⁺ is the active species in acid reactions.Hard
Show Answer

In the Arrhenius model, a base is defined as a substance that produces OH⁻ ions in water. It is specifically the OH⁻ ion that is responsible for all characteristic base properties: turning red litmus blue (by accepting H⁺ from the indicator molecule), neutralising acids (OH⁻ + H⁺ → H₂O), and causing the bitter taste and slippery feel. Just as H⁺ is the carrier of acid reactivity, OH⁻ is the carrier of base reactivity — the cation (e.g., Na⁺) is a spectator ion in most base reactions.

A student dissolves 0.1 g of NaOH and 0.1 g of Ca(OH)₂ separately in 100 mL of water. Which solution would have a higher pH? Justify using moles and ion release. (MYP Criterion D)Hard
Show Answer

Molar mass of NaOH = 40 g/mol; moles of NaOH = 0.1/40 = 0.0025 mol. Each NaOH gives 1 OH⁻, so [OH⁻] = 0.025 mol/L.
Molar mass of Ca(OH)₂ = 74 g/mol; moles = 0.1/74 = 0.00135 mol. Each Ca(OH)₂ gives 2 OH⁻, so [OH⁻] = 0.027 mol/L.
Ca(OH)₂ produces a slightly higher OH⁻ concentration, so it would have a marginally higher pH. (Note: Ca(OH)₂ is only sparingly soluble, so in practice NaOH solution may be clearer, but at these low masses both dissolve sufficiently.)

Explain how a strong base can cause more severe tissue damage than a strong acid of the same concentration.Hard
Show Answer

Strong acids cause coagulative necrosis — they denature proteins, forming a hard eschar that limits further penetration. Strong bases cause liquefactive necrosis — OH⁻ ions saponify fats and hydrolyse proteins continuously, destroying the protective barrier and allowing the base to penetrate deeper into tissue. This makes alkali burns (e.g., from NaOH) often more serious than acid burns of equivalent concentration and volume, because the damage continues after initial contact.

Compare the Arrhenius and Brønsted–Lowry definitions of a base. Which is broader and why?Hard
Show Answer

Arrhenius: a base produces OH⁻ ions in aqueous solution — limits the definition to water-soluble substances. Brønsted–Lowry: a base is any species that accepts a proton (H⁺) — broader because it includes substances that act as bases in non-aqueous solvents and those that don’t produce OH⁻ directly (e.g., NH₃ accepts H⁺ to form NH₄⁺). The B–L definition is broader and applies to a wider range of chemical systems.

Predict and explain what would happen if excess NaOH is added to a neutralised (neutral) solution of NaCl.Hard
Show Answer

NaCl is a salt formed from a strong acid (HCl) and a strong base (NaOH) and does not hydrolyse — it remains neutral in solution. Adding excess NaOH would introduce additional Na⁺ and OH⁻ ions, making the solution alkaline (pH > 7) because there are no H⁺ ions from the salt to neutralise the added OH⁻. The solution would not remain neutral.

Discuss how the concept of pH and bases is applied in the treatment of acid spills in a laboratory. (MYP Criterion D)Hard
Show Answer

When an acid spills, a weak base such as sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO₃) is applied rather than a strong base like NaOH. This is because: (1) NaHCO₃ neutralises the acid without generating excessive heat or introducing a new hazard. (2) It produces CO₂ and water as byproducts, both safe. (3) A strong base like NaOH would overshoot pH and create an alkaline hazard. The strategy is to shift pH gradually toward 7, confirming with pH paper. This illustrates the MYP criterion of applying chemical knowledge to real-world safety decision-making.

A scientist studying ocean chemistry finds the ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1 over 50 years. Analyse the significance of this change. (MYP Criterion D)Hard
Show Answer

Although 8.1 is still basic, the pH scale is logarithmic. A drop of 0.1 units corresponds to approximately 26% more H⁺ ions. This "ocean acidification" is caused by absorption of excess atmospheric CO₂ forming carbonic acid (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃). Consequences include dissolution of calcium carbonate shells of corals and molluscs (CaCO₃ + H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + HCO₃⁻), threatening marine biodiversity. Even small pH shifts in basic solutions can have large ecological impacts, demonstrating why understanding base chemistry and pH is critical in environmental science.

Why does a shirt stained with turmeric turn red-brown when washed with alkaline soap?Medium

Name the pigment in red cabbage responsible for its indicator properties and state why it changes colour.Medium
Show Answer

The pigment is anthocyanin. It changes colour because its molecular structure includes a pH-sensitive chromophore — in acidic conditions H⁺ ions protonate the molecule, changing the wavelengths of light it absorbs; in basic conditions OH⁻ ions deprotonate it, shifting the absorbed wavelengths and thus the observed colour.

Evaluate the suitability of red cabbage juice versus a pH meter for determining the exact pH of a solution in a school lab. (MYP Criterion D)Hard

Analyse why an indicator that only shows two colours (like litmus) is insufficient for precise chemical analysis.Hard
Show Answer

A two-colour indicator can only determine whether a solution is acidic or basic, providing binary (yes/no) information. It cannot distinguish between a strong acid (pH 1) and a weak acid (pH 5), or between a strong base (pH 13) and a weak base (pH 8). In chemistry, the degree of acidity or basicity matters critically — for example, in titrations, identifying the exact equivalence point requires an indicator that changes at the specific pH endpoint, not just "above or below 7." Also, for industrial or medical applications where precise pH control is vital, litmus provides insufficient resolution.

A student claims that because turmeric does not change colour in vinegar, vinegar must be neutral. Evaluate this reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

The reasoning is flawed. Turmeric (curcumin) does not change colour in acidic conditions — it remains yellow in both neutral and acidic solutions and only changes to red-brown above pH ~7.4. Vinegar is acidic (pH ~2.5), but turmeric cannot detect this because it has no colour response to acidity. The student has confused "no colour change" with "neutral," failing to understand the indicator's limited response range. A proper conclusion would require using a different indicator (litmus or red cabbage) to test vinegar's acidity.

Explain the molecular basis of why anthocyanin changes colour with pH, using ideas about protonation and light absorption.Hard
Show Answer

Anthocyanin contains a flavylium cation core with a conjugated pi-electron system. In acidic conditions, H⁺ ions protonate the molecule, stabilising a red/pink form with a certain electron delocalisation pattern that absorbs blue-green light (~520 nm), reflecting red/pink colour. As pH increases, OH⁻ ions deprotonate the molecule through multiple equilibria, changing the conjugation length and distribution of electrons. Different structural forms (quinoidal base, hemiketal, chalcone) absorb different wavelengths, producing purple (neutral), blue, green, and yellow at progressively higher pH. This demonstrates that colour is directly determined by molecular electronic structure.

Compare natural indicators and synthetic indicators in terms of accuracy, availability, cost, and environmental impact.Hard
Show Answer

Accuracy: Synthetic indicators (e.g., phenolphthalein) are more accurate with sharp, defined colour transitions at specific pH values; natural indicators have gradual transitions. Availability: Natural indicators (cabbage, turmeric, hibiscus) are widely available without specialised suppliers. Cost: Natural indicators are far cheaper and can be home-prepared; synthetic indicators require chemical manufacturing. Environmental impact: Natural indicators are biodegradable and non-toxic; synthetic indicators may involve chemical waste and some (e.g., phenolphthalein) have raised health concerns (potential carcinogen). For teaching basic concepts, natural indicators are preferred; for analytical chemistry, synthetic indicators are necessary.

A food scientist wants to use a natural indicator to detect if a soft drink has been adulterated with baking soda. Propose a method and justify your indicator choice. (MYP Criterion B/D)Hard
Show Answer

Explain the chemistry of why phenolphthalein is colourless in acid and pink in base, relating to molecular structure.Hard

Why do electronic pH meters not rely on indicators? Explain the principle of operation of a glass electrode pH meter.Hard

Explain what is meant by saying the pH scale is logarithmic.Medium
Show Answer

Logarithmic means each pH unit represents a 10-fold change in H⁺ ion concentration. So pH 3 has 10 times more H⁺ ions than pH 4, and 100 times more than pH 5. This scale compresses a very wide range of H⁺ concentrations (from 1 mol/L to 0.00000000000001 mol/L) into a convenient 0–14 scale.

Explain why the pH of a solution changes by only 1 unit when you dilute a strong acid 10 times.Medium
Show Answer

pH = −log[H⁺]. Diluting 10 times reduces [H⁺] by a factor of 10. Since log(10) = 1, the change in pH = −log(1/10) = 1. So pH increases by exactly 1 unit per 10-fold dilution (for strong acids at moderate concentrations).

Why is pure water considered neutral (pH 7) at 25°C?Medium

Pure water undergoes self-ionisation: H₂O ⇋ H⁺ + OH⁻. At 25°C, this produces exactly equal concentrations of H⁺ and OH⁻: 1 × 10⁻⁷ mol/L each. pH = −log(10⁻⁷) = 7. Because [H⁺] = [OH⁻], the solution has no net acidity or basicity and is defined as neutral.

Derive the relationship pH = −log[H⁺] and explain what each component means mathematically. (MYP Criterion D)Hard
Show Answer

pH is defined as the negative logarithm (base 10) of the molar concentration of hydrogen ions. Explain why a strong acid at pH 3 reacts faster with a given metal than a weak acid at pH 3.Hard

Both solutions have the same measured [H⁺] at pH 3 = 10⁻³ mol/L initially. Explain why the pH scale as a 0–14 range was established based on water's properties at 25°C and why the "neutral" point shifts at different temperatures. (MYP Criterion D)Hard

Unlike acid rain (regional, reversible with emission cuts), ocean acidification is a global, centuries-long commitment due to CO₂'s atmospheric lifetime. It is therefore arguably the more serious long-term threat.

Analyse why soils developed over granite are more vulnerable to acid rain damage than soils developed over limestone. Include buffering chemistry and nutrient cycling. (MYP Criterion D)Hard
Show Answer

Limestone soils contain CaCO₃ throughout the profile. Acid deposition is immediately buffered: CaCO₃ + 2H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + H₂O + CO₂. This generates Ca²⁺ which replaces lost base cations on exchange sites, and CO₂ is harmlessly released. Soil pH stays near 7 even with moderate acid input. Granite soils: granite is composed of silicates (SiO₂, feldspar) with no carbonate. No alkalinity to buffer H⁺. When H⁺ ions enter soil, they directly displace base cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺) from clay exchange sites, leaching them into groundwater. This causes: (1) nutrient depletion for plants; (2) pH drop to 4–5; (3) Al³⁺ mobilisation (toxic to roots); (4) disruption of microbial decomposition. Recovery takes decades even after acid rain stops.

Evaluate the evidence for and against "natural" volcanic acid rain versus human-made acid rain as the primary cause of historical forest damage in Europe. (MYP Criterion D)Hard
Show Answer

Natural volcanic SO₂ releases: globally, volcanoes emit ‸25 million tonnes SO₂/year. However, mid-20th century European industrial emissions exceeded 60 million tonnes SO₂/year. Spatial correlation: European "Waldsterben" (forest dieback) in the 1970s–80s was concentrated downwind of industrial regions (UK, Ruhr, Silesia) — not near volcanoes. Temporal correlation: forest damage accelerated during industrial expansion and declined after SO₂ emission regulations. Isotopic evidence: sulphur isotope ratios (∆³⁶S) in acid deposits matched fossil fuel signatures, not volcanic sources. Ice core records: pre-industrial sulphate deposition was far lower than 20th century peaks. Conclusion: anthropogenic acid rain is unambiguously the primary cause of European forest damage. Volcanic emissions are a natural background that ecosystems adapted to over millennia; the rapid industrial-era increase overwhelmed this adaptation.

Explain how transboundary air pollution makes acid rain an international political problem as well as a chemistry problem. (MYP global context: Fairness and Development)Hard
Show Answer

Acid rain is chemically formed when SO₂ and NO₂ undergo atmospheric oxidation and dissolution over hours to days while being transported by wind hundreds to thousands of kilometres from their sources. Britain's coal power stations (in the 1970s with tall chimneys to "dilute" local pollution) emitted SO₂ that was deposited as acid rain in Scandinavia. Sweden and Norway suffered damage to their forests and lakes from pollution they did not create. This creates a political dilemma: emitting countries gain economic benefit (cheap electricity) while receiving countries bear environmental costs — a classic "externality" problem. Resolution requires international treaties (LRTAP Convention, 1979 — first international air pollution treaty), which require countries to accept binding SO₂/NO₂ reduction targets. This shows how chemistry (reaction mechanisms, atmospheric chemistry, pH effects) intersects with international law, economics, and environmental justice.

Model the chemical interaction between acid rain (pH 4, H₂SO₄) and a soil containing 5% CaCO₃ by mass. Calculate the acid-neutralising capacity and estimate how long the soil can buffer acid rain of 500 mm/year. Show all working. (MYP Criterion D)Hard

Discuss how acid rain is an example of a "wicked problem" in environmental chemistry — a problem that is technically solvable but socially and politically complex to solve. (MYP global context: Globalisation & Sustainability)Hard
Show Answer

Technically: the chemistry is well understood (SO₂ + NO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ + HNO₃); solutions exist (FGD, catalytic converters, renewable energy to replace fossil fuels). Yet implementation is complex: (1) Economic interests — coal industries, energy companies resist expensive emission controls; developing nations argue that wealthy nations caused the problem and should bear higher costs. (2) Time lag — benefits of emission cuts (lake and forest recovery) take decades; immediate costs fall on industry now. (3) International coordination — without binding global agreements, countries free-ride (emit freely while neighbours pay costs). (4) Multiple pollutants — solving SO₂ without addressing NO₂ is partial; and CO₂ (ocean acidification) requires even deeper energy system transformation. Europe’s partial success (75% SO₂ reduction) shows that international chemistry-based environmental agreements can work, but require sustained political will over decades.

Evaluate the claim: "Since limestone naturally neutralises acid rain, human intervention is unnecessary." Use chemical and ecological evidence to support or refute this. (MYP Criterion D)Hard

The claim is refuted on multiple grounds. (1) Rate mismatch: natural limestone dissolution is slow; acid deposition from industrial emissions can overwhelm the natural buffering rate, causing pH to drop before equilibration. (2) Geographic inequity: large areas (Scandinavia, eastern Canada) sit on granite with negligible natural buffering — no limestone available. Human liming is the only intervention. (3) Scale: industrial-era SO₂ emissions (60+ million t/year peak) far exceed natural volcanic inputs (25 million t/year globally), creating a non-natural acid load. Evidence: pre-industrial lake pH values (from diatom records in lake sediments) were near neutral in granite regions — acid damage is recent and directly traceable to industrialisation.
