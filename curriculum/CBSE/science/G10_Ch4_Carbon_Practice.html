Set A Carbon Bonding — Easy

What is the valency of carbon?Easy
Show Answer

Carbon has a valency of 4 — it forms 4 covalent bonds to complete its outer shell of 8 electrons.
Define catenation.Easy
Show Answer

Catenation is the unique ability of carbon to form covalent bonds with other carbon atoms, creating long chains, branched chains, and rings.
Why does carbon form covalent bonds rather than ionic bonds?Easy
Show Answer

Carbon needs 4 electrons to complete its shell. Gaining or losing 4 electrons requires too much energy, so it shares electrons through covalent bonds instead.
What type of bond holds two carbon atoms together in ethane?Easy
Show Answer

A single covalent bond (C–C) holds the two carbon atoms together in ethane (C₂H₆).
Name two properties of carbon that make millions of organic compounds possible.Easy
Show Answer

Tetravalency (4 bonds) and catenation (C–C chains/rings) together allow carbon to form an enormous variety of compounds.
What is an organic compound? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

An organic compound is any compound primarily made of carbon and hydrogen. Example: methane (CH₄).
How many bonds does carbon form in a CO₂ molecule?Easy
Show Answer

Carbon forms two double bonds (O=C=O), using all 4 of its bonding electrons — two with each oxygen atom.
Give the molecular formula of methane and draw its structural formula description.Easy
Show Answer

Methane = CH₄. Structural: one carbon atom at the centre with 4 hydrogen atoms bonded around it in a tetrahedral arrangement.

Set B Carbon Bonding — Medium

Explain why carbon's tetravalency allows for multiple bond types.Medium
Show Answer

With 4 valence electrons carbon can form single (C–C), double (C=C), or triple (C≡C) bonds. This versatility multiplies the possible molecular structures dramatically.
Distinguish between a chain, branched, and ring carbon structure.Medium
Show Answer

Chain: carbons linked in a straight line (e.g., butane). Branched: a side carbon chain attached to the main chain (e.g., isobutane). Ring: carbons form a closed loop (e.g., cyclohexane).
What is a covalent bond? How is it different from an ionic bond?Medium
Show Answer

A covalent bond involves sharing of electron pairs between atoms. An ionic bond involves transfer of electrons creating ions. Carbon compounds are covalent because sharing is energetically favourable for carbon.
Why is carbon considered the basis of all life forms?Medium
Show Answer

Carbon forms the backbone of proteins, DNA, carbohydrates, and fats — all biological molecules. Its tetravalency and catenation allow the complex, stable structures life requires.
What is an isomer? Give an example using C₄H₁₀.Medium
Show Answer

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements. C₄H₁₀: n-butane (straight chain) and isobutane (branched) are isomers.
How does electronegativity of carbon affect bonding?Medium
Show Answer

Carbon has moderate electronegativity (2.5). It bonds nearly equally with H and forms polar bonds with O or N, allowing diverse functional groups and chemical reactivity in organic molecules.
Explain why ethane (C₂H₆) and ethene (C₂H₄) have different properties despite similar formulas.Medium
Show Answer

Ethane has a C–C single bond (saturated, less reactive). Ethene has a C=C double bond (unsaturated, more reactive — undergoes addition reactions). Bond type changes chemical behaviour.
Write the structural formula of ethanol and identify the type of bond between C–O.Medium
Show Answer

Ethanol: CH₃–CH₂–OH. The C–O bond is a polar covalent bond because oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, creating a partial negative charge on oxygen.

Set C Carbon Bonding — Hard

Justify: "Without catenation, the diversity of life would be impossible."Hard
Show Answer

Life's molecules (DNA, proteins, lipids) require long, stable carbon chains and rings. Without catenation, carbon could only form simple molecules like CO₂. The C–C bond is uniquely strong and stable, enabling chains of thousands of atoms that no other element can replicate at the same scale.
Compare catenation in carbon vs. silicon. Why is silicon-based life unlikely?Hard
Show Answer

Carbon forms stable C–C chains; Si–Si bonds are much weaker and Si–O bonds (silicates) are preferred, forming rigid solids. Silicon dioxide is a solid, unlike gaseous CO₂, making gas exchange and metabolism impossible. Carbon's bond strength + versatility make it uniquely suitable for life.
How does the concept of orbital hybridisation explain carbon's tetravalency?Hard
Show Answer

Carbon's ground state is 2s²2p². During bonding, one 2s electron is promoted to 2p, giving 4 unpaired electrons. These orbitals then undergo sp³ hybridisation, creating 4 equivalent hybrid orbitals arranged tetrahedrally, each capable of forming one bond — hence exactly 4 bonds.
Predict the number of structural isomers of C₅H₁₂ and name them.Hard
Show Answer

C₅H₁₂ has 3 isomers: (1) n-pentane — straight chain; (2) isopentane (2-methylbutane) — one methyl branch; (3) neopentane (2,2-dimethylpropane) — two methyl branches on the central carbon.
A molecule has formula C₃H₄. Determine its degree of unsaturation and possible structures.Hard
Show Answer

Degree of unsaturation = (2×3+2−4)/2 = 2. Possible structures: (1) propyne CH≡C–CH₃ (one triple bond = 2 degrees); (2) propadiene CH₂=C=CH₂ (two double bonds = 2 degrees); (3) cyclopropene (one ring + one double bond).
Explain how bond polarity in C–H vs C–O bonds affects the solubility of organic molecules in water.Hard
Show Answer

C–H bonds are nearly nonpolar → hydrocarbons are hydrophobic (insoluble in water). C–O bonds are polar → –OH groups form hydrogen bonds with water, making alcohols soluble. Longer carbon chains reduce solubility by dominating with nonpolar character.
A student claims benzene (C₆H₆) is a simple alkene with 3 double bonds. Evaluate this claim.Hard
Show Answer

The claim is partially incorrect. Benzene has a ring of 6 carbons with electrons delocalised across all 6 (resonance structure), not 3 fixed double bonds. Evidence: all C–C bonds are equal length (intermediate between single and double), and benzene undergoes substitution (not addition) reactions unlike alkenes.
Using VSEPR theory, predict the bond angle in methane and explain why it is tetrahedral.Hard
Show Answer

Methane has 4 bonding pairs and 0 lone pairs. VSEPR: electron pairs repel equally → arrange in a tetrahedral geometry with bond angles of 109.5°. All 4 C–H bonds are equivalent, and the geometry minimises repulsion between electron pairs.

Set A Allotropes of Carbon — Easy

What is an allotrope?Easy
Show Answer

An allotrope is a different physical form of the same element, having the same atoms but arranged in different structures. Example: diamond and graphite are both pure carbon.
Name three allotropes of carbon.Easy
Show Answer

The three main allotropes are: Diamond, Graphite, and Fullerene (C₆₀).
Which allotrope of carbon is the hardest natural substance?Easy
Show Answer

Diamond is the hardest natural substance. Each carbon is bonded to 4 others in a rigid tetrahedral network, making it extremely hard.
Why is graphite a good conductor of electricity?Easy
Show Answer

In graphite, each carbon uses only 3 of its 4 valence electrons for bonding. The fourth electron is free to move between layers, conducting electricity.
What is the shape of a fullerene (C₆₀) molecule?Easy
Show Answer

Fullerene C₆₀ (Buckminsterfullerene) is shaped like a soccer ball — a hollow sphere made of 20 hexagons and 12 pentagons of carbon atoms.
Why is graphite used as a lubricant?Easy
Show Answer

Graphite has layered structure. The layers are held by weak van der Waals forces and can slide over each other easily, making graphite a good dry lubricant.
Does diamond conduct electricity? Give a reason.Easy
Show Answer

No. In diamond, all 4 valence electrons of each carbon are used in strong covalent bonds. There are no free electrons available to carry electric current.
State one use each of diamond and graphite.Easy
Show Answer

Diamond: cutting tools (drill bits, glass cutters) due to hardness. Graphite: pencil lead (writing) and electrodes in electrolysis due to conductivity.

Set B Allotropes of Carbon — Medium

Compare the bonding in diamond and graphite in terms of bond number and structure.Medium
Show Answer

Diamond: each C forms 4 covalent bonds → 3D tetrahedral network, very hard, no free electrons. Graphite: each C forms 3 bonds → flat hexagonal layers with one free electron per carbon; layers held by weak forces.
Explain why diamond is hard but graphite is soft, even though both are pure carbon.Medium
Show Answer

Diamond: all 4 bonds used → rigid 3D network, no cleavage plane. Graphite: 3 bonds in plane, layers bonded by weak van der Waals forces → layers slide easily → soft. Different bonding arrangement = vastly different physical properties.
What is graphene? How does it relate to graphite?Medium
Show Answer

Graphene is a single layer of graphite — one atom-thick sheet of carbon atoms in a hexagonal lattice. Graphite is a stack of many graphene layers held by weak intermolecular forces.
Why can fullerenes be used for drug delivery in medicine?Medium
Show Answer

Fullerenes are hollow cage molecules. Drug molecules can be encapsulated inside the C₆₀ cage and transported through the body, releasing the drug at the target site — useful in targeted cancer therapy.
What are carbon nanotubes and what makes them special?Medium
Show Answer

Carbon nanotubes are cylindrical allotropes of carbon (rolled graphene). They are stronger than steel yet extremely light, and conduct electricity. Used in nanoelectronics, composite materials, and medical devices.
How does the structure of diamond make it useful in industry?Medium
Show Answer

Diamond's 3D covalent network makes it the hardest known material (Mohs 10). Industrial diamonds coat drill bits and cutting tools for mining and machining hard materials where no other substance can cut.
Why does graphite feel greasy to the touch?Medium
Show Answer

Graphite's hexagonal layers slide easily over each other due to weak van der Waals forces between layers. This sliding causes the greasy feel and makes it useful as a lubricant.
What temperature and pressure conditions form natural diamonds?Medium
Show Answer

Natural diamonds form deep in Earth's mantle at temperatures of ~1000°C and pressures above 45,000 atmospheres. These extreme conditions force carbon atoms into the densely packed tetrahedral structure.

Set C Allotropes of Carbon — Hard

Explain how the same element (carbon) can produce an electrical conductor (graphite) and an insulator (diamond).Hard
Show Answer

Conductivity depends on free electrons. In graphite, sp² hybridisation leaves one unhybridised p-electron per carbon, forming a delocalised π-electron cloud between layers → conductor. In diamond, sp³ hybridisation uses all 4 electrons in σ-bonds → no free electrons → insulator. Same atoms, different hybridisation = opposite electrical properties.
Evaluate the claim: "Graphene will replace silicon in computer chips." Use structural evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Graphene has electron mobility ~200× greater than silicon and is atomically thin, enabling nanoscale transistors. However, graphene has zero band gap (can't switch off current like semiconductors), which is a major challenge. Current research explores modified graphene. The claim is promising but premature — engineering a band gap remains unsolved.
Why does diamond have a very high melting point while fullerene (C₆₀) has a relatively low one?Hard
Show Answer

Diamond: every atom connected by strong covalent bonds throughout → breaking the structure requires enormous energy → very high MP (~3550°C). C₆₀: discrete molecules held by weak van der Waals forces between molecules → little energy needed to separate them → low MP (~600°C). Strong intramolecular bonds ≠ high melting point if intermolecular forces are weak.
How is sp² hybridisation in graphite related to its ability to absorb light and appear black?Hard
Show Answer

sp² hybridisation leaves a delocalised π-electron system in graphite. These π-electrons can absorb photons across a wide range of visible wavelengths (the band gap is essentially zero in extended graphite layers). Since nearly all visible light is absorbed and very little reflected, graphite appears black/opaque.

Why is diamond thermodynamically less stable than graphite at standard conditions, yet it persists for millions of years?Hard
Show Answer

Graphite is the thermodynamically stable form at standard pressure (ΔG of diamond → graphite is negative). However, the conversion requires breaking many strong C–C bonds, creating an enormous kinetic barrier. At room temperature and pressure, there is insufficient energy to overcome this activation energy — so diamond is kinetically stable despite being thermodynamically metastable.
Design an experiment to distinguish between diamond and graphite using physical and chemical tests.Hard
Show Answer

Physical: (1) Hardness test — diamond scratches quartz; graphite does not. (2) Electrical conductivity — connect to circuit; graphite conducts, diamond does not. (3) Lustre — diamond refracts light brilliantly; graphite has metallic sheen. Chemical: (4) Burn in O₂ — both yield CO₂ (confirming carbon), but diamond requires much higher temperature (~800°C) than graphite (~700°C) due to stronger bonding.
Calculate the number of carbon atoms in 1.2 g of diamond (C = 12 g/mol; Nₐ = 6×10²³).Hard
Show Answer

Moles of C = 1.2/12 = 0.1 mol. Number of atoms = 0.1 × 6×10²³ = 6×10²² carbon atoms. Each is bonded to 4 others in the diamond lattice.
How do carbon nanotubes combine properties of both diamond (strength) and graphite (conductivity)?Hard
Show Answer

Nanotubes are rolled graphene sheets. The sp² bonds along the tube axis provide exceptional tensile strength (like the in-plane bonds of diamond in that axis). The delocalised π-electrons along the tube walls give electrical conductivity (like graphite). Chirality (rolling angle) determines whether a nanotube is metallic or semiconducting, making them uniquely tunable.

Set A Hydrocarbons — Easy

What is a hydrocarbon?Easy
Show Answer

A hydrocarbon is an organic compound made up of only carbon and hydrogen atoms. Examples: methane, ethene, ethyne.
What is the general formula of alkanes?Easy
Show Answer

Alkanes follow the general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Example: methane CH₄ (n=1), ethane C₂H₆ (n=2).
What type of bond do alkenes contain?Easy
Show Answer

Alkenes contain a carbon–carbon double bond (C=C). They are unsaturated hydrocarbons with general formula CₙH₂ₙ.
Give the molecular formula and name of the simplest alkyne.Easy
Show Answer

The simplest alkyne is ethyne (acetylene), molecular formula C₂H₂, with a C≡C triple bond.
What does "saturated" mean in the context of hydrocarbons?Easy
Show Answer

A saturated hydrocarbon contains only single C–C bonds and is "saturated" with hydrogen — it cannot add more hydrogen atoms. All alkanes are saturated.
Name the first four members of the alkane series.Easy
Show Answer

Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆), Propane (C₃H₈), Butane (C₄H₁₀).
What test is used to identify an unsaturated hydrocarbon?Easy
Show Answer

The bromine water test: an unsaturated compound decolourises orange bromine water (Br₂ adds across the double/triple bond). Saturated hydrocarbons do not decolourise it.
Write the structural formula of propene.Easy
Show Answer

Propene: CH₂=CH–CH₃. It has 3 carbon atoms and a double bond between C1 and C2, with a methyl group on C3.

Set B Hydrocarbons — Medium

Explain the difference between substitution and addition reactions with examples.Medium
Show Answer

Substitution (alkanes): H is replaced by another atom. CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl. Addition (alkenes): atoms add across the double bond. CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ → CH₂Br–CH₂Br. Addition is possible only when a multiple bond is present.
Why are alkanes less reactive than alkenes?Medium
Show Answer

Alkanes have only strong σ (sigma) single bonds, which require high energy to break. Alkenes have a π (pi) bond in addition to a σ-bond; the π-bond is weaker and more reactive, readily undergoing addition reactions.
What is a homologous series? Identify three properties it follows.Medium
Show Answer

A homologous series is a group of organic compounds with the same general formula and similar chemical properties. Properties: (1) consecutive members differ by CH₂, (2) gradual change in physical properties with carbon number, (3) same functional group → similar reactions.
Write the equation for combustion of propane (C₃H₈).Medium
Show Answer

Complete combustion: C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O. Incomplete combustion (limited O₂) produces carbon monoxide (CO) or soot (C) instead of CO₂.
How does hydrogenation of vegetable oil work, and why is it done?Medium
Show Answer

Vegetable oils contain C=C double bonds. Hydrogenation adds H₂ across these bonds (Ni catalyst, ~200°C), converting liquid oils to solid fats (margarine). Done to increase shelf life, change texture, and raise melting point.
Distinguish between ethene and ethyne using structural formulae and bond type.Medium
Show Answer

Ethene (C₂H₄): H₂C=CH₂ — one C=C double bond (1σ + 1π bond). Ethyne (C₂H₂): HC≡CH — one C≡C triple bond (1σ + 2π bonds). Ethyne is more unsaturated and more reactive than ethene.
Why do boiling points of alkanes increase with chain length?Medium
Show Answer

Longer chains have greater surface area → stronger van der Waals (London dispersion) forces between molecules → more energy required to separate them → higher boiling point. Methane (BP −161°C) vs. octane (BP 126°C).
Name the product formed when ethene reacts with water (hydration reaction).Medium
Show Answer

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → CH₃CH₂OH (ethanol). This is an addition reaction (hydration) of ethene with steam, using a phosphoric acid catalyst at high temperature and pressure.

Set C Hydrocarbons — Hard

Predict the product of: CH₃–CH=CH₂ + HBr. Which carbon does Br attach to? (Markovnikov's rule)Hard
Show Answer

By Markovnikov's rule, H adds to the carbon with more H atoms (C1) and Br adds to the carbon with fewer H atoms (C2). Product: CH₃–CHBr–CH₃ (2-bromopropane) — the major product. The rule reflects carbocation stability: secondary carbocation at C2 is more stable.
A hydrocarbon with formula C₄H₆ undergoes addition with excess Br₂. How many moles of Br₂ are consumed per mole of hydrocarbon?Hard
Show Answer

Degree of unsaturation = (2×4+2−6)/2 = 2. Two degrees could be: one triple bond (2 Br₂ consumed) or two double bonds (2 Br₂ consumed). Either way, 2 moles of Br₂ react per mole of C₄H₆ via addition across each π bond.
Explain why coal gas (containing ethene and other alkenes) has higher calorific value than methane.Hard
Show Answer

Calorific value depends on energy released per gram. Longer chain and unsaturated hydrocarbons in coal gas have more C–H and C–C bonds per molecule, releasing more energy on combustion. However, methane has a very high calorific value per gram (~55 MJ/kg) due to its high H:C ratio. Mixed coal gas is actually lower — the question tests critical evaluation. The key factor is H:C ratio and degree of oxidation.
Compare the reactivity of methane with chlorine in UV light vs. darkness. Explain the mechanism type.Hard
Show Answer

In UV light: reaction proceeds rapidly — UV breaks Cl–Cl bond homolytically, producing Cl• radicals. These react with CH₄ via a free radical chain mechanism (initiation→propagation→termination). In darkness: no reaction occurs — insufficient energy to generate radicals. This demonstrates that alkane halogenation is radical-driven, not ionic.
Fractional distillation separates crude oil into fractions. Why do shorter-chain hydrocarbons come off first?Hard
Show Answer

Shorter chains have lower molecular mass → weaker van der Waals forces → lower boiling points. In the fractionating column, temperature decreases from bottom to top. Short-chain fractions (e.g., gasoline C₅–C₁₂) condense higher up; long-chain fractions (e.g., bitumen C₃₅+) remain liquid at the bottom. This exploits the direct relationship between chain length and boiling point.
Write balanced equations for complete and incomplete combustion of butane (C₄H₁₀), and explain why incomplete combustion is dangerous.Hard
Show Answer

Complete: 2C₄H₁₀ + 13O₂ → 8CO₂ + 10H₂O. Incomplete (limited O₂): 2C₄H₁₀ + 9O₂ → 8CO + 10H₂O (or soot C instead of CO). Danger: CO is colourless, odourless, and binds haemoglobin ~200× more strongly than O₂, causing carbon monoxide poisoning and death.
Why is ethyne (acetylene) used in welding torches despite being a simple hydrocarbon?Hard
Show Answer

Ethyne has a very high heat of combustion (~1300 kJ/mol). When burned with pure oxygen (oxy-acetylene torch), the flame reaches ~3500°C — hot enough to melt steel. Its triple bond stores considerable energy, and the high H:C ratio produces a very hot, clean flame.
Design an experiment to identify whether an unknown gas is methane, ethene, or ethyne using two chemical tests.Hard
Show Answer

Test 1 — Bromine water: Bubble gas through orange Br₂(aq). Methane: no colour change (saturated). Ethene/ethyne: decolourises (unsaturated). Test 2 — Ammoniacal AgNO₃ (Tollens' for alkynes): Bubble gas through AgNO₃/NH₃. Ethyne produces a white/cream precipitate of silver acetylide (Ag–C≡C–Ag). Ethene does not. Conclusion: no decolourisation = methane; decolourises but no precipitate = ethene; decolourises and precipitates = ethyne.

Set A Functional Groups — Easy

What is a functional group?Easy
Show Answer

A functional group is a specific atom or group of atoms in a molecule that determines its chemical properties and reactions. It is the reactive site of the molecule.
Name the functional group present in ethanol and write its formula.Easy
Show Answer

Ethanol contains the hydroxyl group (–OH). It is an alcohol. Formula: CH₃CH₂OH.
What functional group is found in acetic acid (vinegar)?Easy
Show Answer

Acetic acid (ethanoic acid) contains the carboxyl group (–COOH). Formula: CH₃–COOH.
Give one example each of an alcohol, an aldehyde, and a carboxylic acid.Easy
Show Answer

Alcohol: Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH). Aldehyde: Methanal/Formaldehyde (HCHO). Carboxylic acid: Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH).
Write the functional group of a halide compound.Easy
Show Answer

Halide (haloalkane) compounds contain a halogen functional group (–X) where X = F, Cl, Br, or I. Example: chloromethane CH₃Cl.
What is the functional group of an amine? Give an example.Easy
Show Answer

Amines contain the amino group (–NH₂). Example: methylamine (CH₃NH₂) or amino acids which contain –NH₂ and –COOH groups.
Name the product when an alcohol reacts with a carboxylic acid.Easy
Show Answer

When an alcohol reacts with a carboxylic acid (esterification), an ester and water are formed. Example: CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH → CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O.
What is oxidation of ethanol? What product is formed?Easy
Show Answer

Oxidation of ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) using an oxidising agent like acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ gives ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH). The –OH group is oxidised to –COOH.

Set B Functional Groups — Medium

Explain why ethanol is miscible with water but hexanol is not.Medium
Show Answer

Ethanol's –OH group forms hydrogen bonds with water, and its short carbon chain (C₂) doesn't overpower this. Hexanol has a long nonpolar C₆ chain that dominates, making the molecule hydrophobic overall — the –OH group is insufficient to make it water-miscible.
What is denatured alcohol and why is it dangerous to drink?Medium
Show Answer

Denatured alcohol is ethanol with added toxic substances (usually methanol). Methanol (CH₃OH) is metabolised to formaldehyde and formic acid in the body, causing blindness, organ failure, and death. It is added to industrial alcohol to prevent consumption.
Write the equation for esterification of ethanol with ethanoic acid. Name the ester formed.Medium
Show Answer

CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O (conc. H₂SO₄ catalyst). The ester formed is ethyl ethanoate (ethyl acetate), which smells of pear drops/nail polish remover.
How do you convert ethanoic acid back to ethanol? (Reduction reaction)Medium
Show Answer

Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) can be reduced using LiAlH₄ (lithium aluminium hydride) or by catalytic hydrogenation to give ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH). The –COOH group is reduced back to –CH₂OH.
Why do carboxylic acids have higher boiling points than alcohols of similar molecular mass?Medium
Show Answer

Carboxylic acids form dimers — two molecules linked by two hydrogen bonds (via –COOH groups). This effectively doubles the molecular mass in the gas phase, requiring more energy to vaporise → significantly higher boiling point than an alcohol of similar actual mass.
Write the structural formula of glycine (simplest amino acid) and identify its two functional groups.Medium
Show Answer

Glycine: H₂N–CH₂–COOH. Two functional groups: amino group (–NH₂) — basic, and carboxyl group (–COOH) — acidic. This makes glycine amphoteric (can act as acid or base).
How can you distinguish between an alcohol and a carboxylic acid using litmus paper?Medium
Show Answer

Dissolve each in water and test with blue litmus paper. Carboxylic acid: turns blue litmus red (acidic; –COOH donates H⁺). Alcohol: no change (neutral; –OH does not ionise significantly in water).
Why are haloalkanes important as solvents and intermediates in synthesis?Medium
Show Answer

Haloalkanes are polar molecules (C–X bond is polar due to high electronegativity of halogen) that dissolve many organic substances. The halogen is a good leaving group, making them excellent intermediates for nucleophilic substitution — replacing –X with –OH, –NH₂, –CN, etc. in synthesis.

Set C Functional Groups — Hard

A compound with formula C₃H₆O₂ could be a carboxylic acid or an ester. Propose a test to distinguish them.Hard
Show Answer

Carboxylic acid (propanoic acid, CH₃CH₂COOH): (1) Turns blue litmus red; (2) Reacts with NaHCO₃ to release CO₂ gas (effervescence). Ester (methyl ethanoate, CH₃COOCH₃): neither test gives a positive result — esters are neutral and do not react with NaHCO₃. The NaHCO₃ test is definitive since even strong organic acids effervesce.
Explain the mechanism of nucleophilic substitution in chloromethane reacting with NaOH.Hard
Show Answer

OH⁻ is a nucleophile (electron donor). It attacks the electrophilic carbon in CH₃Cl (carbon is δ+ due to C–Cl polarity). In SN2 mechanism: OH⁻ approaches from the back, forms a transition state, and Cl⁻ departs as a leaving group. Product: CH₃OH + Cl⁻. Reaction is concerted (one step), with inversion of configuration.
Why is the –COOH group acidic? Explain in terms of electron delocalisation.Hard
Show Answer

In –COOH, the O–H proton is acidic. When –OH loses H⁺, the carboxylate anion (–COO⁻) is formed. The negative charge is delocalised equally over both oxygens via resonance, stabilising the anion. This stabilisation lowers the energy of the deprotonated form, making proton donation (acidic behaviour) thermodynamically favourable.
Compare the reactivity of primary, secondary, and tertiary alcohols towards oxidation.Hard
Show Answer

Primary (e.g., ethanol): oxidised to aldehyde, then to carboxylic acid (with excess oxidant). Secondary (e.g., propan-2-ol): oxidised to ketone only (no further oxidation — ketones resist). Tertiary (e.g., 2-methylpropan-2-ol): no oxidation under normal conditions (no H on the carbon bearing –OH). Reactivity order: 1° > 2° >> 3°.
An amino acid is amphoteric. Draw the zwitterion form of alanine (CH₃CH(NH₂)COOH) and explain its formation.Hard
Show Answer

In a zwitterion, the –COOH donates a proton internally to –NH₂: –COOH + –NH₂ → –COO⁻ + –NH₃⁺. Alanine zwitterion: CH₃–CH(NH₃⁺)–COO⁻. The molecule is overall neutral but has both positive and negative charges. This form predominates at physiological pH (~7). Zwitterions have high melting points and are water-soluble.
How does the presence of the –OH group explain why ethanol has a much higher boiling point (78°C) than ethane (−89°C) despite similar molecular masses?Hard
Show Answer

Ethanol (MW 46) forms intermolecular hydrogen bonds via the –OH group (O–H···O). These bonds (~20 kJ/mol each) are much stronger than van der Waals forces in ethane (MW 30). Even with slightly higher MW, ethane cannot hydrogen-bond → BP −89°C. Ethanol's H-bonding requires much more energy to overcome → BP 78°C. A 167°C difference explained by one –OH group.
Predict and explain the relative acid strengths: CH₃COOH vs. CCl₃COOH (trichloroacetic acid).Hard
Show Answer

CCl₃COOH is much stronger (pKₐ ≈ 0.66 vs. 4.76 for CH₃COOH). Cl atoms are strongly electronegative and withdraw electron density from the –COOH group via the inductive effect. This stabilises the –COO⁻ anion even more, making proton donation easier. Three Cl atoms amplify this effect. General rule: electron-withdrawing groups near –COOH increase acidity.
Explain why DDT (a chlorinated organic compound) persists in ecosystems and bioaccumulates in food chains.Hard
Show Answer

DDT is highly nonpolar (large chlorinated hydrocarbon structure) → hydrophobic → does not dissolve in water, resists biological degradation. It is lipophilic (dissolves in fats), accumulating in fatty tissues. Each trophic level consumes and concentrates DDT from many prey organisms → biomagnification. Top predators (eagles, humans) receive lethal concentrations even when environmental levels are low.

Set A Soaps and Detergents — Easy

What is saponification?Easy
Show Answer

Saponification is the process of making soap by reacting a fat or oil (triglyceride) with a strong base (NaOH). Equation: Fat + NaOH → Soap + Glycerol.
What are the two parts of a soap molecule?Easy
Show Answer

A soap molecule has: (1) a long hydrophobic (water-repelling) carbon chain (tail) that dissolves in oils, and (2) a hydrophilic (water-attracting) ionic head (–COO⁻Na⁺) that dissolves in water.
What is a micelle?Easy
Show Answer

A micelle is a spherical cluster of soap molecules in water. The hydrophobic tails point inward (surrounding the oil droplet) and the hydrophilic heads point outward into the water, allowing oil to be suspended and washed away.
Why does soap not work well in hard water?Easy
Show Answer

Hard water contains Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions. These react with soap (sodium stearate) to form insoluble scum (calcium stearate) that precipitates out of water, wasting soap and leaving a deposit.
What is the key advantage of synthetic detergents over soaps?Easy
Show Answer

Synthetic detergents (sulphonate-based) do not form scum in hard water because their calcium and magnesium salts are soluble. They work effectively in hard, cold, or acidic water conditions where soaps fail.
Name the raw materials used to make soap by saponification.Easy
Show Answer

Soap requires: (1) a fat or vegetable oil (e.g., coconut oil, palm oil, tallow) and (2) a strong alkali — NaOH (caustic soda) for hard soap, or KOH for soft/liquid soap.
What is the role of glycerol (glycerine) produced in saponification?Easy
Show Answer

Glycerol is a by-product of saponification. It is a moisturising agent used in cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, and food. In soap-making, it is often separated and sold separately for higher profits.
Define surface tension and explain how soap reduces it.Easy
Show Answer

Surface tension is the elastic-like force on a liquid's surface due to cohesive forces. Soap molecules embed at the water surface with their hydrophobic tails pointing up, disrupting H-bonds between water molecules → lowers surface tension, allowing water to spread and wet surfaces better.

Set B Soaps and Detergents — Medium

Describe the stepwise mechanism by which soap removes a grease stain from cloth.Medium
Show Answer

1. Soap molecules surround the grease droplet — hydrophobic tails insert into the grease, hydrophilic heads face water. 2. Micelles form around the grease, encapsulating it. 3. Agitation (scrubbing) breaks grease into smaller micelle-coated droplets. 4. Droplets are suspended in water (emulsified) and rinsed away. The grease never directly contacts water.
Compare soap and detergent molecules in terms of structure and behaviour in hard water.Medium
Show Answer

Soap: sodium/potassium salt of long-chain fatty acid (–COO⁻Na⁺). Calcium stearate = insoluble scum in hard water. Detergent: sodium alkyl sulphonate or sulphate (–OSO₃⁻Na⁺). Calcium salts remain soluble → no scum. Both have hydrophilic head + hydrophobic tail, so cleaning mechanism is identical.
What is biodegradability, and why is it a concern with synthetic detergents?Medium
Show Answer

Biodegradability: ability of a substance to be broken down by microorganisms. Early synthetic detergents had highly branched chains resistant to bacterial enzymes → accumulated in rivers, causing persistent foam. Modern detergents use linear chains that are biodegradable. The concern is eutrophication from phosphate builder compounds, not the surfactant itself.
Write the saponification reaction of glyceryl tristearate (tristearin) with NaOH.Medium
Show Answer

C₃H₅(OOCC₁₇H₃₅)₃ + 3NaOH → 3 C₁₇H₃₅COONa (sodium stearate/soap) + C₃H₅(OH)₃ (glycerol). Three ester bonds are hydrolysed, releasing three soap molecules and one glycerol molecule.
How do anionic, cationic, and non-ionic detergents differ? Give a use for each.Medium
Show Answer

Anionic: negatively charged head (e.g., sodium lauryl sulphate) — used in shampoos, laundry. Cationic: positively charged head (e.g., quaternary ammonium) — used as disinfectants, fabric softeners. Non-ionic: no charge (polyethylene glycol-based) — used in dishwashing liquid, works in hard water, gentle on fabrics.
Explain how soap kills viruses like SARS-CoV-2.Medium
Show Answer

Viruses like SARS-CoV-2 have a lipid envelope. Soap's hydrophobic tails dissolve into and disrupt this lipid membrane, physically destroying the virus. The soap also competes with the virus's surface proteins for skin binding sites. Rinsing carries away the disrupted viral particles. This is more effective than alcohol for non-enveloped viruses on porous surfaces.
Why is hard soap (NaOH-based) solid while soft soap (KOH-based) is liquid/paste?Medium
Show Answer

NaOH produces sodium salts of fatty acids (e.g., sodium stearate) — smaller Na⁺ ions allow tighter ionic packing → solid at room temperature. KOH produces potassium salts — larger K⁺ ions disrupt close packing, lowering the melting point → liquid or soft paste. K-soaps are used in shaving creams and industrial cleaners.
What is the HLB (hydrophilic-lipophilic balance) and why does it matter in detergent design?Medium
Show Answer

HLB is a scale (0–20) measuring a surfactant's preference for water vs. oil. Low HLB (3–6): oil-in-water emulsification favoured → antifoam agents. Medium HLB (8–13): good detergents/emulsifiers for o/w emulsions. High HLB (>13): solubilisers. Matching HLB to the application maximises cleaning efficiency and avoids phase separation.

Set C Soaps and Detergents — Hard

A student uses hard water and soap for laundry. Suggest a chemical method to soften the water and explain the chemistry.Hard
Show Answer

Add washing soda (Na₂CO₃): CO₃²⁻ + Ca²⁺ → CaCO₃↓ (insoluble). The Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ ions that cause hardness are precipitated out, leaving softened water. Alternatively, use an ion exchange resin (zeolite) that swaps Na⁺ for Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺. Boiling removes temporary hardness (HCO₃⁻ salts) but not permanent hardness (sulphate/chloride salts).
Analyse the environmental impact of phosphate builders in detergents on freshwater ecosystems.Hard
Show Answer

Phosphates act as fertilisers for aquatic plants/algae. Excess phosphates from detergent runoff cause eutrophication: algal blooms → block sunlight → aquatic plants die → bacterial decomposition depletes dissolved oxygen → hypoxia kills fish and invertebrates (dead zones). This is why phosphate-free detergents are now mandated in many countries (EU, USA). Zeolites replaced phosphates as water softeners in formulations.
Why do micelles form above a critical concentration (CMC) but not below it?Hard
Show Answer

Below the CMC (Critical Micelle Concentration), the hydrophobic tails are satisfied by adsorbing at the air-water interface (lowering surface tension). Once the surface is saturated, additional soap molecules cannot escape water contact → it becomes thermodynamically favourable to aggregate with tails inward (minimising contact with water) → micelles form spontaneously. Below CMC: surface adsorption preferred. Above CMC: micelle formation preferred. This is driven by the hydrophobic effect (entropy gain from releasing structured water around nonpolar tails).
Calculate the mass of soap produced from 890 g of glyceryl trioleate (MW = 884 g/mol) reacting with excess NaOH. (Sodium oleate MW = 304 g/mol)Hard
Show Answer

Moles of trioleate = 890/884 = 1.007 mol. Each mole produces 3 moles of soap. Moles of soap = 1.007 × 3 = 3.021 mol. Mass = 3.021 × 304 = 918 g of sodium oleate (soap). Plus glycerol by-product: 1.007 × 92 = ~93 g.
Explain why soaps are basic in solution and could irritate skin, whereas synthetic detergents are pH-neutral.Hard
Show Answer

Soap is the salt of a weak acid (fatty acid) and a strong base (NaOH). In water, the carboxylate ion hydrolyses: RCOO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ RCOOH + OH⁻, making the solution basic (pH 9–10). This alkalinity can disrupt the skin's acid mantle (pH ~5.5), causing dryness. Synthetic detergents use sulphonate/sulphate groups (strong acid salts) → no hydrolysis → neutral pH → gentler on skin.
Design an experiment to determine the CMC (Critical Micelle Concentration) of a detergent using surface tension measurements.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Prepare a series of detergent solutions (e.g., 0.001–10 mM). Measure surface tension of each using a tensiometer (or by calibrating drop weight with a burette). Results: Surface tension decreases sharply as concentration increases (surfactant adsorbs at surface). At CMC, surface tension levels off — no further decrease as micelles form instead. CMC = the concentration at the breakpoint in the surface tension vs. log[concentration] graph. Controls: use distilled water; maintain constant temperature (surface tension is temperature-dependent).
Evaluate the sustainability of bio-based vs. petrochemical-based surfactants in detergent manufacturing.Hard
Show Answer

Bio-based (from coconut/palm oil): renewable, biodegradable, lower carbon footprint. Concerns: deforestation for palm oil, land-use change emissions. Petrochemical: fossil-derived, non-renewable, some resist biodegradation, but supply chain is well-established and cheaper. MYP global context: Life sciences shows bio-based surfactants have 40–60% lower lifecycle CO₂, but the palm oil supply chain creates biodiversity loss. Ideal: certified sustainable bio-based + linear chain design for full biodegradability.
How does the structure of a biosurfactant (e.g., rhamnolipid) compare to synthetic surfactants, and what are its advantages?Hard
Show Answer

Rhamnolipids: produced by bacteria (Pseudomonas aeruginosa). Structure: rhamnose (sugar head = hydrophilic) + 3-hydroxy fatty acid (hydrophobic tail). Advantages: (1) produced from renewable/waste substrates; (2) fully biodegradable; (3) low toxicity; (4) effective at low concentrations; (5) antimicrobial properties. Disadvantages: high production cost vs. petrochemical surfactants. Growing use in bioremediation of oil spills and eco-friendly cosmetics.

Set A Carbon Compounds and Environment — Easy

What is biogas? What is its main component?Easy
Show Answer

Biogas is a renewable fuel produced by the anaerobic (without oxygen) decomposition of organic waste by microorganisms. Its main component is methane (CH₄), typically 55–70% of the mixture.
What does "anaerobic digestion" mean?Easy
Show Answer

Anaerobic digestion is the breakdown of organic matter (food waste, animal dung, sewage) by bacteria in the absence of oxygen, producing biogas (CH₄ + CO₂) and digestate (nutrient-rich residue used as fertiliser).
Why is plastic pollution a major environmental crisis?Easy
Show Answer

Plastic is made of long-chain synthetic polymers that do not biodegrade for hundreds of years. It accumulates in landfills, oceans, and ecosystems, harming wildlife through ingestion and entanglement, and polluting food chains as microplastics.
What is the greenhouse effect, and which carbon compound drives it most?Easy
Show Answer

The greenhouse effect is the trapping of solar heat by atmospheric gases. Carbon dioxide (CO₂) from burning fossil fuels is the primary driver of anthropogenic climate change, though methane (CH₄) is ~80× more potent per molecule over 20 years.
Name one advantage of biogas over coal as an energy source.Easy
Show Answer

Biogas is renewable (made continuously from waste), while coal is a finite fossil fuel. Burning biogas releases CO₂ that was recently captured by organisms → near-carbon-neutral cycle. Coal releases ancient carbon stored for millions of years, increasing atmospheric CO₂ net.
What are microplastics and why are they dangerous?Easy
Show Answer

Microplastics are plastic fragments smaller than 5 mm, formed by degradation of larger plastic items. Dangerous because: (1) ingested by marine organisms entering food chains; (2) carry toxic chemicals (PCBs, BPA); (3) found in human blood, lungs, and placenta; (4) nearly impossible to remove from ecosystems once dispersed.
What is the carbon cycle?Easy
Show Answer

The carbon cycle is the movement of carbon through the biosphere, atmosphere, hydrosphere, and lithosphere. Key processes: photosynthesis (CO₂ absorbed), respiration/combustion (CO₂ released), decomposition, ocean absorption, and long-term burial as fossil fuels/limestone.
Give one example of a biodegradable and one non-biodegradable carbon compound.Easy
Show Answer

Biodegradable: glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) — broken down by microbes within days. Non-biodegradable: polyethylene (–CH₂–CH₂–)ₙ — plastic that persists for 500+ years in the environment.

Set B Carbon Compounds and Environment — Medium

Compare the environmental impact of burning biogas vs. burning fossil fuels.Medium
Show Answer

Biogas: CO₂ released was recently fixed by photosynthesis → net neutral carbon balance. Prevents more potent CH₄ from entering atmosphere directly (biogas captures it). Fossil fuels: release carbon sequestered millions of years ago → net addition to atmospheric CO₂ → amplifies greenhouse effect. Biogas also reduces landfill waste and methane emissions from decomposition.
What is the difference between thermoplastics and thermosetting plastics? Give an example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Thermoplastics (e.g., polyethylene/PET): soften on heating, can be remoulded and recycled — polymer chains are not cross-linked. Thermosetting plastics (e.g., Bakelite, epoxy): harden permanently when first heated — cross-linked 3D network cannot be remelted. Thermosets are harder, heat-resistant but not recyclable.
How does burning PVC plastic produce toxic gases? Name the gas.Medium
Show Answer

PVC (polyvinyl chloride) contains chlorine. Burning PVC produces hydrogen chloride (HCl) gas and potentially dioxins — highly toxic persistent organic pollutants. HCl is corrosive to lungs. This is why open burning of PVC waste is extremely dangerous and banned in many countries.
Explain the process of carbon sequestration and its role in combating climate change.Medium
Show Answer

Carbon sequestration: capturing atmospheric CO₂ and storing it to prevent it warming the climate. Natural: forests, oceans, soils. Artificial: CCS (Carbon Capture and Storage) — capturing CO₂ from power plants and injecting it into geological formations. Reforestation increases natural sequestration. Critical for achieving net-zero emissions targets.
What is acid rain and which carbon compound contributes to it?Medium
Show Answer

Acid rain forms when CO₂ dissolves in rainwater: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid, pH ~5.6). More severely, SO₂ and NOₓ from fossil fuel combustion produce H₂SO₄ and HNO₃ (pH as low as 4). Acid rain damages forests, acidifies lakes, and corrodes limestone buildings and marble statues.
Describe three ways an individual can reduce their carbon footprint.Medium
Show Answer

(1) Reduce meat consumption: livestock produce CH₄ (powerful GHG); plant-based diets have ~75% lower GHG per calorie. (2) Use public transport/EV: reduce direct fossil fuel combustion. (3) Energy efficiency: insulate homes, use LED lighting, renewable electricity → reduce CO₂ from power generation. Also: reduce, reuse, recycle plastics; avoid single-use plastics.
What is the "circular economy" concept, and how does it apply to plastics?Medium
Show Answer

A circular economy eliminates waste by keeping materials in use as long as possible: design products for longevity → reuse → recycle → recover energy → never landfill. Applied to plastics: (1) design for recyclability, (2) use bio-based/biodegradable plastics, (3) chemical recycling (pyrolysis back to monomers), (4) Extended Producer Responsibility — manufacturers responsible for end-of-life collection.
Why is ocean acidification considered a greater threat than sea-level rise for coral reefs?Medium
Show Answer

Oceans absorb ~30% of atmospheric CO₂ → forms carbonic acid → pH drops from 8.2 to ~8.0 (30% more acidic since industrial era). Reduced pH decreases carbonate ion (CO₃²⁻) concentration, which corals need to build CaCO₃ skeletons. Bleaching occurs; reefs dissolve. Sea-level rise can be adapted to (movement), but chemical changes to ocean chemistry directly destroy reef-building ability at a molecular level.

Set C Carbon Compounds and Environment — Hard

Evaluate the net carbon balance of a biogas plant processing cow dung. Is it truly carbon-neutral?Hard
Show Answer

Cows eat plants that absorbed CO₂ (recent carbon). Dung digestion → biogas CH₄. Burning CH₄ → CO₂ (returns recently-fixed carbon). Net CO₂ balance ≈ neutral for the carbon cycle. However, the plant itself requires energy to operate (pumps, construction, transport), and any CH₄ leakage from the plant is potent GHG (28× CO₂ over 100 years). Full lifecycle analysis shows biogas is ~80% lower GHG than fossil gas, but not perfectly neutral — calls for methane leak control and renewable energy use in operations.
Analyse the chemistry of why CO₂ is a greenhouse gas but N₂ and O₂ are not.Hard
Show Answer

A molecule is IR-active (greenhouse gas) only if its dipole moment changes during vibration. N₂ and O₂ are homonuclear diatomics — symmetric, no dipole change during stretching → IR inactive. CO₂, though linear and symmetric, has asymmetric stretching and bending modes that create temporary dipoles → absorbs IR radiation at specific wavelengths (15 μm) that Earth emits → reradiates heat back → greenhouse effect. Water vapour, CH₄, and N₂O are also potent for the same reason.
What is the photochemical smog reaction pathway involving unburnt hydrocarbons and NOₓ?Hard
Show Answer

1. NO₂ + UV light → NO + O• (atomic oxygen radical). 2. O• + O₂ → O₃ (ozone — ground level). 3. Unburnt hydrocarbons (VOCs) + O• → peroxy radicals (ROO•). 4. ROO• + NO → NO₂ + RO• (regenerates NO₂ → cycle continues). 5. Products accumulate → peroxyacetyl nitrates (PANs), aldehydes. Result: photochemical smog — hazy, oxidising, damages lungs, eye irritation, kills crops. Major problem in cities with heavy traffic and sunshine (e.g., Los Angeles, Delhi).
Design a biogas system for a rural Indian household with 2 cows. Calculate approximate daily methane output. (1 cow produces ~10 kg dung/day; 1 kg dung → 0.04 m³ biogas; 60% CH₄)Hard
Show Answer

Dung per day = 2 × 10 = 20 kg. Biogas = 20 × 0.04 = 0.8 m³/day. CH₄ = 0.8 × 0.60 = 0.48 m³/day ≈ 480 L CH₄/day. System design: Fixed-dome or floating drum digester (2–4 m³ capacity, ~40-day retention time), inlet pipe for slurry (dung + water 1:1), sealed anaerobic chamber, gas outlet to kitchen stove, overflow tank for digestate → fertiliser. Cost-effective, reduces firewood use (reduces deforestation), improves air quality indoors.
How does extended producer responsibility (EPR) for plastics work? Evaluate its effectiveness with evidence.Hard
Show Answer

EPR: manufacturers legally responsible for the full lifecycle of their plastic packaging — must fund/organise collection and recycling. Implemented in EU (Packaging Directive), Japan, and 60+ countries. Evidence for effectiveness: Germany's EPR system achieves ~66% plastic packaging recycling. Limitations: In low-income countries, collection infrastructure is inadequate; EPR fees can be too low to change behaviour; multinational companies lobby for weaker standards. Net result: EPR is necessary but insufficient alone — needs infrastructure investment, design standards (mono-material packaging), and reduced plastic production.
Explain carbon capture and storage (CCS) technology at a molecular level. What are its limitations?Hard
Show Answer

Capture: CO₂ is absorbed from flue gas using amine solvents (MEA: monoethanolamine). R-NH₂ + CO₂ → R-NHCOO⁻ + H⁺ (reversible). Heating releases pure CO₂. Compression: CO₂ compressed to supercritical state (~100 bar). Storage: injected into depleted oil fields, saline aquifers, or basalt formations (reacts with minerals → carbonates permanently). Limitations: (1) Energy penalty: CCS reduces plant efficiency by 15–25%; (2) Leakage risk from geological formations; (3) High cost (~$50–100/tonne CO₂); (4) Does not address CO₂ already in atmosphere; (5) Not viable for dispersed sources (vehicles). Promising for hard-to-decarbonise industries (cement, steel).
Compare the global warming potential (GWP) of methane and CO₂ over 20-year and 100-year timescales, and explain the difference.Hard
Show Answer

CH₄ GWP (20-year) = ~84; GWP (100-year) = ~28 (vs. CO₂ = 1 by definition). Reason: CH₄ is more efficient per molecule at absorbing IR (due to its vibrational modes) — but it has an atmospheric lifetime of only ~12 years (vs. centuries for CO₂). Over 20 years, its high potency dominates; over 100 years, much of it has degraded to CO₂. Policy implication: reducing methane emissions (from agriculture, fossil fuels) gives faster near-term climate benefits than equal CO₂ cuts, critical for limiting 1.5°C warming by 2050.
"Bioplastics solve the plastic pollution problem." Critically evaluate this statement using chemical evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Partially false. Types: (1) Bio-based but non-biodegradable: bio-PET (made from sugarcane) has identical polymer structure to fossil PET → same pollution, different feedstock. (2) Biodegradable but petroleum-based: e.g., PBAT. (3) Bio-based and biodegradable: PLA (polylactic acid), PHA — degrade via hydrolysis/enzymatic action. Problems: PLA requires industrial composting (58°C+) — does not degrade in oceans or home compost. Biodegradable plastics in oceans degrade into microplastics before fully mineralising. Land use for feedstock crops. Conclusion: Bioplastics reduce fossil dependence but do not eliminate ocean pollution. Need systemic change (reduce production, improve collection) not just material substitution.
