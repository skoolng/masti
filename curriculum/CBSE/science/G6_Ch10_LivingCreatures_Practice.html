What is the smallest unit of life?Easy
Show Answer

The cell is the smallest unit of life. Every living organism is made of one or more cells.

Who proposed Cell Theory in 1855?Easy
Show Answer

Rudolf Virchow proposed the third postulate of Cell Theory in 1855: all cells come from pre-existing cells (omnis cellula e cellula).

State the first postulate of Cell Theory.Easy
Show Answer

All living organisms are made of one or more cells.

State the second postulate of Cell Theory.Easy
Show Answer

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.

State the third postulate of Cell Theory.Easy
Show Answer

All cells arise from pre-existing cells through cell division.

Who first observed cells using a microscope?Easy
Show Answer

Robert Hooke first observed cells in 1665 when he looked at a thin slice of cork under a microscope and saw small box-like compartments he called "cells."

What does the term "cell" come from?Easy
Show Answer

It comes from the Latin word cellula, meaning "small room," because Robert Hooke thought the cells resembled small monks' rooms (cells) in a monastery.

Name two scientists who contributed to Cell Theory before Virchow.Easy
Show Answer

Matthias Schleiden (plants are made of cells, 1838) and Theodor Schwann (animals are made of cells, 1839) both contributed before Virchow.

Are viruses considered alive under Cell Theory? Why?Easy
Show Answer

No. Viruses are not made of cells, so they do not meet the criteria of Cell Theory and are not classified as living organisms.

Give one example each of a unicellular and a multicellular organism.Easy

Unicellular: Amoeba (or Paramecium, bacterium). Multicellular: Human (or tree, dog, mushroom).

SET B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why Cell Theory is called a "theory" rather than a "law."Medium
Show Answer

In science, a theory is a well-tested explanation supported by extensive evidence. Cell Theory is called a theory because it explains why all organisms are made of cells and how new cells form, going beyond a simple observation or law.

How did the invention of the microscope lead to the development of Cell Theory?Medium
Show Answer

The microscope allowed scientists like Hooke, Leeuwenhoek, Schleiden and Schwann to see cells for the first time. Without magnification, cells are invisible to the naked eye, so Cell Theory could not have been formulated without microscopes.

Why is Virchow's contribution considered the most important postulate?Medium
Show Answer

Virchow's postulate ("all cells from pre-existing cells") disproved spontaneous generation — the idea that life arises from non-living matter. It established that life is continuous and cells only come from existing cells.

A student says: "Crystals grow, so they must be alive." Use Cell Theory to evaluate this claim.Medium
Show Answer

This claim is incorrect. Crystals are not made of cells, they do not carry out cellular processes, and they cannot produce new cells. According to Cell Theory, being made of cells is a prerequisite for life.

Distinguish between a unicellular and multicellular organism in terms of Cell Theory.Medium
Show Answer

A unicellular organism consists of a single cell that performs all life functions. A multicellular organism is made of many cells that are specialised. Both satisfy Cell Theory because all their structures are cellular in nature.

Describe the contribution of Anton van Leeuwenhoek to biology.Medium
Show Answer

Leeuwenhoek was the first person to observe living microorganisms (bacteria, protozoa) using his hand-crafted microscopes in the 1670s. He demonstrated that tiny living cells exist in water and other substances, supporting the idea that life is cellular.

How does Cell Theory apply to a skin cell that is produced when you heal from a cut?Medium
Show Answer

When a wound heals, new skin cells are produced by the division of existing skin cells — directly illustrating Virchow's postulate that all cells arise from pre-existing cells.

Why did it take nearly 200 years after Hooke's observation to develop a complete Cell Theory?Medium
Show Answer

Early microscopes had low resolution. It took improvements in microscope technology, along with contributions from many scientists studying different organisms, before enough evidence accumulated to generalise that ALL living things are made of cells.

A bacterium divides to produce two daughter cells. Which postulate of Cell Theory does this illustrate?Medium
Show Answer

This illustrates the third postulate — all cells arise from pre-existing cells. The original bacterium (a pre-existing cell) divides to produce two new cells.

Compare what Schleiden and Schwann each contributed to Cell Theory.Medium
Show Answer

Schleiden (1838) concluded that all plants are made of cells. Schwann (1839) extended this to animals. Together they established the first two postulates: all living organisms are cellular, and the cell is the basic unit of life.

SET C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D (Analysis & Evaluation)

Evaluate Cell Theory as a scientific explanation. What are its strengths and any limitations?Hard
Show Answer

Strengths: Supported by 350+ years of microscopy evidence across all domains of life; explains both structure and reproduction of living things; universally applicable. Limitations: Does not cover viruses (acellular entities); some giant single-celled organisms like Caulerpa (a seaweed) challenge the idea of the "basic unit"; does not explain the origin of the first cell. Overall, Cell Theory remains one of the most robust frameworks in biology.

Design an investigation to test whether a newly discovered substance is living or non-living, applying Cell Theory principles.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: If the substance is living, it will be made of cells. Method: (1) Prepare a thin sample. (2) Stain with a cell stain (e.g., iodine or methylene blue). (3) Observe under a light microscope at 400×. Expected result: Cells with membrane/nucleus visible → living. No cellular structure → non-living. Extension: Check for cell division over time to confirm the third postulate.

A scientist argues that the modern discovery of CRISPR-edited cells does not contradict Cell Theory. Do you agree? Justify your answer.Hard
Show Answer

Agree. CRISPR edits the DNA within existing cells; it does not create cells from non-cellular matter. The edited cell still originated from a pre-existing cell (postulate 3) and is still the functional unit (postulate 2). Cell Theory is not violated — it is simply a tool applied within the framework.

Using Cell Theory, explain why cancer is considered a cellular disease.Hard
Show Answer

Cancer occurs when cells divide uncontrollably. Since all cells arise from pre-existing cells (postulate 3), cancer cells originate from normal body cells whose division regulation is disrupted. The disease is fundamentally cellular: tumours are masses of abnormal cells. Treatment targets cellular processes (e.g., stopping cell division), confirming the cellular basis of the disease.

Critically assess the historical claim of spontaneous generation and explain how Virchow's postulate refuted it.Hard
Show Answer

Spontaneous generation claimed organisms could arise from non-living matter (e.g., maggots from meat). Virchow's refutation (1855): "Omnis cellula e cellula" — every cell from a pre-existing cell — logically excludes any non-cellular origin of life. Pasteur's swan-neck flask experiment (1859) provided the experimental proof. Together, they conclusively replaced spontaneous generation with biogenesis.

How does Cell Theory connect to the IB MYP concept of "systems"? Give a biological example.Hard
Show Answer

Cell Theory shows that the cell is the fundamental system within living organisms — each cell itself contains interacting parts (organelles). Cells form higher-order systems: tissues, organs, organisms. Example: cardiac muscle cells (cells) → heart muscle (tissue) → heart (organ) → circulatory system → human body. Each level is a system made of smaller subsystems, all ultimately cellular.

Compare the scientific methodology used by Hooke (1665) and modern cell biologists. What has changed?Hard
Show Answer

Hooke used a compound light microscope with candlelight illumination, observing dead cork cells — purely descriptive work. Modern cell biologists use electron microscopes (×500,000), confocal laser microscopy, fluorescent tags, gene sequencing, and live-cell imaging. Modern methods are quantitative, molecular, and can observe living cells in real-time. Despite technological advances, the fundamental conclusion — that life is cellular — remains unchanged.

Explain how Cell Theory supports the idea of common ancestry of all life on Earth.Hard
Show Answer

Since all cells arise from pre-existing cells (postulate 3), tracing cell lineages backwards implies all cells today descend from an original ancestral cell — the Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA). The universality of the genetic code, ATP usage, and membrane structure across all domains (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya) further supports common ancestry, consistent with Cell Theory's universal applicability.

A classmate says: "Cell Theory is old and outdated — modern science has moved beyond it." Write a counter-argument using evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Cell Theory remains foundational: (1) Every organism discovered since 1855 — from archaea to newly found deep-sea microbes — is cellular. (2) Medical science (cancer, stem cells, vaccines) is built on cellular principles. (3) Biotechnology (CRISPR, cloning) operates within Cell Theory's framework. The theory has been extended and refined, but never overturned. A scientific theory grows stronger with more evidence — it does not become "outdated."

Assess the ethical implications of using human embryonic stem cells in research, considering Cell Theory's postulate that "all cells come from pre-existing cells."Hard
Show Answer

Scientific aspect: Embryonic stem cells arise from cell division of a fertilised egg — fully consistent with Cell Theory. Ethical debate: If all cells from pre-existing cells, the embryo is a cellular organism from the first division. Some argue harvesting those cells destroys a human life; others argue an early embryo lacks personhood. MYP perspective: Science provides the factual framework (what cells are); ethics provides the value judgement (what we should do with them). Both must be considered in responsible science.

Can most cells be seen with the naked eye?Easy
Show Answer

No. Most cells are microscopic and cannot be seen without a microscope. The average animal cell is about 10–20 micrometres (µm) in diameter.

What unit is used to measure cell size?Easy
Show Answer

Cells are typically measured in micrometres (µm). 1 µm = one-millionth of a metre (1 × 10⁻⁶ m).

What is the magnification range of a typical light microscope?Easy
Show Answer

A typical light (optical) microscope magnifies objects ×100 to ×1,000.

What is the maximum magnification of an electron microscope?Easy
Show Answer

An electron microscope can magnify up to ×500,000 or more, allowing visualisation of organelles and even large molecules.

What type of radiation does an electron microscope use instead of light?Easy
Show Answer

An electron microscope uses a beam of electrons instead of visible light.

Name the two main types of electron microscope.Easy
Show Answer

The Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM) and the Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM).

What is the formula for calculating magnification?Easy
Show Answer

Magnification = Image size ÷ Actual size. (M = I ÷ A)

Give one example of a cell that IS visible to the naked eye.Easy
Show Answer

A hen's egg yolk is a single cell visible to the naked eye. The ostrich egg cell is even larger. Human nerve cells (neurons) can also be very long (up to 1 metre) though thin.

What does the term "resolution" mean in microscopy?Easy
Show Answer

Resolution is the ability to distinguish two points as separate. Higher resolution = clearer, more detailed image.

A cell image is 40 mm wide. The actual cell is 0.04 mm wide. What is the magnification?Easy
Show Answer

Magnification = Image size ÷ Actual size = 40 mm ÷ 0.04 mm = ×1,000.

SET B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why cells are small, using the concept of surface area to volume ratio.Medium
Show Answer

As a cell grows, its volume increases faster than its surface area. A small surface area-to-volume ratio means the cell cannot exchange materials (oxygen, nutrients, waste) fast enough for its volume. Small cells have a high SA:V ratio, making diffusion efficient enough to sustain all cellular processes.

Compare the resolution of a light microscope and an electron microscope.Medium
Show Answer

A light microscope has a resolution of about 0.2 µm — insufficient to see organelles like ribosomes. An electron microscope has a resolution of ~0.1 nm (200× better), allowing visualisation of organelle ultrastructure and even large protein molecules.

A cell has an actual diameter of 25 µm. Under a microscope, its image is 12.5 mm. Calculate the magnification.Medium
Show Answer

Convert: 12.5 mm = 12,500 µm. Magnification = 12,500 ÷ 25 = ×500.

Why can electron microscopes not be used to view living cells?Medium
Show Answer

Electron microscopes require specimens to be fixed, dehydrated, and placed in a high-vacuum chamber. These conditions kill cells. Additionally, the electron beam itself damages biological material. Therefore, only preserved (dead) specimens can be examined.

Describe the difference between TEM and SEM images.Medium
Show Answer

TEM transmits electrons through a thin section of specimen, producing a 2D cross-sectional image of internal structures (high magnification). SEM scans the surface of a specimen with electrons, producing a 3D surface image (lower magnification than TEM but excellent surface detail).

A student uses a ×10 eyepiece and a ×40 objective lens. What is the total magnification?Medium
Show Answer

Total magnification = eyepiece × objective = 10 × 40 = ×400.

Why do scientists use stains when viewing cells under a light microscope?Medium
Show Answer

Most cells and organelles are colourless and transparent. Stains (e.g., iodine for starch/nucleus, methylene blue for nuclei) bind to specific structures and provide colour contrast, making cells and their components visible.

Explain the difference between magnification and resolution in microscopy.Medium
Show Answer

Magnification is how much larger an image appears compared to the object. Resolution is the ability to distinguish fine details as separate points. You can have high magnification but low resolution (a blurry enlarged image), which is useless. Both high magnification AND high resolution are needed for useful microscopy.

The image of a chloroplast in a textbook is 30 mm. The magnification stated is ×15,000. Calculate the actual size in µm.Medium
Show Answer

Actual size = Image size ÷ Magnification = 30 mm ÷ 15,000 = 0.002 mm = 2 µm.

Why is it an advantage to use a light microscope (rather than electron) in a school laboratory?Medium
Show Answer

Light microscopes are cheaper, portable, easy to use, and can view living cells. They do not require a vacuum or complex specimen preparation, making them suitable for school labs where cost and safety matter.

Design an experiment to determine whether increasing cell size affects the efficiency of diffusion. Include hypothesis, variables, method, and expected results.Hard

Hypothesis: Larger cells will have lower diffusion efficiency due to a lower SA:V ratio. IV: Size of agar cube (1 cm, 2 cm, 3 cm). Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of electron microscopy versus light microscopy for cell biology research.Hard
Show Answer

EM advantages: Resolution ~0.1 nm; reveals organelle ultrastructure; magnification up to ×500,000. EM disadvantages: Cannot view living cells; expensive; complex specimen preparation may introduce artefacts; large equipment. LM advantages: Can view living cells; cheap; portable; colour images possible with fluorescent stains. LM disadvantages: Limited resolution (0.2 µm); cannot resolve organelles like ribosomes. Conclusion: Both are complementary; LM for live-cell dynamics, EM for structural details.

Critically evaluate why the surface area to volume ratio is a fundamental constraint on cell size, using mathematical reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

For a cube: SA = 6s², Volume = s³, SA:V = 6/s. As s doubles from 1→2→4: SA:V = 6:3:1.5. This inverse relationship means a cell twice as large has half the relative surface area for exchange. Since metabolic rate scales with volume but exchange rate scales with surface area, beyond ~100 µm diameter, diffusion alone cannot supply enough O₂/nutrients. This sets a physical upper limit on unpaired cell size and explains why large organisms are multicellular.

A scientist obtains both an LM image (×400) and a TEM image (×50,000) of the same cell. Describe what additional information the TEM image provides and explain why.Hard
Show Answer

The TEM image (125× more magnification, far higher resolution) would reveal: ribosome structure, ER membrane details, mitochondrial cristae, nuclear pore structure, and vesicle membranes — none of which are resolvable at ×400. This is because the electron beam has a wavelength ~100,000× shorter than visible light, giving resolution of ~0.1 nm vs 200 nm for LM.

Explain how confocal laser scanning microscopy has advanced our understanding of living cells beyond what standard light microscopy can show.Hard
Show Answer

Confocal microscopy uses laser illumination and a pinhole aperture to capture light from only one focal plane at a time, eliminating out-of-focus blur. Multiple planes are combined into 3D reconstructions. Combined with fluorescent protein tags (e.g., GFP), scientists can track specific proteins or organelles in real time in living cells — impossible with standard LM or EM. This has revealed dynamic processes: vesicle trafficking, mitosis timing, and protein localisation.

The resolution limit of a light microscope is 0.2 µm. Explain the physics behind this limit and why electron microscopes overcome it.Hard
Show Answer

Resolution is limited by the wavelength of the illuminating radiation (Abbe diffraction limit: d ≈ λ/2). Visible light has wavelengths 400–700 nm, giving a resolution limit of ~200 nm (0.2 µm). Electrons accelerated at high voltage have wavelengths of ~0.004 nm — approximately 100,000× shorter than visible light. This gives the electron microscope a theoretical resolution of ~0.002 nm, though practical resolution is ~0.1 nm, still vastly better than light microscopes.

Analyse what can go wrong during specimen preparation for electron microscopy and how this might affect scientific conclusions.Hard
Show Answer

Artefacts can arise from: (1) Chemical fixation — cross-linking proteins may distort their arrangement. (2) Dehydration — can shrink or collapse structures. (3) Heavy-metal staining — may not uniformly coat all surfaces. (4) Sectioning — thin slices may not represent 3D structure accurately. Impact: Scientists may incorrectly interpret artefacts as real structures. Example: early EM images of the smooth ER appeared as disconnected vesicles due to preparation — later discovered to be a continuous membrane network. Peer review and multiple preparation methods help minimise these errors.

Using the concept of cell size limits, explain why oxygen delivery in large animals requires a circulatory system rather than simple diffusion.Hard
Show Answer

Diffusion is only effective over short distances (~1 µm to a few hundred µm). A large animal body may be metres thick; oxygen diffusing from surface to interior cells would take hours or days — far too slow. The SA:V ratio of the whole body is too low for surface diffusion to supply all cells. A circulatory system solves this by transporting oxygen in blood to within a few cell-widths of every cell, where short-range diffusion takes over. This is why even large organisms maintain Cell Theory's small-cell size — each cell still relies on local diffusion.

Calculate: A student measures a cell image as 45 mm under ×900 magnification. Another cell image is 18 mm under ×600. Which cell is actually larger? Show working.Hard
Show Answer

Cell 1: Actual = Image ÷ Magnification = 45 mm ÷ 900 = 0.05 mm = 50 µm. Cell 2: Actual = 18 mm ÷ 600 = 0.03 mm = 30 µm. Cell 1 is larger (50 µm vs 30 µm). This demonstrates that image size alone is misleading — actual size requires knowing the magnification.

Assess the impact of microscopy technology on our current understanding of diseases at the cellular level. Give two specific examples.Hard
Show Answer

Example 1 – Cancer: EM revealed changes in nuclear chromatin structure and organelle disorganisation in cancer cells, helping pathologists diagnose malignancy and understand invasion mechanisms. Example 2 – HIV: TEM first visualised HIV virions budding from T-cells in the 1980s, revealing the infection mechanism and identifying the virus's envelope proteins as drug targets. Without electron microscopy, these discoveries would have been impossible, fundamentally changing medicine. This illustrates how technology drives scientific understanding.

SET A Easy – Recall & Identification

Name the rigid outer layer found in plant cells but not in animal cells.Easy
Show Answer

The cell wall. It is made of cellulose and provides structural support and rigidity to plant cells.

What is the chemical composition of the plant cell wall?Easy
Show Answer

The plant cell wall is primarily made of cellulose, a carbohydrate polymer made of glucose units arranged in long fibres.

What organelle in plant cells carries out photosynthesis?Easy
Show Answer

Chloroplasts. They contain chlorophyll, the green pigment that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis.

What is the large central vacuole in a plant cell filled with?Easy
Show Answer

The central vacuole is filled with cell sap — a watery solution of dissolved sugars, salts, and other molecules.

What is the function of the nucleus in a plant cell?Easy
Show Answer

The nucleus is the control centre of the cell. It contains DNA (genetic information) and controls cell activities, including growth and reproduction.

What colour are chloroplasts, and why?Easy
Show Answer

Chloroplasts are green because they contain chlorophyll, a green pigment that absorbs red and blue light for photosynthesis and reflects green light.

Name the thin, flexible layer that surrounds the cytoplasm in plant cells.Easy
Show Answer

The cell membrane (also called the plasma membrane). It controls what enters and exits the cell and lies inside the cell wall.

List four structures found in a typical plant cell.Easy
Show Answer

Cell wall, cell membrane, nucleus, chloroplasts, large central vacuole, cytoplasm, mitochondria, ribosomes (any four).

Where in the plant cell is the nucleus usually located?Easy
Show Answer

In a mature plant cell, the large central vacuole pushes the nucleus to one side, near the cell membrane.

What is the role of the central vacuole in maintaining the plant's structure?Easy
Show Answer

When filled with water, the central vacuole pushes outward against the cell wall (turgor pressure), keeping the cell firm and the plant upright.

SET B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain the relationship between the cell wall and the cell membrane in a plant cell.Medium
Show Answer

The cell membrane is a selective barrier (phospholipid bilayer) that controls what enters/exits the cell. The cell wall is outside the membrane and is fully permeable — it provides structural support but does not control entry. The two work together: the wall gives rigidity while the membrane controls transport.

Why do only certain plant cells contain chloroplasts?Medium
Show Answer

Only cells exposed to light need chloroplasts for photosynthesis. Green parts of the plant (leaves, green stems) have chloroplasts in their mesophyll cells. Root cells, for example, are underground and do not carry out photosynthesis, so they lack chloroplasts.

Describe what happens to a plant cell when it loses water (plasmolysis).Medium
Show Answer

When a plant cell loses water, the central vacuole shrinks. The cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall in a process called plasmolysis. The cell loses turgor pressure and becomes flaccid — the plant wilts. The cell wall remains intact but the membrane detaches from it.

Explain how the internal structure of a chloroplast is adapted for photosynthesis.Medium
Show Answer

Chloroplasts have stacked membrane discs called grana (singular: granum). These increase surface area for capturing light. The stroma (fluid) surrounding the grana contains enzymes for the Calvin cycle (carbon fixation). The double membrane isolates the internal chemistry from the cytoplasm.

A plant cell is described as "turgid." What does this mean and why is it important?Medium
Show Answer

Turgid means the vacuole is full of water, the cell membrane presses against the cell wall, and the cell is firm due to high turgor pressure. This is important because turgor pressure supports non-woody plant structures (leaves, stems) against gravity and keeps stomata open for gas exchange.

Explain why the cell wall does not prevent osmosis but the cell membrane does regulate it.Medium
Show Answer

The cell wall is made of porous cellulose fibres and is fully permeable — water and molecules pass through freely. The cell membrane is selectively permeable (phospholipid bilayer with protein channels) and controls which molecules enter or leave. Therefore osmosis occurs through the membrane, not the wall.

How does the large central vacuole benefit a plant cell compared to having many small vacuoles?Medium
Show Answer

A single large vacuole maximises storage volume and turgor pressure with minimum membrane material. It stores water, nutrients, pigments, and waste products efficiently. Multiple small vacuoles would have more membrane surface area (expensive energetically) and provide less consistent turgor pressure.

Suggest why leaves have more chloroplasts per cell than roots.Medium
Show Answer

Leaves are the photosynthetic organs and are exposed to sunlight. More chloroplasts per cell maximises the surface area for light absorption and the rate of photosynthesis. Roots are underground; they absorb water and minerals, not light, so they have no need for chloroplasts (and lack them).

Describe the function of the nuclear membrane in a plant cell.Medium
Show Answer

The nuclear membrane (double membrane with pores) separates the DNA in the nucleus from the cytoplasm. Nuclear pores allow mRNA to exit (for protein synthesis) and proteins to enter. This compartmentalisation protects DNA and allows regulation of gene expression.

Besides chloroplasts, name another type of plastid found in plants and state its function.Medium
Show Answer

Chromoplasts store red, orange, and yellow pigments (carotenoids) — found in fruits and flowers to attract pollinators. Amyloplasts (leucoplasts) store starch — found in potato tubers and seeds.

SET C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D

Evaluate the evidence that chloroplasts evolved from free-living cyanobacteria (endosymbiotic theory).Hard
Show Answer

Evidence: (1) Chloroplasts have their own circular DNA similar to bacteria. (2) They have a double membrane (inner = original bacterial membrane, outer = engulfment membrane). (3) They reproduce by binary fission, not mitosis. (4) Their ribosomes are 70S (bacterial size), not 80S (eukaryotic). (5) Antibiotic treatments that stop bacterial ribosomes also stop chloroplast protein synthesis. This is strong evidence for endosymbiosis — a cyanobacterium was engulfed ~1.5 billion years ago and became the chloroplast.

A student observes a plant cell becoming flaccid after being placed in saltwater. Explain the mechanism using osmosis, turgor pressure, and cell wall properties.Hard
Show Answer

Salt water has a lower water potential than cell sap. By osmosis, water moves from high water potential (cell sap) to low (saltwater) through the selectively permeable membrane. The vacuole shrinks, reducing turgor pressure. The cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall (plasmolysis). The cell wall, being rigid, does not shrink — creating a gap between membrane and wall. If water loss continues, the plant wilts. Unlike the membrane, the cellulose cell wall cannot contract, so it stays in place while the membrane detaches inward.

Design an investigation to show that light intensity affects the number of chloroplasts visible per cell. State hypothesis, variables, method, and expected results.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Cells from high-light environments will have more chloroplasts per cell than shaded cells. IV: Light intensity (full sun vs shade). DV: Number of chloroplasts per mesophyll cell. CV: Species of plant, cell type (palisade), preparation method, staining. Method: Grow identical plants under high and low light for 4 weeks. Prepare leaf cross-sections. View at ×400. Count chloroplasts in 20 palisade cells per condition. Expected: High-light cells will have significantly more chloroplasts (plants acclimate by increasing chloroplast number/size in low light to compensate, or maintaining more in high light for maximum photosynthesis rate).

Compare the roles of the cellulose cell wall in plants with the chitin cell wall in fungi. What does this suggest about convergent evolution?Hard
Show Answer

Both cellulose (glucose polymer, β-1,4 linkage) and chitin (N-acetylglucosamine polymer) provide structural rigidity, protection, and shape. Both are polysaccharides but chemically distinct. That two unrelated lineages independently evolved rigid cell walls suggests convergent evolution — the same structural problem (providing cell support) was solved by similar but independently evolved solutions. This implies a strong adaptive advantage to having a cell wall, regardless of the exact chemistry.

Analyse how the structure of a palisade mesophyll cell is specifically adapted for maximum photosynthesis. Refer to at least four structural features.Hard
Show Answer

(1) Tall, columnar shape: maximises the path length for light, increasing chance of absorption. (2) Many chloroplasts (40–50 per cell): maximises light-capturing surface area. (3) Thin cell wall: CO₂ diffuses in easily. (4) Located at top of leaf: first cells to receive direct sunlight. (5) Large vacuole: keeps cell turgid and holds metabolites. (6) Chloroplasts can move within cytoplasm: face the light directly. Each feature directly increases the rate of the light-dependent or light-independent reactions of photosynthesis.

Explain why cellulose is an ideal material for cell walls, considering its molecular structure and properties.Hard
Show Answer

Cellulose is a β-1,4-glycosidic linked glucose polymer. These linkages allow straight chains that hydrogen-bond with adjacent chains, forming microfibrils. Microfibrils are bundled into macrofibrils — extremely strong. Cellulose is: (1) Insoluble (water doesn't weaken it). (2) Permeable (spaces between fibres allow water/ions through). (3) Flexible but strong (can resist turgor pressure without shattering). (4) Biodegradable (decomposed by specific fungi/bacteria). These properties make cellulose uniquely suited for a structural wall that is both strong and permeable.

Using your knowledge of plant cell structure, predict what would happen to a plant cell if the tonoplast (vacuole membrane) was destroyed. Explain your reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

The tonoplast regulates what enters/leaves the vacuole. If destroyed: (1) Cell sap would leak into the cytoplasm — altering pH and ion concentrations. (2) Turgor pressure would be lost — cell becomes flaccid; plant wilts. (3) Hydrolytic enzymes stored in some vacuoles could be released into cytoplasm, digesting organelles (autolysis). (4) Waste products and toxins stored in the vacuole would contaminate the cytoplasm. (5) The cell would likely die rapidly due to cytoplasmic disruption. This demonstrates the critical regulatory role of vacuolar membranes.

A plant cell is placed in distilled water. Describe and explain ALL the changes that would occur over 30 minutes.Hard
Show Answer

0–5 min: Distilled water has very high water potential (Ψ = 0). Cell sap has lower water potential. Water enters by osmosis through the cell membrane into the vacuole. 5–15 min: Vacuole expands, pushing cytoplasm and membrane against the cell wall (turgor pressure increases). Cell becomes turgid. 15–30 min: Cell wall exerts a wall pressure opposing further water entry. Water potential of cell rises to equal that of the water. Equilibrium: Net water movement = 0. The cell is fully turgid — it does NOT burst because the rigid cellulose cell wall prevents over-expansion (unlike animal cells). The cell becomes firm and hard to compress.

Critically evaluate the statement: "The cell wall makes plant cells stronger but also limits their ability to respond to the environment."Hard
Show Answer

Supports the statement: The rigid wall limits shape change, cell migration, phagocytosis (plants cannot engulf food), and rapid movement. Plant cells cannot migrate like animal immune cells. Against the statement: Plants have evolved sophisticated responses within the constraint of cell walls — guard cells change shape (using turgor, not wall flexibility) to open/close stomata; pulvinus cells in Mimosa pudica enable rapid leaf folding; secondary cell walls (lignin) allow vascular tissue to form. Conclusion: Partially true — the wall does impose constraints on motility and flexibility, but plants have evolved diverse mechanisms to respond to their environment within those constraints.

NADH and FADH₂ (carrying electrons from the Krebs cycle) donate electrons to protein complexes (I, II, III, IV) in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As electrons pass through complexes I, III, and IV, protons (H⁺) are actively pumped from the matrix into the intermembrane space, creating a high H⁺ concentration (proton gradient / proton motive force). H⁺ ions flow back down this gradient through ATP synthase (Complex V) — a molecular rotor — which uses this flow to phosphorylate ADP to ATP. This is chemiosmosis (Mitchell, 1961, Nobel Prize 1978). O₂ is the final electron acceptor, combining with electrons and H⁺ to form water. Without O₂, the ETC stops, proton gradient collapses, and ATP synthesis ceases.

Cyanide is a poison that blocks cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV) in the electron transport chain. Predict all consequences on the cell, explaining your reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

(1) ETC stops → no more electron flow. (2) Proton gradient across inner mitochondrial membrane cannot be maintained → ATP synthase stops → ATP production from oxidative phosphorylation ceases. (3) NADH/FADH₂ cannot be re-oxidised → Krebs cycle and glycolysis back up → glucose cannot be efficiently broken down. (4) Cell switches to anaerobic glycolysis — but produces only 2 ATP and rapidly accumulates lactic acid. (5) pH drops → enzyme denaturation. (6) Active transport fails → ion balance disrupted → cells swell → membrane ruptures. (7) Rapid cell death — especially in high-demand cells (neurons, heart muscle). Clinical: Cyanide poisoning causes rapid unconsciousness and cardiac arrest. Treatment: hydroxocobalamin (binds cyanide) or amyl nitrite (creates methaemoglobin to compete for cyanide).

Compare aerobic respiration in mitochondria with fermentation in yeast. What are the evolutionary implications of both existing?Hard
Show Answer

Fermentation (yeast, anaerobic): incomplete glucose breakdown, 2 ATP, no O₂ needed, products: ethanol + CO₂. Aerobic respiration evolved ~2.4 billion years ago after cyanobacterial photosynthesis created atmospheric O₂. Yeast retaining anaerobic fermentation is an evolutionary vestige — useful for survival in O₂-poor environments (e.g., compressed sediment, gut microbiome). The 18–19× ATP advantage of aerobic respiration drove the evolution of complex multicellular life, which requires enormous energy. Both pathways coexist today because anaerobic conditions still occur in nature and within organisms.

Analyse how the body regulates the balance between aerobic respiration and anaerobic glycolysis during exercise of increasing intensity.Hard
Show Answer

Low intensity: Aerobic respiration meets ATP demand fully. O₂ supply (breathing, heart rate) keeps pace. Lactate stays low. Can be sustained indefinitely. (3) Within ~1 billion years, first multicellular organisms appeared (ediacaran fauna, ~600 mya). Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of cell specialisation in multicellular organisms compared to unicellular strategies.Hard

Explain why viruses are not considered alive according to MRS GREN.Medium

Animal (heterotrophic nutrition): Consumes organic molecules from other organisms. Uses digestion to break down food into absorbable molecules. Both obtain carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and minerals — but through completely different mechanisms reflecting their evolutionary strategies.

Explain why excretion is different from egestion (defecation).Medium

Egestion is the removal of undigested food that was never absorbed into the body — faeces contain food residues, bacteria, and dead gut cells, not metabolic products. Egestion is not excretion because the material being expelled was never part of the organism's internal chemistry.

Explain how growth in organisms is different from growth in crystals or icicles.Medium

Additionally, reproduction transmits the genetic information that defines life to future generations. It is a population-level characteristic essential to the continuity of life, even if not every individual exercises it.

Evaluate the philosophical question: "Is life a property of chemistry or a property of information?" Use MRS GREN and Cell Theory to construct your argument.Hard

Life as chemistry: MRS GREN is entirely chemical — respiration, nutrition, excretion, growth are biochemical processes in cells. Cell Theory says life is made of cells — physical, chemical entities. Life emerges from the interaction: self-replicating information (DNA) directing self-sustaining chemistry (cellular metabolism) — precisely what Cell Theory and MRS GREN together describe. This may be the deepest insight in biology: life is the marriage of chemical process and molecular information.
