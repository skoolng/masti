Set A — Recall & Identify
What is coordination in biology?
Easy
Answer

The process by which different organs work together to produce a proper response to a stimulus

Coordination ensures that body systems act in a unified, organised way.
What is a stimulus?
Easy
Answer

Any change in the environment that causes a response in an organism

Examples: light, sound, touch, temperature, chemicals.
Name the two main systems of control and coordination in animals.
Easy
Answer

Nervous system and endocrine (hormonal) system

Nervous system: fast, electrical; Endocrine: slow, chemical via blood.
What is homeostasis?
Easy
Answer

The maintenance of a stable internal environment (body temperature, blood glucose, pH) despite external changes

Homeostasis is maintained by coordination between nervous and endocrine systems.
What is the basic unit of the nervous system?
Easy
Answer

The neuron (nerve cell)

Neurons are specialised cells that transmit electrical signals (nerve impulses).
How does the endocrine system communicate compared to the nervous system?
Easy
Answer

Endocrine: chemical messengers (hormones) transported slowly via blood; Nervous: fast electrical impulses along nerves

The endocrine system is slower but produces longer-lasting effects than the nervous system.
Give one example each of a nervous response and a hormonal response.
Medium
Answer

Nervous: pulling hand away from a hot object (reflex); Hormonal: increased heart rate due to adrenaline during stress

These examples contrast the speed and duration of the two control systems.
Why do multicellular organisms need specialised control systems?
Medium
Answer

Because different cells and organs need to cooperate; communication systems coordinate their activities across large distances within the body

Simple chemical diffusion is too slow for large, complex organisms.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Compare the speed and duration of nervous vs endocrine responses.
Medium
Answer

Nervous: milliseconds, short-lived; Endocrine: seconds to minutes, longer-lasting

Nervous uses electrical signals; endocrine uses hormones that persist until broken down.
A person touches a hot stove and immediately withdraws their hand before feeling pain. Explain the control mechanism.
Hard
Answer

Reflex arc via spinal cord — does not involve brain initially; faster than conscious perception; pain felt afterwards when signal reaches brain

The spinal cord can process and respond to harmful stimuli without waiting for brain input.
Explain why plants need control and coordination despite lacking a nervous system.
Hard
Answer

Plants respond to light (phototropism), gravity (gravitropism), touch (thigmotropism) via chemical hormones (auxins, gibberellins) — coordination without nerves

Plant hormones diffuse through cells to coordinate growth responses to environmental cues.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: Design an experiment to demonstrate that a plant responds to the direction of light.
Hard
Answer

Place seedling in a box with a hole on one side; observe direction of stem bending after 48 hours — stem bends toward light

Positive phototropism caused by unequal auxin distribution on shaded vs lit sides.
Evaluate: How do control and coordination systems maintain wellbeing? What happens when they fail?
Hard
Answer

Proper coordination → homeostasis → health; failure examples: diabetes (hormonal), Parkinson's (nervous) — both disrupt normal function

MYP Criterion D: science-health connection, understanding disease as failure of coordination.

Set A — Recall & Identify
Draw and label the basic structure of a neuron.
Easy
Answer

Cell body (soma) + dendrites (receive signals) + axon (transmit signals) + axon terminals; myelin sheath covers axon for insulation

These are the four main structural components of a typical neuron.
What is the function of dendrites?
Easy
Answer

Receive nerve impulses from other neurons or sensory receptors and pass them to the cell body

Dendrites are the input branches of a neuron — multiple dendrites increase signal reception.
What is the function of the axon?
Easy
Answer

Carry nerve impulses away from the cell body to other neurons or effectors

The axon is the output structure; in motor neurons it may be very long (up to 1 metre).
What is the myelin sheath, and what does it do?
Easy
Answer

A fatty (lipid) covering around axons produced by Schwann cells; insulates the axon and speeds up nerve impulse transmission

Myelinated nerves conduct impulses up to 100× faster than unmyelinated fibres.
Name the three types of neurons.
Easy
Answer

Sensory (afferent), Motor (efferent), Relay (interneuron/association)

Sensory: sense organ → CNS; Relay: within CNS; Motor: CNS → muscle/gland.
What are Schwann cells?
Easy
Answer

Cells that wrap around axons to form the myelin sheath in the peripheral nervous system

In multiple sclerosis, myelin is damaged, slowing nerve conduction.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Explain the path of a nerve impulse from a sensory receptor to a muscle using neuron types.
Medium
Answer

Stimulus → Sensory receptor → Sensory neuron (to CNS) → Relay neuron (in spinal cord/brain) → Motor neuron → Effector (muscle)

This is the basic reflex/response pathway involving all three neuron types.
Why does damage to the myelin sheath slow nerve impulse transmission?
Medium
Answer

Myelin enables saltatory conduction (impulse jumps between Nodes of Ranvier); without it, impulse travels slowly as continuous wave

Multiple sclerosis is caused by immune system attacking myelin — slows signals causing coordination problems.
A relay neuron in the spinal cord integrates signals from 3 sensory neurons. Explain its role.
Hard
Answer

Relay neuron processes and coordinates multiple inputs, deciding whether to fire — acts as a signal integrator preventing unnecessary responses

Integration is key to avoiding overwhelming the motor system with every minor stimulus.
Compare unmyelinated and myelinated nerve fibres in terms of speed and location.
Hard
Answer

Unmyelinated: 0.5–2 m/s, pain/temperature fibres; Myelinated: 70–120 m/s, motor/touch fibres — faster due to saltatory conduction

Speed difference allows prioritisation: touch/motor are fast; pain slightly slower to allow protective responses.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: How would you test the effect of temperature on nerve impulse transmission speed in an organism?
Hard
Answer

Measure reaction time at different temperatures; colder temperatures slow ion channel activity → slower impulses

Ion channels that generate nerve impulses are temperature-sensitive proteins.
Evaluate: Nerve damage from accidents can be permanent. Why can't adult neurons regenerate easily, and what research is being done?
Hard
Answer

Adult CNS neurons have poor regeneration capacity (inhibitory environment + loss of growth signals); research: stem cell therapy, nerve grafts, NGF (nerve growth factor)

MYP Criterion D: scientific research responding to societal need — spinal cord injury treatment.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a nerve impulse?
Easy
Answer

An electrochemical signal that travels along a neuron; caused by sudden changes in electrical charge across the axon membrane

Nerve impulses are all-or-nothing — either they fire fully or not at all.
What maintains the resting potential of a neuron?
Easy
Answer

The sodium-potassium pump (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) which pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in, maintaining a negative internal charge

Resting potential is about −70 mV inside relative to outside.
What triggers an action potential?
Easy
Answer

A stimulus that causes enough depolarisation to reach the threshold potential (about −55 mV)

Below threshold, the impulse fails; at threshold, it fires fully (all-or-nothing principle).
What is depolarisation?
Easy
Answer

The reversal of membrane potential when Na⁺ rushes into the axon, making inside briefly positive (+40 mV)

Depolarisation travels along the axon as the action potential propagates.
What is the all-or-nothing principle of nerve impulse?
Easy
Answer

A neuron either fires a full-strength impulse (if threshold is reached) or does not fire at all — no partial responses

Impulse strength is constant; intensity is coded by frequency of firing.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Explain how the intensity of a stimulus is communicated by a neuron if the impulse is always the same strength.
Medium
Answer

By the frequency of impulses — stronger stimulus → more frequent firing → perceived as stronger sensation

Like Morse code: the speed of dots determines the message intensity, not dot size.
Why does a local anaesthetic (e.g., lidocaine) block pain? Relate to nerve impulse mechanism.
Hard
Answer

Lidocaine blocks Na⁺ channels — prevents depolarisation — nerve cannot generate action potential — no pain signal reaches brain

Understanding the molecular mechanism explains why dentists inject anaesthetic before drilling.
Describe repolarisation and the refractory period.
Medium
Answer

K⁺ channels open → K⁺ flows out → inside becomes negative again (repolarisation); briefly hyper-negative (refractory) → prevents backwards signal travel

The refractory period ensures impulses travel in one direction only.
How does saltatory conduction in myelinated fibres differ from continuous conduction?
Hard
Answer

Myelinated: action potential jumps between Nodes of Ranvier (exposed gaps in myelin) — much faster, less energy; Unmyelinated: continuous wave along whole membrane — slower, more energy

Saltatory conduction is ~100× faster and uses 100× less energy than continuous conduction.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: How do drugs like cocaine or amphetamines affect nerve impulse transmission at the synapse?
Hard
Answer

They block reuptake of neurotransmitters (dopamine, noradrenaline) → neurotransmitters accumulate → continuous stimulation → addiction; long-term receptor downregulation

MYP Criterion D: understanding neurological basis of substance addiction.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a synapse?
Easy
Answer

The junction between two neurons (or between a neuron and an effector) where nerve signals are transmitted

Synapses are not physical connections — there is a small gap called the synaptic cleft.
What are neurotransmitters?
Easy
Answer

Chemical messengers released by the pre-synaptic neuron that cross the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the post-synaptic neuron

Examples: acetylcholine (ACh), dopamine, serotonin, noradrenaline.
What is the synaptic cleft?
Easy
Answer

The tiny gap (~20 nm) between the pre-synaptic and post-synaptic membranes

Neurotransmitters must diffuse across this gap to transmit the signal chemically.
How is a nerve impulse transmitted across a synapse?
Easy
Answer

Action potential → vesicles release neurotransmitter into cleft → binds post-synaptic receptors → new impulse (or inhibition) in post-synaptic cell

The synapse converts an electrical signal to chemical and back to electrical.
What happens to neurotransmitters after they bind to receptors?
Easy
Answer

They are either broken down by enzymes (e.g., acetylcholinesterase breaks ACh) or taken back up by the pre-synaptic cell (reuptake)

Clearance of neurotransmitters ends the signal and allows the synapse to reset.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Why can nerve signals only travel in one direction across a synapse?
Medium
Answer

Because only the pre-synaptic terminal has vesicles with neurotransmitters; post-synaptic membrane has receptors — signal can only go pre→post

Structural asymmetry of synapses enforces unidirectional signal flow.
Explain how organophosphate pesticides (nerve agents) work in terms of synapse function.
Hard
Answer

They inhibit acetylcholinesterase — ACh is not broken down — continuous stimulation of muscles → muscle paralysis and death

Understanding pesticide toxicology requires knowledge of synaptic transmission.
What is the difference between excitatory and inhibitory synapses?
Medium
Answer

Excitatory: neurotransmitter depolarises post-synaptic cell (makes it more likely to fire); Inhibitory: hyperpolarises it (less likely to fire)

Brain function involves balance of excitation and inhibition — imbalance leads to conditions like epilepsy.
How does caffeine affect synaptic transmission?
Hard
Answer

Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors — adenosine normally inhibits neural activity; blocking it increases excitability → alertness and reduced fatigue

Adenosine builds up during waking → sleep drive; caffeine temporarily masks this signal.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Antidepressants like SSRIs block serotonin reuptake. How does this affect synaptic transmission and mood?
Hard
Answer

More serotonin remains in cleft → prolonged receptor binding → increased serotonin signalling → improved mood regulation; takes weeks to work as receptors adjust

MYP Criterion D: pharmacological intervention in mental health — mechanisms and ethics.
Investigate: Design an experiment to test whether a chemical affects the speed of a reflex.
Hard
Answer

Measure baseline reflex time (drop ruler test); apply chemical; remeasure — compare; use controls; consider ethical limits in human experiments

MYP Criterion B: experimental design with controls; MYP ethics of human subjects research.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a reflex action?
Easy
Answer

A rapid, automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that does not require conscious thought

Examples: blinking, withdrawing from pain, knee-jerk, sneezing.
List the components of a reflex arc in order.
Easy
Answer

Receptor → Sensory neuron → Relay neuron (spinal cord) → Motor neuron → Effector

This 5-step pathway is the basic reflex arc; some reflexes bypass the brain entirely.
What is the role of the spinal cord in a spinal reflex?
Easy
Answer

Acts as the integration centre — processes the sensory input and sends motor commands without waiting for the brain

This reduces response time dramatically — critical for avoiding injury.
Distinguish between conditioned and unconditioned reflexes.
Easy
Answer

Unconditioned: inborn, automatic (e.g., salivating at food); Conditioned: learned through association (Pavlov's dog salivating at bell)

Pavlov's experiments showed reflexes can be conditioned — learned behaviour has a neural basis.
Why are reflex responses faster than voluntary responses?
Easy
Answer

Reflex arc bypasses the brain (processed at spinal cord level); shorter pathway = less travel time

Brain processing adds significant delay — reflexes avoid this for critical protective responses.
What is the effector in a reflex arc?
Easy
Answer

The muscle or gland that produces the response to the nerve impulse

In a withdrawal reflex, the effector is the arm muscle that pulls the hand away.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Trace the complete pathway of the knee-jerk reflex.
Medium
Answer

Hammer hits patellar tendon → stretch receptor in quadriceps → sensory neuron → spinal cord → motor neuron → quadriceps contracts → leg kicks

This monosynaptic reflex (only one synapse) is the fastest reflex in the body.
Why do we feel pain after withdrawing our hand from a hot object, not simultaneously?
Medium
Answer

The withdrawal reflex uses the spinal cord (faster); pain signals travel on slower unmyelinated fibres to the brain — brain perceives pain slightly later

Two separate pathways: fast (withdrawal) and slower (pain sensation) operate in parallel.
Explain how Pavlov conditioned dogs to salivate at the sound of a bell.
Hard
Answer

Bell (neutral stimulus) paired repeatedly with food → bell alone triggers salivation; neural pathways established between auditory and salivation centres

Conditioned reflexes involve synaptic strengthening — the basis of associative learning.
Design a simple experiment to measure the reaction time of a person catching a falling ruler.
Hard
Answer

Hold ruler at top, release without warning; partner catches when they see it fall; distance caught = measure of reaction time; repeat and average

Reaction time = time for stimulus (visual) → brain processing → motor response — not a simple spinal reflex.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Why is the existence of reflexes that bypass the brain evolutionarily advantageous?
Hard
Answer

Faster protective responses increase survival — withdrawing from fire in 0.1s vs 0.5s could mean the difference between minor and severe injury

Natural selection favoured faster protective reflexes; slower conscious processing is reserved for complex decisions.
Investigate: Test whether your reflex response time changes with caffeine, fatigue, or distraction. Design the experiment.
Hard
Answer

Independent variable: condition (caffeine/fatigue/distraction); Dependent: ruler catch distance; Controls: same person, same hand, same time; multiple trials

MYP Criterion B: controlled experimental design with clear variables and ethical consideration.

Set A — Recall & Identify
Name the three main regions of the human brain.
Easy
Answer

Forebrain (cerebrum, thalamus, hypothalamus), Midbrain, Hindbrain (cerebellum, pons, medulla oblongata)

The brain is divided into three functional regions based on embryonic development.
What is the function of the cerebrum?
Easy
Answer

Thinking, memory, voluntary movement, sensory processing, language, personality — the seat of intelligence and consciousness

The cerebral cortex (outer layer) is highly folded (gyri and sulci) to increase surface area.
What does the cerebellum control?
Easy
Answer

Balance, coordination of voluntary movements, and fine motor control

Damage to the cerebellum causes ataxia — loss of coordination and balance.
What does the medulla oblongata regulate?
Easy
Answer

Involuntary vital functions: breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, swallowing, vomiting

The medulla is in the brainstem; damage is life-threatening as it controls basic survival functions.
What is the function of the hypothalamus?
Easy
Answer

Regulates body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, and emotional responses; links nervous system to endocrine system via pituitary gland

The hypothalamus is the master regulator of homeostasis.
What protects the brain?
Easy
Answer

Skull (cranium), three membranes called meninges, and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)

CSF acts as a shock absorber; meninges include dura mater, arachnoid, and pia mater.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A patient after a stroke cannot speak but can understand speech. Which brain area is likely damaged?
Hard
Answer

Broca's area (in the left frontal lobe) — controls speech production; Wernicke's area controls comprehension

This illustrates brain lateralisation: specific functions are localised in specific regions.
Why does damage to the left hemisphere of the brain affect the right side of the body?
Hard
Answer

Motor and sensory nerve fibres from each hemisphere cross over (decussate) in the medulla — left brain controls right body side

Contralateral control is a fundamental feature of vertebrate brain organisation.
Explain the role of the thalamus as a relay station.
Medium
Answer

All sensory signals (except smell) pass through the thalamus, which sorts and routes them to appropriate cortical areas

The thalamus acts as a switchboard — damage impairs sensory processing.
How does alcohol affect the brain's control of coordination?
Medium
Answer

Alcohol suppresses cerebellum function → impaired balance and motor coordination; also affects frontal lobe → impaired judgment

This explains why drunk drivers weave and have impaired decision-making.
Explain how memory formation might involve synapse strengthening (long-term potentiation).
Hard
Answer

Repeated activation of synapses → more neurotransmitter receptors → stronger connection → easier future activation; this synaptic strengthening is called LTP

LTP is the leading cellular mechanism for learning and memory — 'neurons that fire together wire together.'
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: fMRI brain scanning has allowed scientists to map brain function. What are the limitations of this technology?
Hard
Answer

fMRI measures blood flow (indirect measure of activity), not neurons firing directly; limited time resolution; expensive; hard to study moving subjects

MYP Criterion D: evaluating scientific tools — understanding what technology can and cannot tell us.
Reflect: The brain continues developing until age 25. What implications does this have for adolescent decision-making and risk?
Hard
Answer

Prefrontal cortex (judgment, impulse control) matures last; adolescents rely more on amygdala (emotion) → impulsive decisions, higher risk-taking — has implications for legal age limits and education

MYP Criterion D: applying neuroscience to societal and ethical questions.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is the central nervous system (CNS)?
Easy
Answer

The brain and spinal cord — the main processing centre of the nervous system

The CNS integrates information and sends out responses.
What is the peripheral nervous system (PNS)?
Easy
Answer

All nerves outside the brain and spinal cord — connects the CNS to the rest of the body

Includes cranial nerves, spinal nerves, sensory and motor nerve fibres.
Distinguish between the somatic and autonomic nervous systems.
Easy
Answer

Somatic: controls voluntary skeletal muscles; Autonomic: controls involuntary organs (heart, lungs, gut, glands)

Somatic = conscious control; Autonomic = unconscious automatic control.
What is the difference between sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions?
Easy
Answer

Sympathetic: 'fight or flight' — increases heart rate, dilates pupils, slows digestion; Parasympathetic: 'rest and digest' — opposite effects

The two divisions maintain balance by opposing each other.
What does grey matter in the spinal cord contain?
Easy
Answer

Cell bodies of neurons and relay neurons — the processing region

Grey matter is where synaptic integration occurs; white matter contains myelinated axons.
How many pairs of spinal nerves does the human spinal cord have?
Easy
Answer

31 pairs (8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal)

Each pair serves a specific region of the body for sensory and motor functions.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A spinal injury at the thoracic level leaves the legs paralysed. Explain using spinal cord anatomy.
Hard
Answer

Injury severs motor and sensory pathways below injury level; signals from brain cannot reach leg muscles; sensory signals from legs cannot reach brain

Injury level determines which body parts are affected — lower injury = less paralysis.
During extreme fear, your heart races and pupils dilate. Which division of the autonomic NS is responsible?
Medium
Answer

Sympathetic division — 'fight or flight' response; adrenaline also released by adrenal glands

The body prepares for action: more blood to muscles, better vision, glucose released.
Explain why a person with a severed spinal cord can still show spinal reflexes below the injury.
Hard
Answer

Spinal reflexes are processed locally in the spinal cord — they don't need brain input; severing only cuts communication to/from brain, not the local reflex circuitry

This is why patients with spinal cord injuries still show knee-jerk reflexes.
How does the autonomic nervous system maintain blood pressure during exercise?
Hard
Answer

Sympathetic neurons increase heart rate and constrict blood vessels to non-essential organs; baroreceptors detect pressure changes and feedback to medulla for fine-tuning

Autonomic regulation maintains blood supply to exercising muscles while protecting vital organs.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Meditation is said to activate the parasympathetic nervous system. What measurable changes would confirm this?
Hard
Answer

Lower heart rate, reduced blood pressure, increased skin temperature (vasodilation), decreased cortisol levels — all measurable physiological markers of parasympathetic dominance

MYP Criterion D: testing a health claim using physiological measurements.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a hormone?
Easy
Answer

A chemical messenger secreted by endocrine glands directly into the blood; acts on distant target organs

Hormones are carried in blood and affect only cells with specific receptors for them.
Which gland is called the 'master gland'?
Easy
Answer

The pituitary gland (hypophysis) — controls other endocrine glands by releasing tropic hormones

Located at the base of the brain; regulated by the hypothalamus above it.
Name the hormones produced by the pancreas and their functions.
Easy
Answer

Insulin (lowers blood glucose) and Glucagon (raises blood glucose)

Together they maintain blood glucose homeostasis — both secreted by the Islets of Langerhans.
What is the function of adrenaline?
Easy
Answer

Increases heart rate, dilates pupils, releases glucose — prepares body for fight or flight

Secreted by adrenal medulla in response to stress; also called epinephrine.
Where is thyroxine produced and what does it regulate?
Easy
Answer

Thyroid gland; regulates basal metabolic rate (BMR), growth, and development

Too little thyroxine → hypothyroidism (low metabolism); too much → hyperthyroidism.
What is a target organ?
Easy
Answer

An organ that responds to a specific hormone because it has the matching receptors

Hormones travel throughout the body but only affect cells with the right receptor proteins.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Explain how insulin and glucagon work together to maintain blood glucose homeostasis.
Hard
Answer

After meal: blood glucose rises → pancreas secretes insulin → cells absorb glucose → blood glucose falls; if too low → glucagon secreted → liver releases glucose — negative feedback loop

This dual-hormone system keeps blood glucose between 70-100 mg/dL.
A person has consistently elevated blood glucose and cannot produce insulin. Identify the condition and treatment.
Medium
Answer

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus; treatment: insulin injections, blood glucose monitoring, controlled diet

In Type 1 DM, immune system destroys insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.
Explain the role of the pituitary gland in puberty.
Hard
Answer

Pituitary secretes FSH and LH → gonads produce oestrogen/testosterone → secondary sexual characteristics develop; growth hormone → growth spurt

The pituitary orchestrates puberty by triggering the gonads and other glands.
Why does stress cause digestive problems? Use nervous and endocrine system knowledge.
Hard
Answer

Sympathetic NS (fight/flight) suppresses digestive activity; cortisol (adrenal cortex) reduces immune function and alters gut motility — chronic stress disrupts digestion chronically

Chronic activation of stress response diverts resources away from maintenance systems.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Anabolic steroids are used by some athletes to build muscle. What are the physiological risks?
Hard
Answer

Anabolic steroids mimic testosterone → liver damage, cardiovascular disease, hormonal imbalance, infertility, aggression, stunted growth in adolescents

MYP Criterion D: evaluating ethics and risks of hormone manipulation for performance enhancement.
Investigate: Design a study to test whether cortisol levels are higher in students before exams.
Hard
Answer

Collect saliva samples before and after exam period; assay cortisol levels; compare; use control group with no exam; consider confounding variables

MYP Criterion B: study design with controls, ethical considerations, and hormone biochemistry.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What are plant hormones (phytohormones)?
Easy
Answer

Chemical messengers in plants that regulate growth and responses to environmental stimuli

Unlike animal hormones, plant hormones are not secreted by specific glands.
What is phototropism?
Easy
Answer

Growth of a plant toward (positive) or away (negative) from light

Stems show positive phototropism; roots show negative phototropism.
How does auxin cause bending toward light?
Easy
Answer

Auxin moves to the shaded side → cells on shaded side elongate more → stem bends toward light

This unequal auxin distribution and resulting differential growth explains phototropism.
What is gravitropism (geotropism)?
Easy
Answer

Growth response to gravity — roots grow downward (positive), shoots grow upward (negative)

Gravity causes auxin to accumulate on the lower side; roots are highly sensitive and inhibited; shoots are stimulated.
Name five major plant hormones and one function each.
Easy
Answer

Auxin (cell elongation), Gibberellin (stem growth/seed germination), Cytokinin (cell division), Abscisic acid (dormancy/stomatal closure), Ethylene (fruit ripening)

These five cover the key hormonal regulators of plant development.
What is thigmotropism?
Easy
Answer

Growth response to touch — e.g., tendrils of climbing plants coil around supports

Mechanical stimulus causes differential cell growth — cells on the contact side stop elongating.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Explain Darwin's experiment with oat coleoptiles that led to discovery of auxin.
Hard
Answer

Darwin showed coleoptile tips are the site of phototropism sensing; Went later showed a chemical diffuses from the tip causing bending — named auxin

This classic experiment chain established plant hormone biology over 1880-1926.
Why do farmers use synthetic auxins (herbicides) at high concentrations to kill weeds?
Hard
Answer

High auxin concentrations inhibit rather than stimulate growth; broadleaf weeds are more sensitive than narrow-leaf crops — selective weed killer

Auxin effect is dose-dependent: low = promotes growth; high = toxic to plants.
Explain how gibberellins cause seed germination.
Medium
Answer

Gibberellins trigger synthesis of amylase → breaks down stored starch to sugars → provides energy for embryo growth

Gibberellin is produced by the embryo; it activates mobilisation of seed food reserves.
How is ethylene used in the fruit industry?
Medium
Answer

Ethylene gas is applied to picked unripe fruits (banana, tomato) to accelerate ripening during transport

Ethylene triggers the fruit's own ripening enzymes — a positive feedback loop.
Explain how abscisic acid (ABA) causes stomatal closure during drought.
Hard
Answer

Water stress → ABA produced in leaves → guard cells lose water (via K⁺ efflux) → stomata close → reduces water loss by transpiration

ABA is the plant's stress hormone — its name refers to its role in leaf abscission (dropping).
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: Design an experiment to demonstrate phototropism in bean seedlings.
Hard
Answer

Grow seedlings in box with single-sided light opening; observe bending direction after 48-72 h; repeat with foil-covered tips as control — tips must be exposed for response

MYP Criterion B: experimental design demonstrating role of the shoot tip in phototropism.
Evaluate: Ethylene-releasing chemicals are sprayed on crops to synchronise ripening for harvest. Assess the benefits and risks.
Hard
Answer

Benefits: uniform ripening for mechanical harvest; Risks: if applied too early → overripening; environmental ethylene drift affects non-target plants

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is diabetes mellitus?
Easy
Answer

A metabolic disorder characterised by elevated blood glucose due to insufficient insulin production or action

Type 1: autoimmune; Type 2: insulin resistance; both result in hyperglycaemia.
What causes goitre?
Easy
Answer

Iodine deficiency → thyroid cannot make thyroxine → pituitary keeps releasing TSH → thyroid enlarges

Adding iodine to table salt (iodised salt) prevents goitre — a simple public health intervention.
What is the effect of excess growth hormone in adults vs children?
Easy
Answer

Children: gigantism (excess height); Adults: acromegaly (enlarged hands, feet, jaw — bones can still grow at certain sites)

Growth hormone is from the anterior pituitary; excess in adulthood cannot lengthen long bones (growth plates closed).
What is hypothyroidism and what are its symptoms?
Easy
Answer

Underactive thyroid → too little thyroxine → low metabolic rate, weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance, slow heart rate

Treatment: synthetic thyroxine (levothyroxine) tablets.
What is the role of iodine in hormone production?
Easy
Answer

Iodine is essential for synthesis of thyroxine (T3 contains 3 iodine atoms; T4 contains 4)

Without iodine, the thyroid cannot function — iodine deficiency is the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Distinguish between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes in terms of cause, treatment, and onset.
Hard
Answer

Type 1: immune destroys beta cells → no insulin → usually childhood onset → insulin injections; Type 2: insulin resistance → usually adult onset → diet/exercise/oral medication first

Type 2 is strongly linked to obesity and lifestyle — the global epidemic of Type 2 DM.
Explain the negative feedback loop that regulates thyroxine production.
Hard
Answer

Low T4 → hypothalamus releases TRH → pituitary releases TSH → thyroid releases T4 → high T4 inhibits TRH and TSH — negative feedback restores balance

This hypothalamus-pituitary-thyroid axis is a model of hormonal negative feedback.
A child shows excessive growth at age 12. Blood tests reveal elevated GH. What is the diagnosis and likely cause?
Medium
Answer

Gigantism; likely caused by a benign tumour of the pituitary gland (pituitary adenoma) secreting excess GH

Early diagnosis allows treatment — surgery, radiation, or GH-blocking drugs.
How does Addison's disease affect the body?
Hard
Answer

Adrenal cortex damaged → insufficient cortisol and aldosterone → low blood pressure, fatigue, weight loss, hypoglycaemia, hyperpigmentation

Cortisol is needed for stress response and glucose regulation; aldosterone for sodium retention.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: The global incidence of Type 2 diabetes is rising rapidly. What are the scientific and societal factors?
Hard
Answer

Scientific: insulin resistance from excess adipose tissue; dietary sugar/fat excess; sedentary lifestyle → chronic hyperglycaemia; Societal: food industry, urban design, poverty, food access

MYP Criterion D: linking biology to public health, economics, and social determinants of health.
Reflect: If you were advising a government on reducing Type 2 diabetes rates, what science-based interventions would you recommend?
Hard
Answer

Sugar taxes, food labelling, school nutrition programs, urban planning for walking, subsidised healthy food, diabetes screening — evidence-based public health policy

MYP Criterion D: applying scientific knowledge to create societal-level interventions.

Hard
Answer

Randomise pre-diabetics to exercise group vs control; measure fasting glucose before and after 12-week program; control for diet; use statistical analysis

MYP Criterion B: clinical study design with randomisation, controls, ethical approval, and outcome measures.
