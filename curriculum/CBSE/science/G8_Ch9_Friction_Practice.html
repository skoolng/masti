SET A Easy – What is Friction (8 Questions)

Define friction in your own words. Easy
Show Answer

Friction is the force that opposes the relative motion between two surfaces in contact. It acts along the surface and in the direction opposite to the motion (or attempted motion).

Is friction a contact force or a non-contact force? Explain. Easy
Show Answer

Friction is a contact force because it only acts when two surfaces are in direct physical contact with each other. Without contact, there is no friction.

In which direction does friction act relative to the direction of motion? Easy
Show Answer

Friction always acts opposite to the direction of motion (or the direction in which the object tends to move). It opposes relative motion between the surfaces.

What causes friction at the microscopic level? Easy
Show Answer

All surfaces have tiny irregularities (bumps and hollows) even if they appear smooth. When two surfaces are in contact, these irregularities interlock, and the resistance to motion caused by these interlocking irregularities is friction.

Can friction exist between a solid and a liquid? Give one example. Easy
Show Answer

Yes. Friction (fluid friction or drag) exists between a solid and a liquid. Example: a swimmer experiences water resistance (drag) while moving through water.

What happens to an object's motion when the applied force exactly equals friction? Easy
Show Answer

When applied force equals friction, the net force is zero. By Newton's first law, the object either remains at rest or moves with constant velocity (no acceleration).

Name the scientist whose laws of motion are closely linked to the study of friction. Easy
Show Answer

Sir Isaac Newton. His three laws of motion form the basis for understanding how friction affects the motion of objects.

State one difference between friction and gravity. Easy
Show Answer

Friction is a contact force that requires surfaces to be touching, while gravity is a non-contact force that acts at a distance between masses without physical contact.

SET B Medium – What is Friction (8 Questions)

Explain why friction is described as a "self-adjusting" force. Medium
Show Answer

Friction adjusts its magnitude to exactly oppose the applied force up to its maximum (limiting) value. If you push lightly, friction is small; push harder, friction increases to match — it self-adjusts until it reaches its maximum limit.

A book is placed on a table. Describe the forces acting on it, including friction. Medium
Show Answer

The book has weight (gravity) acting downward and normal force acting upward from the table. Since the book is at rest, there is no horizontal applied force, so static friction is zero (it's self-adjusting — if no force tries to move it, friction is zero).

Why does a ball eventually stop rolling on a flat surface even if no one pushes it? Medium
Show Answer

Rolling friction between the ball and the surface, along with air resistance, continuously opposes the ball's motion and removes kinetic energy, slowing it down until it stops.

How does friction behave differently on polished vs. rough surfaces? Medium
Show Answer

Polished surfaces have fewer and smaller interlocking irregularities, so friction is lower. Rough surfaces have large interlocking bumps, creating greater friction. The smoother the surface, the less friction (up to a point — extremely smooth surfaces at atomic level can have higher friction due to adhesion).

Distinguish between the frictional force when an object is just about to move vs. when it is already moving. Medium
Show Answer

Just before moving: static (limiting) friction is at its maximum. Once moving: kinetic friction acts, which is slightly less than maximum static friction. That is why it's harder to start moving an object than to keep it moving.

If two surfaces are pressed harder together (greater normal force), what happens to friction and why? Medium
Show Answer

Friction increases. Greater normal force pushes the surface irregularities deeper into each other, increasing interlocking and therefore the force needed to overcome friction.

Why does oil on a floor make it more slippery (less friction)? Medium
Show Answer

Oil fills the gaps between surface irregularities, preventing the solid surfaces from directly interlocking. The fluid layer reduces contact between solid surfaces, dramatically reducing friction.

A heavy box and a light box are made of the same material. Which is harder to push and why? Medium
Show Answer

The heavy box is harder to push. Its greater weight creates a larger normal force, which increases friction. Since friction ∝ normal force (F = µN), more weight means more friction to overcome.

SET C Hard – What is Friction (8 Questions)

Explain the molecular theory of friction. Why does even a "smooth" surface have friction? Hard
Show Answer

At the molecular level, all surfaces have microscopic peaks (asperities). When surfaces touch, only these peaks make actual contact (true contact area is far less than apparent area). Adhesive forces (van der Waals) between molecules at contact points resist sliding. Even "smooth" surfaces have atomic-scale irregularities and strong adhesion, hence friction.

If friction always opposes motion, how can a car accelerate forward? Isn't friction stopping it? Hard
Show Answer

The car's drive wheels push backward on the road; static friction from the road pushes forward on the wheels (Newton's 3rd law). This forward friction on the tyres IS what propels the car. Friction does not oppose the car's forward motion here — it IS the driving force that allows forward acceleration.

A surface has coefficient of friction µ = 0.4. If a 5 kg block rests on it, calculate the maximum static friction force. (g = 10 m/s²) Hard
Show Answer

Normal force N = mg = 5 × 10 = 50 N. Maximum static friction = µN = 0.4 × 50 = 20 N. Any applied force up to 20 N will be balanced by static friction and the block will not move.

Why is it that extremely smooth (polished) surfaces sometimes have MORE friction than moderately rough ones? Hard
Show Answer

When surfaces are highly polished, the true contact area increases enormously (asperities are removed). Intermolecular adhesion forces become dominant over a much larger contact area, causing friction to increase. This is the basis of cold welding in space or between ultra-smooth metal surfaces.

Analyse: A 10 kg box is pushed with 30 N on a surface where µ = 0.25. Does the box move? What is the acceleration? (g = 10 m/s²) Hard
Show Answer

N = 100 N; Max friction = 0.25 × 100 = 25 N. Applied force (30 N) > friction (25 N), so the box moves. Net force = 30 − 25 = 5 N. Acceleration a = F/m = 5/10 = 0.5 m/s².

Why does friction on a wet road differ from a dry road? How does this affect braking distance? Hard
Show Answer

Water acts as a lubricant between the tyre and road, reducing interlocking of surface irregularities and lowering the coefficient of friction. Lower friction means less braking force, so the car takes longer to stop. Braking distance increases significantly — sometimes 2–3× longer on wet roads.

The coefficient of friction between two surfaces is 0.3. If the normal force is doubled, how does the friction force change? Explain using the formula. Hard
Show Answer

F_friction = µN. If N is doubled to 2N: F_friction = µ × 2N = 2µN. Friction doubles. Since µ is a constant property of the surface pair, friction is directly proportional to normal force — doubling N doubles friction.

Compare the work done against friction when the same box is dragged 5 m at constant speed with µ = 0.3 and mass = 4 kg. (g = 10 m/s²) Hard
Show Answer

N = mg = 40 N. Friction force = µN = 0.3 × 40 = 12 N. Work done against friction = F × d = 12 × 5 = 60 J. This energy is converted to heat (thermal energy) at the surfaces.

SET A Easy – Types of Friction (8 Questions)

Name the four main types of friction.Easy
Show Answer

Static friction, kinetic (sliding) friction, rolling friction, and fluid friction (drag).

Which type of friction acts on a book lying still on a table?Easy
Show Answer

Static friction (specifically zero static friction if no horizontal force is applied, but the type that would resist any attempted motion is static friction).

Which type of friction acts when a box is sliding across the floor?Easy
Show Answer

Kinetic friction (also called sliding friction) acts when an object slides across a surface.

Why is rolling friction much less than sliding friction?Easy
Show Answer

A rolling object touches the surface at only one point at a time, so there is much less interlocking of surface irregularities compared to sliding, resulting in much less friction.

What is fluid friction? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

Fluid friction (drag) is the friction force exerted by a fluid (liquid or gas) on an object moving through it. Example: air resistance on a moving car.

Arrange in order from greatest to least: rolling friction, static friction, kinetic friction.Easy
Show Answer

Static friction (greatest) > Kinetic friction > Rolling friction (least). This is why wheels were invented — to replace sliding with rolling and reduce friction.

What type of friction does a fish experience while swimming?Easy
Show Answer

Fluid friction (drag) from water opposing the fish's motion through the liquid.

Which type of friction is felt just before an object starts to move?Easy
Show Answer

Limiting static friction — the maximum value of static friction, reached just at the moment before motion begins.

SET B Medium – Types of Friction (8 Questions)

Explain why it is easier to pull a heavy box on wheels than to drag it directly on the floor.Medium
Show Answer

Wheels convert sliding friction into rolling friction. Rolling friction is much smaller than sliding friction (about 100× less), so much less force is needed to move the box.

A car tyre is spinning on ice without gripping. Which type of friction is acting? Why is it ineffective?Medium
Show Answer

Kinetic (sliding) friction. Ice is very smooth, so the coefficient of kinetic friction is extremely low, providing almost no grip. Static friction (needed for traction) cannot be established on the slippery surface.

Why is it harder to start pushing a stationary box than to keep it moving once it's started?Medium
Show Answer

To start motion, you must overcome maximum static friction (highest friction value). Once moving, only kinetic friction acts, which is less than maximum static friction — so less force is needed to maintain motion.

How does fluid friction differ from solid surface friction in terms of the factors that affect it?Medium
Show Answer

Solid friction depends on normal force and surface roughness (µ). Fluid friction (drag) depends on the object's speed, shape (streamlining), cross-sectional area, and fluid viscosity — it increases with speed, unlike solid friction which is largely speed-independent.

A parachutist reaches terminal velocity. Explain what this means in terms of friction types.Medium
Show Answer

Terminal velocity is when fluid friction (air drag) equals gravity. As speed increases, air drag increases until it exactly balances the downward gravitational force. Net force is zero, so acceleration stops and velocity stays constant (terminal velocity).

Compare the friction acting on a bicycle tyre with that on a skateboard wheel.Medium
Show Answer

Both experience rolling friction. However, bicycle tyres are wider and softer (deform more at contact point), creating a larger contact area and slightly more rolling friction. Skateboard wheels are harder and create a smaller deformation, giving less rolling friction on smooth surfaces.

Why does increasing speed increase fluid drag but not increase solid sliding friction significantly?Medium
Show Answer

Solid friction depends mainly on surface interlocking (constant for a given surface pair), independent of speed. Fluid drag depends on how fast a fluid must be pushed aside — at higher speeds, more fluid is displaced per second, requiring more force, so drag increases (proportional to v² at high speeds).

What type of friction acts between a car tyre and road during normal (non-skidding) driving?Medium
Show Answer

Static friction. The tyre's contact patch is momentarily stationary relative to the road (like a foot on the ground). The car moves forward because static friction pushes the car forward. Only when the tyre skids does kinetic friction take over.

SET C Hard – Types of Friction (8 Questions)

Analyse: A 10 kg block requires 40 N to start moving and 30 N to keep moving. Calculate µ_static and µ_kinetic. (g=10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

N = mg = 100 N. µ_static = F_static/N = 40/100 = 0.40. µ_kinetic = F_kinetic/N = 30/100 = 0.30. As expected, µ_static > µ_kinetic.

Why is rolling friction not zero even though a wheel theoretically touches at only one point?Hard
Show Answer

In reality, surfaces deform under load — the wheel and surface flatten slightly, creating a small contact area. Energy is lost as heat through this deformation (hysteresis). Also, micro-adhesion and micro-sliding at the contact zone contribute. These effects together produce rolling friction despite the theoretical point contact.

A skydiver of mass 70 kg reaches terminal velocity at 55 m/s. What is the air drag force at this point? (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

At terminal velocity, drag = weight = mg = 70 × 10 = 700 N. Net force = 0, acceleration = 0. The drag force exactly balances gravity.

Explain why anti-lock braking systems (ABS) keep the car in static (not kinetic) friction during emergency braking.Hard
Show Answer

Kinetic friction (skidding) is less than static friction. ABS rapidly pumps brakes to prevent the wheel from locking up (which would cause sliding/kinetic friction). By keeping the wheel just rolling (static friction), ABS maximises braking force and also maintains steering control.

Compare fluid friction on a sphere vs. a streamlined body at the same speed. Explain the difference quantitatively if drag ∝ (cross-sectional area × drag coefficient).Hard
Show Answer

A sphere has a high drag coefficient (C_d ≈ 0.47) and larger wake. A streamlined body (C_d ≈ 0.04) has a much lower coefficient. For the same frontal area and speed, drag = ½ρv²C_d·A — the sphere experiences ~12× more drag than a streamlined teardrop shape. This is why aircraft and cars are streamlined.

A wheel of radius 0.3 m rolls without slipping at 6 m/s. At what angular velocity does it spin? What type of friction prevents slipping?Hard
Show Answer

v = rω → ω = v/r = 6/0.3 = 20 rad/s. Static friction between the tyre and road prevents slipping — it ensures the contact point is momentarily at rest (no sliding), maintaining the rolling without slipping condition.

Critically evaluate: "Reducing friction always improves machine efficiency." Is this always true?Hard
Show Answer

Mostly true for harmful friction (engine parts, conveyors). But not always: without sufficient friction, brakes fail, tyres skid, bolts loosen, people slip. Some machines rely on friction (clutches, brakes, belt drives). So blindly reducing all friction can reduce efficiency in systems that depend on it. Context matters.

How does the type of friction change during the phases of a 100 m sprint: start, mid-race, and finish?Hard
Show Answer

Start: static friction between shoe and track is crucial — sprinters push back hard, static friction propels them forward. Mid-race: at high speed, air drag (fluid friction) becomes significant; shoe-track friction remains static (no slipping). Finish: as runner slows or decelerates, braking force requires static friction; air resistance decreases with speed.

SET A Easy – Factors Affecting Friction (8 Questions)

What are the two main factors that affect the magnitude of friction?Easy
Show Answer

1. Nature of the surfaces in contact (roughness/smoothness — the coefficient of friction µ). 2. The normal force pressing the surfaces together (related to the weight of the object).

Write the formula for friction force and define each symbol.Easy
Show Answer

F = µN, where F = friction force (N), µ = coefficient of friction (dimensionless, no units), N = normal force (N, the force pressing surfaces together, equal to weight on a flat surface).

Does the area of contact between the two surfaces affect friction? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

Surprisingly, no — for normal solid friction, the area of contact does not affect friction. A wide flat surface and a narrow edge of the same object experience the same friction (as long as normal force is constant). This is Amontons' Law.

What does a coefficient of friction (µ) of 0 mean?Easy
Show Answer

A µ of 0 means a perfectly frictionless surface — there is no friction between the surfaces regardless of the normal force. This is a theoretical ideal; no real surface has µ = 0.

Is the coefficient of friction (µ) ever greater than 1? What would that mean?Easy
Show Answer

Yes, µ can be greater than 1. This means the friction force exceeds the normal force. It occurs with very sticky or high-grip materials (e.g., rubber on rubber, µ ≈ 1–4) or soft metals. It does not violate any physical law.

How does the weight of an object affect the friction it experiences on a surface?Easy
Show Answer

Heavier objects exert more normal force on the surface. Since F = µN, a larger normal force directly increases friction. A heavier object experiences proportionally more friction.

Which surface pair would have a higher µ: rubber on concrete or ice on steel? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

Rubber on concrete has a much higher µ (≈0.6–0.8) than ice on steel (≈0.03–0.05). Rubber is flexible and grips rough concrete well. Ice is very smooth and acts almost like a liquid lubricant on steel.

What unit does the coefficient of friction have?Easy
Show Answer

The coefficient of friction (µ) is dimensionless — it has no unit. It is a ratio of two forces (F/N), so the units cancel out.

SET B Medium – Factors Affecting Friction (8 Questions)

An object is pushed across a surface. If the weight is tripled but µ remains the same, how does the friction force change?Medium
Show Answer

Friction force = µN = µmg. If weight (mg) is tripled, N triples, so friction triples. F_new = µ × 3N = 3µN = 3 × F_original.

Two blocks (same material, same weight) are placed on the same surface — one flat and one on its edge (smaller area). Compare the friction on each.Medium
Show Answer

Friction is the same for both. Since both have the same weight (same N) and same µ (same materials), F = µN is equal regardless of contact area. Area does not affect friction (Amontons' Law).

How does temperature affect friction in practice? (Consider a car tyre warming up.)Medium
Show Answer

As tyres warm up, rubber becomes more pliable and conforms better to surface irregularities, increasing true contact area and µ (that's why racing tyres need warming — "cold tyres" have less grip). At very high temperatures, rubber can melt/degrade, reducing friction. Temperature changes µ by altering material properties.

Why does adding more weight to a sled increase friction but not change the coefficient µ?Medium
Show Answer

µ depends only on the materials of the two surfaces (their roughness/chemistry) — not on force. Adding weight increases N, which increases F = µN proportionally. The surfaces haven't changed, so µ stays constant. More weight → more friction, but the ratio F/N (which is µ) remains constant.

A wet road has µ = 0.25 versus µ = 0.7 for a dry road. If a 1200 kg car brakes, calculate the braking force in each case. (g = 10 m/s²)Medium
Show Answer

N = mg = 12000 N. Dry: F = 0.7 × 12000 = 8400 N. Wet: F = 0.25 × 12000 = 3000 N. Dry road provides 2.8× more braking force — crucial for safety.

Does the speed at which an object slides affect kinetic friction? Explain why or why not.Medium
Show Answer

For most solid-solid friction, kinetic friction is approximately independent of speed (constant µ_kinetic). This is a simplification — at very high speeds, some materials (e.g., rubber) show speed-dependent friction. But in basic physics, we treat kinetic friction as speed-independent.

Why might the same object have different µ values on different surfaces even at the same weight?Medium
Show Answer

µ depends on the materials of both surfaces — their molecular composition, hardness, and surface texture. Wood on carpet has different interlocking and adhesion than wood on tiles. µ is a property of the surface pair, not just one object.

How does applying sandpaper to a smooth surface change the friction and why?Medium
Show Answer

Sandpaper increases surface roughness by creating more and larger irregularities. This increases interlocking between surfaces, raising µ. The friction force F = µN increases because µ has increased, even if N is unchanged.

SET C Hard – Factors Affecting Friction (8 Questions)

The Bowden-Tabor model explains friction through adhesion. Describe this model and how it reconciles with Amontons' Law.Hard
Show Answer

Bowden-Tabor: true contact occurs only at asperity tips. Real contact area ∝ N (more load → more asperities plastically deform → larger real contact area). Friction ∝ real contact area × shear strength. Since real contact area ∝ N, friction ∝ N — exactly Amontons' Law (F = µN). µ = shear strength / hardness, a material property, explaining why µ is independent of apparent area or load.

A 5 kg block on a 30° inclined plane just starts to slide. What is µ_static? (g = 10 m/s², sin30°=0.5, cos30°=0.866)Hard
Show Answer

At the point of sliding: friction = component of gravity along slope. µ_static × N = mg·sin30°; N = mg·cos30°. µ_static = mg·sin30° / mg·cos30° = tan30° = 0.5/0.866 = 0.577. (µ = tanθ at the critical angle.)

Why do Formula 1 racing tyres have a much higher µ than regular road tyres, even though both are rubber?Hard
Show Answer

F1 tyres use a softer rubber compound (lower shear strength needed, but high surface energy adhesion), wider cross-section (more real contact area at high downforce loads), and generate heat to maintain optimal operating temperature (~100°C). The rubber flows into micro-asperities, dramatically increasing real contact area and adhesion, giving µ values of 1.5–2.0 vs ~0.7 for road tyres.

Calculate the angle of an inclined plane at which a block with µ = 0.6 will just slide. What is this angle called?Hard
Show Answer

At the critical angle: µ = tan(θ). θ = arctan(0.6) = 31°. This is called the "angle of friction" or "angle of repose" — the maximum slope angle before sliding occurs. Any angle greater than this causes the block to slide.

Derive the formula for acceleration of a block sliding down a frictionless vs. friction incline (angle θ, mass m).Hard
Show Answer

Forces along incline: mg·sinθ (down the slope) − f (friction up slope). Frictionless: a = g·sinθ. With friction (f = µmg·cosθ): Net force = mg·sinθ − µmg·cosθ = mg(sinθ − µcosθ). Acceleration a = g(sinθ − µcosθ). Friction reduces acceleration by g·µcosθ.

Two surfaces have µ_static = 0.5. An object of mass 8 kg is on a horizontal surface. A horizontal force of 35 N is applied. Does it move? Find net force or friction. (g=10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

Max static friction = µN = 0.5 × 80 = 40 N. Applied force = 35 N < 40 N. Object does NOT move. Static friction = 35 N (self-adjusts to match applied force). Net force = 0.

Explain why the coefficient of friction is dimensionless and what physical information it encodes about a surface pair.Hard
Show Answer

µ = F_friction / F_normal = (N) / (N) — force divided by force, units cancel. Physically, µ encodes the ratio of tangential resistance to normal load. It reflects the surface pair's combined properties: roughness (mechanical interlocking), adhesion (molecular attraction), and hardness (deformation behaviour). A single dimensionless number captures all these complex interactions.

A 2 kg block is on top of a 5 kg block which is on a floor. µ between blocks = 0.3; µ between lower block and floor = 0.4. A force pushes only the lower block. Find max force to move lower block without disturbing upper block. (g=10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

Normal on lower block from floor = (2+5)×10 = 70 N. Friction floor on lower block (max) = 0.4×70 = 28 N. Upper block exerts friction on lower block (backward): max = 0.3×2×10 = 6 N. To move lower block only: Applied force must overcome floor friction (28 N) but be less than force that would accelerate upper block. For lower block not to accelerate upper: Applied force − 28 (floor) − 6 (upper block friction back on lower) = net on lower block. This is a complex system; key answer: Max friction from floor = 28 N, friction between blocks = 6 N.

SET A Easy – Friction as Necessary Evil (8 Questions)

Give three examples where friction is useful/necessary in daily life.Easy
Show Answer

1. Walking — friction between shoe and ground prevents slipping. 2. Writing — friction between pen/pencil and paper allows marks to form. 3. Braking — friction between brake pads and wheel disc stops vehicles.

Give three examples where friction is harmful.Easy
Show Answer

1. Engine wear — friction between moving metal parts causes wear and damage. 2. Energy waste — friction converts useful mechanical energy into heat. 3. Wear of tyres/shoe soles — friction gradually wears them down.

Why is friction called a "necessary evil"?Easy
Show Answer

Friction is called a "necessary evil" because we cannot live without it (it enables walking, gripping, braking) yet it also causes problems (heat generation, energy loss, wear). It is both essential and harmful — hence "necessary evil."

Why would it be impossible to walk without friction?Easy
Show Answer

When walking, your foot pushes backward on the ground. Friction pushes your foot (and you) forward. Without friction, your foot would slip backward and you could not propel yourself forward — walking would be impossible (like walking on ice).

How does friction help when writing with a pencil?Easy
Show Answer

Friction between the pencil tip (graphite) and the paper surface causes tiny graphite particles to break off and adhere to the paper, leaving a visible mark. Without friction, the pencil would glide without leaving any trace.

What happens to the "lost" energy when friction opposes motion?Easy
Show Answer

The kinetic energy lost to friction is converted into heat (thermal energy). This is why surfaces get warm when rubbed together. The energy is not destroyed (law of conservation of energy) but transformed into a less useful form.

Why do machine parts need to be replaced regularly?Easy
Show Answer

Friction between moving metal parts gradually wears them down, removing material and changing the shape/size of components. Over time this wear makes parts less effective or causes them to fail, requiring replacement.

Name two ways in which friction causes energy loss in a car engine.Easy
Show Answer

1. Friction between pistons and cylinder walls generates heat, wasting fuel energy. 2. Friction in the gearbox and bearings also converts mechanical energy into heat rather than propelling the car.

SET B Medium – Friction as Necessary Evil (8 Questions)

Explain why a car cannot brake effectively on ice and what this tells us about friction's benefits.Medium
Show Answer

Ice has an extremely low µ, so even pressing the brake hard produces very little braking force (F = µN; µ is near zero). The car cannot decelerate quickly and skids. This demonstrates that friction between tyre and road is essential for braking — without it, vehicles are uncontrollable.

Machines in factories need oiling regularly. Explain why in terms of both benefits and costs of friction.Medium
Show Answer

Without oil: friction between metal parts generates heat and wear — harmful effects. Lubrication reduces friction, decreasing heat generation (prevents overheating), reducing wear (extending machine life), and increasing efficiency (more energy goes to useful work). The cost of not oiling (breakdowns, replacements) far outweighs the benefit of any grip from friction in this context.

How does friction benefit rock climbers but harm the rock face over time?Medium
Show Answer

Benefit: Friction between rock shoes (high-µ rubber) and rock surface allows climbers to grip footholds and handholds, preventing falls — essential for safety. Harm: repeated climbing over popular routes gradually polishes the rock surface, reducing its roughness and thus µ, making it more dangerous for future climbers and damaging the natural rock.

Explain why a car engine converts only about 25–30% of fuel energy into motion. Where does the rest go?Medium
Show Answer

About 35% is lost as heat through the cooling system, ~35% exits as hot exhaust gases, and ~5–10% is lost to friction in engine parts, gearbox, and transmission. Only ~25–30% becomes useful mechanical motion. Friction in moving parts is a significant contributor to this inefficiency.

Why is a matchstick designed to use friction productively? Explain the chemistry and physics.Medium
Show Answer

The match head contains potassium chlorate (oxidiser) and sulphur (fuel). The rough striking strip contains red phosphorus. When struck, friction generates enough heat to ignite the phosphorus on the strip, which then ignites the match head chemicals — an exothermic reaction. Physics: friction → heat → chemical activation energy → combustion.

Compare the role of friction when a cyclist is going uphill versus when braking downhill.Medium
Show Answer

Uphill: static friction between tyre and road propels the cyclist forward as they push back. Useful friction. Downhill braking: brake friction (brake pads on rim/disc) is essential to slow the bicycle safely. Useful friction. However, air resistance (fluid friction) opposes motion in both cases — somewhat harmful. Friction plays multiple roles simultaneously.

How does a gecko walk on walls and ceilings? Is this friction or something else?Medium
Show Answer

Gecko feet have millions of tiny hair-like structures (setae) with even tinier spatulae at the tips. These create enormous van der Waals adhesion forces at close range. It is primarily molecular adhesion rather than traditional surface friction, though friction also contributes to their grip sideways on walls.

Explain how friction is both essential and harmful in a car's tyre over its lifetime.Medium
Show Answer

Essential: tyre friction provides traction (acceleration), braking force, and cornering grip — safety-critical. Harmful: the same friction gradually wears down the tyre tread. Worn tyres have less grip in wet conditions and must be replaced for safety. The tyre is literally consumed by the useful friction it provides.

SET C Hard – Friction as Necessary Evil (8 Questions)

Calculate the energy wasted as heat when a 60 kg person walks 1 km, if friction force averages 12 N per step of 0.8 m (total 1250 steps).Hard
Show Answer

Work done against friction per step = 12 × 0.8 = 9.6 J. Total energy wasted = 9.6 × 1250 = 12,000 J = 12 kJ. (In reality, most energy in walking goes to raising the body's centre of mass and air resistance, but this estimates friction contribution.)

Analyse: A 1500 kg car brakes from 20 m/s to rest. If µ = 0.6 and g = 10 m/s², calculate braking distance and heat generated.Hard
Show Answer

Braking force = µmg = 0.6 × 1500 × 10 = 9000 N. Using v² = u² − 2as: 0 = 400 − 2×(9000/1500)×s → 0 = 400 − 12s → s = 33.3 m. Heat = kinetic energy = ½mv² = ½ × 1500 × 400 = 300,000 J = 300 kJ.

Why is the efficiency of an ideal frictionless machine 100%? Why is this physically impossible in practice?Hard
Show Answer

Efficiency = useful output energy / input energy × 100%. Without friction, all input energy becomes useful work — 100% efficiency. Impossible because: all real surfaces have atomic-level irregularities causing friction; quantum mechanical zero-point fluctuations mean atoms always vibrate; and electromagnetic forces between atoms always create some adhesion. Perfect smoothness/frictionlessness violates thermodynamic principles.

Critically compare friction in biological joints (hip/knee) vs. artificial steel bearings. Which is more efficient and why?Hard
Show Answer

Biological joints: µ ≈ 0.001–0.005 (among lowest known) due to synovial fluid lubrication, cartilage, and pressurised fluid film. More efficient than any engineered bearing. Steel ball bearings: µ ≈ 0.001–0.01. Biological joints are comparable or better, self-repairing, and last decades. But they wear out (arthritis) and cannot be machined to precise tolerances like bearings.

A 500 W motor drives a conveyor against 80 N friction at 5 m/s. What percentage of motor power is wasted on friction?Hard
Show Answer

Power wasted by friction = F × v = 80 × 5 = 400 W. Percentage wasted = (400/500) × 100 = 80%. Only 100 W (20%) goes to useful work. This illustrates why reducing friction in industrial machinery is economically critical.

Evaluate the statement: "A world without friction would be a better world." Construct arguments for and against.Hard
Show Answer

For: no energy losses, machines work forever, no wear, no heat buildup. Against: walking impossible, vehicles cannot brake/steer, screws and nails useless, writing impossible, buildings collapse (no grip in joints), musical instruments silent (friction makes sound), fire impossible. Conclusion: completely false — a frictionless world would be uninhabitable. Friction is fundamental to most human technology and biology.

How is the concept of "tribology" relevant to the study of friction as a necessary evil? Give two industrial applications.Hard
Show Answer

Tribology is the science of friction, wear, and lubrication between interacting surfaces. Industrial applications: 1. Engine oil design — tribologists formulate oils that minimise friction losses and wear in internal combustion engines, improving fuel efficiency by 2–5%. 2. Artificial joint design — tribologists develop ultra-low friction surfaces and lubricants for hip/knee replacements to last 20+ years.

In space, two metal surfaces in contact can "cold weld" permanently. Explain this in terms of friction theory and why it is a serious engineering concern.Hard
Show Answer

On Earth, all metal surfaces have thin oxide layers and adsorbed water/gas molecules that separate them. In the vacuum of space, these contaminant layers are absent. When two clean metal surfaces touch, their atoms are in direct contact — metallic bonds form spontaneously across the interface (cold welding). This is extreme adhesion (a form of friction at zero sliding). For spacecraft, this can seize mechanisms (valves, hinges) permanently. Engineers use ceramic coatings, solid lubricants (MoS₂), or dissimilar metals to prevent cold welding.

SET A Easy – Reducing Friction (8 Questions)

Name three methods used to reduce friction between surfaces.Easy
Show Answer

1. Lubrication (oil, grease, or graphite between surfaces). 2. Ball bearings or roller bearings (converting sliding to rolling friction). 3. Streamlining (shaping objects to reduce fluid friction/air drag).

How does oil reduce friction between engine parts?Easy
Show Answer

Oil forms a thin fluid layer between metal surfaces, preventing direct metal-to-metal contact. The surfaces slide on the oil film rather than on each other, dramatically reducing friction and wear.

What is a ball bearing and how does it reduce friction?Easy
Show Answer

A ball bearing is a set of small steel balls placed between two surfaces (inner and outer rings). Instead of the surfaces sliding against each other (high kinetic friction), the balls roll (much lower rolling friction), greatly reducing friction in rotating machinery.

What is streamlining? Give one example of a streamlined object.Easy
Show Answer

Streamlining is shaping an object so it smoothly cuts through a fluid (air or water) with minimum turbulence and drag. Examples: aeroplanes, cars with sloped bonnets, submarines, sharks, dolphins.

Why are fish and dolphins torpedo-shaped (streamlined)?Easy
Show Answer

The torpedo/teardrop shape minimises fluid friction (water drag) as they swim. A streamlined shape allows water to flow smoothly around the body without creating turbulent eddies, reducing drag and allowing faster, more energy-efficient movement.

Name a substance (other than oil) used as a lubricant. State where it is used.Easy
Show Answer

Graphite powder — used as a lubricant in locks and hinges where oil cannot be used (oil attracts dust, graphite does not). Also used in high-temperature applications where oils would burn.

Why are the undersides of skis coated with smooth wax?Easy
Show Answer

Wax fills in the microscopic pores and irregularities on the ski base, creating a very smooth surface. This reduces the coefficient of friction between the ski and snow, allowing the skier to glide faster with less effort.

How has the invention of the wheel helped reduce friction in transportation?Easy
Show Answer

Wheels replace sliding friction with rolling friction. Rolling friction is far less (up to 100× less) than sliding friction, so wheeled vehicles can be moved with much less force than dragging. This was one of humanity's most important inventions.

SET B Medium – Reducing Friction (8 Questions)

Why do cyclists wear helmets shaped like teardrops at high speeds?Medium
Show Answer

At high cycling speeds, air drag becomes a major resistance. A teardrop-shaped helmet is streamlined — it allows air to flow smoothly over and around the head without creating turbulent drag. This can save significant energy at racing speeds (drag increases with v²).

Compare the effectiveness of ball bearings versus lubrication. In what situations is each preferred?Medium
Show Answer

Ball bearings: best for rotating machinery, high-speed applications, where fluid lubricants might be impractical (space, extreme cold). Lubrication: best for sliding surfaces, engine pistons, gearboxes, any high-load slow-moving part. Ball bearings give the lower friction; lubrication is simpler and cheaper to implement.

Explain how a maglev train achieves near-zero friction. What replaces the traditional wheel-track friction?Medium
Show Answer

Maglev trains use magnetic levitation — powerful electromagnets lift the train above the track, eliminating solid-surface contact entirely. There is no rolling or sliding friction. The only resistance is air drag (fluid friction). Propulsion is also magnetic. This allows speeds exceeding 600 km/h.

How does reducing friction in engines improve fuel efficiency?Medium
Show Answer

Friction converts useful mechanical energy into heat. Less friction means more of the fuel's energy actually moves the car rather than heating engine parts. Modern low-viscosity engine oils (e.g., 0W-20) and coated pistons reduce friction losses by 5–10%, directly improving fuel economy and reducing CO₂ emissions.

Why are racing cars very low to the ground and shaped like wedges?Medium
Show Answer

The low, wedge shape reduces the car's frontal area (less air to push aside) and creates a streamlined profile (low drag coefficient). The underbody also channels air to create downforce (ground effect), increasing normal force and thus tyre friction for better cornering — a case where aerodynamics simultaneously reduces drag and increases useful friction.

Explain why wearing socks on a smooth floor is dangerous but wearing rubber-soled shoes is safe.Medium
Show Answer

Socks are made of smooth fabric with low µ on smooth floors — friction is insufficient to prevent slipping. Rubber-soled shoes have high µ (flexible rubber grips surface irregularities well), providing adequate static friction to prevent slipping during walking. Safety = sufficient friction between foot and ground.

How does polishing a metal surface reduce friction? Can over-polishing increase friction?Medium
Show Answer

Polishing removes surface asperities, reducing interlocking and lowering µ. However, if polished to an ultra-smooth finish, the true contact area increases dramatically and molecular adhesion becomes dominant — friction can actually increase (cold welding effect). There is an optimal roughness for minimum friction in engineering applications.

Why must lubricating oil in a car engine be changed regularly?Medium
Show Answer

Over time, engine oil degrades: high temperatures break down oil molecules (oxidation), metallic wear particles contaminate it, and viscosity changes. Degraded oil lubricates poorly, increasing friction and wear. Regular oil changes maintain effective lubrication and extend engine life.

Calculate the drag force on a car (C_d=0.28, A=2.2 m², ρ_air=1.2 kg/m³) at 30 m/s. Formula: F_drag = ½ρC_dAv²Hard
Show Answer

F = ½ × 1.2 × 0.28 × 2.2 × 900 = ½ × 1.2 × 0.28 × 2.2 × 900 = 0.5 × 1.2 × 0.616 × 900 = 0.5 × 665.3 = 332.6 N ≈ 333 N. At highway speeds, aerodynamic drag dominates — this is why streamlining is so important for fuel economy.

Why does a superhydrophobic coating (like on a lotus leaf) reduce friction between water and a surface?Hard
Show Answer

Superhydrophobic surfaces have microscopic air pockets trapped in their texture. Water droplets sit on these air cushions rather than fully contacting the solid surface (Cassie-Baxter state). The contact area is mostly air-water (extremely low friction) rather than solid-water. Drag is dramatically reduced — relevant for ship hulls and self-cleaning surfaces.

Compare hydrodynamic lubrication vs. boundary lubrication. When does each occur in a car engine?Hard
Show Answer

Hydrodynamic lubrication: at high speeds, a full oil film separates surfaces — no solid contact, very low friction (µ ≈ 0.001). Boundary lubrication: at startup/low speeds, oil film breaks down, surfaces partially contact — additive molecules bond to surfaces. Startup: boundary lubrication (highest wear risk). Running: hydrodynamic (lowest friction). Most engine wear occurs at startup when full oil film hasn't formed.

Graphene has µ < 0.001 at the nano-scale. Why is it difficult to use as a macroscale lubricant?Hard
Show Answer

At the nanoscale, single graphene layers show "superlubricity" (near-zero friction) because their atomic-scale smoothness prevents interlocking. At macroscale: manufacturing defects, edges, and stacking arrangements increase friction; graphene layers are hard to deposit uniformly over large surfaces; they can tear under load; cost of production is still very high. Research continues into practical graphene-based lubricants.

A car's drag coefficient drops from 0.35 to 0.28 with a redesigned body. By what percentage does aerodynamic drag reduce at highway speed? (All other factors constant.)Hard
Show Answer

Drag ∝ C_d. Reduction = (0.35 − 0.28)/0.35 × 100 = 0.07/0.35 × 100 = 20% reduction in drag. This directly translates to roughly 5–8% improvement in highway fuel economy since aerodynamic drag dominates at high speed.

Why do competitive swimmers shave their bodies and wear full-body suits? Quantify the benefit if suits reduce drag by 15%.Hard
Show Answer

Body hair creates micro-turbulence (increases drag). Full-body polyurethane suits are superhydrophobic and very smooth — they reduce fluid friction by trapping air bubbles and minimising turbulence. If a swimmer experiences 50 N drag normally: reduced drag = 50 × 0.85 = 42.5 N. Energy saved over a 100m race is significant at elite level where margins are hundredths of a second.

Design a thought experiment to prove that rolling friction is less than sliding friction without using measuring instruments.Hard
Show Answer

Place two identical books on a table. Lay one flat (slides); place pencils under the other (rolls). Push both simultaneously with equal force from the same height ramp. Observation: the rolling book moves faster and farther, decelerating more slowly. Since both have equal mass (equal N) and experienced the same impulse, the slower deceleration of the rolling book proves it has less friction retarding it. No instruments needed — just observation of relative displacement.

Explain how active drag reduction systems (DRS) in Formula 1 reduce friction. What is the physics trade-off involved?Hard
Show Answer

DRS (Drag Reduction System) opens a flap in the rear wing, reducing the wing's angle of attack. This reduces aerodynamic drag by ~20 km/h speed gain. Trade-off: the rear wing's downforce also decreases (less normal force on rear tyres → less frictional grip for cornering/braking). DRS is only allowed on straight sections — drivers must close it before corners to restore downforce and grip friction. Reducing one type of friction (air drag) reduces another (tyre grip).

Why do sprinters wear spiked shoes?Easy
Show Answer

Spikes dig into the track surface and increase friction (grip), preventing slipping when the sprinter pushes back hard against the ground. More friction = more forward propulsive force = faster starts and acceleration.

Why do footballers wear cleated/studded boots?Easy
Show Answer

Cleats penetrate the grass/mud surface, providing extra grip (friction) for pushing off, turning, and stopping quickly. Without cleats, players would slip on wet grass, especially during sudden direction changes.

How does a cricket ball create "swing" using friction?Easy
Show Answer

One side of the cricket ball is kept smooth (polished) and the other is rough (worn/raised seam). Air flows faster over the smooth side (less friction) creating lower pressure, while the rough side creates turbulence and higher pressure. The pressure difference pushes the ball sideways — this is swing bowling.

Why do swimmers wear full-body suits in competitions?Easy
Show Answer

Full-body suits are made of water-repellent, smooth materials that reduce fluid friction (drag) between the swimmer's body and water. Less drag means faster swimming with the same muscle effort.

Why is chalk used by gymnasts and weightlifters on their hands?Easy
Show Answer

Chalk (magnesium carbonate) absorbs sweat from hands. Wet/sweaty hands have much lower friction with metal bars. Dry, chalky hands have higher friction with gymnastic bars and barbells, providing a better grip and preventing equipment from slipping.

Why are tyres on racing bicycles smooth (slick), while mountain bike tyres are knobby?Easy
Show Answer

On smooth, dry roads: slick tyres maximise contact area with the road surface, giving good grip (static friction). On rough/muddy terrain: knobby tyres dig into the ground, providing mechanical grip where smooth tyres would slip and lose traction.

How does waxing a cricket bat help the batsman?Easy
Show Answer

Waxing preserves the bat's wood, but it doesn't directly help batting. However, oiling/waxing maintains the bat's surface integrity, preventing cracking from dryness — this keeps the bat's playing surface consistent. (The main help in batting comes from technique, not friction modification.)

Why do bowlers in cricket rub the ball on their trousers/shirt?Easy
Show Answer

To keep one side of the ball smooth and shiny (by polishing it on clothing) while the other side naturally roughens during play. This asymmetry in surface roughness creates the pressure difference needed for swing bowling.

SET B Medium – Applications in Sports (8 Questions)

Explain how topspin in tennis affects the ball's bounce using friction principles.Medium
Show Answer

Topspin means the ball rotates forward (top of ball moves in direction of travel). When it bounces, the rotating surface contacts the court — friction between the spinning ball and court accelerates the ball forward and pushes it down, giving a low, fast bounce that is hard to return. Friction between ball and court converts spin energy into forward velocity.

How does the Magnus effect (related to friction and spin) make a spinning football curve in the air?Medium
Show Answer

A spinning ball drags air around it (air friction). One side moves with the ball's spin direction (faster relative air flow, lower pressure — Bernoulli) and the other side against it (slower, higher pressure). This pressure difference creates a sideways force (Magnus force) perpendicular to both velocity and spin axis, curving the ball's path. Used in free kicks, banana shots.

Swimmers use different strokes at different speeds. How does fluid friction influence the choice of stroke?Medium
Show Answer

At high speeds, fluid friction (drag) increases as v². Freestyle (front crawl) is most streamlined — body is horizontal, reducing frontal area. Breaststroke creates more drag due to arm/leg movements catching water. In competitive swimming, drag management is critical — every stroke is refined to minimise drag while maximising propulsive force.

Why do curling players sweep the ice in front of a sliding stone?Medium
Show Answer

Sweeping the ice melts a microscopic layer of surface ice, reducing friction between stone and ice. Less friction slows deceleration, making the stone travel farther and curve less. Players use sweeping to precisely control where the stone stops — sweeping = controlling friction in real time.

How does friction help a goalkeeper catch a fast football without it slipping through their hands?Medium
Show Answer

Goalkeeper gloves have textured, high-friction latex palms that grip the ball's surface. The high µ between glove and ball means even with the large force of a fast shot, the friction force (µN, where N is the grip force) is large enough to decelerate and hold the ball rather than letting it slide through.

Explain why skiers tuck into a compact position (crouching) at high speed.Medium
Show Answer

In a tuck position, the skier reduces their frontal cross-sectional area (A) and improves streamlining (lower C_d). Drag force = ½ρC_dAv². Smaller A and C_d means less drag at the same speed, allowing the skier to go faster with the same gravitational force driving them downhill.

How does friction between a tennis racket's strings and the ball create spin?Medium
Show Answer

When a racket face is angled and brushes upward across the ball, friction between the strings and ball surface grips the ball and drags the top surface backward relative to the ball's centre. This creates a torque that sets the ball spinning (topspin). More string friction = more spin potential. Open string patterns allow more ball deformation and friction.

Why do long-distance runners prefer lighter, minimal shoes while sprinters use spiked shoes?Medium
Show Answer

Sprinters need maximum static friction for explosive push-off (spikes provide grip). Distance runners need to conserve energy over time — extra shoe weight wastes energy with each step (imagine lifting 200g extra 1000s of times). They need adequate but not extreme friction. Carbon-fibre plate shoes provide energy return and some grip without excessive weight.

Quantitatively explain reverse swing in cricket. At what point does a cricket ball start to reverse swing and why?Hard
Show Answer

Normal swing: smooth side generates laminar flow; rough seam side generates turbulent flow; turbulence = earlier separation = higher pressure on rough side → ball swings toward smooth side. After ~40 overs, ball is very rough on both sides. At high speeds (>30 m/s), even the "smooth" side develops turbulent flow. Now the seam side (if positioned correctly) generates laminar flow, reversing the pressure difference → ball swings the opposite way (reverse swing). Speed and ball condition determine the transition point.

A cyclist of mass 75 kg (+ 8 kg bike) rides at 10 m/s against 30 N air drag and 5 N rolling friction. What power must they generate?Hard
Show Answer

Total friction/drag force = 30 + 5 = 35 N. Power = F × v = 35 × 10 = 350 W. This is a typical power output for a fit recreational cyclist. At higher speeds, air drag dominates (drag ∝ v²), so power needed increases as v³.

Analyse the physics of a 100 m sprinter: from start to finish, how does the relationship between static friction, air drag, and velocity change?Hard
Show Answer

0–30m (acceleration phase): High static friction force drives acceleration; low air drag (v is small; drag ∝ v²). 30–70m: Maximum velocity phase — static friction ≈ air drag + rolling friction; net force ≈ 0; muscle force balances total resistance. 70–100m: Fatigue reduces muscle force; drag still high (high v); slight deceleration. Static friction is always the propulsive force; drag is always the resistance. As v increases, maintaining acceleration requires exponentially more power.

How does dimpling on a golf ball reduce drag compared to a smooth ball at the same speed? Use Bernoulli's principle.Hard
Show Answer

Counter-intuitively, dimples reduce drag. A smooth ball has laminar flow that separates early, creating a large turbulent wake (high pressure drag). Dimples trip the boundary layer into turbulence earlier, energising it to stay attached to the ball surface longer. Late separation = smaller wake = less pressure drag. Dimpled balls experience ~50% less drag than smooth balls at golf ball speeds. Without dimples, a golf ball would travel only half as far.

Compare the coefficient of friction requirements for a sprinting shoe vs. a swimming suit. Calculate friction force for a sprinter pushing with 400 N if µ = 0.8.Hard
Show Answer

Sprinting: needs maximum static friction (µ = 0.7–1.2) between shoe and track to convert maximum muscle force to forward motion. Friction force = µN = 0.8 × 400 = 320 N forward thrust. Swimming: needs minimum fluid friction — the suit reduces drag by being superhydrophobic and smooth. These are opposite requirements — sports equipment is optimised for the specific friction type needed.

Why does a spinning cricket ball's trajectory curve even in still air (ignoring wind)? Link to friction forces quantitatively.Hard
Show Answer

A spinning ball (e.g., leg-spin) has angular velocity ω. Air friction drags air along with the spinning surface. One side: surface moves with airflow → relative velocity decreases → less drag → lower pressure. Other side: against airflow → higher relative velocity → more drag → higher pressure. Net lateral force (Magnus) = C_L × ½ρAv² × (ωR/v), where C_L is Magnus lift coefficient. For a cricket ball spinning at 2000 rpm at 25 m/s, deflection can reach 0.5–1.0 m over 20 m flight — sufficient to beat a batsman.

The shark-skin inspired swimsuit (Speedo LZR Racer) was banned from Olympics. Explain the physics and the ethical issue.Hard
Show Answer

Physics: Shark skin has V-shaped riblets (denticles) aligned with water flow that reduce turbulent drag by channelling water into micro-vortices that reduce boundary layer friction. The LZR Racer used polyurethane panels that also trapped air (superhydrophobic), reduced body vibration, and compressed the swimmer's body (less form drag). Combined 2–3% drag reduction. Ethics: technology giving unfair advantage over natural ability; 23 world records broken at 2008 Olympics wearing it. FINA banned suits beyond hip-level for men/shoulders for women. Physics vs. the spirit of sport.

Evaluate: "The future of high-performance sport will be won entirely by friction management, not by athlete training." Agree or disagree with evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Disagree with "entirely" but acknowledge importance. Evidence FOR friction management: dimpled golf balls, shark-skin suits, spike shoes, F1 tyres all provide measurable performance gains. LZR Racer: 23 world records. Evidence AGAINST: Eliud Kipchoge ran sub-2h marathon (Breaking2) — carbon plate shoes helped but his VO₂max, training, pacing strategy, and biomechanics were dominant. In most sports, the marginal gains from friction engineering are 1–5%; athlete physiology and training can yield 10–30% improvement. Conclusion: friction management is increasingly important at elite margins but cannot substitute for fundamental athletic capability. Both are necessary.
