History of Atomic Models

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy History of atomic structure Bohr model of the atom
CrashCourse Atomic Theory and Atomic Structure

Set A Recall & Concept

Name the four atomic models in chronological order and their proposers. Easy
Show Answer

1. Dalton's solid sphere model (1803). 2. Thomson's plum pudding model (1897). 3. Rutherford's nuclear model (1911). 4. Bohr's planetary model (1913).

Describe Dalton's atomic model. What were its key assumptions? Easy
Show Answer

Dalton proposed atoms are tiny, solid, indivisible spheres. All atoms of an element are identical; different elements have different atoms; atoms combine in simple ratios to form compounds.

Describe Thomson's plum pudding model and what discovery led to it. Easy
Show Answer

Thomson discovered the electron (1897). He proposed the atom as a sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded like plums in a pudding. The atom was overall neutral.

What key feature did Rutherford's model introduce that Thomson's model lacked? Medium
Show Answer

Rutherford introduced the concept of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the centre of the atom, with electrons orbiting around it in mostly empty space.

What did Bohr add to Rutherford's model? Easy
Show Answer

Bohr proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus in specific, fixed energy levels (shells). Electrons cannot spiral inward; they exist only in defined shells with specific energies.

Why was Dalton's model eventually revised? Medium
Show Answer

Thomson's discovery of the electron proved atoms are not indivisible. Later, Rutherford's experiment showed the positive charge is concentrated in a small nucleus, not spread throughout the atom.

Why is scientific modelling important even if models are later proved wrong? Medium
Show Answer

Models represent the best understanding at a given time. Each model explained known data and made testable predictions. Revised models incorporate new evidence, improving understanding. Science progresses through this cycle of modelling, testing, and refinement.

What limitation does the Bohr model have that modern quantum mechanics addresses? Hard
Show Answer

The Bohr model accurately predicts hydrogen's spectrum but fails for multi-electron atoms. It treats electrons as particles in fixed orbits, whereas quantum mechanics describes them as probability clouds (orbitals), using wave functions to describe electron positions.

Set B Application & Analysis

Describe the four atomic models showing key differences in electron placement. Medium
Show Answer

Dalton: solid undivided sphere. Thomson: sphere with + charge throughout, electrons scattered inside. Rutherford: tiny + nucleus at centre, electrons orbiting far away in mostly empty space. Bohr: nucleus + electrons in fixed, numbered shells at specific distances.

How did Thomson's cathode ray experiment provide evidence for the electron? Medium
Show Answer

Thomson used cathode ray tubes and found rays deflected toward positive plates, indicating the rays carried negative charge. He measured the charge-to-mass ratio, showing these particles (electrons) were the same regardless of the cathode material — proving electrons are universal subatomic particles.

A student says "Rutherford's model is wrong because it couldn't explain electron stability." Is this a valid criticism? Hard
Show Answer

Valid. Classical physics predicts orbiting electrons would continuously radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus. This doesn't happen — Bohr resolved this by proposing electrons only exist in stable, non-radiating quantised orbits.

What evidence disproved Thomson's plum pudding model? Medium
Show Answer

Rutherford's gold foil experiment (1911): most alpha particles passed straight through (inconsistent with spread-out positive charge), and some were deflected at large angles — only possible if positive charge is concentrated in a tiny dense nucleus.

Create a timeline of atomic model development from 1800 to 1932 with key scientists and discoveries. Medium
Show Answer

1803: Dalton – solid sphere. 1897: Thomson – discovers electron, proposes plum pudding. 1909: Geiger & Marsden – gold foil experiment. 1911: Rutherford – nuclear model. 1913: Bohr – electron shells. 1932: Chadwick – discovers neutron.

Explain why the discovery of the neutron (Chadwick, 1932) was important for completing the nuclear model. Hard
Show Answer

Before Chadwick, the nucleus was known to have protons but measured atomic masses were roughly double the proton number. Neutrons explained the extra mass without adding charge, completing the picture of nuclear composition as protons + neutrons.

How did Bohr explain the emission of specific coloured light (spectral lines) by heated hydrogen gas? Hard
Show Answer

When electrons gain energy, they jump to higher shells. When they fall back, they emit photons of specific wavelengths (colours) corresponding exactly to the energy difference between shells. This explained hydrogen's distinct spectral lines, which earlier models could not.

Compare Rutherford's model to the solar system analogy. Where does this analogy break down? Hard
Show Answer

Similarity: central massive body (nucleus/sun) with smaller bodies orbiting. Breakdown: electrons are not drawn by gravity but by electrostatic attraction; electrons do not have fixed elliptical orbits but exist in probability clouds; the analogy wrongly implies continuous orbits, which violates quantum rules.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

If Thomson's model were correct, what result would you expect from the gold foil experiment? What actually happened and what did it prove? Hard
Show Answer

If Thomson's model were correct, alpha particles would pass through with only slight deflections — like bullets through a cloud. Actually: most passed straight through (empty space), some deflected at large angles, ~1 in 8000 bounced back. This proved a tiny, dense, positive nucleus exists.

Why did each atomic model represent progress, even though each was later revised? Use evidence from the history of atomic theory. Medium
Show Answer

Dalton explained chemical reactions and ratios. Thomson explained cathode rays. Rutherford explained alpha particle deflection. Bohr explained spectral lines. Each model explained more phenomena than the previous, representing genuine scientific progress through evidence-based revision.

How did technology (cathode ray tubes, radioactive sources, particle accelerators) drive advances in atomic theory? Hard
Show Answer

Without cathode ray tubes, Thomson couldn't discover electrons. Without radioactive alpha sources, Rutherford couldn't probe the nucleus. Later accelerators revealed quarks inside protons. Each technological advance enabled experiments at smaller scales, revealing new subatomic structure.

Write a short dialogue between Dalton and Rutherford where Rutherford explains why the solid sphere model is incomplete. Medium
Show Answer

Rutherford: "Mr. Dalton, I fired alpha particles at gold. Most passed straight through — the atom is mostly empty space!" Dalton: "But atoms are indivisible spheres!" Rutherford: "Not quite — a tiny dense nucleus carries all the positive charge, and electrons orbit far away." Dalton: "Then your evidence forces me to revise my model."

How does the history of the atom illustrate the nature of science (NOS) concept that knowledge is tentative yet reliable? Hard
Show Answer

Tentative: each model was revised when new evidence emerged. Reliable: each revision was evidence-based and retained what worked. The progression shows science self-corrects — models are not permanent truths but the best explanations supported by current evidence.

Why might Bohr's model be called a "quantised" model? What does quantisation mean in this context? Hard
Show Answer

Quantised means energy comes in discrete, fixed amounts (quanta) rather than a continuous range. Electrons can only exist in specific energy levels (shells). They absorb or emit exactly the energy difference between shells as photons. This contradicted classical physics and launched quantum theory.

If an alien civilization developed atomic theory independently, would they likely end up with the same model? Justify using scientific reasoning. Hard
Show Answer

Yes — the universe operates by consistent physical laws. Any civilization performing similar experiments would observe the same phenomena and draw similar conclusions. The specific names might differ, but the structural model (nucleus + electrons) would converge, demonstrating that scientific knowledge is universal and observation-based.

In 50 words, describe what you would add to Bohr's model if you had just read about quantum mechanics in 1930. Hard
Show Answer

Replace fixed circular orbits with probability distributions (orbitals). Electrons don't travel in defined paths; they exist in 3D clouds of probability. Each orbital has a specific shape (s, p, d) and energy. This explains multi-electron atoms and chemical bonding far better than Bohr's circles.

Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Rutherford's experiment explained
CrashCourse Atomic Theory and Gold Foil

Set A Recall & Concept

Describe the setup of Rutherford's gold foil experiment. Easy
Show Answer

A radioactive source of alpha particles was aimed at a thin gold foil. A circular zinc sulphide (ZnS) detection screen surrounded the foil to detect flashes wherever alpha particles struck it at any angle.

What are alpha particles and what charge do they carry? Easy
Show Answer

Alpha particles are helium-4 nuclei: 2 protons + 2 neutrons. They carry a charge of +2 and have a mass of 4 atomic mass units (u).

State the three types of results observed in the gold foil experiment. Easy
Show Answer

1. Most alpha particles passed straight through (undeflected). 2. Some were deflected at small angles. 3. A very few (~1 in 8000) bounced back at large angles (>90°) or were reflected back.

What conclusion did Rutherford draw from the fact that most alpha particles passed straight through? Easy
Show Answer

Most of the atom is empty space. Since most alpha particles passed through without deflection, the atom cannot be a solid sphere — there is vast empty space between the nucleus and electrons.

What conclusion did Rutherford draw from the few alpha particles that bounced back? Medium
Show Answer

The positive charge must be concentrated in a tiny, dense region (nucleus) at the centre. Only a dense concentrated positive charge could repel the +2 alpha particle enough to deflect it back.

Why was gold chosen for the foil in Rutherford's experiment? Medium
Show Answer

Gold can be beaten into extremely thin sheets (a few hundred atoms thick) while remaining intact. A thin single-layer foil ensures single-encounter scattering events, making the results easier to interpret.

Rutherford said finding a large deflection was "as if you fired artillery shells at tissue paper and they bounced back." Explain this analogy. Medium
Show Answer

He expected alpha particles (heavy, fast) to easily pass through the "soft" plum pudding atom. Finding large deflections was completely unexpected — like a cannon shell bouncing off paper. This meant something very dense and hard existed in the atom: the nucleus.

Why couldn't Thomson's plum pudding model explain large-angle deflections? Hard
Show Answer

In Thomson's model, positive charge is spread thinly throughout the atom. A diffuse positive charge could only deflect alpha particles slightly. Large-angle deflections require an intensely concentrated repulsive force, only possible from a tiny dense nucleus.

Set B Application & Analysis

If 10 000 alpha particles are fired at gold foil, roughly how many would bounce back based on Rutherford's results? Easy
Show Answer

Approximately 1 in 8000 bounces back. From 10 000: about 10 000/8000 ≈ 1–2 alpha particles reflected back.

Compare the relative sizes: if the nucleus were a marble (1 cm diameter), how large would the atom be? Medium
Show Answer

The nucleus is about 1/100 000 the diameter of the atom. If the nucleus is 1 cm, the atom would be ~100 000 cm = 1000 m = 1 km across. Most of the atom is empty space.

Predict what would happen if Rutherford had used aluminium foil (Z=13) instead of gold (Z=79). Would more or fewer particles deflect at large angles? Hard
Show Answer

Fewer large-angle deflections with aluminium. Gold has Z=79 (more protons) creating stronger nuclear repulsion. Aluminium's nucleus (Z=13) repels alpha particles less strongly, resulting in fewer large-angle deflections.

Why did Rutherford use alpha particles rather than electrons for the gold foil experiment? Medium
Show Answer

Alpha particles are heavy (+2 charge) and fast-moving, carrying enough momentum to probe the nucleus. Electrons are ~7000 times lighter and would be deflected by even the electron cloud, not revealing nuclear structure.

Design a modified gold foil experiment to estimate the size of the nucleus. What measurements would you need? Hard
Show Answer

Measure the fraction f of particles deflected beyond 90°. If each nucleus has cross-section area πr² and foil has n atoms/m², then f = nπr². Measuring f and n allows calculation of nuclear radius r. Rutherford estimated nuclear radius ~10²¹&sup4; m this way.

What further experiment proved the existence of protons? Who conducted it? Medium
Show Answer

Rutherford (1917) bombarded nitrogen with alpha particles and detected hydrogen nuclei (protons) emitted. This proved nuclei contain protons. He named the proton from the Greek "protos" (first).

How did Geiger and Marsden contribute to Rutherford's experiment? Why is team science important? Medium
Show Answer

Geiger and Marsden conducted the actual observations — painstakingly counting scintillation flashes in the dark for hours. Their careful data provided the evidence Rutherford analysed. Science requires collaboration: theoretical insight (Rutherford) + meticulous experimentation (Geiger/Marsden).

The experiment found ~1 in 8000 alpha particles bounced back. What does this tell us about the fraction of atomic volume occupied by the nucleus? Hard
Show Answer

Since only 1/8000 interact strongly (hit the nucleus), the nucleus occupies roughly 1/8000 of the atom's cross-sectional area. Since volume scales as r³, nuclear volume fraction is even smaller — about 10²¹&sup5; of atomic volume. The nucleus is unimaginably small compared to the atom.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

Write a lab report abstract (50 words) for Rutherford's experiment: aim, method summary, results, and conclusion. Hard
Show Answer

Aim: Investigate atomic structure by scattering alpha particles. Method: Fired alpha particles at thin gold foil; detected scattering pattern with ZnS screen. Results: Most particles passed through; ~1 in 8000 deflected back. Conclusion: Atoms have a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space.

If the nucleus is so small and dense, what keeps electrons from falling into it? (Consider Bohr's explanation.) Hard
Show Answer

Bohr proposed electrons orbit in fixed, stable energy levels. In permitted orbits, electrons do not radiate energy. The centripetal force needed to maintain the orbit exactly equals the electrostatic attraction. Only quantum mechanical rules stabilise the electron in its shell.

How would the gold foil results differ if the foil were much thicker (100 layers)? Explain the complications. Hard
Show Answer

With multiple layers, an alpha particle slightly deflected by one nucleus could be deflected again by another — multiple-scattering effects. The angular distribution becomes broader and harder to interpret because it's no longer a single-encounter event. Single-layer foil was crucial for clean results.

Rutherford's experiment disproved Thomson's model. What does this tell us about the importance of experimental evidence in science? Medium
Show Answer

Science progresses when experiments contradict existing theories. Thomson's model was widely accepted but disproved by a single well-designed experiment. This shows: (1) no theory is immune to revision, (2) experimental evidence overrides theoretical preference, (3) science is self-correcting.

Using density = mass/volume and V = (4/3)πr³, discuss why nuclear density is ~10¹&sup7; kg/m³ when a nucleus radius is ~10²¹&sup5; m and mass ~4 × 10²²&sup7; kg. Hard
Show Answer

V = (4/3)π(10²¹&sup5;)³ ≈ 4.19 × 10²&sup4;&sup5; m³. Density = 4×10²²&sup7;/4.19×10²&sup4;&sup5; ≈ 10¹&sup7; kg/m³. Nuclear density is ~10¹&sup7; kg/m³ — about 100 trillion times denser than water. Almost all atomic mass is crammed into the tiny nucleus.

If an alien civilization received only the gold foil data (no theory), what model would they likely propose? Hard
Show Answer

Observations: mostly no deflection (empty space), occasional large deflection (hard small object), rare backward bounce (very dense repulsive centre). They would logically propose: tiny dense positive centre surrounded by mostly empty space — the same conclusion as Rutherford. Physical evidence is universal.

Evaluate whether Rutherford's experiment was safe by modern standards. What precautions would a modern lab require? Hard
Show Answer

Not fully safe by modern standards. Alpha sources emit ionising radiation; Geiger and Marsden were exposed during long sessions. Modern precautions: lead shielding; dosimetry badges; limited exposure time; fume hood; proper radioactive waste disposal; institutional ethics review.

How did the gold foil experiment change humanity's understanding of matter? Explain its broader philosophical impact. Medium
Show Answer

It revealed that "solid" matter is mostly empty space — a profound philosophical shift. The solidity we feel is an illusion of electromagnetic forces. This launched nuclear physics, led to nuclear energy, and transformed our understanding of what matter fundamentally is.

Subatomic Particles — Proton, Neutron, Electron

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Subatomic particles: protons, neutrons, electrons
CrashCourse Atoms and Elements

Set A Recall & Concept

For each subatomic particle, state its charge, relative mass, and location in the atom. Easy
Show Answer

Proton: charge +1, mass 1 u, location: nucleus. Neutron: charge 0, mass 1 u, location: nucleus. Electron: charge −1, mass ~1/1836 u (negligible), location: shells/orbitals around nucleus.

Why is the overall charge of an atom zero? Easy
Show Answer

In a neutral atom, the number of protons (positive charge) equals the number of electrons (negative charge), so the charges cancel to give zero net charge.

What holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus despite protons repelling each other? Medium
Show Answer

The strong nuclear force acts at very short range (<10²¹&sup5; m) and is far stronger than the electrostatic repulsion between protons. It holds nucleons (protons and neutrons) tightly together.

Why is the mass of an electron often considered negligible in atomic mass calculations? Medium
Show Answer

An electron's mass is about 1/1836 of a proton's mass. Electrons contribute less than 0.05% to total atomic mass and are ignored in most mass calculations.

What is the difference between nucleons and subatomic particles? Easy
Show Answer

Nucleons are particles inside the nucleus (protons and neutrons). Subatomic particles include all particles smaller than an atom: protons, neutrons, AND electrons (electrons are not nucleons).

Who discovered the neutron and when? What experiment was used? Medium
Show Answer

James Chadwick discovered the neutron in 1932 by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles. The uncharged, mass-1 particle was identified as the neutron by its ability to knock protons from paraffin wax.

What happens to atomic charge when an electron is removed from a neutral atom? Easy
Show Answer

Removing an electron removes −1 charge. The atom now has more protons than electrons, giving it a net positive charge of +1. It becomes a positive ion (cation).

Why are neutrons particularly useful as probes in nuclear physics research? Hard
Show Answer

Neutrons carry no charge, so they are not repelled by the positively charged nucleus. They can penetrate deeply into nuclei without electrostatic deflection, making them ideal for probing nuclear structure and inducing nuclear reactions (e.g., nuclear fission).

Set B Application & Calculation

An atom has 17 protons and 18 neutrons. What is its atomic number, mass number, and number of electrons? Easy
Show Answer

Atomic number Z = 17. Mass number A = 17 + 18 = 35. Electrons = 17 (neutral atom). Element: Chlorine-35.

An ion has 11 protons, 12 neutrons, and 10 electrons. State its charge and symbol. Medium
Show Answer

Charge = protons − electrons = 11 − 10 = +1. Z=11 is Sodium. Symbol: Na⁺ (sodium cation, formed by losing 1 electron).

Calculate the total number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in 3 atoms of Ag-107 (silver, Z=47). Medium
Show Answer

Per atom: protons = 47; neutrons = 107−47 = 60; electrons = 47. For 3 atoms: protons = 141; neutrons = 180; electrons = 141.

An atom has equal numbers of protons and neutrons, and mass number 40. Identify the element. Easy
Show Answer

Protons = neutrons and total = 40, so protons = 20, neutrons = 20. Z = 20 = Calcium (Ca-40).

A fluoride ion (F²⁻) has 10 electrons. How many protons does it have? Write its electron configuration. Medium
Show Answer

F⁻ has charge −1, meaning 1 more electron than protons. Electrons = 10, so protons = 9. Z = 9 = Fluorine. Electron configuration: 2, 8 (like neon — stable full outer shell).

What is the total charge of a nucleus containing 26 protons and 30 neutrons? Easy
Show Answer

Nuclear charge = 26 × (+1) = +26. Neutrons contribute 0 charge. Total nuclear charge = +26 (Iron nucleus, Fe).

An atom X has 15 protons, 16 neutrons. Write the full symbol. If it gains 3 electrons, write the new symbol. Medium
Show Answer

Neutral atom: P-31 (Phosphorus, A=31, Z=15). If it gains 3 electrons: P³⁻ (phosphide ion) with 18 electrons, charge −3. Electronic configuration: 2, 8, 8.

Why does nuclear density (~10¹&sup7; kg/m³) vastly exceed the density of any ordinary material (~10³ kg/m³)? Hard
Show Answer

In ordinary matter, most atomic volume is empty space (electrons far from nucleus). Nuclear density is achieved only within the nucleus where nucleons are tightly packed by the strong nuclear force. Ordinary matter is ~10¹&sup4; times less dense because it includes the vast empty atomic volume.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

Why is the electron's mass "approximately zero" in atomic mass units, yet electrons determine almost all of chemistry? Hard
Show Answer

Electrons have negligible mass (1/1836 u) so barely affect atomic mass. However, chemistry is entirely about electrons: bonding (sharing/transferring electrons), reactivity (valence electrons), and electrical properties all depend on electrons. Mass determines physical properties; electrons determine chemical behaviour.

If a neutron is uncharged, how can it help stabilise a nucleus against proton-proton repulsion? Hard
Show Answer

Neutrons contribute to the strong nuclear force (which attracts all nearby nucleons regardless of charge) without adding electrostatic repulsion. They act as "nuclear glue" — increasing attractive binding while not worsening proton-proton repulsion.

Predict the likely properties of Pb-208 (Z=82, N=126). Why is it unusually stable? Hard
Show Answer

Pb-208 is doubly magic: both 82 protons and 126 neutrons are "magic numbers" in the nuclear shell model (completely filled nuclear shells). This gives it exceptional stability — it is the heaviest completely stable nucleus.

Design an experiment to demonstrate that an atom has more empty space than solid matter. Hard
Show Answer

Use Rutherford's approach: fire projectiles at target and measure penetration. If solid, none pass through; if mostly empty, most pass through. Alternatively, calculate atomic and nuclear radii (nuclear: ~10²¹&sup5; m; atomic: ~10²¹&sup0; m). Volume ratio shows nucleus occupies ~10²¹&sup5; of atomic volume, confirming mostly empty space.

Why do scientists say quarks are "inside" protons? Does this mean subatomic particles are not truly fundamental? Hard
Show Answer

High-energy accelerator experiments (CERN) revealed protons/neutrons are composed of quarks held by gluons. So protons/neutrons are not fundamental — they have internal structure. Currently, quarks and electrons appear to be truly fundamental (point-like, no internal structure detected). Science continues probing deeper.

Compare the forces acting inside an atom: gravitational, electrostatic, and strong nuclear. Rank their relative strengths. Hard
Show Answer

Strong nuclear force: strongest (~1), short-range <10²¹&sup5; m. Electrostatic force: ~10²² weaker than strong force (keeps electrons in orbit). Gravitational force: ~10²³&sup9; weaker than strong force (completely negligible between subatomic particles). Inside atoms: strong >> electrostatic >>>> gravity.

If a proton and electron are 10²¹&sup0; m apart, what force keeps the electron orbiting, and why doesn't it fall into the nucleus? Hard
Show Answer

Electrostatic (Coulomb) attraction keeps the electron orbiting. It doesn't fall in because: (1) quantum mechanics forbids electrons from existing within the nucleus (uncertainty principle — confinement to nuclear size would require enormous momentum); (2) electrons exist in discrete quantised energy states with a minimum (ground state) energy — they cannot go lower.

Explain how knowledge of subatomic particles led to practical applications in medicine (X-rays, PET scans) and energy (nuclear power). Hard
Show Answer

Electrons led to X-rays (high-energy photons from decelerated electrons). PET scans use positrons (antielectrons) emitted by radioactive tracers; positron-electron annihilation produces gamma rays for imaging metabolism. Nuclear power exploits neutron-induced uranium fission (Fermi, 1942), releasing energy from mass via E=mc². Every medical imaging technology and nuclear application traces back to subatomic particle discoveries.

Atomic Number (Z) and Mass Number (A)

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Atomic number and mass number
CrashCourse Atoms and the Periodic Table

Set A Recall & Concept

Define atomic number (Z). What does it determine about an element? Easy
Show Answer

Atomic number Z = number of protons in the nucleus. It uniquely identifies the element: all atoms of the same element have the same Z. It determines the element's position in the periodic table and its chemical properties.

Define mass number (A) and write the formula relating A, Z, and neutron number N. Easy
Show Answer

Mass number A = total nucleons (protons + neutrons) in the nucleus. A = Z + N, so N = A − Z.

In the notation S-32 (sulphur, Z=16), identify the atomic number, mass number, and number of neutrons. Easy
Show Answer

Atomic number Z = 16. Mass number A = 32. Neutrons = A − Z = 32 − 16 = 16.

Two atoms have the same mass number but different atomic numbers. Can they be the same element? Easy
Show Answer

No. Element identity is determined by atomic number (proton number). Different atomic numbers = different elements, even if mass numbers are the same. Such atoms are called isobars.

Why is atomic number a better identifier of an element than mass number? Medium
Show Answer

Mass number can vary between atoms of the same element (isotopes have different neutron numbers but same Z). Atomic number is fixed for all atoms of an element — it uniquely defines the element. You cannot change Z without changing to a different element.

What are isobars? Give an example. Medium
Show Answer

Isobars are atoms of different elements (different Z) with the same mass number A. Example: Ar-40 (Z=18) and K-40 (Z=19) — both A=40 but different elements with different chemical properties.

An element has Z=26. Name it, give its symbol, and state how many electrons a neutral atom has. Easy
Show Answer

Z=26 is Iron (Fe). A neutral atom has 26 electrons (equal to proton number).

Explain why the relative atomic mass of chlorine is 35.5, not a whole number. Hard
Show Answer

Chlorine naturally exists as two isotopes: Cl-35 (75%) and Cl-37 (25%). The relative atomic mass is the weighted average: (35 × 0.75) + (37 × 0.25) = 26.25 + 9.25 = 35.5. Being an average, it is not a whole number.

Set B Application & Calculation

He-4 has Z=2. How many protons, neutrons, and electrons does it have? Easy
Show Answer

Protons = Z = 2. Neutrons = A−Z = 4−2 = 2. Electrons = 2 (neutral atom).

An unknown element X has 29 protons and 34 neutrons. Identify it and write its symbol with mass number. Easy
Show Answer

Z=29 is Copper (Cu). A = 29+34 = 63. Symbol: Cu-63.

Potassium (K, Z=19, A=39). Calculate neutron number and write the nuclear symbol. Easy
Show Answer

Neutrons = A−Z = 39−19 = 20. Symbol: K-39 (39 as superscript, 19 as subscript).

Magnesium (Mg, Z=12, A=24) forms the ion Mg²⁺. How many protons, neutrons, and electrons does Mg²⁺ have? Medium
Show Answer

Protons = 12. Neutrons = 24−12 = 12. Electrons = 12−2 = 10 (lost 2 electrons to form +2 ion).

Calculate the weighted average atomic mass of bromine: Br-79 (50%) and Br-81 (50%). Medium
Show Answer

Ar = (79 × 0.50) + (81 × 0.50) = 39.5 + 40.5 = 80. Bromine's relative atomic mass = 80.

An element has Ar = 6.94. Its two isotopes are mass 6 (abundance x%) and mass 7 (abundance (100−x)%). Find x. Hard
Show Answer

6(x/100) + 7(1−x/100) = 6.94. 7 − x/100 = 6.94. x/100 = 0.06. x = 6. So 6% Li-6 and 94% Li-7. Element = Lithium.

How many neutrons are in Pb-208 (lead, Z=82)? Easy
Show Answer

Neutrons = A−Z = 208−82 = 126. Lead-208 is the most stable heavy nucleus (doubly magic: 82 protons, 126 neutrons).

Uranium-235 (Z=92) undergoes fission to form barium-141 (Z=56) and another element. Find the other element's Z and A (3 neutrons released). Hard
Show Answer

Conservation of mass: 235+1 = 141+A+3 → A = 92. Conservation of charge: 92 = 56+Z_x → Z_x = 36 = Krypton. The other element is Kr-92.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

Why does the periodic table arrange elements by atomic number rather than atomic mass? Give a historical example where this mattered. Hard
Show Answer

Arranging by Z gives consistent ordering by element identity. Before isotopes were understood, arranging by mass placed tellurium (Ar=127.6) before iodine (Ar=126.9), violating chemical properties. Moseley (1913) showed arrangement by Z correctly places Te (Z=52) before I (Z=53), matching chemical properties.

If you changed the number of protons in an atom, would you still have the same element? Explain with an example. Hard
Show Answer

No — you would have a completely different element. Removing 1 proton from gold (Z=79) gives platinum (Z=78); adding 1 proton gives mercury (Z=80). This is nuclear transmutation. Alchemists dreamed of it; it actually occurs in particle accelerators and nuclear reactions.

Compare neutron-to-proton ratios for carbon (A=12, Z=6) and lead (A=207, Z=82). What trend do you notice for heavy elements? Hard
Show Answer

Carbon: N/Z = 6/6 = 1.00. Lead-207: N/Z = 125/82 = 1.52. Heavier elements need proportionally more neutrons to remain stable. Extra neutrons dilute the proton-proton repulsion, providing additional strong force binding without adding positive charge.

Two elements have the same number of neutrons but different atomic numbers. What are they called? Give an example and explain any similarities. Medium
Show Answer

Isotones: same N, different Z. Example: S-34 (Z=16, N=18) and Ar-36 (Z=18, N=18). They share nuclear stability properties related to neutron number, but chemical behaviour is completely different because Z differs.

How does knowledge of atomic number and mass number underpin nuclear medicine (e.g., cancer treatment with radiation)? Hard
Show Answer

Doctors choose specific isotopes based on Z and A. E.g., I-131 (Z=53, A=131) for thyroid cancer — iodine is absorbed by thyroid tissue, and extra neutrons make it radioactive, emitting beta/gamma radiation to destroy cancer cells. Precise knowledge of Z and A enables targeted isotope selection.

Explain why transmutation is so energy-intensive compared to ordinary chemical reactions. Hard
Show Answer

Chemical reactions involve only electrons (at ~eV energy scale). Transmutation requires changing proton number — overcoming the strong nuclear force and electrostatic repulsion inside the nucleus (at ~MeV scale, millions of times more energetic). This is why alchemists could never turn lead to gold with chemical methods.

A nucleus emits an alpha particle. How do Z and A change? Write a general equation and give a real example. Medium
Show Answer

Alpha decay: ⁴⁺Z X → (A-4)/(Z-2) Y + ⁴He. Z decreases by 2, A decreases by 4. Example: U-238 (Z=92) → Th-234 (Z=90) + He-4 (alpha).

Why is Pb-208 (Z=82, N=126) unusually stable? Connect to atomic number and neutron number. Hard
Show Answer

Nuclear shell model: certain "magic numbers" (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) completely fill nuclear shells. Pb-208 has both 82 protons AND 126 neutrons as magic numbers — "doubly magic." Both proton and neutron shells are completely full, making it the heaviest completely stable nucleus.

Define isotopes. Give the three isotopes of carbon and state which is radioactive. Easy
Show Answer

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A). Carbon isotopes: C-12 (stable), C-13 (stable), C-14 (radioactive — used in carbon dating).

Why do isotopes of an element have the same chemical properties? Easy
Show Answer

Chemical properties are determined by the number of electrons, which equals Z. Since isotopes have the same Z, they have the same electrons and identical chemical behaviour. Extra neutrons only affect mass and nuclear stability.

How does carbon-14 dating work? What is the principle? Medium
Show Answer

Living organisms continuously absorb C-14. When they die, absorption stops and C-14 decays (half-life 5730 years). Measuring the remaining C-14/C-12 ratio gives the age of organic material (useful up to ~50 000 years).

What is a half-life? Give the half-life of carbon-14. Easy
Show Answer

Half-life is the time for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. Carbon-14 has a half-life of approximately 5730 years.

Why do some isotopes decay (become radioactive) while others are stable? Medium
Show Answer

Nuclear stability depends on the neutron-to-proton ratio. Too many or too few neutrons relative to protons makes a nucleus unstable — it decays (alpha, beta, or gamma emission) to reach a more stable configuration.

Name one medical and one archaeological application of isotopes. Easy
Show Answer

Medical: I-131 to treat thyroid cancer; Tc-99m for medical imaging. Archaeological: C-14 carbon dating to date organic artefacts and fossils.

How are hydrogen's isotopes (deuterium and tritium) used in nuclear energy? Hard
Show Answer

Deuterium (H-2) and tritium (H-3) are used as fuel in nuclear fusion reactors. They fuse to produce helium + a neutron + 17.6 MeV of energy. This is the reaction powering the sun; scientists are working to replicate it for clean energy (e.g., ITER project).

What is the difference between stable and radioactive isotopes? Easy
Show Answer

Stable isotopes do not spontaneously decay. Radioactive (unstable) isotopes have imbalanced proton/neutron ratios; they emit radiation to release energy and become more stable. Most elements have both stable and radioactive isotopes.

Set B Application & Calculation

An artefact has C-14 activity that is 25% of a living sample. How old is it? (half-life = 5730 years) Medium
Show Answer

25% = (1/2)² → 2 half-lives. Age = 2 × 5730 = 11 460 years.

A 100 g sample of iodine-131 (half-life = 8 days) is left for 24 days. How much remains? Medium
Show Answer

24 days / 8 days = 3 half-lives. Remaining = 100 × (1/2)³ = 100/8 = 12.5 g.

Compare the neutron numbers of U-233, U-235, and U-238 (all Z=92). Easy
Show Answer

U-233: N = 233−92 = 141. U-235: N = 235−92 = 143. U-238: N = 238−92 = 146. Same element but increasing neutron numbers = isotopes.

Calculate the relative atomic mass of neon: Ne-20 (90.48%), Ne-21 (0.27%), Ne-22 (9.25%). Medium
Show Answer

Ar = (20 × 0.9048) + (21 × 0.0027) + (22 × 0.0925) = 18.096 + 0.0567 + 2.035 = 20.19 ≈ 20.2.

After 3 half-lives, what fraction remains? If initial mass was 160 g, how much remains? Easy
Show Answer

Fraction = (1/2)³ = 1/8. Mass = 160 × (1/8) = 20 g.

Uranium-238 (half-life = 4.5 billion years). A rock contains U-238 and its decay product Pb-206 in a 1:1 ratio. How old is the rock? Hard
Show Answer

1:1 ratio means half the U-238 has decayed → 1 half-life. Age = 4.5 billion years.

Tritium (H-3) has a half-life of 12.3 years. A warhead manufactured in 1999 is checked in 2023. What fraction of tritium remains? Hard
Show Answer

Time = 2023−1999 = 24 years = 24/12.3 ≈ 1.95 half-lives. Fraction = (1/2)^1.95 ≈ 0.259 ≈ 26%. About 26% remains, which is why warheads must be periodically recharged with tritium.

Why can't C-14 dating be used for objects older than ~50 000 years? Suggest an isotope for dating billion-year-old rocks. Hard
Show Answer

After ~8–9 half-lives (~50 000 years), C-14 activity is too small to measure accurately. For billion-year-old rocks, use K-40 (half-life = 1.25 billion years) or U-238 (half-life = 4.5 billion years) — their longer half-lives match geological timescales.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

Evaluate the ethical implications of using radioactive isotopes in medicine. What benefits and risks must doctors weigh? Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: targeted cancer treatment (I-131), precise imaging (Tc-99m), fewer side effects than surgery. Risks: radiation exposure to healthy tissue, long-term mutation risk, radioactive waste disposal. Doctors use the ALARA principle — minimum radiation for maximum diagnostic/therapeutic benefit.

An archaeologist claims a wooden artefact is 15 000 years old based on C-14 dating. Critically evaluate this claim. What assumptions must hold? Hard
Show Answer

Assumptions: (1) atmospheric C-14/C-12 ratio was constant over 15 000 years (calibration curves correct for variation). (2) No contamination with older/newer carbon. (3) Organism was alive and exchanging carbon until death. (4) Accurate half-life measurement. Well-calibrated dating is reliable to ±1–5%, so ≈15 000 ± 150–750 years.

Compare physical and chemical properties of protium (H-1), deuterium (H-2), and tritium (H-3). Why is heavy water (D&sub2;O) different from ordinary water? Hard
Show Answer

All three have identical chemical properties (1 proton, 1 electron). Physically: masses differ (1, 2, 3 u). Heavy water (D&sub2;O) has mass 20 vs 18 for H&sub2;O: higher melting point (3.8°C), higher boiling point (101.4°C), higher density (1.105 g/cm³). It is toxic in large amounts (slows biological reactions).

Why is nuclear power based on U-235 fission rather than U-238? Connect to isotopic properties. Hard
Show Answer

U-235 is fissile — it readily undergoes fission when struck by a slow neutron, releasing energy + more neutrons (chain reaction). U-238 is not directly fissile with slow neutrons. Natural uranium is only 0.7% U-235; reactors require enrichment to 3–5%. Different neutron numbers (143 vs 146) create dramatically different nuclear properties.

Describe how isotope analysis of ancient ice cores can reveal past temperatures. Hard
Show Answer

Ice cores and shells contain different ratios of oxygen isotopes (O-16/O-18). During cold periods, lighter O-16 evaporates more readily and falls as snow, enriching ice with O-16. During warm periods, more O-18 is available. Measuring O-isotope ratios allows reconstruction of temperature records going back 800 000 years.

Write a letter from a radiochemist to a sceptical newspaper editor explaining why radioactive isotopes used in medicine are safe. Hard
Show Answer

Dear Editor, Medical isotopes are selected for short half-lives (minutes to days) so they decay to harmless levels quickly. Doses are carefully calculated to benefit target tissue with minimum exposure to healthy tissue. Regulatory bodies (AERB in India) monitor all use. The diagnostic and therapeutic benefits — early cancer detection, thyroid treatment — far outweigh the small, carefully managed radiation risk. Yours sincerely, Dr. [Radiochemist].

If you were a scientist in 1910 (before isotopes were discovered), how would you explain chlorine's atomic mass of 35.5? Medium
Show Answer

In 1910, a scientist might think chlorine has a non-integer atomic mass as a fundamental property, or that measurements are imprecise. The discovery of isotopes (Soddy, 1913) resolved the mystery: 35.5 is a weighted average of Cl-35 (75%) and Cl-37 (25%), explaining a long-standing puzzle in chemistry.

How do stable and radioactive isotopes both have industrial applications? Give one specific example of each. Hard
Show Answer

Stable isotopes: O-18 is used in isotope-ratio mass spectrometry for climate research and food authentication. Radioactive isotopes: Co-60 (gamma emitter) is used in industrial radiography to find cracks in metal pipelines and in food irradiation to extend shelf life without heating.

Set A Recall & Concept

State the maximum number of electrons in shells 1, 2, 3, and 4. Easy
Show Answer

Shell 1: max 2. Shell 2: max 8. Shell 3: max 18 (or 8 for elements up to Z=20). Shell 4: max 32. General rule: max = 2n² where n is shell number.

Write the electronic configuration of sodium (Z=11) and chlorine (Z=17). Easy
Show Answer

Sodium (Na, Z=11): 2, 8, 1 (1 valence electron). Chlorine (Cl, Z=17): 2, 8, 7 (7 valence electrons).

What are valence electrons and why are they important? Easy
Show Answer

Valence electrons are electrons in the outermost shell. They determine chemical reactivity, bonding behaviour, and periodic table group. Elements with the same valence electron count have similar chemical properties.

What electronic configuration gives an atom maximum stability? Give two examples. Easy
Show Answer

A full outer shell (2 for shell 1; 8 for shells 2–3) gives maximum stability. Examples: Helium (2), Neon (2,8), Argon (2,8,8) — all noble gases with full outer shells, chemically inert.

How does sodium achieve a stable electronic configuration when it reacts with chlorine? Medium
Show Answer

Sodium (2,8,1) loses its 1 valence electron to become Na⁺ with configuration 2,8 (like neon). Chlorine (2,8,7) gains that electron to become Cl⁻ with configuration 2,8,8 (like argon). Both achieve stable full outer shells.

Define cation and anion. Which does sodium form and which does chlorine form? Easy
Show Answer

Cation: positive ion (loses electrons). Anion: negative ion (gains electrons). Sodium forms a cation (Na⁺: loses 1 electron). Chlorine forms an anion (Cl⁻: gains 1 electron).

Write the electronic configurations of the first 10 elements (H to Ne). Medium
Show Answer

H: 1 | He: 2 | Li: 2,1 | Be: 2,2 | B: 2,3 | C: 2,4 | N: 2,5 | O: 2,6 | F: 2,7 | Ne: 2,8.

Why do elements in the same group of the periodic table have similar chemical properties? Medium
Show Answer

Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons. Since chemical reactions involve valence electrons, elements with the same valence count react similarly. E.g., all Group 1 elements (Li, Na, K) have 1 valence electron and react similarly with water.

Write the electronic configuration of: (a) Aluminium (Z=13), (b) Calcium (Z=20), (c) Argon (Z=18). Easy

An element has electronic configuration 2, 8, 6. Name the element, its group, and predict what ion it forms. Medium

Total electrons = 16, Z=16 = Sulphur (S). Group 16 (6 valence electrons). It gains 2 electrons to form S²⁻ (sulphide ion), achieving 2,8,8 configuration.

Magnesium (Z=12) reacts with oxygen (Z=8) to form MgO. Show the electron transfer and resulting electronic configurations. Medium

Mg (2,8,2) loses 2 electrons → Mg²⁺ (2,8) like neon. O (2,6) gains 2 electrons → O²⁻ (2,8) like neon. MgO is ionic: Mg²⁺O²⁻.

Which elements have the configurations (a) 2, 8, 4 and (b) 2, 8, 8, 1? Name them and predict their reactivity. Medium

(a) Z=14 = Silicon. 4 valence electrons — forms covalent bonds, semiconductor. (b) Z=19 = Potassium. 1 valence electron — very reactive metal, loses 1 electron easily to form K⁺.

Describe electron shell diagrams for Na, Cl, Na⁺, and Cl⁻. Compare the ion diagrams to the neutral atoms. Medium

An element X is in period 3 with 3 valence electrons. Identify X and state how many electrons it loses when forming an ion. Easy
Show Answer

Period 3 with 3 valence electrons = 2,8,3 = Z=13 = Aluminium (Al). It loses 3 electrons to form Al³⁺ (configuration 2,8, like neon).

How many electrons does each shell contain for argon (2,8,8)? Verify that argon is chemically inert. Easy
Show Answer

Shell 1: 2 (full). Shell 2: 8 (full). Shell 3: 8 (full for period 3). Total = 18 = Z=18 = Argon. With a full outer shell (8 electrons), argon has no tendency to gain or lose electrons — chemically inert (noble gas).

Predict the formula of the compound formed between calcium (Z=20) and chlorine (Z=17). Justify using electronic configurations. Hard
Show Answer

Ca (2,8,8,2) loses 2 electrons → Ca²⁺. Cl (2,8,7) gains 1 electron → Cl⁻. To balance: 1 Ca²⁺ + 2 Cl⁻ → formula CaCl&sub2; (calcium chloride).

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

Explain why noble gases are chemically inert using electronic configuration and the octet rule. Medium
Show Answer

Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe) all have full outer electron shells (He: 2; others: 8 outer electrons = octet). The octet rule states 8 outer electrons = maximum stability. With full outer shells, noble gases have no energetic driving force to gain, lose, or share electrons — essentially unreactive.

Why do transition metals (like iron, Z=26) not follow the simple 2,8,8 filling rule? What complicates their configurations? Hard
Show Answer

Transition metals have d orbitals close in energy to the outer s orbital. Electrons fill 3d and 4s orbitals in a complex order. Fe configuration: 2,8,14,2 (using d orbitals). This creates variable valence states (Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺) and special magnetic/catalytic properties not seen in main group elements.

A mystery element has configuration 2,8,8,2. Predict its (a) atomic number, (b) group, (c) ion formed, (d) bonding type. Medium
Show Answer

(a) Z = 2+8+8+2 = 20 = Calcium. (b) Group 2 (2 valence electrons). (c) Ion: Ca²⁺ (loses 2 electrons). (d) Ionic bonding — Ca²⁺ transfers electrons to non-metals, forming ionic compounds with high melting points.

How does electronic configuration explain why period 2 elements are limited to an octet but period 3 elements can exceed 8 valence electrons? Hard
Show Answer

Period 2 has only 2s and 2p orbitals (max 8 electrons). Period 3 also has 3d orbitals available, allowing "expanded octets." This permits sulphur (SF&sub6;: 12 electrons around S) and phosphorus to form more than 4 bonds when energetically favourable.

Lithium (2,1) and sodium (2,8,1) are both in Group 1. Why is sodium more reactive despite having the same number of valence electrons? Hard
Show Answer

Sodium's valence electron is in shell 3, further from the nucleus than lithium's (shell 2). Greater distance + more electron shielding = weaker nuclear attraction = lower ionisation energy. It's easier to remove Na's outer electron, making sodium more reactive.

Explain how electronic configuration determines whether NaCl forms by ionic bonding and H&sub2;O by covalent bonding. Medium
Show Answer

NaCl: Na (2,8,1) — metal, low ionisation energy; Cl (2,8,7) — non-metal, high electron affinity. Large electronegativity difference → electron transfer → ionic bond. H&sub2;O: H (1) and O (2,6) — both non-metals, similar electronegativities, neither fully transfers electrons → share electrons → covalent bond.

How does the periodic table's structure reflect electronic configurations? Explain periods and groups in terms of electrons. Hard
Show Answer

Periods = shell numbers being filled. Period 1: shell 1 filling (2 elements). Period 2: shell 2 filling (8 elements). Groups = valence electron number: Group 1 = 1 valence electron; Group 17 = 7; Group 18 = full outer shell. The entire periodic table is a map of electron configurations.

Design a "most reactive element" competition between fluorine (Z=9) and cesium (Z=55). What drives each element's extreme reactivity? Hard
Show Answer

Fluorine (2,7): most electronegative; small size means valence electrons are close to a highly charged nucleus (9 protons), giving enormous electron affinity. It desperately wants 1 more electron. Cesium (2,8,18,18,8,1): 6 shells, outermost electron very far from nucleus and shielded — extremely easy to remove. In a reaction, Cs immediately donates its electron to F — one of the most violent reactions in chemistry. Two different drivers: F wants to gain; Cs wants to lose.
