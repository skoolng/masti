Set A Recall — Factual Knowledge Easy

What does "objectivity" mean in the context of scientific research?
Show Answer

Objectivity means making observations and drawing conclusions based on evidence alone, without letting personal feelings, beliefs, or bias influence the results.

Easy
Define "peer review" and explain its purpose in science.
Show Answer

Peer review is the process by which independent experts in the same field evaluate a scientist's work before publication. Its purpose is to check for errors, verify methods, and ensure conclusions are supported by evidence.

Easy
What is "informed consent" in scientific research involving human participants?
Show Answer

Informed consent means participants are fully told about the nature, risks, and benefits of the research before agreeing to take part. Participation must be voluntary and participants can withdraw at any time.

Easy
Name the three Rs used to guide ethical animal research.
Show Answer

Replace (use alternatives to animals where possible), Reduce (use the minimum number of animals needed), and Refine (improve procedures to minimise suffering).

Easy
What is "data fabrication" and why is it considered scientific misconduct?
Show Answer

Data fabrication means inventing or making up results that were never actually collected. It is misconduct because it creates false knowledge, wastes resources, and can harm the public if decisions are based on fake evidence.

Easy
What is the difference between data sharing and data privacy in research?
Show Answer

Data sharing means making research data available to other scientists to verify results or build new work. Data privacy means protecting participants' personal information from being disclosed without consent. Both are ethical obligations.

Easy
Give one example of a historical breach of research ethics involving human subjects.
Show Answer

The Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932–1972) in the USA, where African American men with syphilis were left untreated without consent even after penicillin became available, to study the disease's progression.

Easy
Why must scientists disclose conflicts of interest when publishing research?
Show Answer

A conflict of interest (e.g., funding from a company that benefits from the results) can bias research. Disclosure allows readers and reviewers to evaluate whether the findings might be influenced by financial or personal interests.

Easy
What does "replication" mean in science, and why is it important?
Show Answer

Replication means repeating an experiment to see if the same results are obtained. It is important because a result that cannot be reproduced by other scientists may be due to error, bias, or fraud.

Easy
State one reason why open-access publication of scientific findings benefits society.
Show Answer

Open access allows anyone — including scientists in developing countries, policymakers, and the public — to read and use research findings without a paywall, accelerating the application of knowledge for social benefit.

Set B Analyse — Data & Arguments Medium

A pharmaceutical company funds a study showing its drug is safe. The same company owns the journal publishing the study. Identify two ethical concerns with this scenario.
Show Answer

1. Conflict of interest: the funder benefits financially from a positive result, creating pressure to publish favourable data. 2. Publication bias through journal ownership: negative or neutral results may be suppressed, giving an inaccurate picture of the drug's safety profile.

Medium
A researcher tests a new painkiller on mice without seeking ethics committee approval. Explain why this is problematic even if the research produces valid data.
Show Answer

Without ethics committee approval, there is no independent check that suffering was minimised (Refine), that the number of animals was justified (Reduce), or that alternatives were considered (Replace). The research may be valid but is ethically unacceptable and would likely be rejected by reputable journals.

Medium
Analyse the statement: "Because peer review exists, all published science is trustworthy." Do you agree? Use evidence.
Show Answer

No. Peer review reduces but does not eliminate errors. The "replication crisis" showed many high-profile psychology and medicine studies could not be reproduced. Peer reviewers may miss fraud, have their own biases, or lack access to raw data. Peer review is a quality filter, not a guarantee.

Medium
A scientist finds their experiment results are slightly different from their hypothesis. They adjust two data points to make results "cleaner." Classify this action and explain its consequences.
Show Answer

This is data manipulation/falsification — a form of scientific misconduct. Consequences include: retraction of published work, damage to scientific reputation, wasted follow-up research built on false data, and potential public harm if the research informs medical or policy decisions.

Medium
Examine the argument that animal testing should be completely banned. Present one argument for and one against.
Show Answer

For: Animals cannot give consent and suffer pain; many animal models do not predict human responses accurately (only ~8% of drugs that pass animal trials succeed in humans). Against: Some life-saving discoveries (e.g., insulin, polio vaccine) required animal research, and no fully reliable alternatives exist for all tests yet. The 3Rs provide a middle ground.

Medium
A news headline reads: "Scientists prove coffee causes cancer." What questions should you ask before accepting this claim?
Show Answer

Was this peer-reviewed? Was it a correlation or controlled experiment? What was the sample size? Was it in humans or animals? Who funded it? Has it been replicated? What is the effect size? The word "prove" is itself a red flag — science uses evidence to support, not prove.

Medium
Why might a scientist in a lower-income country face different ethical pressures compared to one in a high-income country? Give two examples.
Show Answer

1. Funding pressure: limited grants may push researchers to produce results that satisfy funders rather than pursue objective inquiry. 2. Regulatory gaps: weaker ethics oversight may allow clinical trials with less rigorous informed consent standards, sometimes exploiting vulnerable populations.

Medium
Data from a long-term study on climate change is stored only on one researcher's private hard drive and destroyed when they retire. Explain the ethical issue and its impact on science.
Show Answer

This violates data sharing and preservation norms. Long-term datasets are irreplaceable; their loss prevents future scientists from reanalysing trends with new methods or verifying original conclusions, undermining the cumulative nature of scientific knowledge.

Medium
Compare the ethical obligations of a scientist publishing in an academic journal versus a science journalist writing for the public.
Show Answer

Scientists must accurately report methods and data, disclose conflicts, and submit to peer review. Journalists must translate findings accurately without sensationalising, attribute sources, and represent uncertainty honestly. Both share a duty of accuracy but scientists have formal accountability mechanisms (retraction, misconduct procedures) while journalism relies more on editorial standards.

Medium
A government wants to keep a scientific report on pesticide safety confidential for "commercial reasons." Evaluate the ethical tension involved.
Show Answer

Tension between commercial confidentiality (protecting corporate investment) and the public's right to know risks affecting health and environment. Scientific ethics favour transparency and data sharing; suppressing safety data violates the principle that science should serve the public good and prevents independent verification.

"Science should always be completely transparent — all raw data must be publicly released immediately after collection." Critically evaluate this statement using ethical principles and real examples.
Show Answer

Issue: Tension between open science and participant privacy/intellectual property. Scientific basis: Open data accelerates discovery and allows error-checking; however, raw genomic or medical data can re-identify individuals. Benefits: Reproducibility, faster progress (e.g., COVID-19 genome shared in 10 days enabled vaccine development). Risks: Privacy violation, premature release of unverified data causing public panic. Recommendation: Mandate data sharing after anonymisation and upon publication, with embargoes for sensitive personal data — as per FAIR data principles. Limitations: Anonymisation is not always foolproof; small communities may still be re-identified.

Hard
The Nuremberg Code (1947) established that human experimentation requires voluntary consent. Evaluate whether this principle is always followed today and what safeguards exist.
Show Answer

Issue: Protection of human research participants globally. Scientific basis: The Nuremberg Code and later Declaration of Helsinki (1964) require voluntary informed consent, ethical review, and proportional risk-benefit assessment. Benefits of these codes: Protection against exploitation; established IRB/ethics committee systems. Evidence of failures: Guatemala syphilis experiments (1940s, revealed 2010); some COVID trial shortcuts in lower-income countries. Recommendation: Mandatory independent ethics review for all human research; global treaty enforcement. Limitations: Enforcement varies by country; power imbalances between researchers and vulnerable participants persist.

Hard
Evaluate the societal impact of the "publish or perish" culture in academic science. Does pressure to publish frequently compromise scientific integrity?
Show Answer

Issue: Academic career systems reward quantity of publications over quality, creating incentives for misconduct. Scientific basis: Studies show p-hacking (manipulating analysis until p<0.05), HARKing (hypothesising after results are known), and salami slicing (splitting one study into many papers) inflate the literature. Benefits of high publication rates: Rapid dissemination. Risks: Replication crisis — only 36–50% of psychology studies replicated in large-scale attempts. Recommendation: Reform evaluation metrics to reward replication, pre-registration, and data sharing (e.g., DORA declaration). Limitations: Individual institutions resist reform; changing incentive structures takes decades.

Hard
A scientist discovers that their published findings on a widely prescribed drug were based on an error. They fear career damage if they retract the paper. Evaluate the ethical course of action and its societal consequences.
Show Answer

Issue: Individual vs collective responsibility in science. Scientific basis: Retraction is the established mechanism for correcting the scientific record; erroneous drug efficacy data can lead to inappropriate prescribing, patient harm, and misallocated research funding. Benefits of retraction: Protects public health; upholds scientific integrity. Costs: Career damage, institutional embarrassment. Recommendation: Retract promptly and transparently; institutions must protect whistle-blowers and frame retraction as responsible conduct, not failure. Limitations: Social stigma around retraction persists; systemic reform of how retractions are perceived is slow.

Hard
Evaluate the argument that privately funded scientific research (e.g., by pharmaceutical or tech corporations) is inherently less trustworthy than publicly funded research.
Show Answer

Issue: Source of funding as a bias factor in science. Scientific basis: Meta-analyses show industry-funded drug trials are 4× more likely to report favourable results than independent trials. Benefits of private funding: Enables large-scale trials governments cannot afford; drives translational research. Risks: Selective reporting, suppression of negative results (e.g., tobacco industry's internal research on cancer). Recommendation: Require full pre-registration of trials; mandatory disclosure; independent re-analysis of industry data before regulatory approval. Limitations: Pre-registration compliance is imperfect; global harmonisation of rules is incomplete.

Hard
Should scientific data collected by a government agency be freely accessible to citizens? Evaluate using examples of both secrecy and openness in government science.
Show Answer

Issue: Democratic accountability vs national security/commercial sensitivity. Scientific basis: Open government data (e.g., India's Open Government Data platform, NASA's open satellite data) has enabled citizen science and policy analysis. Secrecy examples: EPA suppression of fracking contamination data (USA, 2000s); classified nuclear site health data. Benefits of openness: Public trust, independent verification, innovation. Risks: Security concerns for dual-use research; premature disclosure of incomplete data. Recommendation: Default to open access after anonymisation and national security review; establish independent oversight committees. Limitations: "National security" can be misused as justification for hiding inconvenient environmental truths.

Hard
The CRISPR-Cas9 gene-editing baby experiment by He Jiankui (2018) was condemned worldwide. Evaluate the ethical failures involved and what international governance should look like.
Show Answer

Issue: Germline human gene editing without societal consensus. Scientific basis: He edited embryos to disable CCR5 (HIV resistance), creating heritable changes. The science was premature — off-target effects not fully understood. Ethical failures: No valid informed consent (parents misled about risks); no independent ethics approval; violated international moratoriums. Benefits claimed: HIV resistance. Risks: Unknown heritable mutations passed to future generations without their consent. Recommendation: International binding treaty (not just voluntary guidelines) with criminal penalties for non-compliance; WHO oversight committee. Limitations: Science advances faster than governance; non-signatory nations can become safe havens for rogue research.

Hard
Evaluate whether the three Rs (Replace, Reduce, Refine) are sufficient to make animal research ethically acceptable, or whether a complete ban is justified.
Show Answer

Issue: Moral status of animals vs advancement of medical science. Scientific basis: ~115 million animals used in research globally per year; 3Rs framework reduces suffering but does not eliminate it. Organoids, computer models, and organ-on-chip technologies are emerging alternatives. Benefits of 3Rs: Pragmatic middle ground; enabled numerous medical breakthroughs. Limitations: Many tests (e.g., whole-body toxicity, behavioural neuroscience) have no validated alternatives yet. Recommendation: Maintain 3Rs as minimum standard while mandating R&D investment in alternatives; set phase-out timelines for species with highest sentience (great apes already banned in most countries). Limitations: Validating new alternatives takes decades; banning all animal research today would halt some life-saving medicine development.

Hard
"Science is value-free — scientists should only report facts, not recommend policies." Critically evaluate this view from a Criterion D perspective.
Show Answer

Issue: Role of scientists in public decision-making. Scientific basis: The fact-value distinction holds that science can describe what is, not what ought to be done. However, scientists have domain expertise relevant to evaluating policy options. Argument for value-free view: Prevents politicisation of science; maintains public trust in objectivity. Against: Climate scientists remained silent for decades; lead scientists withheld recommendations on leaded petrol despite evidence of harm. Recommendation: Scientists should clearly separate empirical findings from policy recommendations but are ethically entitled to make recommendations when evidence clearly shows public harm. Transparency about which statements are scientific vs value judgements is essential. Limitations: The line between "fact" and "recommendation" is blurry in complex socio-scientific issues.

Hard
Evaluate the ethical responsibilities of scientists in communicating uncertainty to the public, using examples from COVID-19 or climate science.
Show Answer

Issue: Public understanding and trust in science during uncertainty. Scientific basis: Science works through probabilistic claims and updating with new evidence — early COVID-19 guidance changed on masks, transmission, and reinfection as evidence accumulated. Benefits of communicating uncertainty: Builds long-term trust; prepares public for evolving guidance. Risks of poorly communicated uncertainty: Can be exploited by denialists ("scientists don't even know"); fuels conspiracy theories. Evidence: WHO's early "no evidence of human-to-human transmission" tweet (Jan 2020) damaged credibility. Recommendation: Train scientists in science communication; use probabilistic language ("90% confident") rather than binary statements; distinguish between "uncertain" and "unknown." Limitations: Media and social media compress nuance; scientists cannot control how findings are reported.

Set A Recall — Factual Knowledge Easy

What is Bt cotton and which gene does it contain?
Show Answer

Bt cotton is a genetically modified cotton plant that contains a gene from the soil bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis. This gene produces a protein (Cry protein / Bt toxin) that is toxic to certain insect pests, particularly the bollworm, reducing the need for insecticide sprays.

Easy
What nutrient does Golden Rice produce and why was it developed?
Show Answer

Golden Rice produces beta-carotene (a precursor to vitamin A) in its endosperm. It was developed to address vitamin A deficiency (VAD), which causes blindness and increases child mortality in regions where rice is the staple food.

Easy
How is human insulin produced using bacteria in biotechnology?
Show Answer

The human insulin gene is inserted into a plasmid (circular bacterial DNA) using restriction enzymes and DNA ligase. The recombinant plasmid is placed into E. coli bacteria, which multiply and produce human insulin protein. This is purified and used to treat diabetes.

Easy
Define "gene flow" and explain why it is a concern with GM crops.
Show Answer

Gene flow is the transfer of genes from one population to another, often via pollen. With GM crops, modified genes (e.g., herbicide resistance) could spread to wild relatives or non-GM crops, potentially creating "superweeds" or contaminating organic farms.

Easy
State two benefits of GM crops for farmers in developing countries.
Show Answer

1. Higher yields: pest-resistant or drought-tolerant crops reduce losses and increase food output. 2. Reduced pesticide costs: insect-resistant crops (like Bt cotton) require fewer pesticide applications, saving money and reducing farmer exposure to chemicals.

Easy
What does "herbicide-tolerant GM crop" mean? Give one example.
Show Answer

A herbicide-tolerant crop is genetically modified to survive exposure to a specific herbicide (weed killer). Example: Roundup Ready soybean is modified to tolerate glyphosate herbicide, allowing farmers to spray fields and kill weeds without harming the crop.

Easy
What is the difference between selective breeding (traditional) and genetic modification?
Show Answer

Selective breeding involves choosing organisms with desired traits to reproduce together over many generations — it works within the same or related species. Genetic modification directly inserts, deletes, or alters specific genes, often across species boundaries, in a single generation.

Easy
Name one country where Bt cotton is widely grown and describe its impact on pesticide use.
Show Answer

India. After the introduction of Bt cotton in 2002, insecticide use on cotton fell by approximately 50% in many regions, reducing costs for farmers and decreasing environmental contamination from chemical pesticides.

Easy
What is meant by "biodiversity loss" in the context of GM monocultures?
Show Answer

Biodiversity loss occurs when large areas are planted with a single GM variety, replacing diverse local crop varieties. This reduces genetic diversity, making agriculture vulnerable to new pests or diseases, and can eliminate wild plant species from agricultural landscapes.

Easy
Give one social concern about large corporations owning patents on GM seeds.
Show Answer

If corporations patent GM seeds, farmers must repurchase seeds each season rather than saving them, increasing dependence on large companies and potentially making farming unaffordable for small-scale farmers in developing countries.

Easy

Set B Analyse — Data & Arguments Medium

A study shows Bt cotton farmers in Andhra Pradesh, India, initially increased profits but experienced debt crises a decade later. Analyse two reasons why initial benefits may not be sustained.
Show Answer

1. Pest resistance: bollworms evolved resistance to Bt toxin over time, requiring farmers to revert to expensive pesticides. 2. Seed costs: patented Bt seeds must be repurchased each year at higher prices, and when yield advantages diminish, debt accumulates. Some studies also note that secondary pests (not targeted by Bt toxin) increased, requiring additional sprays.

Medium
Critics argue Golden Rice will not solve vitamin A deficiency because distribution, not production, is the real problem. Evaluate this argument.
Show Answer

This is a valid structural argument: VAD is most severe in communities with poor access to diverse food, healthcare, and markets. Simply growing Golden Rice does not address poverty, food storage challenges, or cultural food preferences. However, if distribution infrastructure is in place, Golden Rice adds a passive dietary source of beta-carotene that requires no behaviour change — complementing, not replacing, broader interventions.

Medium
Compare the environmental risks of GM herbicide-tolerant crops with the environmental benefits they claim to provide.
Show Answer

Claimed benefit: allows targeted herbicide use, reducing overall volume of multiple herbicides. Risks: selection pressure creates herbicide-resistant "superweeds" (e.g., Palmer amaranth resistant to glyphosate in USA); encourages overuse of a single herbicide, potentially contaminating water systems; may reduce plant diversity in field margins affecting pollinators.

Medium
Analyse why public acceptance of GMOs differs significantly between the USA (largely accepting) and the European Union (largely resistant). Identify two social factors.
Show Answer

1. Regulatory framing: EU uses the precautionary principle (ban unless proven safe); USA uses "substantial equivalence" (permitted unless proven harmful) — shaping public trust differently. 2. Cultural values around food: European food culture places high value on "natural" food and traditional agriculture, whereas the USA has historically valued technological innovation and corporate agriculture.

Medium
A scientist claims: "There is scientific consensus that approved GM foods are safe to eat." Is this accurate? What does scientific consensus mean and does it apply here?
Show Answer

Largely accurate for currently approved GM foods: major bodies (WHO, National Academies of Sciences USA, European Academies of Science) agree that GM foods in the market have not demonstrated human health risks. However, "consensus" means overwhelming expert agreement, not unanimity; it also does not address long-term ecological impacts, which are contested.

Medium
How does gene flow from GM crops to wild relatives illustrate the principle that technology can have unintended consequences? Give a specific example.
Show Answer

In Mexico, studies detected transgenes from GM maize in wild teosinte (maize's wild ancestor) despite import bans on GM maize. This illustrates that pollen dispersal is difficult to control across distances, and once a gene enters a wild population it cannot be recalled — a permanent, unintended ecological change potentially affecting genetic diversity.

Medium
Biotechnology companies argue that GM crops reduce pesticide use. Present data or reasoning that complicates this claim.
Show Answer

Initial reductions in insecticide use with Bt crops are real, but herbicide use has increased significantly with herbicide-tolerant crops — in the USA, glyphosate use increased by 15× between 1996 and 2014 on GM soybean/corn/cotton. Total pesticide (insecticide + herbicide) use is often not lower. Additionally, resistance evolution requires progressively higher doses.

Medium
Analyse the ethical tension between a farmer's right to choose non-GM seeds and their neighbour's GM crop potentially cross-pollinating their field.
Show Answer

This is a conflict between two rights: the GM farmer's right to grow their legal crop and the non-GM farmer's right to maintain their chosen farming system (organic certification, for example, prohibits GM contamination). The harm falls asymmetrically on the non-GM farmer who loses organic status and income. This raises questions about who bears the cost of contamination — typically the GM seed company in some jurisdictions, but not all.

Medium
Why might small-scale subsistence farmers in Africa benefit more from drought-tolerant GM crops than from insect-resistant GM crops? Analyse using context.
Show Answer

Subsistence farmers in sub-Saharan Africa face water scarcity as a primary constraint, not necessarily insect pressure. Drought-tolerant varieties (e.g., WEMA maize) address their most binding limitation. They also typically cannot afford chemical inputs even if pests were the issue, whereas drought tolerance is an intrinsic crop trait requiring no additional purchases.

Medium
Evaluate whether labelling GM food is a matter of science or a matter of values.
Show Answer

Scientifically, if approved GM foods are equivalent in safety and nutrition to conventional foods, there is no scientific basis for mandatory labelling to protect health. However, labelling is also a matter of consumer autonomy and values — people have the right to make choices based on personal ethics (e.g., religious beliefs, environmental concerns) even without a health risk. Most ethicists support mandatory labelling on rights grounds, not safety grounds.

Evaluate whether developing countries should embrace GM crops as a primary strategy for achieving food security, considering economic, ecological, and social dimensions.
Show Answer

Issue: Food insecurity in developing nations — 800M+ people undernourished globally. Scientific basis: GM crops can increase yield (Bt maize in Africa: +24% yield), reduce pest damage, and incorporate nutritional traits. But food insecurity is multifactorial — access, distribution, poverty, and infrastructure matter more than production alone. Benefits: Higher yields; reduced crop losses; drought tolerance relevant to climate-stressed regions. Risks: Corporate dependency; loss of traditional seed saving; gene flow; inadequate regulatory capacity in some nations. Evidence: Bangladesh Bt brinjal (eggplant): reduced pesticide use by 61%, increased farmer income significantly. Recommendation: GM crops should be one tool among many — alongside land reform, irrigation infrastructure, and market access — with strong national biosafety regulation and preferential development of public-sector GM crops (not only corporate). Limitations: GM crop development is expensive; public institutions in developing countries lack R&D funding; benefit realization depends on intact supply chains.

Hard
Critically evaluate the societal and scientific arguments for and against the commercial release of Golden Rice in India and Bangladesh.
Show Answer

Issue: Vitamin A deficiency affects ~190 million children globally; rice-eating populations in South and Southeast Asia most vulnerable. Scientific basis: Golden Rice 2 contains ~37 µg beta-carotene per gram; human trials show it converts to vitamin A effectively. Approved by Bangladesh in 2021 (world first). Benefits: Passive, low-cost delivery of vitamin A through existing dietary staple; no behaviour change required. Risks/Criticisms: Greenpeace argues it diverts attention from systemic solutions (dietary diversity, supplementation programmes); cultural resistance to yellow rice in some communities; yields of early varieties were lower than conventional rice. Recommendation: Deploy alongside (not instead of) vitamin supplementation, breastfeeding promotion, and dietary diversification; ensure farmers have access to freely saved seeds (Golden Rice is publicly licensed). Limitations: Beta-carotene conversion varies by individual fat intake; addressing all VAD requires systemic food systems change.

Hard
"The ecological risks of GM crops are so uncertain that a global moratorium on their release should be implemented." Evaluate this statement using the precautionary principle and evidence.
Show Answer

Issue: Managing technological risk under uncertainty in ecological systems. Scientific basis: Ecological risks include gene flow, non-target organism effects (e.g., Bt toxin and non-target insects), and biodiversity reduction. Evidence of serious harm is limited but long-term ecosystem studies are rare. Precautionary principle: When an action risks harm and scientific uncertainty is high, burden of proof shifts to those proposing the action. Against moratorium: 30 years and billions of hectares of GM cultivation have not demonstrated catastrophic ecological harm; moratorium would delay benefits to food security and nutrition. For precaution: Gene flow is permanent and irreversible; ecosystems are complex and surprises emerge on 50–100 year timescales, beyond current study periods. Recommendation: Case-by-case risk assessment rather than blanket moratorium, with mandatory post-release monitoring requirements and clear trigger criteria for suspension. Limitations: "Case-by-case" assessment requires capacity that many countries lack; cumulative effects of many GM crops released simultaneously are hard to assess individually.

Hard
Evaluate the ethical implications of a multinational seed company patenting GM crop varieties that are based on genes originally found in traditional crop varieties developed by indigenous farmers over centuries.
Show Answer

Issue: Biopiracy and intellectual property in biotechnology. Scientific basis: Traditional crop varieties contain locally adapted traits (e.g., drought tolerance genes) developed through generations of selective breeding by indigenous communities. GM companies isolate and patent these traits. Benefits claimed: Commercialisation funds further R&D; products benefit farmers globally. Ethical problems: Indigenous communities receive no compensation; their knowledge and genetic heritage is privatised. Violates Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) principles of fair and equitable benefit-sharing. Example: Neem patents (USA companies patenting traditional Indian uses), basmati rice patent controversy. Recommendation: Strengthen enforcement of the Nagoya Protocol requiring prior informed consent from source communities and benefit-sharing; establish independent international biopiracy review tribunal. Limitations: Many biopiracy cases involve knowledge that was already in the public domain, making legal challenge difficult; power asymmetry between corporations and indigenous communities in legal proceedings.

Hard
Evaluate the role of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing in the future of crop development, assessing whether it raises different ethical concerns from traditional transgenics (foreign-gene GMOs).
Show Answer

Issue: Regulatory and ethical classification of new gene-editing technologies. Scientific basis: CRISPR-Cas9 can make precise cuts in an organism's own DNA to knock out or modify existing genes without inserting foreign DNA. This produces changes that could theoretically arise through natural mutation or conventional breeding. Benefits: More precise; faster; cheaper; no foreign DNA means potentially lower regulatory burden and public resistance. Examples: disease-resistant wheat, hornless cattle, high-oleic soybean. Ethical questions: (1) If no foreign gene is present, should gene-edited crops be regulated as GMOs? EU Court of Justice (2018) ruled yes; USA FDA largely said no for some edits. (2) Off-target edits still possible. (3) Same corporate control and patent issues apply. Recommendation: Regulate based on the nature of the trait/outcome rather than the technique; require transparency on edits made even if exempted from GMO labelling. Limitations: Technique vs outcome regulation is philosophically contested; public trust requires more explanation than just "no foreign DNA."

Hard
India banned Bt brinjal (eggplant) in 2010 but approved Golden Rice trials and Bt cotton. Evaluate whether India's approach to GM crop regulation is scientifically consistent and socially equitable.
Show Answer

Issue: National GM crop regulation under political, scientific, and social pressures. Scientific basis: Bt brinjal moratorium was imposed by Minister Jairam Ramesh following public consultations, not primarily on new scientific evidence (GEAC had approved it). Bt cotton has been commercially grown since 2002. Benefits of moratorium: Allowed more time for independent biosafety studies; responded to concerns from brinjal farmers and civil society. Costs: Bangladesh went ahead with Bt brinjal in 2013 with positive results — Indian farmers missed pesticide reduction benefits. The decision-making process was more political than scientific. Social equity dimension: Small vegetable farmers (often poor) were denied a pest-management tool while cotton farmers (larger commercial operations) had access to Bt technology. Recommendation: India needs a transparent, independent science-based regulatory body insulated from political and corporate pressure, with clear evidence standards for both approval and moratorium decisions. Limitations: Science-based regulation still involves value choices about acceptable risk levels; public participation is legitimate even if it doesn't follow scientific consensus.

Hard
Corporations claim that without patent protection on GM seeds, there would be no investment in agricultural biotechnology. Critically evaluate this argument from multiple perspectives.
Show Answer

Issue: Intellectual property and innovation incentives in biotechnology vs. equitable access. Scientific basis: Patent protection allows companies to recoup R&D investment (~$136M average to bring a new GM crop to market per CropLife). Public goods argument: if seeds can be freely replicated, private investment collapses. Evidence for corporations: 90% of GM crop investment is private sector; without patents, development might stall. Evidence against: CGIAR public research centres developed drought-tolerant crops (WEMA maize) without patents and distributed freely; Golden Rice was publicly licensed. India's termination-of-license system restricts seed saving even without patents through contract. Counter: Patents on seeds of staple crops restrict farmers' rights to save seed — a practice fundamental to food sovereignty for 10,000 years. Recommendation: Two-tier system: patents permitted for commercial crops in high-income markets; compulsory licensing or public sector alternatives mandated for staple food crops in food-insecure regions. Limitations: Defining "food-insecure region" is politically contested; companies may exit markets where returns are capped.

Hard
Evaluate the claim that biotechnology, specifically GM crops, is essential to feeding a global population of 10 billion by 2050 in a climate-stressed world.
Show Answer

Issue: Global food security under population growth and climate change. Scientific basis: FAO projects 60–70% more food needed by 2050; climate change threatens yields (wheat -6% per 1°C warming). GM options include drought-tolerant varieties, nitrogen-fixing cereals, and heat-tolerant crops in development. Benefits: Yield gains (average 22% in meta-analyses); pest resistance reduces losses; biofortification addresses nutritional gaps. Limits of the claim: A 2017 IPES-Food report showed that agroecological methods matched or exceeded industrial yields in many contexts with lower environmental cost; one-third of current food production is wasted — eliminating waste alone could feed 2 billion more people. Shifting diets away from meat would free 75% of agricultural land. Recommendation: GM crops are one necessary tool but not sufficient alone; food security requires waste reduction, diet shifts, equitable distribution, and ecological farming integration. Over-reliance on any single technological fix is dangerous. Limitations: Diet and waste changes require massive social and political will; GM crop development timelines may not match urgency of climate impact.

Hard
Evaluate the tension between a farmer's economic freedom to grow GM crops and a community's right to maintain a "GM-free" region for cultural, ecological, or economic (e.g., export premium) reasons.
Show Answer

Issue: Individual vs community rights in agricultural biotechnology governance. Scientific basis: Pollen dispersal can travel kilometres; buffer zones reduce but do not eliminate cross-pollination. What is the "intermittency problem" with solar and wind energy?
Show Answer

Solar and wind power are intermittent — they only generate electricity when the sun shines or wind blows. This creates reliability challenges because supply must match demand at all times. Solutions include battery storage, pumped hydro storage, grid interconnection across large areas, and backup from dispatchable sources (gas, hydro, nuclear).

Limitations: Distinguishing deserving low-income fossil-fuel states from wealthy petrostates is politically difficult; production decline coordination across competing producers is an international collective action problem.

Evaluate the potential and limitations of hydrogen as a clean energy carrier, including its production methods and applications.
Show Answer

Issue: Role of hydrogen in decarbonising hard-to-abate sectors. Scientific basis: Hydrogen can store energy, fuel vehicles (fuel cells), and serve as industrial feedstock (steel, chemicals). "Green hydrogen" is produced by electrolysis of water using renewable electricity — producing only water when consumed. Currently, 96% of hydrogen is "grey" (from natural gas, with CO₂ emissions) or "blue" (grey + carbon capture, unproven at scale). Green hydrogen costs ~$4–7/kg vs grey hydrogen ~$1–2/kg (2023) — improvement expected as renewable electricity cheapens. Applications where hydrogen is best fit: Long-haul shipping, aviation (liquid hydrogen or ammonia), steel making (replacing coal in blast furnaces), long-duration seasonal energy storage. Limitations: Energy inefficiency: electrolysis is ~70% efficient; fuel cell is ~60% — so only 42% of input electricity is recovered; direct electrification where possible is more efficient. Infrastructure: hydrogen requires new pipelines/storage; highly flammable. Recommendation: Focus green hydrogen on sectors where direct electrification is impossible (aviation, shipping, steel); do not use it for passenger cars or heating where better alternatives exist; invest in electrolyser manufacturing scale-up to reduce green hydrogen costs. Limitations: Green hydrogen at scale requires enormous additional renewable capacity; timeline for cost-competitiveness is uncertain and production forecasts have often been optimistic.

Evaluate the ethical dimensions of the "resource curse" — the phenomenon where countries with abundant fossil fuel resources often have worse development outcomes than resource-poor countries.
Show Answer

Issue: Political economy of fossil fuel wealth and human development. Scientific basis / economic evidence: "Resource curse" (Sachs and Warner, 1995 and subsequent literature): oil-exporting states like Nigeria, Angola, Venezuela have not translated oil revenues into broad human development — high inequality, authoritarianism, and conflict are more common. Mechanisms: Dutch Disease (oil exports raise exchange rates, making other exports uncompetitive); rent-seeking replaces productive economic activity; oil revenues allow governments to avoid taxing citizens, reducing accountability; resource enclaves benefit foreign corporations and local elites. Ethical dimensions: International companies extract resources with contracts signed by non-representative governments — complicity in governance failures. Corrupt oil contracts violate intergenerational equity (resources belong to future citizens). Counterexamples: Norway, Botswana (diamonds) — resource wealth managed well through strong institutions and transparent funds. Recommendation: International mandatory disclosure standards for extractive contracts (Publish What You Pay); strengthen Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI); IMF conditions on sovereign wealth fund establishment; as fossil fuels are phased out, transition support for resource-dependent populations is a global obligation. Limitations: Resource governance reform requires political will from elites who benefit from opacity; international standards are voluntary; multinational corporations can exit markets that impose transparency requirements.

Evaluate whether the rapid decline in the cost of renewable energy alone is sufficient to drive the global energy transition at the pace required, or whether additional policy interventions are necessary.
Show Answer

Issue: Market vs policy drivers of energy transition. Scientific basis: Solar PV costs fell 90% between 2010 and 2023; wind fell 70%. In 2023, renewables were the cheapest new electricity source in most markets. Recommendation: Cost reductions are a necessary but not sufficient condition — policy must remove non-price barriers (permitting, grid, finance) and cover sectors where price signals alone are insufficient (buildings, heavy industry, agriculture). Limitations: Policy design is country-specific; political economy varies enormously; there is no single global energy transition policy template.

Analyse why recycling alone cannot solve the plastic pollution crisis, despite strong public support for recycling programmes.
Show Answer

Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) laws make plastic manufacturers responsible for collecting and recycling their packaging. Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of this approach.
Show Answer

Strengths: Internalises end-of-life costs into production decisions, incentivising producers to design for recyclability; reduces municipal waste management burden; creates manufacturer-funded collection infrastructure. Weaknesses: (1) "Plastic credits" — companies can pay fees instead of physically collecting, with fees potentially too low to change behaviour; (2) Fraud in credit systems has been documented in India and elsewhere; (3) Does not reduce production volumes; (4) Implementation and enforcement capacity varies by country. EPR works best when fees are set high enough to drive behavioural change and when third-party verification prevents credit fraud.

Nanoplastics (<1 micrometre) have been found in human placentas, lungs, and brains. Analyse the scientific uncertainty and the precautionary principle's application here.
Show Answer

This means reducing plastic production and improving waste management now, without waiting for proof of specific health harm — consistent with precautionary regulation of other ubiquitous novel pollutants.

What is the difference between groundwater and surface water? Which is more at risk from climate change?
Show Answer

Groundwater is water stored underground in aquifers (permeable rock, sand, or gravel formations). Surface water is found in rivers, lakes, and reservoirs. Analyse the long-term sustainability of drilling deeper bore wells as a response to groundwater depletion in rural India.
Show Answer

This is a "tragedy of the commons" scenario: as shallow wells dry up, farmers individually drill deeper to reach remaining water. Each deeper well reduces the water table further for all users. Eventually, aquifers are depleted — a process that may be irreversible on human timescales for some geological formations. It is also increasingly expensive (deeper drilling costs more, requires more powerful pumps). Groundwater recharge through rainwater harvesting and reduced extraction is the only sustainable solution, but requires collective governance of a shared resource that individuals have no incentive to conserve individually.

Critically evaluate whether privatisation of water utilities improves service delivery for the poor, drawing on global evidence.
