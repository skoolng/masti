Pure Substances vs Mixtures

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Define a pure substance. Give two examples. Easy
Show Answer

A pure substance contains only one type of particle throughout — either a single element or a single compound. It has a fixed composition and definite melting and boiling points. Examples: pure water (H₂O), pure gold (Au).

Define a mixture. How does it differ from a pure substance? Easy
Show Answer

A mixture contains two or more different substances physically combined (not chemically bonded). It has a variable composition, and its properties depend on the proportions of its components. Unlike pure substances, mixtures do not have sharp, fixed melting/boiling points.

Is air a pure substance or a mixture? Justify your answer. Easy
Show Answer

Air is a mixture. It contains nitrogen (~78%), oxygen (~21%), argon, CO₂, and other gases in variable proportions. Each gas retains its own properties, and air does not have a single fixed boiling point.

Give two examples each of: (a) elements, (b) compounds, (c) mixtures. Easy
Show Answer

(a) Elements: oxygen (O₂), copper (Cu).
(b) Compounds: water (H₂O), salt (NaCl).
(c) Mixtures: seawater, air.

How does the melting point of a pure substance differ from that of a mixture? Easy
Show Answer

A pure substance has a sharp, fixed melting point — it melts completely at one exact temperature. A mixture melts over a range of temperatures (lower than the pure substance's melting point, and not at a single value).

Is salt water a pure substance or a mixture? Give a reason. Easy
Show Answer

Salt water is a mixture. It contains water and dissolved sodium chloride in variable proportions. The ratio of salt to water can vary, unlike a pure compound.

What is the difference between a homogeneous mixture and a heterogeneous mixture? Easy
Show Answer

A homogeneous mixture has uniform composition throughout (e.g., salt water, air — you cannot see separate phases). A heterogeneous mixture has non-uniform composition — different parts look different (e.g., sand and gravel, oil and water).

Can the components of a mixture be separated by physical means? Give an example. Easy
Show Answer

Yes. The components of a mixture can always be separated by physical methods. Examples: filtering sand from water, evaporating salt from salt water, using a magnet to separate iron filings from sand.

SET B Developing (Medium)

Explain why the boiling point of a pure substance is used as a test for purity. Medium
Show Answer

A pure substance boils at one exact, fixed temperature (e.g., pure water at 100°C at 1 atm). If impurities are present, the boiling point is elevated and occurs over a range of temperatures rather than one sharp point. So a sharp, standard boiling point confirms purity.

Compare the properties of water (H₂O) and a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen gases. Why are they so different? Medium
Show Answer

Water (H₂O) is a compound — hydrogen and oxygen are chemically bonded in a fixed 2:1 ratio, forming a liquid with completely different properties (extinguishes fire, is liquid at room temperature). A mixture of H₂ and O₂ gases retains the individual properties of both gases (flammable, supports combustion) and can be in any ratio. The chemical bonding in H₂O creates entirely new properties.

A student claims chocolate is a homogeneous mixture. Is this correct? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

Mostly incorrect. Chocolate contains cocoa solids, cocoa butter, sugar, milk (and more) combined. Under a microscope, different phases can be seen, making it a heterogeneous mixture at the microscopic level, though it appears uniform to the naked eye.

Describe two physical properties you could use to identify if a white powder is pure salt (NaCl) or a mixture of salt and sugar. Medium
Show Answer

1. Melting point: Pure NaCl melts at 801°C; a mixture will begin to melt at a lower temperature and over a range. 2. Taste test (safe lab setting): Pure NaCl tastes purely salty; the mixture would taste both salty and sweet. (Chromatography or solubility tests are also valid.)

Explain why granite is classified as a heterogeneous mixture. Medium
Show Answer

Granite is made up of several minerals — quartz, feldspar, mica — that are visibly distinct (different colours and textures can be seen with the naked eye). The composition is not uniform throughout, and each mineral retains its own properties. This makes it a heterogeneous mixture.

Is 18-carat gold a pure substance or a mixture? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

18-carat gold is a mixture (alloy). It contains 75% gold mixed with other metals like silver or copper. Pure gold is 24 carats. Because the proportions can vary and no chemical bonding occurs, it is a mixture, not a pure compound.

Why does impure ice melt at a temperature below 0°C? Explain using particle theory. Medium
Show Answer

Dissolved impurities (e.g., salt) disrupt the regular ice crystal lattice. The impurity particles position themselves between water molecules, weakening the intermolecular bonds. This means less energy is needed to break the lattice, so melting begins below 0°C. This is called freezing point depression.

Give three differences between a compound and a mixture in tabular form. Medium
Show Answer

Compound: Fixed composition; formed by chemical reaction; components cannot be separated by physical means; has different properties from its constituent elements.
Mixture: Variable composition; formed by physical mixing; components can be separated by physical methods; components retain their individual properties.

SET C Extended (Hard)

A sample of water boils between 99.5°C and 100.8°C. Is this sample pure? Explain and suggest what this tells you about any contaminants. Hard
Show Answer

No, the sample is impure. Pure water boils sharply at 100°C (at 1 atm). Boiling over a range (99.5–100.8°C) indicates the presence of impurities. The elevated maximum of 100.8°C suggests a dissolved substance that causes boiling point elevation (e.g., a small amount of dissolved salt). The spread indicates the concentration changes as the impurity concentrates during boiling.

Explain the difference between elements and compounds as types of pure substances. Why is H₂ a pure substance but H₂O is also a pure substance despite having two elements? Hard
Show Answer

An element contains only one type of atom (e.g., H₂ contains only hydrogen atoms). A compound contains two or more different elements chemically bonded in a fixed ratio (e.g., H₂O has H and O bonded 2:1). Both are pure substances because each has a fixed, definite composition that cannot be changed without a chemical reaction. H₂O is pure because every molecule has exactly the same structure (always 2H:1O) — it is not a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen.

Explain using examples how the same two substances can form both a mixture and a compound, and why their properties differ greatly. Hard
Show Answer

Iron (Fe) and sulfur (S): As a mixture (iron filings + sulfur powder), each retains its properties — iron is magnetic, sulfur is yellow. They can be separated by a magnet. As a compound (iron sulfide, FeS), formed by heating, it has neither the magnetism of iron nor the yellow colour of sulfur — it is a grey solid with entirely different properties that cannot be separated physically.

Design an experiment to determine whether an unknown white crystalline substance is a pure compound or a mixture. Describe three tests you would carry out. Hard
Show Answer

1. Melting point test: Use a melting point apparatus. A pure substance melts sharply at one temperature; a mixture melts over a range at a lower temperature than expected. 2. Thin-layer chromatography: Dissolve a small amount in solvent and run on TLC plate. A pure substance shows a single spot; a mixture shows multiple spots. 3. Boiling point test: Heat a solution with a thermometer. Pure substance boils at one constant temperature; mixture boils over a range.

Why does the freezing point of seawater differ from that of pure water? Use this to explain why roads are salted in winter. Hard
Show Answer

Dissolved salts in seawater disrupt the hydrogen bonding network needed to form ice crystals. More energy must be removed to freeze seawater, so it freezes below 0°C (at about −2°C for typical seawater). On roads: spreading salt lowers the freezing point of water below the ambient temperature, preventing or melting ice formation. This is freezing point depression — a colligative property of mixtures.

Classify each of the following as element, compound, homogeneous mixture, or heterogeneous mixture, and justify: (a) copper wire, (b) vinegar, (c) concrete, (d) carbon dioxide, (e) bronze. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Element — pure copper (Cu). (b) Homogeneous mixture — acetic acid dissolved uniformly in water. (c) Heterogeneous mixture — cement, sand, gravel, water visible as separate phases. (d) Compound — CO₂, fixed ratio of C and O, chemically bonded. (e) Homogeneous mixture (alloy) — copper and tin mixed uniformly; no chemical bonding.

Evaluate the statement: "All compounds are pure, but not all pure substances are compounds." Is this true? Explain fully. Hard
Show Answer

The statement is true. All compounds (e.g., H₂O, NaCl) are pure substances — they have fixed, definite compositions. However, pure substances include both compounds AND elements (e.g., pure gold, pure oxygen). An element like gold (Au) is pure (only gold atoms) but is not a compound because it contains only one type of element. So the set of pure substances includes both elements and compounds.

Explain why alloys (e.g., stainless steel) are useful despite being mixtures rather than pure metals. Discuss at least two properties. Hard
Show Answer

Alloys are mixtures of metals (sometimes with non-metals) designed to have superior properties. 1. Hardness/strength: Pure iron is relatively soft; adding carbon (stainless steel) disrupts the regular crystal lattice, making movement of dislocations harder → stronger. 2. Corrosion resistance: Adding chromium to steel prevents oxidation (rust), extending the material's life. 3. Melting point control: Alloying can lower melting points (solder = Pb/Sn mix) for ease of manufacturing. Mixtures are preferred over pure metals here because the mixture's emergent properties outperform those of the individual components.

Solutions

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Define solute, solvent, and solution. Give one example of each. Easy
Show Answer

Solute: the substance that dissolves (e.g., salt). Solvent: the substance that does the dissolving (e.g., water). Solution: the homogeneous mixture formed (e.g., salt water).

Is a solution a homogeneous or heterogeneous mixture? Explain. Easy
Show Answer

A solution is homogeneous. The solute particles are evenly distributed throughout the solvent at the particle (atomic/molecular) level, so the mixture looks uniform and transparent throughout.

What does it mean when we say a solution is "saturated"? Easy
Show Answer

A saturated solution contains the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. Any additional solute added will remain undissolved.

State the formula for concentration of a solution in g/L. Easy
Show Answer

Concentration (g/L) = Mass of solute (g) ÷ Volume of solution (L).

Calculate the concentration of a solution made by dissolving 20 g of salt in 0.5 L of water. Easy
Show Answer

Concentration = 20 g ÷ 0.5 L = 40 g/L.

How does temperature generally affect the solubility of a solid solute in water? Easy
Show Answer

In general, increasing temperature increases the solubility of solid solutes in water. More solute can dissolve at higher temperatures (e.g., more sugar dissolves in hot tea than cold tea).

Name a common solvent other than water. Easy
Show Answer

Examples: ethanol, acetone, petrol (hexane), methylated spirits. Water is called the universal solvent because it dissolves more substances than any other liquid.

What is the difference between a dilute solution and a concentrated solution? Easy
Show Answer

A dilute solution has a relatively small amount of solute per unit volume. A concentrated solution has a large amount of solute per unit volume. These are comparative, not absolute, terms.

SET B Developing (Medium)

Explain the "like dissolves like" principle with one polar and one non-polar example. Medium
Show Answer

"Like dissolves like" means polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes, and non-polar solvents dissolve non-polar solutes. Polar example: water (polar) dissolves salt (ionic/polar). Non-polar example: petrol (non-polar) dissolves grease (non-polar).

A student dissolves 45 g of sugar in water to make 300 mL of solution. Calculate the concentration in g/L. Medium
Show Answer

300 mL = 0.3 L. Concentration = 45 / 0.3 = 150 g/L.

How does increasing temperature affect the solubility of gases in water? Give a real-life example of this effect. Medium
Show Answer

Increasing temperature decreases the solubility of gases in water. As water warms, dissolved gas molecules gain energy and escape. Real-life example: warm rivers contain less dissolved oxygen, threatening aquatic life (thermal pollution). Also: warm fizzy drinks go flat faster than cold ones (CO₂ less soluble).

Explain what happens at the particle level when salt (NaCl) dissolves in water. Medium
Show Answer

Water molecules are polar — the slightly negative oxygen end is attracted to positive Na⁺ ions, and the slightly positive hydrogen ends attract negative Cl⁻ ions. Water molecules surround (solvate/hydrate) the Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions, pulling them away from the crystal lattice one by one. The ions then disperse uniformly throughout the solution.

What is a supersaturated solution? How is it different from a saturated solution? Medium
Show Answer

A supersaturated solution contains more solute than a saturated solution at the same temperature — it has dissolved more than the normal maximum. It is made by heating a saturated solution (dissolves more) then carefully cooling it. It is unstable: any disturbance (e.g., scratching the container) causes sudden crystallisation of the excess solute.

How does pressure affect the solubility of gases? Name a practical application. Medium
Show Answer

Higher pressure increases the solubility of gases (Henry's Law). Application: Carbonated drinks (soda) are bottled under high CO₂ pressure so more gas dissolves. When the bottle is opened, pressure drops and CO₂ escapes as bubbles (fizz).

How many grams of solute are in 250 mL of a 60 g/L solution? Medium
Show Answer

Mass = concentration × volume = 60 g/L × 0.25 L = 15 g.

Explain why oil and water do not form a solution. What type of mixture do they form instead? Medium
Show Answer

Oil is non-polar; water is polar. "Like dissolves like" — polar and non-polar substances do not mix at the molecular level because the intermolecular attractions between water molecules are stronger than between water and oil. They form an emulsion (a suspension or colloid) — a heterogeneous mixture where oil droplets are temporarily dispersed in water but separate on standing.

SET C Extended (Hard)

A 500 mL solution contains 35 g of NaCl and 15 g of sugar. Calculate (a) the total solute concentration, (b) the individual concentrations of NaCl and sugar. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Total mass = 50 g; concentration = 50/0.5 = 100 g/L.
(b) NaCl: 35/0.5 = 70 g/L; Sugar: 15/0.5 = 30 g/L.

Explain the difference between miscible and immiscible liquids. Give one example of each, and describe the mixture type formed by immiscible liquids. Hard
Show Answer

Miscible liquids dissolve in each other in all proportions to form a homogeneous solution (e.g., ethanol and water). Immiscible liquids do not mix and form separate layers (e.g., oil and water). When shaken, immiscible liquids form a heterogeneous mixture (emulsion/suspension) that separates on standing into distinct layers.

A student heats a saturated copper sulfate solution and adds more copper sulfate until it dissolves. On slow cooling, large blue crystals form. Explain this process step by step in terms of solubility. Hard
Show Answer

1. Heating increases the kinetic energy of water molecules, allowing more Cu²⁺ and SO₄²⁻ ions to be hydrated → solubility increases. 2. Adding more CuSO₄ at high temperature allows more to dissolve (not saturated at this temperature). 3. On slow cooling, solubility decreases. 4. The solution becomes supersaturated. 5. Excess solute crystallises out slowly, forming large, well-structured crystals (rapid cooling gives small crystals).

Evaluate the environmental impact of thermal pollution (warm water discharged by power stations into rivers) on aquatic organisms, using your knowledge of gas solubility. Hard
Show Answer

Warm water has lower dissolved oxygen (O₂) solubility. Aquatic organisms (fish, invertebrates) need dissolved O₂ to respire. In warmer water, O₂ levels drop below survival thresholds, causing fish kills and disrupting ecosystems. Decomposers increase metabolic rate → consume more O₂ → further reduces dissolved O₂ (eutrophication risk). Long-term: biodiversity loss, death of sensitive species, algal blooms in nutrient-rich warm water.

Explain why adding sugar to water raises its boiling point above 100°C. What is this phenomenon called? Hard
Show Answer

This is called boiling point elevation (a colligative property). Sugar molecules in solution reduce the number of water molecules at the surface that can escape into the vapour phase. To reach the vapour pressure equal to atmospheric pressure (required for boiling), the solution must be heated to a higher temperature. The more solute dissolved, the higher the boiling point elevation.

A solution needs to be made with a concentration of 25 g/L. How many grams of solute are needed to make 750 mL? Show your working. Hard
Show Answer

Volume = 750 mL = 0.75 L. Mass = concentration × volume = 25 × 0.75 = 18.75 g.

Distinguish between solubility and rate of dissolving. Describe three factors that affect each. Hard
Show Answer

Solubility: the maximum amount of solute that dissolves at a given temperature/pressure — affected by (1) temperature, (2) pressure (for gases), (3) nature of solute and solvent.
Rate of dissolving: how quickly the solute dissolves — affected by (1) stirring/agitation, (2) particle size (smaller particles dissolve faster — more surface area), (3) temperature (higher → faster dissolving). Note: stirring does not change solubility — just the speed of reaching equilibrium.

Explain why deep-sea divers can suffer from "the bends" when surfacing too quickly. Link this to gas solubility principles. Hard
Show Answer

At depth, divers breathe compressed air under high pressure. Henry's Law: increased pressure → increased solubility of N₂ in blood and tissues. If they surface too quickly, pressure drops suddenly. N₂ comes out of solution rapidly, forming bubbles in the bloodstream and joints — causing "the bends" (decompression sickness), which is extremely painful and potentially fatal. Ascending slowly allows N₂ to diffuse out gradually through the lungs without bubble formation.

Colloids and Suspensions

SET A Foundational (Easy)

What is a colloid? State the typical particle size range. Easy
Show Answer

A colloid is a mixture in which particles of one substance are dispersed (suspended) throughout another, but the particles are too small to settle out quickly. Particle size: 1–100 nm (larger than true solutions, smaller than suspensions).

What is a suspension? How does it differ from a colloid? Easy
Show Answer

A suspension has particles larger than 100 nm that are visible and eventually settle due to gravity (e.g., muddy water). A colloid has smaller particles (1–100 nm) that do not settle easily and remain dispersed. Suspensions are cloudy/opaque; colloids may appear slightly cloudy or opalescent.

What is the Tyndall effect? In what type of mixture is it observed? Easy
Show Answer

The Tyndall effect is the scattering of a beam of light by colloidal particles, making the light path visible. It is observed in colloids (e.g., a beam of light through milk is visible). True solutions do not show the Tyndall effect because solute particles are too small to scatter light.

Give two examples of colloids found in everyday life. Easy
Show Answer

Examples: milk (fat droplets in water), fog/mist (water droplets in air), mayonnaise, foam, aerosol sprays, blood, smoke.

Will a suspension settle if left undisturbed? Will a colloid settle? Easy
Show Answer

A suspension will settle (particles are large enough for gravity to pull them down). A colloid does not settle easily — particles are small enough to remain dispersed due to Brownian motion (random collisions with solvent molecules).

Can colloids be separated by ordinary filtration? Why? Easy
Show Answer

Colloid particles cannot be separated by ordinary filter paper — they are too small to be caught by filter paper pores. Special techniques like centrifugation or semi-permeable membranes (dialysis) are needed.

Give one example of a solid-in-liquid colloid and a gas-in-liquid colloid. Easy
Show Answer

Solid-in-liquid colloid (sol): paint (pigment particles in liquid). Gas-in-liquid colloid (foam): whipped cream (air bubbles in cream).

Why does muddy river water appear cloudy? Easy
Show Answer

Muddy water is a suspension — fine clay and soil particles are dispersed in water. These particles scatter and absorb light, making the water appear cloudy (turbid). Given time, the particles settle to the bottom.

SET B Developing (Medium)

Compare solutions, colloids, and suspensions in terms of particle size, stability, and filtration. Medium
Show Answer

Solution: particles <1 nm; stable (do not settle); pass through filter paper AND semi-permeable membranes.
Colloid: 1–100 nm; relatively stable (Brownian motion); pass through filter paper but not semi-permeable membrane.
Suspension: >100 nm; unstable (settle); cannot pass through filter paper.

Explain why fog reduces visibility. Use knowledge of the Tyndall effect. Medium
Show Answer

Fog is a colloid (water droplets in air, ~1–100 nm). When light from headlights or sunlight passes through fog, the water droplets scatter light in all directions (Tyndall effect). This scattering diffuses the light beam, reducing contrast and making distant objects difficult to see. The denser the fog (more particles), the greater the scattering and visibility reduction.

Explain Brownian motion and its role in keeping colloid particles suspended. Medium
Show Answer

Brownian motion is the random, erratic movement of colloidal particles caused by unequal collisions from surrounding solvent molecules (which are in constant motion). This random jostling prevents the colloidal particles from staying in one place and settling due to gravity, keeping them continuously dispersed throughout the medium.

Classify each as solution, colloid, or suspension: (a) orange juice with pulp, (b) clear apple juice, (c) blood, (d) cement in water. Medium
Show Answer

(a) Orange juice with pulp: suspension (visible pulp particles settle). (b) Clear apple juice: solution (no visible particles, transparent). (c) Blood: colloid (cells and proteins dispersed in plasma). (d) Cement in water: suspension (cement particles settle).

How could you use a laser pointer to distinguish between a true solution and a colloid? Describe the expected result for each. Medium
Show Answer

Shine the laser through each sample in a dark room. True solution: the beam passes through invisibly — no light scattering (particles too small). Colloid: the beam path is clearly visible as a glowing cone or line (Tyndall effect — colloidal particles scatter light sideways). This is a simple and definitive test.

What is an emulsion? Give two examples and state how they are stabilised. Medium
Show Answer

An emulsion is a colloid of two immiscible liquids — one liquid dispersed as droplets in another (liquid-in-liquid colloid). Examples: milk (fat in water) and mayonnaise (oil in water). They are stabilised by emulsifying agents (emulsifiers) such as lecithin in milk or egg yolk in mayonnaise, which have both hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts, preventing the droplets from coalescing.

Explain why you cannot see individual particles in a colloid with the naked eye, but you can in a suspension. Medium
Show Answer

The human eye can resolve objects down to about 0.1 mm (100,000 nm). Colloid particles are 1–100 nm — far below the resolution limit of the naked eye, so they are invisible. Suspension particles are typically above 1000 nm and can often be seen as cloudiness or specks, and they settle forming visible sediment.

Describe the process and principle of centrifugation. In what situation would you use it instead of filtration to separate a mixture? Medium
Show Answer

Centrifugation spins samples at high speed, creating a centrifugal force many times greater than gravity. Denser particles move outward and settle to the bottom (pellet), while less dense liquid stays on top (supernatant). Use it instead of filtration for: colloids (particles too small for filter paper), blood cell separation, cream from milk — anywhere particles are too fine for physical filtration.

SET C Extended (Hard)

A student has three beakers labelled A (salt water), B (milk), C (muddy water). Design three experiments to identify which is which without tasting. Hard
Show Answer

1. Tyndall test: Shine a laser. A shows no scattering (solution); B shows a clear beam path (colloid); C shows scattering (suspension).
2. Standing test: Leave undisturbed for 30 min. C forms sediment (suspension); A and B remain unchanged.
3. Filtration test: Filter through paper. C — sediment on filter (suspension); A and B pass through (solution and colloid). Then evaporate filtrates: A leaves white crystals (NaCl); B leaves a fat/protein residue.

Explain why adding an electrolyte (like salt) to a colloid causes it to coagulate (clump and settle). What is this process called? Hard
Show Answer

This is called coagulation (or flocculation). Colloidal particles are stabilised by electrical charges on their surfaces — like charges repel, keeping particles apart. Adding an electrolyte introduces oppositely charged ions that neutralise these surface charges. Once neutralised, particles can approach each other, van der Waals attractions cause them to clump (coagulate), and they become large enough to settle by gravity. Used in water treatment to remove colloidal impurities.

Compare the industrial use of electrostatic precipitators and centrifugation in removing colloids, stating which type of colloid each is best suited for. Hard
Show Answer

Electrostatic precipitators: used for aerosols (solid or liquid particles in gas, e.g., smoke). Charged particles are attracted to oppositely charged plates and stick — then fall off as solid residue. Best for air/gas-phase colloids. Centrifugation: best for liquid colloids (sols, emulsions) where particles need to be physically separated from a liquid medium by enhanced sedimentation. Used in dairy (cream separation), blood labs, industrial chemical separation.

Explain the significance of colloids in the human body, giving two specific biological examples. Hard
Show Answer

1. Blood: a colloidal suspension of red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, and proteins (albumin) in plasma. The colloid nature keeps cells suspended and flowing through capillaries. 2. Cytoplasm: a colloid (proteins and organelles dispersed in water) — its gel-like colloid properties allow it to change viscosity for cellular processes like cytokinesis. Colloid properties (Brownian motion, surface charge) are essential for these biological systems to function.

Why does the sky appear blue? Link this explanation to the Tyndall effect. Hard
Show Answer

Earth's atmosphere contains colloidal-like scattering particles (gas molecules, dust, aerosols). Sunlight contains all visible wavelengths. Shorter wavelength blue light (~450 nm) is scattered much more strongly than longer wavelength red light by atmospheric particles (Rayleigh scattering — related to the Tyndall effect). The scattered blue light reaches our eyes from all directions across the sky. At sunset, light travels a longer atmospheric path, scattering out most blue light, leaving red/orange hues.

Describe how water treatment plants remove colloidal clay particles from river water. Include the relevant separation principle. Hard
Show Answer

Clay particles carry negative surface charges, keeping them dispersed as a colloid. Water treatment adds a coagulant such as alum (aluminium sulfate), which releases Al³⁺ ions. These neutralise the surface charges on clay particles, causing coagulation — particles clump together (flocculation). The larger flocs sink (sedimentation). The clarified water is then filtered through sand and gravel to remove remaining particles, and finally disinfected with chlorine.

Design an investigation to show that milk is a colloid and not a true solution, using two different pieces of experimental evidence. Hard
Show Answer

Evidence 1 – Tyndall Effect: Shine a laser/torch through milk in a dark room. The beam is clearly visible (scattering) — confirms colloidal particles. Same test on salt water shows no visible beam. Evidence 2 – Dialysis/Filtration: Place milk in a dialysis bag (semi-permeable membrane) in water. Small molecules pass through; large colloidal fat/protein particles do not. After time, test the surrounding water — no fat present. This shows particles are larger than molecular size (not a true solution) but too small to be filtered by ordinary paper (not a suspension).

Explain the difference between lyophilic and lyophobic colloids. Which is more stable and why? Hard
Show Answer

Lyophilic colloids ("solvent-loving"): dispersed particles have a strong affinity for the dispersion medium (solvent). They are easily formed and very stable because the particles are solvated (surrounded by solvent molecules). Examples: starch, gelatin, protein solutions. Lyophobic colloids ("solvent-fearing"): particles have little affinity for the medium. They are less stable and coagulate more easily — stabilised only by electrical charge. Examples: gold sol, silver sol, ferric hydroxide. Lyophilic colloids are more stable because solvation provides a physical barrier preventing coagulation, whereas lyophobic colloids rely only on charge repulsion.

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Name the separation technique used to separate sand from water. Describe how it works. Easy
Show Answer

Filtration. The sand-water mixture is poured through a filter funnel lined with filter paper. Sand particles are too large to pass through the tiny pores in the filter paper (they form the residue). Water passes through (filtrate).

How would you separate salt from salt water? Name the technique. Easy
Show Answer

Evaporation. Heat the salt water; the water evaporates as vapour, leaving solid salt crystals behind in the evaporating dish.

What physical property does magnetic separation exploit? Give one example of its use. Easy
Show Answer

Magnetic separation exploits magnetic properties. It separates magnetic materials from non-magnetic ones. Example: separating iron filings from sand by passing a magnet over the mixture.

What is sieving? Give two examples of where it is used. Easy
Show Answer

Sieving separates particles of different sizes using a mesh or grid with holes of a specific size. Examples: (1) separating coarse sand from fine sand in construction; (2) separating flour from bran in a kitchen sieve.

What is the difference between filtration and evaporation as separation techniques? Easy
Show Answer

Filtration separates an insoluble solid from a liquid using a physical barrier (filter paper). Evaporation separates a dissolved solid (solute) from its liquid solvent by heating the solution until the solvent vaporises, leaving the solute behind.

Name the separation technique based on the different boiling points of liquids. Easy
Show Answer

Distillation (or fractional distillation for mixtures with similar boiling points). The liquid with the lower boiling point vaporises first, is condensed, and collected separately.

What apparatus is used to collect a purified liquid in distillation? Easy
Show Answer

A condenser (Liebig condenser) — cold water flows around the outer jacket, cooling the vapour inside the inner tube back into liquid (the distillate), which is collected in a receiving flask.

Why can you not use filtration to separate a dissolved salt from water? Easy
Show Answer

Salt dissolves in water — its ions (Na⁺ and Cl⁻) are at the particle level and are far too small to be caught by filter paper pores. The salt passes straight through with the water. Filtration only separates insoluble solid particles from liquids.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A mixture contains sand, iron filings, and salt dissolved in water. Describe a step-by-step procedure to separate all three components. Medium
Show Answer

Step 1: Magnetic separation — pass a magnet over the mixture to remove iron filings. Step 2: Filtration — filter the remaining salt water + sand; sand stays as residue; salt water is the filtrate. Step 3: Evaporation — heat the filtrate to evaporate water, leaving salt crystals.

Explain the difference between simple distillation and fractional distillation. When is each used? Medium
Show Answer

Simple distillation: separates a liquid from a dissolved solid or two liquids with a large difference (>25°C) in boiling points. Fractional distillation: separates two or more miscible liquids with close boiling points (e.g., ethanol/water) using a fractionating column that allows repeated condensation and re-evaporation, gradually enriching the vapour in the more volatile component.

How does a separating funnel work? What type of mixture is it used for? Medium
Show Answer

A separating funnel separates two immiscible liquids (e.g., oil and water). The mixture is added to the funnel and allowed to settle into two separate layers (denser liquid at the bottom). The tap is opened to drain off the denser layer first, then closed, and the upper layer is poured from the top. Separation is based on density differences.

What property does crystallisation rely on for separation? Describe the steps involved. Medium
Show Answer

Crystallisation relies on differences in solubility at different temperatures. Steps: (1) Dissolve the solid in hot solvent (make a saturated solution). (2) Filter to remove insoluble impurities. (3) Slowly cool the filtrate — the main solute crystallises out (lower solubility at lower temperature) while impurities (present in smaller amounts) remain in solution. (4) Filter to collect pure crystals; wash and dry.

In what order would the following property-based separation techniques be applied to a mixture of chalk powder, iron filings, sand, and copper sulfate solution? Medium
Show Answer

1. Magnetic separation: remove iron filings. 2. Filtration: remove chalk and sand (residue) from copper sulfate solution (filtrate). 3. Sieving: if needed, separate chalk from sand by particle size. 4. Evaporation/crystallisation: obtain copper sulfate crystals from solution.

Why must a condenser have cold water running through it (not hot water) during distillation? Medium
Show Answer

The vapour from the heated flask must be cooled below its boiling point to condense back into liquid. Cold water flowing through the outer jacket of the condenser absorbs heat from the vapour in the inner tube, causing condensation. If hot water were used, the vapour would not cool enough to condense and would escape as gas.

Explain what sublimation is and give an example of a substance that undergoes sublimation. Medium
Show Answer

Sublimation is the direct conversion of a solid to a gas without passing through the liquid phase. It can be used to separate a sublimable substance from a non-sublimable mixture. Examples: iodine (turns directly to purple vapour when heated), dry ice (CO₂), camphor, ammonium chloride.

Match each separation technique with its key physical property: (1) filtration, (2) magnetic separation, (3) distillation, (4) chromatography, (5) centrifugation. Medium
Show Answer

(1) Filtration → particle size. (2) Magnetic separation → magnetic properties. (3) Distillation → boiling point. (4) Chromatography → affinity for stationary/mobile phase (solubility + adsorption). (5) Centrifugation → density.

SET C Extended (Hard)

Design a separation procedure to obtain pure water from seawater. Explain why each step is necessary. Hard
Show Answer

1. Filtration: remove large suspended particles (seaweed, sand). 2. Simple distillation: boil the filtered seawater; water evaporates, leaving dissolved salts and minerals behind; vapour is condensed in a Liebig condenser to produce distilled water. Why each step: filtration removes undissolved matter; distillation separates the water from all dissolved solutes — other methods (evaporation, reverse osmosis) may also be used but distillation gives highest purity.

Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of evaporation versus crystallisation as methods for obtaining a solid solute from solution. Hard
Show Answer

Evaporation: Simple and fast; no special equipment needed. Disadvantage: if heated too strongly, the solid may decompose or other impurities also concentrate and crystallise; gives a product mixed with impurities if not careful; no selectivity. Crystallisation: More selective — pure crystals form while impurities remain in the mother liquor; gives a purer product; can use differences in solubility. Disadvantage: slower process; requires careful temperature control; some product remains in solution (yield loss).

A mixture contains blue ink (water-soluble dye), sand (insoluble), and alcohol. Describe a complete separation procedure to isolate all three. Hard
Show Answer

1. Simple distillation: distil the liquid mixture — alcohol (lower boiling point) distils over first and is collected; the aqueous ink solution and sand remain. 2. Filtration: filter the remaining mixture — sand stays as residue; blue ink solution is the filtrate. 3. Evaporation: gently evaporate the filtrate to concentrate/obtain the dye solid. (Or paper chromatography to identify the dye components.)

Why is fractional distillation preferred over simple distillation for separating ethanol from water? Explain using boiling point concepts. Hard
Show Answer

Ethanol bp = 78°C; water bp = 100°C. The boiling points are only 22°C apart — both evaporate to some extent at temperatures between 78–100°C. Simple distillation would give a mixture of ethanol and water vapour. The fractionating column (packed with glass beads or plates) acts as a series of mini-condensation and re-evaporation steps (theoretical plates). The more volatile ethanol enriches upward; water condenses back down. By the time vapour reaches the top, it is nearly pure ethanol. Multiple simple distillations would be needed otherwise — fractional distillation achieves this in one continuous operation.

Explain the principle of reverse osmosis used in modern water desalination. How does it differ from distillation as a separation method? Hard
Show Answer

In osmosis, water moves from low to high solute concentration through a semi-permeable membrane. Reverse osmosis applies pressure greater than the osmotic pressure to the saltwater side, forcing water molecules (but not dissolved ions) through the membrane from high to low concentration — the reverse of normal osmosis. This produces pure water without heating. Compared to distillation: reverse osmosis uses less energy (no phase change), can be continuous, and is more scalable; distillation uses heat to phase-change water, which is energy-intensive but produces very pure water and can handle a wider range of contaminants.

Explain how crude oil refining relies on fractional distillation. Name four fractions obtained and their approximate boiling point ranges. Hard
Show Answer

Crude oil (a complex mixture of hydrocarbons) is heated and vapour enters a fractionating column that is hot at the bottom and cool at the top. Shorter-chain hydrocarbons (lower bp) rise higher before condensing; longer-chain (higher bp) condense lower. Fractions: (1) Petroleum gas (<25°C); (2) Petrol/gasoline (25–75°C); (3) Kerosene/jet fuel (150–250°C); (4) Diesel (250–350°C); also fuel oil, lubricating oil, bitumen at higher ranges.

Compare filtration, centrifugation, and dialysis as techniques for separating colloids, stating which is most effective and why. Hard
Show Answer

Filtration: ineffective for colloids — colloidal particles (1–100 nm) pass through ordinary filter paper pores (~1–10 μm). Centrifugation: effective — high-speed spinning creates forces that can sediment colloidal particles that gravity alone cannot settle; good for laboratory-scale separation of suspensions and some colloids. Dialysis: uses semi-permeable membranes — small solute molecules pass through while large colloidal particles are retained; best for purifying colloids (removing small molecule impurities from colloidal protein solutions, e.g., in blood dialysis). Most effective overall: dialysis for purification of colloids from small solutes; centrifugation for separating colloidal particles themselves from the medium.

Design an experiment to separate a mixture of three dyes using paper chromatography. Describe how you would calculate Rf values and what they tell you about purity. Hard
Show Answer

Method: Draw pencil baseline on chromatography paper; spot the mixed dye at the centre. Use capillary tube; allow spot to dry. Place in a beaker with solvent (below the baseline); cover and allow to run until near the top. Mark solvent front. Measure: (a) distance from baseline to solvent front (D_solvent), (b) distance from baseline to centre of each dye spot (D_substance). Rf = D_substance / D_solvent. Rf values are constant for each pure substance in a given solvent. If a spot has a unique Rf (not matching any known), it may be a new/unknown dye. A single spot in all solvent systems = likely pure; multiple spots = mixture of dyes. Rf range 0–1 (dimensionless).

SET A Foundational (Easy)

What is fractional distillation? How is it different from simple distillation? Easy
Show Answer

Fractional distillation separates a mixture of liquids with similar boiling points using a fractionating column. Unlike simple distillation (which separates mixtures with very different boiling points), fractional distillation allows multiple condensation–evaporation cycles in the column to gradually separate components.

What property of liquids does fractional distillation rely on? Easy
Show Answer

Fractional distillation relies on differences in boiling points of the component liquids.

What is the purpose of the fractionating column in fractional distillation? Easy
Show Answer

The fractionating column (usually packed with glass beads or has bubble-cap trays) provides a temperature gradient (hotter at bottom, cooler at top) and a surface for repeated partial condensation and re-evaporation. This gradually enriches the rising vapour in the more volatile (lower boiling point) component.

Name two industrial processes that use fractional distillation. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Petroleum refining — separating crude oil into petrol, kerosene, diesel, etc. (2) Liquefied air separation — separating liquid air into nitrogen, oxygen, and argon.

In the fractional distillation of liquid air, which gas is collected first at the top: nitrogen or oxygen? Why? Easy
Show Answer

Nitrogen is collected first (at the top). Nitrogen bp = −196°C; Oxygen bp = −183°C. Nitrogen has the lower boiling point and is more volatile, so it rises to the cooler top of the column and distils over first.

What are fractions in the context of petroleum refining? Easy
Show Answer

Fractions are groups of hydrocarbon molecules with similar boiling point ranges that are collected at different levels of the fractionating column during petroleum distillation. Each fraction has a different range of carbon chain lengths and boiling points.

Which fraction from crude oil is used as jet fuel (aviation fuel)? Easy
Show Answer

Kerosene (paraffin), with a boiling point range of approximately 150–250°C, is used as aviation fuel (jet fuel, Jet A/Jet A-1).

What fraction from petroleum has the lowest boiling point and what is it used for? Easy
Show Answer

Petroleum gas (LPG) — the lightest fraction with the lowest boiling point (<25°C). Used as fuel in gas stoves, heating, and as a feedstock for making plastics (ethene, propene).

SET B Developing (Medium)

Describe, in order, the major fractions obtained from crude oil distillation, from lowest to highest boiling point. Medium
Show Answer

From lowest to highest boiling point: (1) Petroleum/refinery gas (<25°C). (2) Petrol/gasoline (25–75°C). (3) Naphtha (75–150°C). (4) Kerosene/jet fuel (150–250°C). (5) Diesel/gas oil (250–350°C). (6) Fuel oil/heavy oil (350–400°C). (7) Lubricating oil (400°C+). (8) Wax and bitumen (residue).

Explain why higher fractions of petroleum (longer carbon chains) have higher boiling points. Medium
Show Answer

Longer carbon chains have greater molecular mass and more electrons, meaning stronger van der Waals (London dispersion) intermolecular forces. More energy (higher temperature) is required to overcome these forces and vaporise the molecules, hence higher boiling points.

Why is liquid air first cooled before fractional distillation? What temperature must be reached? Medium
Show Answer

Air must be cooled below the boiling point of oxygen (−183°C) to liquefy its components. This is done by compression and cooling cycles (Linde process). Only once liquefied can the mixture be fractionally distilled, since distillation operates on liquids — you cannot fractionally distil a gas mixture directly without liquefying it.

How is the fractional distillation column in petroleum refining designed to maintain a temperature gradient? Medium
Show Answer

The crude oil is heated to ~350°C and enters at the bottom. Heat rises. The column has bubble-cap trays (plates) at different heights. The bottom is hottest (heavy fractions condense), and it gets progressively cooler toward the top (light fractions condense higher up). Vapour passes upward through holes in each tray; liquids can flow downward through overflow pipes — maintaining a continuous temperature gradient.

Why is bitumen the residue left at the bottom of the petroleum fractionating column? Medium
Show Answer

Bitumen consists of very long-chain hydrocarbons (C40+) with extremely high boiling points (above 500°C). At the operating temperatures of the column, bitumen does not vaporise — it remains as a thick, viscous liquid at the bottom. It is not a vapour fraction but a non-volatile residue.

What is cracking in petroleum refining? Why is it industrially important? Medium
Show Answer

Cracking is a chemical process that breaks long-chain hydrocarbon molecules (heavy, less useful fractions like fuel oil) into shorter, more useful molecules (petrol, ethene for plastics). It is industrially vital because demand for petrol and short-chain hydrocarbons greatly exceeds their natural abundance in crude oil, while heavy fractions are in excess. Cracking allows refiners to convert low-demand fractions into high-demand products.

Compare the uses and properties of petrol and diesel as petroleum fractions. Medium
Show Answer

Petrol (gasoline): C5–C10 hydrocarbons, bp 25–75°C, more volatile, used in petrol engines (spark ignition), less viscous. Diesel: C14–C20 hydrocarbons, bp 250–350°C, less volatile, used in diesel engines (compression ignition), more viscous, higher energy density per litre.

Explain how the fractional distillation of liquid air produces industrial quantities of oxygen for hospitals. Medium
Show Answer

Air is compressed and cooled until it liquefies (–196°C). The liquid air is then fractionally distilled. Nitrogen (bp –196°C) evaporates first and exits at the top. Oxygen (bp –183°C) remains longer, concentrating in the column; it is collected as a liquid fraction at a higher level than heavier argon. Liquid oxygen is stored in insulated cryogenic containers and transported to hospitals for medical use.

SET C Extended (Hard)

Compare and contrast the fractional distillation of petroleum and liquid air in terms of (a) feed material, (b) temperature range, (c) products, and (d) industrial importance. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Petroleum: crude oil (fossil fuel mixture); Liquid air: compressed and cooled atmospheric air.
(b) Petroleum: ~350°C feed, column descends to ambient; Liquid air: cryogenic, ~−196°C to −183°C range.
(c) Petroleum: petrol, kerosene, diesel, bitumen etc.; Liquid air: nitrogen, oxygen, argon, noble gases.
(d) Petroleum: fuels for transport, plastics feedstock; Liquid air: oxygen for medical/steel-making, nitrogen for fertilisers, argon for welding — both are economically critical industrial processes.

Explain why the process of fractional distillation is considered a physical rather than a chemical change. How could you confirm this? Hard
Show Answer

Fractional distillation involves only changes of state (vaporisation and condensation) — no new chemical bonds are formed or broken. The molecules in each fraction are identical to those in the original mixture. Confirmation: (1) The individual fractions have the same chemical composition as when they were part of the original mixture. (2) Re-mixing the fractions would reconstitute the original mixture (theoretically). (3) No new chemical compounds are detected; only separation into existing components occurs.

Explain the environmental and economic arguments for and against continued expansion of petroleum refining capacity globally. Hard
Show Answer

For: Fuels billions of vehicles and industries; relatively energy-dense fuels; existing infrastructure; provides plastics/chemicals essential to modern life; economic driver (jobs, national income for oil-rich countries). Against: Fossil fuel combustion releases CO₂ → climate change; oil spills cause environmental damage; finite resource (peak oil concerns); pollution from refineries (SO₂, NOx, VOCs); drives geopolitical conflicts; renewable energy alternatives are available. Conclusion: A transition away from petroleum is environmentally necessary but economically complex, requiring investment in alternatives and significant socioeconomic planning.

What is vacuum distillation? When is it used in petroleum refining and why? Hard
Show Answer

Vacuum distillation reduces the pressure below atmospheric, which lowers the boiling points of all components. It is used for heavy petroleum fractions (lubricating oils, vacuum gas oil, asphalt) that would need temperatures above ~400°C at atmospheric pressure. Such high temperatures would cause thermal decomposition (cracking) of the hydrocarbons before they could be distilled. By reducing pressure, these heavy fractions can be distilled at lower temperatures (~300°C) without decomposition, preserving the molecular structure of valuable products.

A mixture of liquids A (bp 65°C) and B (bp 98°C) is to be separated. Critically compare simple distillation and fractional distillation for this separation, suggesting which to use. Hard
Show Answer

The boiling point difference is 33°C. Simple distillation is effective only for large differences (>25°C ideally). At 33°C separation, simple distillation would give a largely enriched product but not pure separation — both A and B would co-distil to some degree. Fractional distillation is strongly preferred: the fractionating column with multiple theoretical plates would achieve near-pure separation of A (collected first at ~65°C) and B (at ~98°C). Multiple simple distillations could also work but would be more time-consuming and waste product.

Explain the role of argon in liquid air fractional distillation. What is its boiling point, and why is it challenging to separate from oxygen? Hard
Show Answer

Argon bp = −185.9°C; Oxygen bp = −183°C. Their boiling points differ by only ~2°C, making separation extremely difficult. A very efficient fractionating column with many theoretical plates is needed. Crude argon first separated from oxygen-rich stream then further purified in a secondary column. Pure argon is commercially valuable for inert atmosphere welding and semiconductor manufacturing. The challenge highlights the limits of fractional distillation — extremely close boiling points require either very tall columns or alternative separation methods.

Explain why the development of zeolite catalysts has changed how refineries crack petroleum. What advantage do catalytic crackers have over thermal crackers? Hard
Show Answer

Thermal cracking: uses very high temperatures (500–900°C) and pressures; mainly produces alkenes and smaller alkanes. Catalytic cracking: uses zeolite catalysts at lower temperatures (~450–550°C) and lower pressures. Advantages: lower energy cost (lower temperature); greater selectivity (more useful short-chain petrol-range products); less undesired residue; produces more branched hydrocarbons (higher octane petrol — better engine performance); easier to control product distribution by adjusting catalyst and conditions. Zeolite catalysts have specific pore sizes that shape-selectively break certain C-C bonds, giving much better product control than random thermal cracking.

Explain how a student could model fractional distillation in a school laboratory using a mixture of ethanol (bp 78°C) and water (bp 100°C). Describe the expected results and how to confirm purity of the collected fractions. Hard
Show Answer

Setup: Round-bottom flask with ethanol-water mixture; fractionating column (spiral/Vigreux) connected to a Liebig condenser; thermometer at column head; receiving flask. Procedure: heat slowly; first fraction collected when thermometer reads ~78°C — this is enriched ethanol; as temperature rises toward 100°C, water fraction comes over. To confirm purity: (1) measure boiling point — pure ethanol: 78°C sharp; pure water: 100°C sharp. (2) Density measurement (ethanol: 0.79 g/mL; water: 1.00 g/mL). (3) Flame test — ethanol burns with blue flame; water does not burn. Expected result: first fraction (78–80°C) is ~95% ethanol (absolute without azeotrope break); transition fraction; final fraction is nearly pure water.

SET A Foundational (Easy)

What is chromatography used for? Give two applications. Easy
Show Answer

Chromatography separates and identifies components in a mixture based on differences in their affinity for the stationary phase and mobile phase. Applications: (1) Food dye analysis (e.g., checking food colourings), (2) Drug testing in sports/medicine, (3) Ink analysis in forensic science.

In paper chromatography, what are the (a) stationary phase and (b) mobile phase? Easy
Show Answer

(a) Stationary phase: paper (cellulose fibres and bound water). (b) Mobile phase: the solvent that moves up the paper carrying the mixture components with it.

Write the formula for Rf value and explain what each letter represents. Easy
Show Answer

Rf = distance moved by substance / distance moved by solvent front. "Rf" stands for retardation factor. It is always between 0 and 1.

A substance moves 4.5 cm on a chromatogram where the solvent front moved 9.0 cm. Calculate the Rf value. Easy
Show Answer

Rf = 4.5 / 9.0 = 0.5.

Why must the pencil baseline be below the solvent level in paper chromatography? Easy
Show Answer

Actually, the baseline must be above the solvent level, not submerged in it. If the spot is placed below the solvent surface, the dyes will dissolve directly into the solvent rather than being carried upward by the rising solvent, ruining the separation.

What does a single spot on a chromatogram suggest about the substance tested? Easy
Show Answer

A single spot suggests the substance may be a single pure compound (only one component with one Rf value). However, it is not definitive — two different compounds with the same Rf in that solvent would also give one spot.

What does multiple spots on a chromatogram indicate? Easy
Show Answer

Multiple spots indicate the sample is a mixture — it contains more than one component with different affinities for the stationary phase and mobile phase, causing them to travel different distances.

Why is pencil used to draw the baseline in chromatography, not pen? Easy
Show Answer

Pencil marks are made of graphite (insoluble, inert) and will not dissolve in the solvent. Pen ink would dissolve in the solvent and travel up the chromatogram, creating interfering spots and making results unreliable.

SET B Developing (Medium)

Explain why different compounds have different Rf values in the same solvent. Medium
Show Answer

Different compounds have different molecular structures and polarities, giving them different levels of attraction to the stationary phase (paper) vs the mobile phase (solvent). Compounds with stronger affinity for the stationary phase move more slowly (lower Rf); compounds more soluble in the mobile phase move faster (higher Rf). Each compound's unique Rf is like a "fingerprint" in a given solvent.

A student runs chromatography on an unknown ink. The spots travel 3 cm, 5 cm, and 7.5 cm. The solvent front is at 10 cm. Calculate the Rf values and state what this indicates about the ink. Medium
Show Answer

Rf₁ = 3/10 = 0.30; Rf₂ = 5/10 = 0.50; Rf₃ = 7.5/10 = 0.75. Three spots indicate the ink is a mixture of at least three different dye compounds.

How can chromatography be used to test the purity of a drug sample? Describe the procedure and expected results for a pure vs impure sample. Medium
Show Answer

Spot the drug sample on TLC plate alongside a known pure reference. Run in appropriate solvent. Pure drug: one spot with the same Rf as the reference — confirms pure and matches known compound. Impure drug: extra spots with different Rf values — indicates the presence of impurities or different compounds. The Rf of the sample must match the reference exactly for identity confirmation.

What is the difference between paper chromatography and thin-layer chromatography (TLC)? Medium
Show Answer

In paper chromatography, the stationary phase is chromatography paper (cellulose). In TLC, the stationary phase is a thin layer of silica gel or alumina coated on a glass/aluminium plate. TLC is faster, gives sharper spots, can use non-polar solvents effectively, and is easier to visualise (UV lamp or iodine staining for colourless compounds). TLC is more commonly used in laboratories.

Explain what melting point tells us about the purity of a substance. What is observed for a pure substance vs an impure one? Medium
Show Answer

A pure substance melts sharply at one specific temperature (e.g., pure aspirin melts at exactly 135°C). An impure substance melts at a lower temperature and over a temperature range (e.g., 125–132°C). This is due to freezing point depression — impurities disrupt the crystal lattice, reducing the energy needed to melt. The melting range indicates degree of impurity; the wider the range, the more impure.

Why can gas chromatography (GC) separate more complex mixtures than paper chromatography? Medium
Show Answer

Gas chromatography uses a long capillary column (tens of metres) coated with a liquid stationary phase inside a temperature-controlled oven. It separates compounds based on their volatility and interaction with the stationary phase. The very long separation path gives thousands of theoretical plates (vs. hundreds for paper), and precise temperature programming allows fine-tuning. It can separate dozens of volatile compounds with extremely similar properties — far beyond the capability of paper chromatography.

A forensic scientist receives two ink samples from a crime scene and a suspect's pen. The TLC chromatograms have the following Rf values (in ethanol): Crime ink: 0.23, 0.57, 0.81. Suspect ink: 0.23, 0.57, 0.81. Can you conclude the inks are identical? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

The matching Rf values in ethanol provide strong evidence that the inks have the same components. However, you cannot be 100% certain — different inks could coincidentally have the same Rf in one solvent. To strengthen the conclusion: repeat with a different solvent system. If Rf values match in two or more different solvents, the evidence becomes very strong (but still not absolute proof without chemical analysis).

Why is the concept of purity important in the pharmaceutical industry? Give two consequences of an impure drug. Medium
Show Answer

Purity is critical because the exact dosage of the active compound must be known — impurities can alter effective concentration. Consequences of impurity: (1) Reduced efficacy — less active ingredient means the drug may not work at the intended dose. (2) Toxic side effects — some impurities may be toxic or cause allergic reactions (e.g., thalidomide's S-enantiomer impurity caused birth defects in the 1960s). Regulations require pharmaceutical purity to be above 99.9% for most drugs.

SET C Extended (Hard)

Explain how HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography) improves upon standard column chromatography in terms of speed, resolution, and sensitivity. Hard
Show Answer

HPLC pumps solvent under high pressure (up to 400 bar) through a column packed with very fine particles (1.7–5 μm). Small particles give greater surface area → higher number of theoretical plates → better resolution in shorter time. Benefits: (1) Speed: minutes vs hours. (2) Resolution: can separate compounds with very similar structures/Rf. (3) Sensitivity: UV or mass-spectrometer detectors can detect nanogram quantities. (4) Quantitative: peak area proportional to concentration. Used for drug analysis, food safety testing, environmental monitoring.

A student performs TLC on three substances: A (Rf = 0.45), B (Rf = 0.45 in ethanol, Rf = 0.62 in hexane), C (Rf = 0.45 in ethanol, Rf = 0.62 in hexane). Are B and C the same compound? Justify. Hard
Show Answer

B and C have identical Rf values in both solvent systems (0.45 in ethanol and 0.62 in hexane). This is very strong evidence they are the same compound. Each solvent tests a different chemical property (polarity, H-bonding). Matching in two different systems means their behaviour relative to different stationary/mobile phase combinations is identical — highly characteristic of the same molecule. (Note: co-spotting B and C and getting one spot would be definitive confirmation.)

Explain why boiling point alone is insufficient to confirm the identity of an unknown pure liquid. What combination of tests would you use? Hard
Show Answer

Different compounds can have the same or similar boiling points by coincidence. Boiling point only confirms a single physical property. To confirm identity: (1) Boiling point — must match reference. (2) Density/refractive index — unique to most pure liquids. (3) Chromatography (GC/HPLC) — Rf or retention time compared to reference. (4) Spectroscopy (IR/NMR) — molecular fingerprint that is unique to each compound. Using multiple orthogonal tests dramatically increases confidence in identification.

Explain how the choice of solvent affects the Rf value in paper chromatography. What properties should an ideal solvent have? Hard
Show Answer

The solvent (mobile phase) determines how well different components are carried up the paper. A highly polar solvent will carry polar compounds higher (higher Rf). A non-polar solvent will carry non-polar compounds better. Rf values are meaningless without specifying the solvent used. An ideal solvent: (1) Dissolves the sample well. (2) Gives a range of Rf values (0.2–0.8) for different components — good spread. (3) Does not react with components or paper. (4) Safe to use in the lab. (5) Moves at a practical rate (not too fast or slow).

Describe what co-spotting (co-chromatography) is and how it is used to confirm the identity of an unknown compound in TLC. Hard
Show Answer

Co-spotting: applying a mixture of the unknown compound AND a known pure reference compound to the same spot on the TLC plate. Run the chromatogram. If the unknown and reference are the same compound, the mixed spot produces one single spot at the same Rf as each pure sample. If different, two separate spots appear. Co-spotting is more reliable than comparing individual spots on separate lanes (eliminates variation due to different plates/runs). A matching co-spot strongly supports identical identity; a separated co-spot confirms they are different.

Explain why a sharp melting point both confirms purity AND identity of a compound. How could you use melting point to detect adulteration of a drug? Hard
Show Answer

Each pure compound has a unique, definitive melting point (e.g., aspirin 135°C; paracetamol 169°C). A sharp melting at the expected value confirms: (1) purity (no impurities, which would depress/broaden the mp), and (2) likely identity (matches the known mp of the suspected compound). To detect adulteration: measure the melting point of the suspect drug sample. If it melts below the expected temperature or over a wide range, impurities or adulterants are present. To identify the adulterant: compare mp of the isolated impurity with known compound reference values, combined with chromatography or spectroscopy.

Discuss how forensic scientists use chromatography (GC-MS) to identify illegal drugs. Explain both the separation and identification stages. Hard
Show Answer

GC stage (separation): The sample is vaporised and carried by an inert gas (helium) through a capillary column coated with a liquid stationary phase. Each compound has a unique retention time based on volatility and interaction with the column — compounds elute separately, detected as peaks on a chromatogram. MS stage (identification): Each eluting compound enters a mass spectrometer, which fragments the molecule with electron bombardment. The fragmentation pattern (mass spectrum) is unique to each compound — a molecular fingerprint. This is compared against a database library of millions of known compounds. GC-MS can detect nanogram quantities with extremely high specificity, making it the gold standard in forensic, environmental, and pharmaceutical analysis.

Evaluate the ethical implications of using drug purity testing in competitive sport. Why must both sensitivity and specificity of the chromatographic method be high? Hard
Show Answer

Sensitivity (ability to detect at low concentrations) must be high because athletes may micro-dose or substance levels may fall to trace amounts. High sensitivity prevents false negatives (missing a doper). Specificity (ability to distinguish the target compound from others) must be high to prevent false positives — incorrectly accusing a clean athlete (e.g., a legal supplement that gives a similar GC signal). A false positive destroys a career unjustly. Ethically: (1) Athletes have the right to be presumed innocent — false positives are a serious injustice. (2) Dopers undermine fair competition — high sensitivity necessary to catch them. (3) Analysis errors have legal consequences — results must be reproducible and auditable. Multiple confirmatory analyses (different methods) are always used before imposing sanctions.
