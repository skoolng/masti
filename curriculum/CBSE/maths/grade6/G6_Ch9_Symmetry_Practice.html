What is Symmetry? (Line Symmetry / Mirror Symmetry)

Set A Basic Identification of Line Symmetry

What does it mean for a shape to have a line of symmetry?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A line of symmetry divides a shape into two identical halves that are mirror images of each other.

Method: Definition of Line Symmetry
Step 1: Imagine folding the shape along the line.
Step 2: Check if both halves land exactly on top of each other.
Step 3: If they match perfectly, the fold line is a line of symmetry.
Key Rule: A line of symmetry is a fold line where both halves are perfect mirror images.
A butterfly has its wings spread open. Is the line down the centre of its body a line of symmetry? Why?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — the butterfly's body is a vertical line of symmetry.

Method: Fold Test for Line Symmetry
Step 1: Identify the vertical axis — the centre line of the butterfly's body.
Step 2: Imagine folding the butterfly along this line.
Step 3: The left wing lands exactly on the right wing — both match perfectly.
Key Rule: If folding along a line makes both halves coincide, that line is a line of symmetry.
True or False: Every shape has at least one line of symmetry.
Easy
Answer

Answer: False — many shapes have NO line of symmetry.

Method: Check Fold Test on Various Shapes
Step 1: Try folding a scalene triangle — no fold makes both halves match.
Step 2: Try folding a parallelogram — diagonals and midlines both fail the test.
Step 3: Conclude: some shapes have 0 lines of symmetry.
Key Rule: Not every shape is symmetric — a shape must have at least one valid fold line to have line symmetry.
If you place a mirror along the line of symmetry of a shape, what do you see?
Easy
Answer

Answer: You see the complete original shape.

Method: Mirror Test
Step 1: Place the mirror exactly along the line of symmetry.
Step 2: The half of the shape in front of the mirror reflects behind it.
Step 3: The reflected image reconstructs the missing half — the full original shape appears.
Key Rule: A mirror placed on a line of symmetry recreates the entire shape from just one half.
A heart shape (like a Valentine's heart) — does it have a line of symmetry? If yes, describe where it is.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — the heart has a vertical line of symmetry from top centre to bottom point.

Method: Identifying the Axis of Symmetry
Step 1: Locate the top dip (centre of the two bumps) and the bottom point of the heart.
Step 2: Draw a vertical line connecting these two points.
Step 3: The left half is a perfect mirror image of the right half along this line.
Key Rule: A vertical line of symmetry divides a shape into left and right mirror halves.
Look at the letter "M". Does it have a line of symmetry? Describe its position.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — the letter "M" has a vertical line of symmetry through its centre.

Method: Check Vertical Axis for Letters
Step 1: Draw a vertical line down the middle of the letter "M".
Step 2: The left leg and peak mirror the right leg and peak.
Step 3: Fold test: left half matches right half exactly.
Key Rule: A letter has vertical symmetry if its left half is a mirror image of its right half.
A rectangular picture frame is placed on a wall. How many lines of symmetry does it have? Name them.
Easy
Answer

Answer: 2 lines of symmetry — one horizontal, one vertical.

Method: Checking Axes for a Rectangle
Step 1: Check the horizontal midline — connects midpoints of the two longer sides; folding top onto bottom works. ✓
Step 2: Check the vertical midline — connects midpoints of the two shorter sides; folding left onto right works. ✓
Step 3: Check diagonals — sides of different lengths don't match, so diagonals fail. ✗
Key Rule: A rectangle (non-square) has exactly 2 lines of symmetry, both through midpoints of opposite sides.
A child folds a square piece of paper in half diagonally and cuts a small triangle from the fold. When the paper is unfolded, is the resulting shape symmetric? Why?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Yes — the fold line is the line of symmetry, so the result is always symmetric.

Method: Paper Folding and Cutting
Step 1: Fold the square diagonally — this fold IS the line of symmetry.
Step 2: Any cut made through the folded paper cuts both layers simultaneously.
Step 3: When unfolded, each cut appears mirrored on both sides of the fold line.
Key Rule: Folding before cutting guarantees symmetry — the fold line becomes the line of symmetry.
Explain the difference between a line of symmetry and a line of reflection in geometry.
Medium
Answer

Answer: A line of symmetry is a special line of reflection where the shape maps exactly onto itself.

Method: Comparing the Two Concepts
Step 1: A line of reflection can map a shape to any position — same shape, different location.
Step 2: A line of symmetry is a line of reflection where the image lands exactly on the original shape.
Step 3: Conclusion: every line of symmetry is a line of reflection, but not vice versa.
Key Rule: Line of symmetry = line of reflection that maps the shape onto itself.
A flag is symmetric about its vertical centre line. If the left half shows a red stripe then a white stripe (from left), what does the right half show from the centre outward?
Medium
Answer

Answer: From centre outward — white stripe, then red stripe.

Method: Applying Mirror Symmetry to Patterns
Step 1: Left half from left edge inward: red stripe, then white stripe (reaching centre).
Step 2: The right half mirrors the left — reverse the order moving away from centre.
Step 3: Right half from centre outward: white stripe (mirrors innermost left), then red stripe (mirrors outermost left).
Key Rule: A vertical line of symmetry reverses the left-to-right order when reading the pattern from the centre.

Set B Applying Symmetry Concepts

A rangoli design is symmetric about both a vertical and a horizontal axis. If the top-left quarter has a flower with 3 petals visible, how many petals does the full rangoli show?
Easy
Answer

Answer: 12 petals total.

Method: Using Double Symmetry to Count
Step 1: The rangoli has symmetry about both vertical and horizontal axes — this creates 4 equal quarters.
Step 2: The top-left quarter has 3 petals visible.
Step 3: Each of the 4 quarters has the same 3 petals → total = 3 × 4 = 12 petals.
Key Rule: Bilateral symmetry about two axes means the full design is 4 times any single quarter.
A shape has exactly one line of symmetry. It divides the shape into a left half and a right half. The left half has an area of 18 cm². What is the total area of the shape?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Total area = 36 cm².

Method: Using Symmetry to Find Total Area
Step 1: A line of symmetry divides the shape into two congruent (identical) halves.
Step 2: Left half area = 18 cm²; right half must also = 18 cm².
Step 3: Total area = 18 + 18 = 36 cm².
Key Rule: A line of symmetry creates two equal halves — total area = 2 × area of one half.
Is a line of symmetry always inside the shape? Give an example to support your answer.
Easy
Answer

Answer: No — the line of symmetry passes through the shape but can extend beyond it.

Method: Understanding Line of Symmetry Position
Step 1: A line of symmetry is an infinite line — it has no fixed length.
Step 2: For it to be a symmetry axis, it must cross the shape (not miss it entirely).
Step 3: It intersects the boundary but extends infinitely in both directions beyond the shape.
Key Rule: A line of symmetry always passes through the shape but is not restricted to lie inside it.
Two students disagree. Aanya says a line of symmetry must be vertical. Rohan says it can be at any angle. Who is correct?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Rohan is correct — a line of symmetry can be at any angle.

Method: Testing Different Axis Orientations
Step 1: A vertical line of symmetry: divides into left/right halves — valid for shapes like "A" or a rectangle.
Step 2: A horizontal line: divides into top/bottom halves — valid for "B" or rectangle.
Step 3: A diagonal line: valid for a square (45°) or rhombus — any angle can work.
Key Rule: A line of symmetry can be at any angle — what matters is that it creates two mirror-image halves.
A symmetric building facade has 5 windows on the left side of its central door. How many windows are on the right side? How many windows total?
Easy
Answer

Answer: 5 windows on the right; 10 windows total.

Method: Applying Bilateral Symmetry
Step 1: The building facade has a vertical line of symmetry through the central door.
Step 2: Left side has 5 windows — the right side must mirror the left exactly.
Step 3: Right side = 5 windows; total = 5 + 5 = 10 windows.
Key Rule: In a bilaterally symmetric design, each element on one side has a matching element on the other side.
A kite shape has one diagonal as its line of symmetry. If the top triangle formed has a base of 8 cm and a height of 6 cm, what is the area of the full kite?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Total area = 48 cm² (assuming top and bottom triangles are equal).

Method: Using Symmetry to Find Kite Area
Step 1: Area of the top triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × 8 × 6 = 24 cm².
Step 2: The kite's line of symmetry is the vertical diagonal — left and right halves mirror each other.
Step 3: If the kite is symmetric top-to-bottom as well, the bottom triangle also = 24 cm²; total = 48 cm².
Key Rule: The kite's vertical diagonal is its axis of symmetry — the two sides are mirror images.
A snowflake has 6 lines of symmetry. A designer creates an artificial snowflake with only 3 lines of symmetry. Do both still have symmetry? Explain.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Yes, both have symmetry — a shape needs only one line of symmetry to be called symmetric.

Method: Understanding Degrees of Symmetry
Step 1: The natural snowflake has 6 lines of symmetry — higher degree of symmetry.
Step 2: The artificial snowflake has 3 lines of symmetry — still symmetric, just less so.
Step 3: Any shape with at least 1 line of symmetry qualifies as having line symmetry.
Key Rule: More lines of symmetry = higher symmetry, but even 1 line makes a shape symmetric.
A playing card shows the number "8". When you rotate it 180° it looks the same. Does this mean it has a line of symmetry? Explain the difference.
Medium
Answer

Answer: No — looking the same after rotation means rotational symmetry, not line symmetry. These are different properties.

Method: Distinguishing Line vs Rotational Symmetry
Step 1: Line symmetry = a fold creates two matching halves (reflection test).
Step 2: Rotational symmetry = the shape looks identical after rotating by some angle less than 360°.
Step 3: A "6" rotated 180° becomes "9" — not the same; but "8" rotated 180° = "8" (rotational symmetry). "8" also has line symmetry, but they are distinct properties.
Key Rule: Rotational symmetry and line symmetry are independent — a shape can have one, both, or neither.
A shape has a horizontal line of symmetry. Point P is 4 cm above the line of symmetry and 3 cm to the right. Where is its mirror image point P'?
Medium
Answer

Answer: P' is 4 cm below the line of symmetry and 3 cm to the right.

Method: Reflecting a Point Across a Horizontal Axis
Step 1: P is 4 cm above the horizontal line of symmetry and 3 cm to the right.
Step 2: Reflecting across a horizontal axis: vertical distance flips (above → below), horizontal position stays the same.
Step 3: P' = 4 cm below the line, 3 cm to the right — same horizontal position, vertical position mirrored.
Key Rule: Reflection across a horizontal axis flips the vertical position only; the horizontal position is unchanged.
A traditional Indian rangoli is drawn on a square grid. It is symmetric about both diagonals and both midlines of the square. How many lines of symmetry does this rangoli have?
Hard
Answer

Answer: 4 lines of symmetry — same as a square.

Method: Counting All Axes for a Square Grid Design
Step 1: Both diagonals of the square are lines of symmetry (2 lines).
Step 2: Both midlines — vertical and horizontal — are also lines of symmetry (2 lines).
Step 3: Total = 2 + 2 = 4 lines of symmetry.
Key Rule: A square-based symmetric design has 4 lines of symmetry — 2 midlines and 2 diagonals.

Set C Multi-Step and Real-World Problems

A designer creates a logo that must look the same when reflected in a vertical mirror. She draws one half: a circle of radius 3 cm, a rectangle 2 cm × 5 cm, and a star. What shapes appear in the full logo and how many of each?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Full logo has 1 circle (if centred on axis), 2 rectangles, 2 stars.

Method: Reflecting Elements Across Vertical Axis
Step 1: Each shape on the left half reflects to a matching shape on the right half.
Step 2: A circle centred exactly on the axis of symmetry appears as 1 full circle in the complete logo.
Step 3: The rectangle and star (off-axis) each double → 2 rectangles, 2 stars.
Key Rule: Elements on the axis stay singular; elements off the axis double when the shape is completed by reflection.
A mirror is placed vertically on a table. A pencil is 15 cm in front of the mirror and 8 cm to the right of centre. Where does the mirror image of the pencil appear?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Mirror image is 15 cm behind the mirror and 8 cm to the right of centre (as seen in the mirror, 8 cm to the left as viewed from in front).

Method: Mirror Reflection Rules
Step 1: The distance from object to mirror = distance from image to mirror; pencil is 15 cm in front → image is 15 cm behind.
Step 2: The lateral position (8 cm to the right) is preserved from the mirror's perspective but appears reversed when you face the mirror.
Step 3: The mirror is a line of symmetry between the real pencil and its image.
Key Rule: A mirror reflection places the image at equal distance behind the mirror, with left-right reversed as seen by the viewer.
A fabric pattern repeats a symmetric motif every 10 cm. The full motif is 8 cm wide and symmetric about its vertical centre. If you see the left 4 cm of one motif, what does the right 4 cm look like?
Medium
Answer

Answer: The right 4 cm is the exact mirror image of the left 4 cm — every detail reversed.

Method: Applying Vertical Line Symmetry to Patterns
Step 1: The motif is 8 cm wide and symmetric about its vertical centre line.
Step 2: The left 4 cm contains the left half of the design.
Step 3: The right 4 cm is the mirror image — same details but horizontally flipped.
Key Rule: In a vertically symmetric motif, the right half is always the horizontal mirror of the left half.
A child cuts a paper by folding it in half (vertical fold) and making two cuts. When unfolded, how many cuts appear and why?
Medium
Answer

Answer: 4 cuts appear — 2 cuts × 2 layers = 4.

Method: Folded Paper Cutting
Step 1: When the paper is folded vertically, there are 2 layers.
Step 2: Each cut goes through both layers simultaneously — creating 2 cuts at once (original + mirror image).
Step 3: 2 cuts made while folded → 2 × 2 = 4 cuts when unfolded.
Key Rule: Folding doubles every cut — each cut on one half creates its mirror image on the other half.
A building's front wall is 40 m wide and symmetric about its centre. A window is placed 6 m from the left edge. Where must the matching window be placed?
Medium
Answer

Answer: The matching window is 34 m from the left edge.

Method: Reflecting Position Across Vertical Axis
Step 1: Total width = 40 m; centre (axis of symmetry) is at 40 ÷ 2 = 20 m from the left.
Step 2: Window is 6 m from left edge → distance from centre = 20 − 6 = 14 m (to the left of centre).
Step 3: Mirror position is 14 m to the right of centre → 20 + 14 = 34 m from the left edge.
Key Rule: To reflect a position across a vertical axis: mirror distance = (total width) − original position.
A symmetric butterfly wing pattern is described as having 7 spots on one wing. The spots are at distances 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 cm from the body. How are the spots arranged on the other wing?
Medium
Answer

Answer: The other wing also has 7 spots at distances 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 cm from the body — exact mirror of the first wing.

Method: Applying Bilateral Symmetry
Step 1: The butterfly has bilateral symmetry — the body is the line of symmetry.
Step 2: Each spot on the left wing has a mirror counterpart on the right wing at the same distance from the body.
Step 3: 7 spots at 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 cm → mirror wing also has spots at 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 cm.
Key Rule: Bilateral symmetry means every element on one side has an identical mirror element at the same distance on the other side.
A word is written, and when a mirror is placed on its right side, the reflected image completes a longer symmetric word. The original word is "WOW". What does the full symmetric display look like and why?
Medium
Answer

Answer: The full display reads "WOW | WOW" — the word followed by its mirror image, appearing as "WOWWOW".

Method: Checking Letter Symmetry Then Word Reflection
Step 1: Check each letter: W has vertical symmetry ✓, O has vertical symmetry ✓, W has vertical symmetry ✓.
Step 2: Since all letters are vertically symmetric, the reflected word looks identical to the original.
Step 3: Original "WOW" + reflected "WOW" combined = "WOWWOW" (the last W of original shares the mirror line with first W of reflection).
Key Rule: A word made of vertically symmetric letters reads the same in a vertical mirror.
A quilt block is symmetric about both its vertical and horizontal centre lines. The top-left quadrant uses 5 red squares and 4 blue squares. How many red and blue squares does the entire quilt block use?
Hard
Answer

Answer: 20 red squares and 16 blue squares in the full quilt block.

Method: Using Four-Fold Symmetry to Count Tiles
Step 1: The quilt is symmetric about both vertical and horizontal centre lines → 4 identical quadrants.
Step 2: Top-left quadrant: 5 red + 4 blue squares.
Step 3: Full design: red = 5 × 4 = 20; blue = 4 × 4 = 16 (assuming no squares sit on the axes).
Key Rule: Double bilateral symmetry creates 4 equal quadrants — multiply any quadrant's count by 4 for the total.
Point A(2, 5) is reflected in the line x = 4. Find the coordinates of A'.
Hard
Answer

Answer: A' = (6, 5).

Method: Reflecting a Point Across Vertical Line x = k
Step 1: Formula: x' = 2k − x. Here k = 4, x = 2 → x' = 2(4) − 2 = 8 − 2 = 6.
Step 2: The y-coordinate is unchanged when reflecting across a vertical line: y' = y = 5.
Step 3: A' = (6, 5). Check: distance from A(2,5) to x=4 is 2 units; distance from A'(6,5) to x=4 is also 2 units. ✓
Key Rule: Reflecting across x = k: new x-coordinate = 2k − old x; y-coordinate stays the same.
A logo has a vertical line of symmetry. Point P on the logo is at (−3, 7) relative to the centre. Point Q is at (5, 7). Is Q the mirror image of P? If not, where is the mirror image of P?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Q is NOT the mirror image of P. The correct mirror image of P is P' = (3, 7).

Method: Reflecting Across the Vertical Axis (x = 0)
Step 1: Reflecting across the vertical axis (x = 0): x' = −x. So P(−3, 7) → P' = (3, 7).
Step 2: The y-coordinate stays the same: y' = 7.
Step 3: Q = (5, 7) ≠ (3, 7), so Q is NOT the mirror image of P.
Key Rule: Reflecting across the y-axis negates the x-coordinate: (x, y) → (−x, y).

Set D Complex Patterns, Proofs, Combined Concepts

Can a shape have an infinite number of lines of symmetry? Give an example and explain.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Yes — a circle has infinitely many lines of symmetry.

Method: Identifying Lines of Symmetry for a Circle
Step 1: Any line through the centre of a circle is a diameter and divides it into two equal semicircles.
Step 2: Folding along any diameter makes both halves match perfectly — so every diameter is a line of symmetry.
Step 3: There are infinitely many diameters (at every possible angle) → infinitely many lines of symmetry.
Key Rule: A circle is the only common shape with infinite lines of symmetry — every diameter qualifies.
Prove or disprove: "If a shape has two lines of symmetry, they must intersect inside the shape."
Hard
Answer

Answer: True for connected shapes — two lines of symmetry must intersect inside or on the boundary.

Method: Reasoning About Intersecting Lines of Symmetry
Step 1: Each line of symmetry must pass through the interior of the shape (otherwise it couldn't divide it into two halves).
Step 2: Two lines that both pass through the interior of a bounded, connected shape must cross somewhere.
Step 3: That crossing point lies within or on the boundary of the shape.
Key Rule: For a connected shape, any two lines of symmetry always intersect inside or on the shape.
A shape has exactly two lines of symmetry that are perpendicular to each other. What can you conclude about the shape's rotational symmetry?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Two perpendicular lines of symmetry imply rotational symmetry of order 2 (180° rotation).

Method: Connecting Lines of Symmetry to Rotational Symmetry
Step 1: Reflecting across one perpendicular line, then the other, is equivalent to a 180° rotation.
Step 2: Since both reflections map the shape to itself, the composition (180° rotation) also maps it to itself.
Step 3: The intersection point of the two lines is the centre of this 180° rotation.
Key Rule: Two perpendicular lines of symmetry always generate rotational symmetry of order 2 about their intersection.
A symmetric design is created on a coordinate grid. Line of symmetry is y = x. Point P is at (3, 7). Find the mirror image P'.
Hard
Answer

Answer: P' = (7, 3).

Method: Reflecting Across the Line y = x
Step 1: The rule for reflecting across y = x is: swap the x and y coordinates.
Step 2: P = (3, 7) → P' = (7, 3).
Step 3: Verify: midpoint of PP' = ((3+7)/2, (7+3)/2) = (5, 5), and (5,5) lies on y = x. ✓
Key Rule: Reflecting across y = x swaps the coordinates: (x, y) → (y, x).
A figure has a line of symmetry along y = −x. Point Q is at (4, −2). Find Q'.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Q' = (2, −4).

Method: Reflecting Across the Line y = −x
Step 1: The rule for reflecting across y = −x is: (x, y) → (−y, −x).
Step 2: Q = (4, −2) → Q' = (−(−2), −4) = (2, −4).
Step 3: Verify: midpoint of QQ' = ((4+2)/2, (−2+(−4))/2) = (3, −3), and y = −x gives −3 = −3. ✓
Key Rule: Reflecting across y = −x: (x, y) → (−y, −x) — negate and swap both coordinates.
A designer claims: "A shape with 3 lines of symmetry must be a triangle." Is this true? Justify.
Hard
Answer

Answer: False — other shapes besides triangles can have 3 lines of symmetry.

Method: Exploring Shapes with 3 Lines of Symmetry
Step 1: An equilateral triangle has exactly 3 lines of symmetry — one from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side.
Step 2: A 3-pointed star (trefoil) also has 3 lines of symmetry — one through each point.
Step 3: Any shape with 3-fold dihedral symmetry (D₃) has 3 lines of symmetry — it need not be a triangle.
Key Rule: 3 lines of symmetry means 3-fold dihedral symmetry (D₃) — the shape shares this property with an equilateral triangle but is not necessarily one.
A rangoli has 8 lines of symmetry, all passing through the centre. What angles do consecutive lines of symmetry make with each other?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Consecutive lines of symmetry make 22.5° angles with each other.

Method: Finding Angle Between Lines of Symmetry
Step 1: All 8 lines of symmetry pass through the centre and span a total of 180° (since a line in one direction = same line in the opposite direction).
Step 2: 8 lines divide 180° equally: angle = 180° ÷ 8 = 22.5°.
Step 3: Verify: 8 × 22.5° = 180°. ✓
Key Rule: n lines of symmetry through a centre are spaced 180°/n apart from each other.
Two mirrors are placed at an angle of 60° to each other. How many images of an object placed between them will you see? How does this relate to symmetry?
Hard
Answer

Answer: 5 images — creating a 6-fold symmetric pattern like a kaleidoscope.

Method: Two-Mirror Image Formula
Step 1: Formula: number of images = (360° ÷ angle between mirrors) − 1.
Step 2: Angle = 60° → images = (360° ÷ 60°) − 1 = 6 − 1 = 5 images.
Step 3: The 5 images + 1 original = 6 copies total, arranged with 6-fold symmetry like a snowflake.
Key Rule: Two mirrors at angle θ create (360°/θ) − 1 images; the result has 360°/θ -fold symmetry.
The letter "S" does not have a line of symmetry but does have rotational symmetry. Explain what type of symmetry "S" has and what order.
Hard
Answer

Answer: "S" has rotational symmetry of order 2 — no line of symmetry.

Method: Testing S for Both Types of Symmetry
Step 1: Try folding "S" — no vertical, horizontal, or diagonal fold produces two matching halves. No line symmetry.
Step 2: Rotate "S" by 180° about its centre — the top curves to the bottom and vice versa, producing the same "S". ✓
Step 3: It matches at 180° and again at 360° → order 2 rotational symmetry.
Key Rule: A shape with rotational symmetry of order 2 only (no line symmetry) maps to itself after 180° rotation but cannot be folded to match.
If a shape has n lines of symmetry where n ≥ 2, prove that the shape must also have rotational symmetry. What is the minimum order of rotation?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Yes — n lines of symmetry (n ≥ 2) always imply rotational symmetry of order n.

Method: Composing Two Reflections
Step 1: Two lines of symmetry meeting at angle θ: reflecting across both produces a rotation by 2θ.
Step 2: For n lines equally spaced, adjacent lines meet at angle 180°/n → rotation by 2 × 180°/n = 360°/n.
Step 3: This rotation maps the shape to itself, so the shape has rotational symmetry; minimum order = n.
Key Rule: n lines of symmetry through a common centre always produce rotational symmetry of order n.

Lines of Symmetry in Basic Shapes (Triangle, Square, Rectangle, Circle)

Set A Basic Identification

How many lines of symmetry does a square have?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A square has 4 lines of symmetry.

Method: Checking All Possible Axes for a Square
Step 1: Vertical midline — connects midpoints of top and bottom sides; folding left onto right works. ✓
Step 2: Horizontal midline — connects midpoints of left and right sides; folding top onto bottom works. ✓
Step 3: Both diagonals — equal sides meet at corners, so diagonal folds also work. ✓ ✓ Total = 4 lines.
Key Rule: A square has 4 lines of symmetry: 2 midlines and 2 diagonals.
How many lines of symmetry does an equilateral triangle have?
Easy
Answer

Answer: An equilateral triangle has 3 lines of symmetry.

Method: Identifying Symmetry Lines in an Equilateral Triangle
Step 1: From vertex A to the midpoint of side BC — fold creates two matching halves. ✓
Step 2: From vertex B to the midpoint of AC — fold works. ✓
Step 3: From vertex C to the midpoint of AB — fold works. ✓ Total = 3 lines.
Key Rule: An equilateral triangle has 3 lines of symmetry — one from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side.
How many lines of symmetry does a rectangle (that is not a square) have?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A non-square rectangle has 2 lines of symmetry.

Method: Testing Rectangle Axes
Step 1: Vertical midline (joins midpoints of the two shorter sides) — folding left onto right works since both halves are equal. ✓
Step 2: Horizontal midline (joins midpoints of the two longer sides) — folding top onto bottom works. ✓
Step 3: Diagonals — length ≠ width for a non-square rectangle, so diagonal folds fail. ✗
Key Rule: A non-square rectangle has exactly 2 lines of symmetry — both through midpoints of opposite sides.
How many lines of symmetry does a circle have?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A circle has infinite lines of symmetry — every diameter is one.

Method: Checking All Diameters of a Circle
Step 1: Any straight line through the centre of a circle divides it into two equal semicircles.
Step 2: Folding along any such diameter makes both halves coincide perfectly.
Step 3: There are infinitely many possible diameters (one for every direction) → infinite lines of symmetry.
Key Rule: Every diameter of a circle is a line of symmetry — a circle has infinitely many.
Does a scalene triangle have any lines of symmetry?
Easy
Answer

Answer: No — a scalene triangle has 0 lines of symmetry.

Method: Applying the Fold Test
Step 1: All three sides of a scalene triangle are different lengths.
Step 2: Try folding vertex-to-opposite-side midpoint: unequal sides mean the two halves don't match.
Step 3: No possible fold creates two identical halves → 0 lines of symmetry.
Key Rule: A scalene triangle (all sides different) has no lines of symmetry.
How many lines of symmetry does an isosceles triangle have?
Easy
Answer

Answer: An isosceles triangle has 1 line of symmetry.

Method: Finding the Symmetry Line
Step 1: An isosceles triangle has two equal sides meeting at the apex (top vertex).
Step 2: Draw a line from the apex straight down to the midpoint of the base (unequal side).
Step 3: Folding along this line maps the two equal sides onto each other — both halves match. ✓
Key Rule: An isosceles triangle has exactly 1 line of symmetry — from the apex to the midpoint of the base.
Does a rectangle have the same number of lines of symmetry as a square? Explain.
Easy
Answer

Answer: No — a rectangle has 2, a square has 4 lines of symmetry.

Method: Comparing Rectangle and Square Axes
Step 1: Both rectangle and square have 2 midline axes of symmetry (vertical and horizontal).
Step 2: A square also has 2 diagonal axes — because all 4 sides are equal, diagonal folds work. ✓
Step 3: A non-square rectangle's diagonal fold fails because unequal sides don't match. ✗
Key Rule: A square has 4 lines of symmetry (2 midlines + 2 diagonals); a non-square rectangle has only 2 (midlines only).
Which has more lines of symmetry: a square or an equilateral triangle? How many more?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A square has 1 more line of symmetry — square has 4, equilateral triangle has 3.

Method: Counting and Comparing
Step 1: Equilateral triangle: 3 lines of symmetry (one from each vertex to the opposite midpoint).
Step 2: Square: 4 lines of symmetry (2 midlines + 2 diagonals).

Hard
Answer
