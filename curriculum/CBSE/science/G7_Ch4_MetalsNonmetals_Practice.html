SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain why elements in the same group of the Periodic Table have similar chemical properties.Medium
Show Answer

Elements in the same group have the same number of valence (outermost) electrons. Because chemical reactions involve these valence electrons, elements with the same count behave similarly in reactions.

Why did Mendeleev leave gaps in his original Periodic Table?Medium
Show Answer

Mendeleev left gaps to maintain the pattern of properties and predicted undiscovered elements would fill them. His predictions for gallium and germanium were later confirmed.

Describe how elements are arranged in the modern Periodic Table.Medium
Show Answer

Elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number. They are placed in periods (rows) and groups (columns) so elements with similar properties align vertically.

A student says "Period 3 has more elements than Period 2." Is this correct? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

This is incorrect. Both Period 2 and Period 3 contain exactly 8 elements. Later periods (4 and 5) contain 18 because d-block transition metals are included.

Iron (Fe) is in Period 4, Group 8. What does its position tell you about its properties?Medium
Show Answer

Period 4 tells us iron has 4 electron shells. Group 8 (transition metal, d-block) tells us it is a metal with variable oxidation states (+2 and +3).

Distinguish between a period and a group, using one example each.Medium

A period is a horizontal row — Period 2 contains Li, Be, B, C, N, O, F, Ne. A group is a vertical column — Group 1 contains H, Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr.

About 75–80% of all known elements are metals — roughly 90 out of 118 elements.

Sodium (Na) is in Group 1, Period 3. Carbon (C) is in Group 14, Period 2. Which is a metal and which is a non-metal? Justify.Medium
Show Answer

Sodium is a metal — it is on the far left (Group 1). Carbon is a non-metal — it is on the right side (Group 14).

Name two elements from Group 18 and explain why they are classified as non-metals.Medium

Helium (He) and Neon (Ne) are noble gases. They are gases, poor conductors, and have full outer electron shells making them chemically inert — classic non-metal character.

How does the Periodic Table help a chemist predict whether a new element will be a metal or non-metal?Medium
Show Answer

By knowing the element's atomic number and position, a chemist can predict: left/centre = metal; right = non-metal. The diagonal metalloid band marks the boundary.

SET C MYP Analysis & Evaluation — Hard

Evaluate why the modern Periodic Table is considered a better model than Mendeleev's original table. Use specific evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Mendeleev ordered by atomic mass, causing anomalies (e.g., argon before potassium). The modern table orders by atomic number, eliminating these inconsistencies. It also includes all 118 elements, noble gases (discovered after Mendeleev), and correctly positions d-block transition metals.

Compare lithium and sodium (Group 1) reacting with water. IV: type of Group 1 metal. DV: rate of reaction (bubble production, temperature change). CV: volume of water, metal piece size, temperature. Both produce hydrogen and hydroxide, but sodium reacts faster — supporting same-group similarity.

A new element with atomic number 119 is synthesised. Predict its likely properties using the Periodic Table.Hard
Show Answer

Element 119 would be in Period 8, Group 1 (below francium). Predicted: highly reactive metal, forms +1 ions, reacts explosively with water, low electronegativity, silvery solid. Reasoning: all Group 1 elements share these properties due to one valence electron.

"The Periodic Table is not just a chart — it is a predictive tool." Justify with two specific examples.Hard
Show Answer

1) Mendeleev predicted eka-silicon (germanium) — density, boiling point, and reactions matched his predictions. 2) Modern chemists predict properties of superheavy elements before synthesis using periodic trends in atomic radius and ionisation energy.

Analyse why hydrogen is placed in Group 1 even though it is a non-metal. What does this reveal about the limits of classification?Hard
Show Answer

Hydrogen has one valence electron like Group 1 metals, hence its placement. But it is a gas, forms H+ and H- ions, and shares covalent bonds. This reveals classification systems are models with boundaries — real elements may straddle categories.

Compare properties of a Period 2 metal (lithium) with a Period 2 non-metal (oxygen). Use Periodic Table position to explain differences.Hard
Show Answer

Li (Group 1): solid, conducts electricity, reacts with water, low electronegativity, gives up electrons. O (Group 16): gas, insulator, high electronegativity, gains electrons. Moving right across Period 2 increases nuclear charge without adding shells, pulling electrons more tightly — causing the shift from metallic to non-metallic character.

MYP Criterion D: Evaluate the social and historical importance of Mendeleev's Periodic Table.Hard
Show Answer

Mendeleev's table (1869) transformed chemistry from a list of facts into a predictive science. Successful predictions of undiscovered elements gave scientists confidence to search for new ones. It unified knowledge, guided industrial chemistry, enabled pharmaceutical design, and is one of science's greatest intellectual achievements.

Why do transition metals (Groups 3–12) show more variable properties than main-group metals? Connect to electron arrangement.Hard
Show Answer

Transition metals fill the d sub-shell (up to 10 electrons). These d electrons are shielded differently, enabling multiple stable oxidation states (e.g., Fe2+ and Fe3+), coloured compounds, and catalytic activity. Main-group metals have simpler s-shell filling, giving fixed oxidation states.

A student argues the Periodic Table is culturally biased because most element names come from European languages. Critically evaluate this view.Hard
Show Answer

The argument has merit: many elements named after European scientists or places (europium, francium). However, nihonium (Japan), moscovium (Russia), and ancient names (aurum, ferrum) show broader cultural input. The scientific content — atomic number, properties — is culturally neutral; only nomenclature reflects historical dominance.

Construct a reasoned argument for why the Periodic Table should be the foundation of all chemistry learning.Hard
Show Answer

The Periodic Table organises all chemical knowledge: it predicts bonding (electronegativity trends), reactivity (ionisation energy), physical state, and compound formation. Without understanding element position, students cannot predict reactions, understand trends, or design new materials. It is the single most powerful conceptual tool in chemistry.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain why metals are good conductors of electricity using the concept of free electrons.Medium
Show Answer

In metals, the outermost electrons are loosely held and form a sea of free electrons throughout the metal lattice. When a voltage is applied, these electrons drift in one direction, carrying charge — this constitutes electric current.

Why are metals used to make saucepans? Link to at least three physical properties.Medium
Show Answer

Metals are used because they are good conductors of heat (heat spreads quickly), have high melting points (don't melt on a stove), and are malleable (can be shaped into a pan).

Copper is preferred over gold for electrical wiring. Give two reasons why, using physical properties.Medium
Show Answer

1) Copper is ductile — it can be drawn into long thin wires. 2) Copper is an excellent conductor of electricity and far less expensive than gold, making it practical for large-scale wiring.

A student drops a metal rod and a glass rod onto the floor. Only the metal produces a ringing sound. Explain why.Medium
Show Answer

Metals are sonorous — their ordered crystal lattice transmits vibrations efficiently, producing a ringing sound. Glass is brittle and absorbs vibrations differently, producing a dull sound or breaking instead.

Explain the difference between malleability and ductility with one industrial example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Malleability = hammered into sheets. Example: aluminium foil made by rolling aluminium. Ductility = drawn into wires. Example: copper drawn into electrical cables. Both involve plastic deformation without fracture.

Mercury is classified as a metal even though it is liquid. Justify this classification.Medium
Show Answer

Mercury is still a metal because it conducts electricity, has a metallic lustre, has a high density, and is in Group 12 (d-block) of the Periodic Table. Its liquid state is an exception, not a disqualification.

Why is aluminium used in aeroplane construction? Name three relevant physical properties.Medium
Show Answer

Aluminium has a low density (lightweight), is malleable (can be shaped into panels), and is resistant to corrosion. Low density is especially critical — aircraft must be as light as possible to fly efficiently.

Compare the melting points of iron (~1538 °C) and mercury (−39 °C). What does this indicate about metallic bond strength?Medium
Show Answer

Iron's very high melting point indicates very strong metallic bonds — many electrons shared in a strong lattice. Mercury's very low melting point indicates weak metallic bonds, which is why it is liquid at room temperature.

Why is copper preferred over iron for water pipes?Medium
Show Answer

Copper is resistant to corrosion (doesn't rust like iron), is malleable (can be bent into shapes), and does not react with water under normal conditions — making it safer and more durable for plumbing.

State one metal that is an exception to (a) solid at room temperature and (b) high melting point.Medium
Show Answer

(a) Mercury — liquid at room temperature. (b) Gallium — melts at just 29.8 °C, barely above room temperature, making it the lowest-melting solid metal under normal conditions.

SET C MYP Analysis & Evaluation — Hard

Explain using metallic bonding theory why metals are malleable and ductile but ionic compounds (like table salt) are brittle.Hard
Show Answer

In metals, positive ions sit in a sea of delocalised electrons. When force is applied, layers of ions slide past each other while the electron sea maintains bonding — hence malleability and ductility. In ionic crystals, shifting layers brings same-charge ions adjacent, causing electrostatic repulsion and fracture — hence brittleness.

MYP Criterion B: Design an experiment to compare the thermal conductivity of copper, iron, and aluminium. Identify all variables.Hard
Show Answer

Attach a wax bead at equal distances along identical rods of each metal. Apply equal heat at one end. Measure time for wax to melt at each distance. IV: type of metal. DV: time for wax to melt. CV: rod dimensions, wax type, heat source, bead position. Expected: Copper > Aluminium > Iron in conductivity.

A wire of metal X breaks when bent repeatedly, unlike copper. What property does metal X lack? How could it be tested?Hard
Show Answer

Metal X lacks ductility (it is brittle). Tests: a bend test (measure angle before fracture); a tensile test (measure elongation before breaking); a hardness test. Brittle metals fracture at low strain; ductile metals deform plastically before breaking.

Evaluate whether physical properties alone are sufficient to classify an element as a metal. Give a counterexample.Hard
Show Answer

Physical properties alone are insufficient. Counterexample: graphite (carbon) — a non-metal — conducts electricity like a metal due to delocalised electrons. Conversely, mercury is liquid. Classification must also consider chemical behaviour and atomic structure.

Why do metals with higher atomic number not always have higher melting points? Analyse Group 1 trends (Li to Cs).Hard
Show Answer

Going down Group 1, atomic radius increases, so delocalised electrons are further from the nucleus — metallic bonds weaken. Melting points decrease: Li (180°C) → Na (98°C) → K (63°C) → Rb (39°C) → Cs (28°C). Higher atomic number does not mean stronger bonding.

A jeweller uses gold for rings and silver for necklaces. Critically compare physical properties that make each suitable.Hard
Show Answer

Both are lustrous, malleable, ductile, and corrosion-resistant. Gold: higher density, more malleable, never tarnishes. Silver: better conductor, slightly harder (more durable for chains), tarnishes slowly. "Better" depends on context: gold for prestige and tarnish-resistance; silver for detailed work and cost. Neither is objectively superior for all uses.

How does the "sea of electrons" model simultaneously explain electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, and metallic lustre?Hard
Show Answer

(a) Electrical: free electrons drift under voltage, carrying charge. (b) Thermal: electrons carry kinetic energy; they collide with ions, transferring energy rapidly. (c) Lustre: free electrons at the surface absorb and re-emit light across all wavelengths, producing shine. All three arise from the same delocalised electron model.

Lead is used in radiation shielding despite being a poor conductor and relatively soft. What property makes it suitable, and what does this reveal about materials science?Hard
Show Answer

Lead's very high density (11.3 g/cm³) means dense atoms absorb gamma rays and X-rays effectively. This demonstrates that in materials science, the most relevant property for the application is selected — a material does not need to excel in all properties.

Critically evaluate the statement: "All metals look and feel the same."Hard
Show Answer

This is false. Gold is yellow, copper is reddish-brown, most others are grey. Mercury is liquid. Sodium is so soft it cuts with a knife; tungsten is extremely hard. Lithium floats on water. Osmium is the densest natural element. Metals vary enormously in physical properties despite sharing the general trend of metallic lustre and solid state.

MYP Criterion D: Discuss the environmental impact of extracting metals with very high melting points (e.g., tungsten). Consider energy use.Hard
Show Answer

High-melting-point metals like tungsten require extremely high-temperature furnaces, consuming vast energy (often fossil-fuel-derived), increasing CO2 emissions. Mining also causes habitat destruction and water pollution. The benefit of long-lasting products must be weighed against extraction costs. LED lighting (less tungsten) shows how materials choices have sustainability consequences.

Write the balanced chemical equation for magnesium reacting with oxygen.Medium

Explain why metal oxides are described as basic oxides.Medium

Metal oxides are basic because they react with acids to form salt and water (neutralisation), and many dissolve in water to produce alkaline (basic) solutions. Example: CaO + H2O → Ca(OH)2 (alkaline).

Why does iron rust faster in saltwater than in fresh water?Medium
Show Answer

Saltwater contains dissolved ions (Na+ and Cl-) that act as an electrolyte, increasing electrical conductivity and accelerating the electrochemical corrosion process. Ions allow electrons to transfer more readily, speeding up oxidation of iron.

A student adds calcium to cold water. Describe their observations and name the products.Medium
Show Answer

Calcium sinks, reacts steadily, bubbles of hydrogen gas form, solution becomes milky/cloudy, heat is released. Products: calcium hydroxide [Ca(OH)2] and hydrogen gas [H2]. Equation: Ca + 2H2O → Ca(OH)2 + H2.

Compare the reactions of sodium and magnesium with cold water. What does this tell you about their reactivity?Medium

Sodium reacts vigorously with cold water (fizzes, floats, may ignite). Magnesium reacts very slowly with cold water but reacts readily with steam. This confirms sodium is more reactive than magnesium.

What observation confirms hydrogen gas is produced when a metal reacts with an acid?Medium

The burning splint test: a lit splint held at the mouth of the test tube produces a "squeaky pop" sound when hydrogen is present.

Explain what happens at the atomic level when iron is oxidised to form iron oxide.Medium

Iron atoms each lose electrons (are oxidised) to form Fe2+ or Fe3+ ions. These ions bond with O2- ions (from oxygen molecules that gained electrons). The result is an ionic compound: iron oxide (Fe2O3).

Why is gold described as "chemically inert" and why is this valued?Medium
Show Answer

Gold does not react with oxygen, water, or most acids — it is highly unreactive (at the bottom of the reactivity series). This means gold does not corrode or tarnish, making it ideal for jewellery, coins, and electronic contacts.

Name one metal that does not react with dilute hydrochloric acid and explain why.Medium

Copper does not react with dilute HCl because it is below hydrogen in the reactivity series — it lacks the reactivity needed to displace hydrogen from an acid.

Balance the following equation and identify what is oxidised and reduced: Fe + O2 → Fe2O3Hard
Show Answer

Balanced: 4Fe + 3O2 → 2Fe2O3. Oxidised: Iron (Fe) — loses electrons (0 to +3). Reduced: Oxygen (O2) — gains electrons (0 to −2). This is a redox reaction.

Explain why the reaction of potassium with water is more dangerous than lithium's, using electron configuration and reactivity.Hard
Show Answer

Potassium's valence electron is in the 4th shell (vs. 2nd for Li), further from the nucleus and held less tightly. Potassium therefore loses its electron more easily, reacting explosively with water — producing hydrogen gas so rapidly it ignites. Lithium reacts steadily and safely by comparison.

A metal M reacts with oxygen to form M2O3. Determine the valency of M. Write the balanced equation if M is aluminium.Hard
Show Answer

In M2O3: 3 oxygens contribute −6 charge; 2 M atoms provide +6, so each M has valency +3. For aluminium: 4Al + 3O2 → 2Al2O3.

Evaluate: "Corrosion of metals is always harmful." Discuss with evidence for and against.Hard
Show Answer

For (harmful): Rusting of iron causes structural failure in bridges and pipes — costs 3–4% of global GDP annually. Against (beneficial): The corrosion layer on aluminium (Al2O3) is a protective passivation layer preventing further corrosion. Copper's green patina similarly protects underlying metal. Corrosion is context-dependent.

A student finds a shiny metal object and wants to identify if it is sodium or copper. Describe three chemical tests and predict results for each metal.Hard

1) Add to water: Sodium → vigorous reaction, H2 gas, alkaline solution. Copper → no reaction. 2) Add to dilute HCl: Sodium → violent reaction. Copper → no reaction. 3) Heat in air: Sodium → burns yellow flame, white oxide. Copper → surface turns black (copper oxide), no flame.

MYP Criterion B: Design an experiment to find the order of reactivity of three unknown metals (X, Y, Z) using dilute hydrochloric acid. Include safety precautions.Hard

Add equal masses of X, Y, Z to equal volumes of same-concentration HCl. Measure bubble rate (or use gas syringe for H2 volume per minute). IV: type of metal. DV: rate of H2 production. Why do some metals form a protective oxide layer (passivation) while others continue to corrode? Compare iron and aluminium.Hard
Show Answer

Aluminium: forms a thin, dense Al2O3 layer that adheres tightly to the surface, blocking further oxygen contact. Iron: forms rust (hydrated Fe2O3) which is porous, flaky, and expands — it does not protect the underlying metal. Key difference: the physical properties of the oxide layer — dense/adherent vs. porous/flaky.

Write the net ionic equation for magnesium reacting with dilute sulphuric acid. Identify the spectator ions.Hard
Show Answer

Full: Mg + H2SO4 → MgSO4 + H2. Full ionic: Mg + 2H+ + SO42- → Mg2+ + SO42- + H2. Net ionic: Mg + 2H+ → Mg2+ + H2. Spectator ion: SO42- (unchanged on both sides).

How does the formation of basic metal oxides relate to the acidic nature of non-metal oxides? Explain in terms of pH and reaction with water.Hard
Show Answer

Metal oxides dissolve in water to produce alkaline solutions (e.g., Na2O + H2O → 2NaOH, pH > 7). Non-metal oxides dissolve in water to produce acidic solutions (e.g., CO2 + H2O → H2CO3, pH < 7). Metallic elements form basic oxides; non-metallic elements form acidic oxides — a fundamental principle in acid-base chemistry.

Critically evaluate: "The more reactive a metal, the more useful it is in everyday life."Hard
Show Answer

Against: The most reactive metals (K, Na, Ca) are dangerous to handle, react with air and water, and require special storage — limiting everyday use. For: Moderately reactive metals like aluminium and iron are extremely useful. Conclusion: Moderate reactivity is most useful; very high or very low reactivity limits application. The statement is an oversimplification.

SET A Recall & Recognition — Easy

Write the reactivity series from most to least reactive, listing at least 10 metals.Easy
Show Answer

Most to least reactive: K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > [H] > Cu > Ag > Au. Hydrogen is included as a reference point — metals above H displace it from acids; those below do not.

Which metal is most reactive: potassium, iron, or copper?Easy
Show Answer

Potassium (K) is the most reactive of the three. Order: K > Fe > Cu in the reactivity series.

Which metals in the reactivity series are found as native (pure) elements in nature?Easy
Show Answer

Metals at the bottom of the reactivity series — copper, silver, and gold — can be found as native elements, because they are too unreactive to combine with other elements.

What is a displacement reaction?Easy
Show Answer

A displacement reaction occurs when a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from a solution of its salt. Example: Fe + CuSO4 → FeSO4 + Cu.

Why is hydrogen included in the reactivity series even though it is not a metal?Easy
Show Answer

Hydrogen is included as a reference point. Metals above H in the series can displace hydrogen from dilute acids; metals below H (copper, silver, gold) cannot.

Name the two products formed when iron reacts with copper sulphate solution.Easy
Show Answer

Iron(II) sulphate (FeSO4) and copper (Cu). The iron displaces the copper: Fe + CuSO4 → FeSO4 + Cu.

What observation would you make when zinc is added to blue copper sulphate solution?Easy
Show Answer

The blue colour fades (as Cu2+ ions are removed from solution) and a layer of reddish-brown copper deposits on the zinc. Zinc + copper sulphate → zinc sulphate + copper.

Will silver displace copper from copper sulphate solution? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

No. Silver is below copper in the reactivity series, so it cannot displace copper. Displacement only occurs when the free metal is more reactive than the metal in solution.

Name the most reactive and least reactive metals in the standard reactivity series.Easy
Show Answer

Potassium (K) is the most reactive (at the top). Gold (Au) is the least reactive (at the bottom).

What does it mean if a metal is "high in the reactivity series"?Easy
Show Answer

A metal high in the reactivity series reacts vigorously with oxygen, water, and acids. It loses electrons easily and is a strong reducing agent. It is hard to extract from its ore.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain why gold is used for jewellery while sodium cannot be, using the reactivity series.Medium
Show Answer

Gold is at the bottom of the reactivity series — it does not react with air, water, or most acids, maintaining its shine indefinitely. Sodium is at the top — it reacts explosively with water and rapidly with oxygen. Jewellery must be chemically stable in contact with skin; gold satisfies this perfectly.

Predict whether magnesium will displace iron from iron sulphate solution. Write the equation.Medium
Show Answer

Yes, because magnesium is more reactive than iron. Equation: Mg + FeSO4 → MgSO4 + Fe. The solution colour changes as Fe2+ ions are replaced by Mg2+ ions, and iron metal deposits on the magnesium.

Explain how galvanising protects iron from rusting, using the reactivity series.Medium
Show Answer

Galvanising coats iron with zinc. Even if the zinc coating is scratched, zinc (higher in the reactivity series than iron) preferentially oxidises, protecting the exposed iron. Zinc "sacrifices" itself — this is called sacrificial protection.

A student places silver in copper sulphate solution. Predict and explain what happens.Medium
Show Answer

Nothing visible happens. Silver is below copper in the reactivity series, so a less reactive metal cannot displace a more reactive one from its salt solution. Displacement requires the free metal to be higher than the dissolved metal.

Explain the thermite reaction and describe its use in welding railway tracks.Medium
Show Answer

Thermite: aluminium displaces iron from iron oxide — 2Al + Fe2O3 → Al2O3 + 2Fe — a highly exothermic reaction producing molten iron (~2500°C). The molten iron fills the gap between rail sections and solidifies as a strong weld. Used worldwide for rail maintenance where electric welding is impractical.

Design an experiment to rank copper, iron, and zinc in order of reactivity using displacement reactions.Medium
Show Answer

Test each metal in solutions of the other metals' salts: Fe in CuSO4 (if copper deposits → Fe more reactive); Zn in FeSO4 (if iron deposits → Zn more reactive); Zn in CuSO4 (if copper deposits → Zn more reactive). Results confirm: Zn > Fe > Cu. Positive result = colour change in solution + metal deposit on surface.

Explain how sacrificial anodes protect ships' steel hulls from corrosion.Medium
Show Answer

Blocks of zinc or magnesium (more reactive metals) are bolted to the steel hull. In seawater, these anodes corrode preferentially because they are higher in the reactivity series than the iron in steel. The anode gives up electrons first, protecting the steel. Anodes are replaced periodically.

Each class of element has properties optimally suited to specific functions — there is no hierarchy, only fitness for purpose.

Explain why non-metal oxides are acidic while metal oxides are basic.Medium
Show Answer

Non-metals have high electronegativity — they attract electrons strongly. Non-metal oxides (e.g., CO2, SO3) react with water to produce oxyacids (H2CO3, H2SO4) by donating H+ ions. Metal oxides contain metal cations and O2- ions; O2- is a strong base that accepts H+ from water, producing hydroxide ions (OH-) — alkaline solutions. The difference in electronegativity determines whether the oxide is acidic or basic.

Fluorine is the most powerful oxidising agent (highest electronegativity); reactivity decreases down the group (Cl > Br > I) as atomic radius increases and electron affinity decreases.

Argon has a completely full outer shell — no tendency to gain, lose, or share electrons. It forms no compounds under normal conditions. Both are non-metals; their chemical behaviour differs completely based on their electron configurations.

How does the concept of electronegativity explain why oxygen reacts with metals to form ionic oxides but with non-metals to form covalent oxides?Hard
Show Answer

The same nitrogen oxide chemistry operates across scales — from atmospheric chemistry to molecular medicine — with beneficial or harmful outcomes determined entirely by concentration and location.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain why silicon is described as a semiconductor rather than a conductor or an insulator.Medium
Show Answer

Pure silicon has 4 valence electrons in a covalent lattice. At absolute zero it is an insulator. At room temperature, thermal energy excites a small fraction of electrons into the conduction band, giving limited conductivity — between that of a conductor and an insulator. This controllable conductivity is what makes silicon valuable for electronics.

Explain how doping silicon with phosphorus creates an n-type semiconductor.Medium
Show Answer

Phosphorus has 5 valence electrons. When a P atom replaces a Si atom in the lattice (which needs only 4), the extra (fifth) electron is loosely held and easily moves freely through the structure. These extra free electrons are the charge carriers — making the material an n-type (negative charge carrier) semiconductor with greatly improved conductivity.

Compare silicon and carbon: both are in Group 14. Why is silicon a semiconductor but diamond (carbon) an insulator?Medium
Show Answer

In diamond, C-C bonds are extremely strong — the energy gap (band gap) between valence and conduction bands is very large (~5.5 eV), requiring enormous energy to free electrons. In silicon, Si-Si bonds are weaker (larger atoms, more diffuse orbitals) — the band gap is smaller (~1.1 eV), allowing thermal energy to promote electrons. Silicon's band gap lies in the ideal range for electronic devices.

Why is silicon called the "backbone of the digital age"?Medium
Show Answer

Every digital device — computers, smartphones, tablets, vehicles, appliances — contains silicon transistors. A modern smartphone contains over a billion transistors. The ability to switch electrical signals on/off at billions of times per second enables all digital computation, communication, and data storage. Without silicon semiconductors, there would be no internet, no digital technology, no modern computing.

Explain why arsenic (a metalloid) is extremely toxic despite being an element.Medium
Show Answer

Arsenic (As) interferes with cellular energy production. Arsenate (AsO43-) mimics phosphate in biochemical reactions but forms unstable compounds, uncoupling ATP synthesis (the cell's energy currency). This disrupts respiration at the cellular level. Additionally, arsenic binds to sulfur groups in enzymes, denaturing them. Even small chronic exposures (e.g., in contaminated groundwater) cause cancer, nerve damage, and cardiovascular disease.

Boron is a metalloid used in glass and ceramics. How do its intermediate properties make it useful?Medium
Show Answer

Boron oxide (B2O3) added to glass creates borosilicate glass (Pyrex). Boron's covalent bonds strengthen the glass network while its intermediate size fits into the silicate structure. The result: glass that withstands thermal shock (lab glassware, ovenproof dishes) due to a very low coefficient of thermal expansion — a direct result of boron's metalloid bonding characteristics.

How do solar cells (made of silicon) convert sunlight into electricity? Describe the basic principle.Medium
Show Answer

A solar cell consists of a p-n junction — doped silicon layers joined together. Incoming photons of sunlight knock electrons free in the silicon (photovoltaic effect). The p-n junction creates a built-in electric field that pushes freed electrons in one direction, creating a direct current (DC). The current is collected by metal contacts on the cell surface and flows through an external circuit.

Explain why germanium was used in early transistors but was largely replaced by silicon.Medium
Show Answer

Germanium's band gap (0.67 eV) is smaller than silicon's, making early transistors work at lower voltages. However, germanium: melts at a lower temperature (938°C vs. 1414°C for Si), making it less suitable for high-temperature processing; silicon dioxide (SiO2) forms an excellent insulating layer naturally on silicon during manufacturing, enabling more complex chips; silicon is also far more abundant and cheaper. Silicon's properties proved more suitable for scaled-up integrated circuit manufacture.

Antimony (a metalloid) is used in flame retardants. How does its metalloid nature make it suitable?Medium
Show Answer

Antimony trioxide (Sb2O3) acts as a synergist with halogen-based flame retardants. When a material containing Sb2O3 + halogen compounds burns, Sb2O3 reacts with HCl or HBr to form antimony halides (SbCl3, SbBr3) — dense vapours that dilute oxygen and cool the flame, interrupting the combustion chain. Antimony's metalloid character allows it to form stable but volatile halide compounds suited to this gas-phase flame suppression mechanism.

A student says: "Metalloids are simply metals that haven't decided yet." Evaluate this statement scientifically.Medium
Show Answer

This is scientifically inaccurate. Metalloids are not transitional or undecided — they are well-defined elements with specific electron configurations that give them genuinely intermediate and unique properties. Silicon is not a "confused" metal — its 4-valence-electron covalent semiconductor structure is precisely what makes it uniquely valuable. Classifying metalloids as failed or transitional metals misunderstands that property spectra are continuous, and the metalloid "category" describes a real and distinct subset of chemical behaviour.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain at the atomic level why adding carbon to iron makes steel stronger than pure iron.Medium
Show Answer

Carbon atoms are much smaller than iron atoms. They fit into the gaps (interstices) between iron atoms, distorting the lattice. This distortion locks the lattice planes in place — dislocations (slipping planes) cannot move as easily. A mere 0.2% carbon can double iron's strength — a dramatic example of small-composition effects on mechanical properties.

Why is stainless steel preferred over regular steel for surgical instruments?Medium
Show Answer

Surgical instruments must: (1) not corrode in contact with blood and body fluids; (2) withstand steam sterilisation (134°C); (3) not contaminate tissues; (4) maintain sharp edges. Regular steel rusts under these conditions; stainless steel's chromium oxide layer provides complete corrosion resistance while maintaining hardness.

Explain why bronze replaced copper for tools and weapons in ancient civilisations.Medium
Show Answer

Pure copper was too soft for effective tools and weapons — it deformed easily on impact. Bronze (copper + tin) is significantly harder and more durable due to lattice disruption. Bronze weapons held sharper edges longer and were stronger in combat, enabling the Bronze Age civilisations (~3,300–1,200 BCE).

Compare the suitability of brass and steel for making musical instrument bodies.Medium
Show Answer

Brass is preferred for wind instruments: it resonates with a rich tone at musical frequencies, is easily shaped and soldered, and resists corrosion from breath. Steel produces a harsher sound, is heavier, and is more difficult to form into thin-walled curved tubes. Acoustic properties of the alloy matter as much as mechanical ones.

Why are two different "grades" of steel (low-carbon and high-carbon) produced? Give one use for each.Medium
Show Answer

Low-carbon steel (0.05–0.25% C): relatively soft and ductile, easy to weld — used for car body panels and structural beams. High-carbon steel (0.6–2.1% C): very hard but more brittle — used for cutting tools, knife blades, and springs. Carbon content controls the hardness/ductility trade-off.

A silversmith uses sterling silver instead of pure silver for rings. Explain why, using physical properties of each.Medium
Show Answer

Pure silver is too soft (hardness ~2.5 Mohs) — it would scratch and deform with normal wear. Sterling silver (92.5% Ag + 7.5% Cu) is significantly harder and more wear-resistant. Copper atoms disrupt silver's lattice, increasing hardness while maintaining the silvery appearance and workability needed for jewellery making.

Why is aluminium alloyed with copper and magnesium (duralumin) for aircraft construction rather than using pure aluminium?Medium
Show Answer

Pure aluminium is relatively soft and not strong enough for aircraft structural members. Duralumin's copper and magnesium additions disrupt the aluminium lattice, significantly increasing yield strength and hardness while keeping the alloy's low density. The result is a material with a strength-to-weight ratio suitable for aircraft frames — something pure aluminium cannot provide.

Explain what "age hardening" is, using duralumin as an example.Medium
Show Answer

Age hardening involves dissolving Cu and Mg in aluminium at high temperature, then rapidly quenching (cooling) to trap them. Over time, fine precipitate particles form within the lattice, pinning dislocation movement. Duralumin's strength actually increases over 24–96 hours after quenching — enabling the high strength-to-weight ratio critical for aircraft use.

How does an alloy differ from a compound? Use steel vs. iron carbide as an example.Medium
Show Answer

An alloy is a physical mixture — atoms of different elements mixed in variable proportions, no fixed formula, properties intermediate and adjustable. A compound has a fixed ratio and distinct properties (e.g., iron carbide, Fe3C, has a specific formula and crystal structure). In low-carbon steel, carbon is dissolved in iron (alloy); in high-carbon steel, Fe3C precipitates form (a compound within the alloy).

Why is 24-carat gold (pure gold) not used for coins or rings, even though it is the most valuable form?Medium
Show Answer

24-carat gold is pure gold — extremely soft and malleable. It would scratch, dent, and deform with everyday use in coins or rings. Gold alloys (18-carat = 75% gold + 25% other metals like copper or silver) are harder, more durable, and hold shape better. The alloying reduces value per gram but dramatically increases practical usefulness and longevity.

Critically analyse: Are high-entropy alloys (HEAs) the future of materials science? What advantages and barriers exist?Hard
Show Answer

HEAs contain 5+ elements in near-equal proportions (e.g., CoCrFeMnNi). Advantages: exceptional combinations of strength AND ductility at cryogenic and high temperatures; radiation resistance (nuclear applications); oxidation resistance. Traditional alloys are limited by one dominant element. HEAs exploit multi-element interactions to create novel properties. Evaluate the transition from steel to carbon fibre composites in automotive and aerospace manufacturing. Is it environmentally beneficial overall?Hard
Show Answer

Recyclability: carbon fibre is difficult to recycle structurally — most shredded for low-value filler. Lifecycle assessment: CO2 savings in use phase exceed manufacturing cost after ~80,000–100,000 km in cars; better justified in aviation (long service life). Analyse how the microstructure of an alloy (grain size, phase composition) determines its macroscopic mechanical properties.Hard
Show Answer

Grain size: smaller grains → more grain boundaries → impede dislocation movement → greater strength (Hall-Petch relationship: strength ∝ 1/√d). Phase composition: multi-phase alloys (martensite + bainite in steel) — martensite provides hardness, bainite provides toughness. Heat treatment controls both. Modern electron microscopy (EBSD) directly images microstructure, enabling precise property engineering from atomic scale upward — the core method of modern metallurgy.

Critically evaluate: Can biodegradable metals (magnesium alloys for medical implants) revolutionise surgery? What are the risks?Hard
Show Answer

Magnesium alloys corrode gradually in body fluids, releasing Mg2+ (non-toxic, promotes bone healing), and completely dissolve in weeks to months — eliminating second surgeries to remove implants. Benefits: reduced trauma, cost savings. Risks: corrosion rate must precisely match healing — too fast → mechanical failure before bone heals; H2 gas bubbles from Mg corrosion can cause inflammation. Alloy composition (adding Zn, Sr) fine-tunes degradation rate. Clinical trials (2024–2025) show promising results; widespread adoption likely within 5–10 years, but careful patient-specific rate matching remains a challenge.

Investigate: Compare the density and strength-to-weight ratio of steel, aluminium alloy, and titanium alloy. What does this reveal about material selection for aerospace?Hard
Show Answer

Steel: density ~8 g/cm3, yield strength ~500 MPa, specific strength ~62 kN·m/kg. Al alloy (7075): density ~2.8 g/cm3, strength ~500 MPa, specific strength ~179 kN·m/kg. Ti alloy (Ti-6Al-4V): density ~4.4 g/cm3, strength ~900 MPa, specific strength ~205 kN·m/kg. Titanium has the best specific strength but is ~5x more expensive than aluminium. Aerospace uses aluminium alloy for fuselage skin (cost-effective), titanium for engine components (temperature + strength), and steel for undercarriage (high load, lower cost). No single alloy is optimal — selection is always application-specific.

Evaluate: How does the development of superalloys (nickel-based alloys used in jet engines) illustrate the principles of alloy design?Hard
Show Answer

Jet engine turbine blades must operate at >1000°C under extreme mechanical stress and oxidising conditions. Nickel superalloys (Ni + Cr, Co, Al, Ti, W, Re) achieve this through: (1) solid solution strengthening (multiple elements in Ni lattice); (2) precipitation hardening (gamma-prime Ni3Al precipitates); (3) oxide dispersion (fine Al2O3 particles block dislocation movement); (4) thermal barrier coatings (ceramic top-coat). Each component of the alloy is designed to address a specific failure mode. Superalloy development illustrates that alloy design is not empirical trial-and-error but systematic application of materials science principles — a triumph of applied chemistry and physics.

Critically evaluate the statement: "An alloy is always better than the pure metals it contains."Hard
Show Answer

The statement is an overgeneralisation. Alloys are better than pure metals for specific target properties — hardness, corrosion resistance, or strength-to-weight ratio — but they often sacrifice other properties. Steel is harder than iron but less ductile and harder to forge. Duralumin is stronger than pure aluminium but has reduced corrosion resistance (copper promotes galvanic corrosion). Gold alloys are harder than pure gold but may tarnish (copper turns green). The more accurate statement is: alloys provide targeted property improvements for specific applications, at the potential cost of other properties. Alloy selection always involves trade-offs.

MYP Criterion D: Discuss the social, economic, and environmental impact of the global steel industry. Is steel production compatible with sustainable development?Hard
Show Answer

Social: steel industries employ millions globally; steel infrastructure enables modern development (housing, transport, water supply). Economic: ~$900 billion/year industry. Environmental: steel production (~8% of global CO2 emissions) uses iron ore mining (habitat destruction), coal (emissions), and vast water supplies. Sustainable development challenge: demand is rising (urbanisation in Asia, Africa) while decarbonisation pressure increases. Mitigation: hydrogen-based direct reduction (green steel — using H2 instead of coke), electric arc furnaces using recycled scrap (already 30% of production), carbon capture at blast furnaces. What is an ore? Give one example.Easy

An ore is a naturally occurring rock or mineral containing a metal or metal compound in sufficient concentration to be economically extracted. Example: haematite (Fe2O3) is the ore of iron; bauxite (Al2O3.2H2O) is the ore of aluminium.

Name three environmental impacts of metal mining.Easy

Why is recycling aluminium more energy-efficient than producing it from ore?Easy
Show Answer

Recycled aluminium uses only about 5% of the energy needed to produce primary (new) aluminium from bauxite ore. This is because electrolysis (used for primary production) is very energy-intensive; recycling only requires melting the metal down.

What is "precious metals recovery" and why is it important?Easy
Show Answer

Precious metals recovery is the extraction of valuable metals (gold, silver, platinum, palladium) from electronic waste (e-waste) such as old phones, computers, and circuit boards. What is the circular economy principle as applied to metals?Easy
Show Answer

The circular economy designs products to keep metals in use as long as possible: collect and recycle scrap metal, use recycled metal in new products, design for disassembly. This reduces mining demand, energy use, and environmental impact compared to the "extract → use → discard" linear model.

Why is gold mining in the Amazon rainforest particularly harmful?Easy
Show Answer

Gold mining in the Amazon: (1) destroys rainforest — deforestation for mining pits; (2) mercury pollution — mercury used in gold extraction contaminates rivers and poisons fish and indigenous communities; (3) displaces and harms indigenous peoples living on the land.

Why is titanium expensive despite being common in Earth's crust?Easy
Show Answer

Titanium is the 9th most abundant element but is difficult to extract. The Kroll process (TiO2 + chlorine → TiCl4, then TiCl4 + magnesium → Ti) is energy-intensive, slow, and expensive. The high processing cost — not scarcity — makes titanium expensive.

Name two "rare earth metals" and state one use each.Easy
Show Answer

Neodymium (Nd): used in powerful permanent magnets (electric vehicle motors, wind turbines). Europium (Eu): used as a red phosphor in LED screens and energy-saving lights. Rare earth metals are essential for green energy technologies.

What percentage of energy is saved when steel is recycled compared to producing it from ore?Easy
Show Answer

Recycling steel uses about 75% less energy than producing primary steel from iron ore in a blast furnace. About 25% of the primary production energy is needed for recycling via electric arc furnace.

Name one environmental benefit of reducing metal consumption in product design.Easy

Reducing metal use in products means less mining, which causes less habitat destruction, water pollution, and CO2 emissions from ore extraction and smelting. Lighter products (e.g., thinner aluminium cans) also use less fuel in transport — a secondary benefit.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain why aluminium was once more valuable than gold, and how electrolysis changed this.Medium
Show Answer

Before 1886, aluminium could only be extracted by expensive chemical reduction, making it rarer than gold. The Washington Monument apex was capped with aluminium as a luxury metal. The Hall-Heroult electrolysis process (1886) enabled cheap production from abundant bauxite. Within years, aluminium's price fell ~100-fold, democratising its use in packaging, transport, and construction.

Explain how iron is extracted from haematite in a blast furnace. Include the key chemical reactions.Medium
Show Answer

Haematite (Fe2O3) + coke + limestone heated with hot air blast. C + O2 → CO2; CO2 + C → 2CO. Fe2O3 + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO2 (CO reduces iron oxide). Limestone decomposes: CaCO3 → CaO + CO2; CaO + SiO2 → CaSiO3 (slag removes impurities). Liquid iron collects at the bottom.

Why is recycling electronic waste (e-waste) described as "urban mining"? What metals can be recovered?Medium
Show Answer

E-waste (old phones, computers) contains higher concentrations of precious metals than most natural ores — gold (~350 g/tonne of e-waste vs. 5 g/tonne of gold ore), silver, copper, palladium, and cobalt. Recovering metals from e-waste is called "urban mining" — treating cities as mines of already-processed materials. It reduces need for new primary mining.

Discuss the ethical issues associated with cobalt mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) and its link to EV battery production.Medium

~70% of world cobalt comes from DRC. Issues: child labour in artisanal mines, unsafe working conditions, inadequate wages, and environmental contamination. Responses: battery manufacturers shifting to cobalt-free chemistries (LFP batteries), blockchain traceability, and "responsible sourcing" programmes. Consumer choice and corporate due diligence can drive change.

Apply the reactivity series to explain why gold and platinum are found as native (pure) elements, while iron is always found as an ore.Medium
Show Answer

Gold and platinum are at the very bottom of the reactivity series — they do not combine with oxygen, water, or acids over geological time. They occur as pure metallic nuggets. Iron (high in the series) readily oxidises to iron oxides and sulphides — hence always found as ore compounds like haematite (Fe2O3), never as pure metal.

Explain how electrolysis is used to purify copper. Why is high purity essential for electrical use?Medium
Show Answer

Impure copper (99%) is used as the anode; pure copper as the cathode; copper sulphate as electrolyte. Impure anode dissolves (Cu → Cu2+ + 2e-); pure copper deposits on the cathode (Cu2+ + 2e- → Cu). Impurities fall as "anode slime." Result: 99.99% pure copper. High purity is essential because even 0.1% impurities can reduce electrical conductivity by ~20% — critical for efficient power transmission.

1) Collect aluminium cans for recycling — saves 95% of manufacturing energy. 2) Return old phones for e-waste recycling — recovers gold, silver, copper, and cobalt. 3) Use stainless steel water bottles instead of single-use cans — reduces total metal demand through longevity. These actions directly reduce mining demand and lower carbon footprint.

Evaluate why deep-sea mining has been proposed to address metal scarcity, and state two environmental concerns.Medium

(2) Sediment plumes from mining could affect filter-feeding organisms for hundreds of km, disrupting food chains. MYP Criterion D: Critically evaluate whether the global "Green Energy Transition" (solar, wind, EVs) is creating a new form of resource colonialism in metal-rich developing nations.Hard
Show Answer

The green energy transition requires massive increases in cobalt (Congo), lithium (Bolivia, Chile, Argentina), copper (Chile, Peru), and rare earths (China, Myanmar). Phosphorus is an essential non-metal for all life (DNA, ATP, bones), mined from finite phosphate rock. Analyse whether phosphorus depletion poses a greater long-term threat than metal depletion, and what responses are needed.Hard
Show Answer
