SET A Recall – Living vs Non-Living

Name any four characteristics that all living things share.
Show Answer

Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition (any four from MRS GREN).

Easy
Is a stone living or non-living? Give one reason.
Show Answer

Non-living. A stone has never been alive and does not carry out any life processes.

Easy
Give two examples of things that were once alive but are no longer considered living.
Show Answer

Paper (made from wood/trees) and coal (formed from ancient plant matter).

Easy
What is meant by the term "non-living"?
Show Answer

Non-living things are objects that do not carry out life processes. They have either never been alive (e.g., rock) or were once living (e.g., dead wood).

Easy
List three examples of living things and three examples of non-living things.
Show Answer

Living: dog, mango tree, mushroom. Non-living: water, rock, plastic bottle (any valid examples accepted).

Easy
Can a living thing grow? Give an example.
Show Answer

Yes. A seed grows into a plant; a puppy grows into a dog.

Easy
Do non-living things reproduce? Explain briefly.
Show Answer

No. Non-living things cannot produce offspring. Reproduction is exclusive to living organisms.

Easy
Why is fire sometimes confused for a living thing?
Show Answer

Fire moves, grows, needs oxygen (like respiration), and produces waste gases. However, it cannot reproduce, respond to stimuli in a purposeful way, or maintain a cellular structure, so it is non-living.

Easy
Name the process by which living things get rid of waste products.
Show Answer

Excretion.

Easy
Is a virus considered living or non-living? State one reason for each side of the debate.
Show Answer

Debated: For living – viruses reproduce and carry genetic material. For non-living – viruses are not made of cells and cannot carry out metabolism independently.

SET B Apply – Living vs Non-Living

A student says a car is living because it moves and needs fuel. Identify two errors in this reasoning.
Show Answer

1. A car's movement is not self-generated from internal biological processes. 2. A car cannot reproduce or grow, so it fails the full set of life criteria.

Medium
Classify the following as living, once-living, or never-living: a wooden table, a breathing fish, coal, a plastic toy.
Show Answer

Wooden table – once-living. Fish – living. Coal – once-living. Plastic toy – never-living.

Medium
Explain why a seed buried in dry soil is considered living even though it shows no visible activity.
Show Answer

The seed is in a dormant state but still carries out very slow cellular respiration and retains the potential to germinate, reproduce, and grow. Its cells are alive.

Medium
A student lists these as characteristics of a robot: moves, senses, needs energy. Why is the robot still non-living?
Show Answer

A robot cannot reproduce, does not grow, does not carry out cellular respiration or excretion, and is not made of living cells. It requires human-made programming, not biological processes.

Medium
How is coal evidence that non-living things can be formed from once-living material?
Show Answer

Coal formed over millions of years from compressed ancient plant and animal matter. The organisms that produced it were once alive; the resulting coal is non-living but retains stored chemical energy from those organisms.

Medium
Give an example of a living thing that does NOT visibly move and explain how it still qualifies as living.
Show Answer

A cactus appears motionless but carries out photosynthesis (nutrition), grows, reproduces (flowers/seeds), respires, excretes waste, and responds slowly to light. It satisfies all seven MRS GREN criteria.

Medium
Why must a living thing carry out ALL seven life processes and not just some?
Show Answer

Each process is essential for survival. Missing even one (e.g., no excretion leads to toxic build-up; no reproduction means species extinction). All seven together define life as a complete functional system.

Medium
Compare how a living plant and a non-living crystal both "grow". How is their growth fundamentally different?
Show Answer

A plant grows by cell division and differentiation from within (intrinsic growth). A crystal grows by the addition of molecules to its outer surface from the environment (extrinsic accretion). Plant growth is biological; crystal growth is a physical/chemical process.

Medium
Identify the odd one out and explain: Amoeba, Oak tree, River, Mushroom.
Show Answer

River – it is non-living. Amoeba, oak tree, and mushroom are all living organisms that carry out life processes; a river is a body of water with no biological life processes of its own.

Medium
A dead leaf falls from a tree. Is the leaf living or non-living? Justify your answer.
Show Answer

Non-living (or once-living). Once detached and dead, the leaf's cells no longer carry out metabolism. It was once living as part of the tree but no longer satisfies any of the MRS GREN criteria.

Hard
A classmate argues that soil is living because plants grow in it. Construct a scientific counter-argument and also acknowledge any validity in their claim.
Show Answer

Counter-argument: Soil itself (mineral particles, water, air) is non-living. Plants do not grow "from" soil the way offspring come from parents. Validity: Soil contains billions of living microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) that make it biologically active. The soil ecosystem is living; the mineral component is not.

Hard
Evaluate the statement: "The seven characteristics of life are a perfect definition of what it means to be alive." Use evidence to support and challenge this claim.
Show Answer

Support: The seven characteristics (MRS GREN) apply universally to all known organisms. Challenge: Viruses reproduce and have DNA but are not cellular. Mules cannot reproduce but are biologically alive. Scientists continue to debate edge cases, suggesting the definition, while useful, is not absolute.

Hard
MYP Criterion D: "Should scientists spend more resources searching for life on other planets than solving biodiversity loss on Earth?" Evaluate this using scientific reasoning.
Show Answer

For space search: Discovering extraterrestrial life would redefine biology and could yield solutions not imagined on Earth. Against: Earth is losing species at 1,000× natural background rates; once extinct, species are gone forever. Balanced view: Both are scientifically valid, but the urgency of Earth's biodiversity crisis demands immediate priority. Long-term investment in astrobiology can continue in parallel without diverting core conservation funding.

Hard
You observe something under a microscope that has a cell membrane and DNA but does not divide. Is it living? Explain the scientific uncertainty here.
Show Answer

A cell membrane and DNA suggest it could be living (or once-living). If it cannot divide, it may be a dead cell, a dormant cell, or a highly specialised terminal cell (like a red blood cell). Scientific uncertainty exists because lack of one trait (reproduction) does not automatically mean non-living — context, age, and state must be considered.

Create a classification table comparing fire, a robot, a virus, and a bacterium against all seven MRS GREN criteria. What conclusions can you draw?
Show Answer

Fire: Movement✓, Respiration~(oxidation), Sensitivity✗, Growth~(spreads), Reproduction✗, Excretion~(CO₂), Nutrition~. Robot: M✓(powered), R✗, S✓(sensors), G✗, Rep✗, Ex✗, N✗. Hard
A scientist discovers a substance on Mars that moves toward light and releases gas bubbles. Is this enough evidence to declare it living? What further tests would you recommend?
Show Answer

Not sufficient. Movement toward light could be a physical phenomenon (e.g., thermotaxis in crystals). Further tests: Check for cellular structure (microscopy), test for self-reproduction, test for DNA/RNA, measure metabolic reactions (oxygen consumption), and observe response to environmental stimuli over time.

Hard
Analyse why classifying once-living things (paper, coal) as separate from never-living things matters scientifically.
Show Answer

Once-living things contain organic molecules (carbon compounds, complex polymers) that originated from biological processes. They store chemical energy (coal is burned as fuel), contain evolutionary information, and decompose biologically. Never-living things (metals, minerals) have no organic origin. This distinction affects ecology, geology, carbon cycle studies, and energy science.

SET A Recall – MRS GREN

What does the acronym MRS GREN stand for?
Show Answer

Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition.

Easy
Define respiration as a life process (not breathing).
Show Answer

Respiration is the chemical process inside cells that releases energy from glucose for use in life processes. Equation: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy.

Easy
What is sensitivity in living things? Give one example.
Show Answer

Sensitivity is the ability to detect and respond to changes in the environment (stimuli). Example: a plant bending toward light (phototropism); a human pulling hand away from a hot object.

Easy
What is excretion? Why is it important?
Show Answer

Excretion is the removal of waste products produced by chemical reactions inside the body. It is important because waste (e.g., urea, CO₂) is toxic if it builds up in cells.

Easy
Name the two types of reproduction in living things.
Show Answer

Sexual reproduction (involves two parents and fertilisation) and asexual reproduction (involves one parent, e.g., budding, binary fission).

Easy
What is nutrition in the context of MRS GREN?
Show Answer

Nutrition is the process by which organisms obtain and use food/energy. Plants make their own food via photosynthesis (autotrophs); animals eat other organisms (heterotrophs).

Easy
Is movement in plants the same as movement in animals? Explain briefly.
Show Answer

No. Animals move their whole bodies (locomotion). Plants show growth movements (e.g., roots growing down, stems growing toward light) but cannot move from place to place.

Easy
Define growth as a characteristic of living things.
Show Answer

Growth is a permanent increase in size and mass due to the production of new cells through cell division. It is controlled from within the organism.

Easy
Give one example of excretion in plants and one in animals.
Show Answer

Plants: release oxygen as a waste product of photosynthesis; store waste in leaves that are shed. Animals: kidneys excrete urea in urine; lungs excrete carbon dioxide.

Easy
How many characteristics must an organism show to be classified as living?
Show Answer

All seven (MRS GREN). Showing fewer does not qualify an organism as living.

Easy

SET B Apply – MRS GREN

A tree absorbs water and minerals, makes glucose, and releases oxygen. Which THREE MRS GREN processes are described here?
Show Answer

Nutrition (absorbing water/minerals and making glucose via photosynthesis), Excretion (releasing oxygen as waste), Respiration (using glucose for energy — implied by cellular function).

Medium
Why is breathing NOT the same as respiration in the MRS GREN context?
Show Answer

Breathing (ventilation) is the physical movement of air in and out of lungs — a mechanical process. Respiration is the chemical reaction inside cells that releases energy from glucose. They are related but distinct processes.

Medium
Map each MRS GREN letter to a specific example in a frog's life.
Show Answer

M – frog leaps to catch prey; R – cells break down glucose for energy; S – detects vibrations/predators; G – tadpole grows into adult frog; R – lays eggs in water (reproduction); E – excretes urea in urine; N – eats insects for nutrition.

Medium
Explain how a cactus satisfies the "sensitivity" criterion even though it cannot run away from danger.
Show Answer

A cactus responds to stimuli: its roots grow toward water (hydrotropism), its stem grows toward light (phototropism), and it closes stomata in the heat to prevent water loss — all responses to environmental changes.

Medium
A student confuses egestion (removal of undigested food) with excretion. Clarify the difference.
Show Answer

Excretion removes metabolic waste produced by chemical reactions inside cells (e.g., urea, CO₂). Egestion removes undigested material that was never absorbed into cells (e.g., faeces). Only excretion is a MRS GREN life process.

Medium
How does asexual reproduction in bacteria illustrate the "R" (Reproduction) in MRS GREN?
Show Answer

Bacteria reproduce by binary fission — one cell splits into two identical cells. This is asexual (one parent, no fertilisation) and occurs rapidly, sometimes every 20 minutes. It satisfies the reproduction criterion without sexual partners.

Medium
A plant in a dark room still grows but looks pale and thin (etiolated). Which MRS GREN criterion is being most severely affected? Explain.
Show Answer

Nutrition is most affected. Without light, photosynthesis cannot occur fully, so the plant cannot make adequate glucose. Growth continues using stored starch, but the plant is nutritionally starved, leading to pale, elongated growth.

Medium
Explain why respiration must occur continuously, even when an organism is sleeping.
Show Answer

Cells require energy constantly to maintain basic functions: heart beating, brain activity, maintaining body temperature, cell repair. Without continuous respiration, cells would run out of ATP and die within minutes.

Medium
Some organisms reproduce only once in their lifetime (e.g., Pacific salmon). Does this mean they fail the reproduction criterion? Justify your answer.
Show Answer

No. The criterion requires that the organism has the capacity to reproduce — not that it must do so repeatedly. Pacific salmon reproduce once (semelparous), produce thousands of eggs, and die. The criterion is satisfied.

Medium
How does a Venus flytrap demonstrate sensitivity and nutrition together?
Show Answer

Sensitivity: tiny trigger hairs inside the trap detect the touch of an insect (stimulus → response: trap closes). Nutrition: the trapped insect is digested and absorbed to provide nitrogen nutrients, supplementing what poor soil cannot supply.

Medium

Design an investigation to demonstrate that plants carry out respiration at night. State your hypothesis, variables, and expected results.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Plants respire at night, producing CO₂. Method: Place a healthy plant in a sealed transparent bag in complete darkness for 8 hours; include a CO₂ indicator (limewater or CO₂ probe). Hard
Evaluate whether MRS GREN is a universally accepted definition of life. What are its limitations for classifying edge cases like prions and dormant tardigrades?
Show Answer

Limitations: Prions are misfolded proteins that "reproduce" by converting normal proteins but have no metabolism or cells. Dormant tardigrades (water bears) suspend nearly all MRS GREN functions but revive — do they temporarily become non-living? MRS GREN is a useful teaching framework, not an absolute scientific definition. Biologists increasingly focus on criteria like metabolism, homeostasis, and evolutionary capacity.

Hard
Create a controlled experiment to test the effect of temperature on the rate of respiration in yeast. Identify all variables.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Higher temperature (up to ~35°C) increases yeast respiration rate. Method: Set up 5 identical flasks (yeast + glucose solution) at 10°C, 20°C, 30°C, 40°C, 50°C. Measure CO₂ produced per minute with a gas syringe. IV: temperature. DV: CO₂ production rate. Controlled variables: yeast mass, glucose concentration, flask size, time measured. Expected: rate increases to ~35°C then drops as enzymes denature above 40°C.

Hard
MYP Criterion D: "The needs of farmers in developing countries to clear forests for nutrition are more important than protecting biodiversity." Evaluate this statement using MRS GREN and ethical reasoning.
Show Answer

Scientific link: Humans satisfy "nutrition" by farming — a basic MRS GREN need. Deforestation destroys habitats of countless species, disrupting their nutrition and survival. Ethical perspectives: Farmers' food security is a human rights issue; species' survival is an ecological responsibility. Balanced conclusion: Sustainable farming, agroforestry, and government support can satisfy human nutritional needs without mass deforestation — the two goals need not be mutually exclusive.

Hard
A classmate says "animals are better at MRS GREN than plants because they can move." Critically evaluate this statement.
Show Answer

Flawed reasoning. MRS GREN is not a competition — either an organism satisfies all criteria or it doesn't. Plants have sophisticated forms of movement (tropisms), respiration, sensitivity (photoreceptors), growth, reproduction, excretion, and nutrition (photosynthesis). Animals and plants satisfy MRS GREN equally; their strategies differ but neither is "better."

Hard
Analyse how disrupting ONE MRS GREN process can cascade and affect all others. Use a specific organism as an example.
Show Answer

Example: A frog loses the ability to excrete (kidney failure). Toxic urea builds up in blood, poisoning cells → respiration rate drops (less energy) → sensitivity decreases (nerve damage) → movement impaired → nutrition intake drops → growth stops → reproduction ceases → organism dies. One failed process creates a cascade failure across all life functions.

Hard
Investigate: How would you test whether woodlice prefer damp or dry environments, and which MRS GREN criterion does this test relate to?
Show Answer

Method: Choice chamber with damp cotton on one side and dry cotton on the other. Place 10 woodlice in the middle; count how many move to each side after 10 minutes. Repeat 3 times. This tests Sensitivity (response to humidity stimulus) and also relates to Nutrition/survival conditions. Expected: woodlice move to damp side (they need moisture to respire through gills).

Hard
Compare growth in a unicellular organism (amoeba) with growth in a multicellular organism (human). How does the concept of "G" in MRS GREN apply differently?
Show Answer

Amoeba: growth means the single cell enlarges before dividing — the whole organism is one cell. Human: growth involves trillions of cells dividing, differentiating into specialised types, and organising into tissues and organs. In both cases growth is intrinsic and biological, but in multicellular organisms it is far more complex, regulated by hormones and genetic programmes.

Hard
Evaluate the importance of excretion for maintaining homeostasis. What would happen at a cellular level if excretion stopped?
Show Answer

Homeostasis requires constant internal balance. Without excretion: CO₂ builds up → blood becomes acidic (pH drops) → enzymes denature → metabolic reactions fail. Urea builds up → poisoning of nerve and kidney cells. Excess water is not removed → cells swell and burst (cytolysis). Ultimately, cells cannot maintain the stable internal environment needed for all biochemical reactions.

Hard
Research extension: Some deep-sea organisms have never been exposed to sunlight. How do they satisfy the "Nutrition" (N) criterion without photosynthesis? What does this tell us about the diversity of life?
Show Answer

Deep-sea organisms use chemosynthesis (bacteria around hydrothermal vents oxidise hydrogen sulphide for energy) or eat other organisms (heterotrophy). This shows that nutrition does not require sunlight — life has evolved multiple energy-capture strategies. It broadens our definition of habitable environments, including implications for life on other planets (e.g., Europa's ocean).

SET A Recall – Unicellular vs Multicellular

What is a unicellular organism? Give two examples.
Show Answer

An organism made of only one cell. Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, bacteria (e.g., E. coli).

Easy
What is a multicellular organism? Give two examples.
Show Answer

An organism made of many cells. Examples: Human, oak tree, mushroom, elephant.

Easy
How does a unicellular organism carry out all life functions?
Show Answer

A single cell performs all life functions — digestion, respiration, excretion, movement, and reproduction — within one cell. No division of labour between cells exists.

Easy
What is cell specialisation?
Show Answer

Cell specialisation is when cells develop specific structures and functions to perform particular tasks in a multicellular organism (e.g., muscle cells for contraction, nerve cells for signals, red blood cells for oxygen transport).

Easy
Name three types of specialised cells found in the human body.
Show Answer

Muscle cells, nerve cells (neurons), red blood cells (erythrocytes), white blood cells, sperm cells (any three).

Easy
Is bacteria unicellular or multicellular?
Show Answer

Unicellular. Each bacterium is a single prokaryotic cell.

Easy
What is the correct order of organisation in a multicellular organism, from smallest to largest?
Show Answer

Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ system → Organism.

Easy
How does an amoeba move?
Show Answer

An amoeba moves using pseudopodia — temporary extensions of its cytoplasm that flow outward, pulling the rest of the cell along.

Easy
Name one advantage of being multicellular.
Show Answer

Cell specialisation allows different cells to perform different functions more efficiently, enabling the organism to grow larger, have a longer lifespan, and perform complex functions (e.g., thinking, running).

Easy
What is a paramecium and how does it feed?
Show Answer

A paramecium is a unicellular protist. It feeds by sweeping bacteria and food particles into an oral groove using hair-like cilia; food is then engulfed in a food vacuole and digested.

Easy

SET B Apply – Unicellular vs Multicellular

Compare how a unicellular amoeba and a multicellular rabbit each obtain nutrition.
Show Answer

Amoeba: the single cell engulfs food particles directly using phagocytosis (pseudopodia wrap around prey); digestion occurs in food vacuoles within the same cell. Rabbit: specialised cells in the digestive system (stomach, intestine) work together — different organs handle ingestion, digestion, absorption, and excretion, with nutrients transported via blood to all body cells.

Medium
Explain why unicellular organisms are limited in size compared to multicellular organisms.
Show Answer

A single cell must exchange all materials (oxygen, nutrients, waste) across its surface. As a cell gets bigger, the surface area to volume ratio decreases, making exchange inefficient. Multicellular organisms solve this with specialised organs (lungs, intestines, kidneys) that massively increase exchange surfaces.

Medium
A nerve cell has long extensions (axons) and a red blood cell has no nucleus. How does each adaptation suit its function?
Show Answer

Nerve cell: long axons transmit electrical signals rapidly over long distances to muscles and other nerves. Red blood cell: no nucleus creates more space for haemoglobin, maximising oxygen-carrying capacity; biconcave shape increases surface area for gas exchange.

Medium
Describe how a bacterium (unicellular) reproduces and compare it to how a human (multicellular) reproduces.
Show Answer

Bacterium: binary fission — one cell copies its DNA and splits into two identical daughter cells. Asexual; very fast (every 20 min). Human: sexual reproduction — sperm and egg (specialised cells) fuse at fertilisation; the zygote divides and develops into a foetus over 9 months. Much slower; produces genetically unique offspring.

Medium
If a single liver cell is removed from a human, can it survive and function independently? Explain.
Show Answer

In controlled lab conditions, liver cells can survive briefly in culture media but cannot perform the full range of liver functions alone (detoxification, bile production, etc.). They are specialised and interdependent; they rely on other cell types for oxygen, nutrients, and hormonal signals. They cannot survive long-term independently.

Medium
Why are unicellular organisms considered evolutionarily successful despite their simplicity?
Show Answer

Unicellular organisms (bacteria) are the most abundant life forms on Earth. They reproduce extremely rapidly, can mutate and evolve quickly, survive extreme environments (hot springs, deep sea), and have persisted for ~3.5 billion years. Their simplicity is not a weakness — it confers resilience and adaptability.

Medium
How does Volvox represent a transitional stage between unicellular and multicellular life?
Show Answer

Volvox is a colonial green alga — thousands of individual cells live together in a hollow sphere. Some cells specialise (reproductive cells vs. somatic cells), showing early division of labour. It demonstrates how single cells can cooperate, representing an evolutionary stepping stone to true multicellularity.

Medium
Explain the concept of "division of labour" in multicellular organisms and its advantage.
Show Answer

Division of labour means different cells specialise in different tasks (muscle cells → movement; nerve cells → signalling; epithelial cells → protection). Each cell type is highly efficient at its specific job. This allows the organism to perform complex functions simultaneously and at a scale impossible for a single cell.

Medium
Why can bacteria cause infections in multicellular animals but not the other way around?
Show Answer

Bacteria are independent unicellular organisms with all machinery to reproduce rapidly. They invade host cells, compete for nutrients, and produce toxins. Animal cells cannot infect bacteria because animal cells lack the independent survival mechanisms and cannot penetrate bacterial cell walls.

Medium
Compare how a unicellular organism and a multicellular organism respond to a harmful chemical in their environment.
Show Answer

Unicellular (e.g., amoeba): the whole organism detects and moves away from the chemical using chemotaxis, or it may die — there is no protection from other cells. Multicellular (e.g., human): skin acts as a barrier; immune cells neutralise toxins; liver detoxifies chemicals in blood; the organism can respond at multiple levels simultaneously.

Medium

SET C Investigate & Evaluate – Unicellular vs Multicellular

Design an investigation to observe unicellular organisms in pond water. State your method, safety precautions, and how you would identify living unicellular organisms.
Show Answer

Method: Collect 10 mL pond water. Place a drop on a microscope slide, add a coverslip, observe under low power then high power. Look for organisms showing movement, feeding, or response to light. Safety: wash hands, do not mouth-pipette, dispose of slide carefully. Hard
Evaluate the claim: "Unicellular organisms are inferior to multicellular organisms." Use scientific evidence to argue for and against.
Show Answer

Against (unicellular not inferior): Bacteria have survived all five mass extinctions; they can be found in every habitat on Earth; they process more biomass than any other group; some are extremophiles surviving conditions lethal to multicellular life. For multicellular advantage: complexity allows intelligence, size, and longer lifespan. Conclusion: "inferior" is value-laden; evolutionarily, unicellular organisms are extraordinarily successful.

Hard
A scientist proposes that the first living thing on Earth was unicellular. What evidence supports this and what does it imply about the evolution of multicellular life?
Show Answer

Evidence: Fossil record shows prokaryote microfossils (~3.5 billion years ago) predating any multicellular fossils (~600 million years ago); ribosomal RNA analysis shows all life shares a common unicellular ancestor; simplest life forms (bacteria) are unicellular. Implication: multicellular life evolved from unicellular ancestors multiple times (plants, animals, fungi evolved independently from protist ancestors), suggesting complexity is not inevitable but emerges given sufficient time and selection pressure.

Hard
MYP Criterion D: "In a world with antibiotic-resistant bacteria, should more funding go toward understanding unicellular biology than developing new medicines?" Evaluate this ethical and scientific dilemma.
Show Answer

Scientific case for understanding unicellular biology: Antibiotic resistance arises from rapid bacterial evolution; understanding gene transfer, mutation rates, and biofilm formation could prevent resistance entirely. Medical case for new medicines: millions die from antibiotic-resistant infections now; new drugs provide immediate relief. Ethical tension: basic science vs. applied medicine. Balanced view: both are necessary — fundamental research (unicellular biology) enables future breakthroughs, while applied research saves current lives. Funding cuts to either compromise humanity's long-term health security.

Hard
Analyse how cancer represents a breakdown of the multicellular "contract" — the idea that cells cooperate for the good of the organism.
Show Answer

In healthy multicellular organisms, cells follow genetic rules: divide only when needed, differentiate to serve organism needs, self-destruct when damaged (apoptosis). Cancer cells mutate and "defect" — they divide uncontrollably, ignore signals to stop, invade other tissues, and redirect resources for their own growth. This is analogous to cells "behaving selfishly" rather than cooperating, undermining the organism's survival.

Hard
Compare the advantages and disadvantages of asexual reproduction (as seen in unicellular organisms) versus sexual reproduction (multicellular organisms) in terms of survival and adaptation.
Show Answer

Asexual: Fast, no need for a mate, every individual reproduces, creates genetically identical clones. Disadvantage: no genetic variation — one disease can wipe out the whole population. Sexual: Genetic variation through recombination creates diverse offspring; populations can adapt to changing environments. Disadvantage: slower, requires two parents, only half the population reproduces (females). In stable environments, asexual is efficient; in changing environments, sexual reproduction provides evolutionary resilience.

Hard
Investigate: Design an experiment to compare the rate of reproduction in a unicellular organism (yeast) vs. a multicellular organism (bean plant) under optimal conditions. What would your data look like after 7 days?
Show Answer

Yeast experiment: Count cells using a haemocytometer every 6 hours in a glucose broth at 30°C. Bean plant: measure height/leaf count every 24 hours. Expected data: Yeast — exponential growth curve reaching billions of cells in 24–48 hours before levelling (S-curve). Bean — linear or slow sigmoidal growth measured in millimetres. Conclusion: unicellular organisms reproduce vastly faster than multicellular organisms.

Explain how angiosperms are better adapted for seed dispersal than gymnosperms.

Why are there always more rabbits than foxes in an ecosystem? Explain using energy flow.

The total energy available for foxes is 100× less than that available for rabbits. This means far fewer foxes can be sustained by any given land area. Predator populations are always smaller than prey populations due to this energy loss at each trophic level.

Ecosystem services provided: Storm surge protection (Sundarbans reduced cyclone Aila's (2009) impact on inland communities by ~30% of wave energy); carbon sequestration (mangroves store 5× more carbon/ha than tropical forests); fisheries nursery (70% of Bay of Bengal commercial fish species breed in mangroves); drinking water filtration; biodiversity habitat for 400+ bird species, Bengal tigers, Irrawaddy dolphins. Development value: coal port projects (Rampal Power Plant) would deliver ~1,320 MW electricity. Conservation vs. development analysis: The Bangladesh Government's own EIA acknowledged risk to UNESCO World Heritage status. Long-term cost of losing mangrove storm protection (costs of cyclone damage to coastal communities) likely exceeds power plant economic benefit. This is a concrete case where conservation has a quantifiable economic rationale exceeding development.

Investigate: How would you assess whether a reforestation project has successfully restored biodiversity — not just tree cover? Design your monitoring protocol.
Show Answer

Tree cover alone does not equal biodiversity (monoculture eucalyptus plantations have fewer species than degraded native forest). Monitoring protocol: Year 1: Establish 50 permanent monitoring plots (10m × 10m). Baseline: plant species richness (all plants in plot), bird species (point count surveys), invertebrate diversity (pitfall traps, sweep nets). Year 3 and Year 5: Repeat measurements. Calculate: (1) Native vs. exotic species ratio. (2) Return of indicator species (specialist species that only live in mature habitat). (3) Understorey regeneration (seedlings of native trees). (4) Soil carbon and invertebrate diversity. Success criteria: measurable increase in native species richness, return of specialist invertebrates and birds, self-sustaining native tree regeneration without planting.

Evaluate the role of seed banks (e.g., Svalbard Global Seed Vault) in conservation. What can they achieve and what are their limitations?
Show Answer

Achievements: Svalbard holds 1.3 million seed varieties including crop wild relatives crucial for breeding drought/disease-resistant crops; already used to restore Syrian genebank seeds destroyed by civil war. Protects against crop failure, disease, and extinction of agricultural biodiversity. Limitations: (1) Seeds require controlled conditions — power failure or flooding (Svalbard flooded in 2017 as permafrost melted) could destroy collections. (2) Cannot store seeds of many tropical and aquatic species (recalcitrant seeds cannot survive drying). (3) A stored species is not a functioning ecosystem — genetic diversity without habitat interaction cannot prevent ecological collapse. (4) Creates a false sense of security: "we can always restore it" discourages urgent in-situ protection. Seed banks are an important last resort, not a substitute for preventing extinction.

Critically evaluate de-extinction technology (e.g., bringing back the woolly mammoth or dodo). Should science pursue this? What are the ecological and ethical concerns?
Show Answer

Scientific possibility: CRISPR gene editing can modify Asian elephant DNA with mammoth sequences (Colossal Biosciences project). Dodo DNA has been mapped. For: restores ecosystem engineers (mammoth grasslands sequestered carbon); increases biodiversity; inspires conservation funding. Against: Ecological concerns — de-extincted species would enter habitats radically changed since their extinction; no mother to teach mammoth behaviour; may become invasive in modified ecosystems. Ethical concerns: enormous resources (billions of dollars) diverted from preventing current extinctions; sends message that extinction is reversible — reducing urgency of current conservation. Scientific consensus: preventing extinction of living species is far more effective and ethical than de-extinction. De-extinction is scientifically fascinating but practically and ethically problematic.

Analyse how indigenous communities have historically contributed to biodiversity conservation in India, and what modern conservation policy can learn from them.
Show Answer

Indigenous contributions: Bishnoi community (Rajasthan) has protected Blackbuck and trees for 500+ years — foundation of modern conservation ethics in India; Appiko movement (1983) in Western Ghats inspired by Chipko; Sacred Groves (Devravani) — community-protected forest patches maintained by over 100,000 villages across India, preserving endemic species outside formal protection. These show centuries of successful in-situ conservation predating modern park systems. Lessons for policy: Sacred Groves protect more area than national parks in some states; indigenous ecological knowledge (IEK) documents species and seasonal patterns not in scientific literature; Community Reserves (Wildlife Act 2006) now legalise community conservation. Modern policy must partner with, not displace, indigenous custodians to achieve lasting conservation.

Evaluate India's performance on global biodiversity goals (e.g., CBD Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework 2022 targets). What has been achieved and what remains to be done?
Show Answer

Kunming-Montreal targets (2022): 30×30 (protect 30% of land and ocean by 2030); halt species extinction; restore 30% of degraded ecosystems; reduce pesticide use by 50%. India's status: Currently ~5% of land under strict protection (national parks + sanctuaries); ~26% under some form of forest cover; tiger and rhino numbers have grown. Gaps: Protection remains fragmented and biased toward forests — grasslands, wetlands, and coastal ecosystems are underprotected; many protected areas are under-resourced; 30×30 requires tripling protected area. What remains: Expanding wildlife corridors, reforming forest governance to include community forests, addressing ocean plastics, and reducing agricultural biodiversity loss. India has the institutional frameworks but requires political will and funding scale-up.

Research extension: The concept of "rewilding" involves restoring large predators and allowing ecosystems to recover naturally with minimal human management. Evaluate whether rewilding is a viable conservation strategy for India.
Show Answer

Evidence for rewilding: Yellowstone wolf reintroduction restored ecosystem without intensive management; European rewilding (Dutch Oostvaardersplassen) created self-sustaining habitats in 30 years. India context: Cheetah reintroduction (Kuno National Park, 2022) is India's first rewilding attempt — 20 African cheetahs introduced. Challenges: India has 1.4 billion people; available wilderness is fragmented; large predator rewilding requires vast unfragmented landscapes. Success criteria require predator-prey balance, natural prey populations, and absence of livestock competition. Viability: Promising in large protected landscapes (Madhya Pradesh, Northeast India) where corridors connect reserves. Realistic for India as a targeted strategy in specific landscapes, not a nationwide approach given land pressure. Requires accompanying land use reform and community compensation.
