Set A Easy — Asexual Reproduction in Plants

Define vegetative propagation.Easy
Show Answer

Vegetative propagation is a type of asexual reproduction in plants where a new plant grows from a vegetative part (root, stem, or leaf) of the parent plant, without the involvement of seeds or spores.
Name the asexual reproductive structure used by strawberry plants.Easy
Show Answer

Runners (stolons) — horizontal stems that grow along the ground and produce new plants at nodes.
Which part of the onion plant is used for vegetative propagation?Easy
Show Answer

The bulb — an underground modified stem with fleshy leaf bases storing food, which sprouts new shoots.
How does a potato reproduce asexually?Easy
Show Answer

Through tubers — underground storage stems (like the potato itself) that have "eyes" (buds). Each eye can sprout into a new plant.
What is meant by the term "clone" in the context of vegetative propagation?Easy
Show Answer

A clone is an organism that is genetically identical to the parent because it was produced by asexual reproduction — all offspring from one parent share the exact same DNA.
What is the difference between a cutting and grafting?Easy
Show Answer

Cutting: a stem piece is removed and planted in soil to grow roots and become a new plant (e.g., rose). Grafting: a stem (scion) from one plant is joined to the rooted stem (rootstock) of another to grow as one plant (e.g., mango, apple).
Give two advantages of vegetative propagation over seed reproduction.Easy
Show Answer

(1) New plants are genetically identical to the parent — preserving desirable traits. (2) Faster growth and earlier fruiting compared to growing from seed.
Name the type of asexual reproduction in yeast.Easy
Show Answer

Budding — a small outgrowth (bud) forms on the parent yeast cell, grows, and eventually pinches off to form a new organism.

Set B Medium — Asexual Reproduction in Plants

Explain how grafting is carried out and why the scion and rootstock must be closely related species.Medium
Show Answer

A stem (scion) with desired fruit quality is sliced and joined to a rooted stock plant (rootstock). The cambium layers are aligned and bound. Closely related species are needed because their vascular tissues must fuse for water and nutrients to flow between rootstock and scion; distantly related plants reject each other.
Why do farmers prefer to grow sugar cane from stem cuttings rather than seeds?Medium
Show Answer

Stem cuttings produce plants identical (clones) to the parent, preserving high sugar content and disease resistance. Seeds may show genetic variation (due to sexual reproduction), giving unpredictable quality. Cuttings also grow faster and begin producing crops earlier.
Distinguish between a runner, rhizome, and stolon with one plant example each.Medium
Show Answer

Runner: thin horizontal stem above soil surface; nodes touch ground and root (strawberry). Rhizome: thick horizontal underground stem with nodes and internodes (ginger, fern). Stolon: horizontal stem that may be above or below ground, connects parent to new plantlet (mint). All produce clones via asexual reproduction.
What is tissue culture and how does it differ from traditional vegetative propagation?Medium
Show Answer

Tissue culture (micropropagation) grows new plants from tiny plant cells or tissue in a sterile nutrient medium in a lab. Unlike traditional methods (runners, cuttings) which need a large piece of the parent, tissue culture can produce thousands of identical plants from a few cells, useful for rare or disease-free plants.
Explain why asexually reproduced populations are more vulnerable to disease than sexually reproducing populations.Medium
Show Answer

All clones share identical DNA. If a pathogen overcomes one individual's defences, it can devastate the entire population. Sexual reproduction creates genetic diversity — some individuals will have genes conferring resistance, allowing the population to survive outbreaks. The 1840s Irish Potato Famine is a classic example of clone vulnerability.
How does regeneration in Planaria differ from vegetative propagation in plants?Medium
Show Answer

Planaria regeneration: a fragment grows back missing parts using specialised cells (neoblasts) — this is also asexual. Plant vegetative propagation: specific organs (stems, roots, leaves) grow new whole plants. Both produce clones, but regeneration can occur from almost any fragment in Planaria, while plants need organised vegetative structures.
List three differences between asexual and sexual reproduction.Medium
Show Answer

(1) Asexual: one parent; Sexual: two parents. (2) Asexual: offspring are genetically identical (clones); Sexual: offspring show genetic variation. (3) Asexual: faster, no gametes needed; Sexual: slower, requires gamete formation and fertilisation.
Why is Bryophyllum (Kalanchoe) considered unusual among plants for asexual reproduction?Medium
Show Answer

Bryophyllum develops buds (plantlets) along the notches of its leaves while still attached to the parent plant. These tiny plantlets fall off and root in the soil. Leaf-based propagation is unusual — most vegetative reproduction uses stems or roots, not leaves actively producing complete offspring.

Set C Hard — Asexual Reproduction in Plants

Evaluate the evolutionary trade-off between asexual and sexual reproduction strategies in plants from an IB MYP perspective.Hard
Show Answer

Asexual reproduction is energetically efficient and guarantees transmission of the entire genome. It succeeds in stable environments where parent traits are perfectly suited. However, lack of variation means populations cannot adapt to environmental change, disease, or new predators — leading to extinction risk. Sexual reproduction incurs higher energy costs (producing gametes, attracting pollinators) and mixes genes unpredictably, but generates variation that drives natural selection and long-term survival. Most plants use both strategies, maximising flexibility.
Explain the role of mitosis in vegetative propagation and contrast it with the role of meiosis in sexual reproduction.Hard
Show Answer

Vegetative propagation relies entirely on mitosis — cells divide to produce genetically identical daughter cells, building the new plant without changing chromosome number (remains 2n). Sexual reproduction uses meiosis to halve chromosome number to n (haploid gametes), then fertilisation restores 2n. Mitosis preserves genetic information; meiosis creates variation through crossing-over and independent assortment — the raw material for evolution.
Why is grafting used in mango cultivation instead of growing from seeds? Discuss economic and genetic reasons.Hard
Show Answer

Genetic: Mango seedlings show high variation — fruit from seeds may be small, fibrous, or low-sugar. Grafted plants are clones of premium varieties (Alphonso, Kesar), guaranteeing consistent quality. Economic: Grafted trees begin fruiting in 3–4 years vs. 7–10 years for seedlings, improving farmer ROI. Rootstock also confers disease resistance and drought tolerance, reducing losses. Grafting thus combines genetic fidelity with economic efficiency.
Describe the cellular mechanism of regeneration in Hydra and compare it to binary fission in Amoeba.Hard
Show Answer

Hydra regeneration: interstitial cells (totipotent stem cells) at the wound site divide by mitosis and differentiate into missing tissue types to restore the complete body plan. Binary fission in Amoeba: the single cell's DNA replicates, nucleus divides by mitosis, and the cell splits into two equal daughter cells — no specialisation needed. Both are asexual and produce clones, but regeneration requires cellular differentiation while binary fission is a simple whole-cell division.
How does air layering (marcotting) work as a vegetative propagation technique? What advantage does it have over ordinary cuttings?Hard
Show Answer

Air layering: a ring of bark is removed from a branch, the wound packed with moist moss and covered with plastic film. Roots form at the wound while the branch is still attached and nourished by the parent. Once roots are established, the branch is cut and planted. Advantage: the new plant is never without a water/nutrient supply (unlike a cutting that must survive while forming roots), giving much higher success rates for difficult-to-root species like lychee.
Critically assess whether clonal agriculture (monoculture from cuttings) is sustainable, using the example of the Cavendish banana.Hard
Show Answer

Cavendish bananas are propagated clonally — 100% genetically identical worldwide. This ensures uniform flavour and shelf life. However, the Fusarium wilt fungus (TR4 strain) exploits the complete genetic uniformity — once it infects one plantation, it spreads globally with no resistant individuals surviving. The Gros Michel banana was wiped out in the 1950s for the same reason. Sustainable agriculture requires genetic diversity (seed banks, wild varieties) alongside commercial monocultures as insurance against catastrophic failure.
Explain how totipotency of plant cells makes tissue culture possible, and contrast this with animal cell differentiation.Hard
Show Answer

Totipotency: every plant cell retains the complete genome and theoretically can develop into a whole organism. In tissue culture, a single cell is placed in a nutrient medium with plant hormones (auxin/cytokinin), triggering dedifferentiation into a callus (undifferentiated mass), then redifferentiation into roots and shoots. In contrast, most differentiated animal cells are no longer totipotent — epigenetic changes silence most genes. Only zygotes and early embryonic cells can form all tissue types, making animal cloning far more technically challenging than plant tissue culture.
A horticulturist wants to rapidly multiply 10,000 identical disease-free orchid plants. Compare and evaluate three asexual methods she could use.Hard
Show Answer

(1) Tissue culture: most efficient — starts from a few sterile cells, produces 10,000 plants in months, 100% disease-free, identical. Cost: high lab setup. (2) Division of pseudobulbs: splits mature clumps into pieces, each growing a new plant. Slower (months-years for 10,000), but no lab needed. (3) Keiki propagation: some orchids naturally produce keikis (baby plants on flower spikes); can be detached and potted. Very slow, not scalable to 10,000. Conclusion: tissue culture is the only practical method at this scale; the others suit small-scale hobbyists.

Set A Easy — Flower Structure & Pollination

Name the four main whorls of a typical flower from outside to inside.Easy
Show Answer

Calyx (sepals) → Corolla (petals) → Androecium (stamens) → Gynoecium (carpel/pistil).
What is the function of the stamen in a flower?Easy
Show Answer

The stamen is the male reproductive organ. It consists of the anther (produces pollen grains containing male gametes) and the filament (stalk supporting the anther).
Name the parts of the carpel (pistil).Easy
Show Answer

Stigma (sticky surface for pollen reception), style (connects stigma to ovary), and ovary (contains ovules where female gametes are formed).
Define pollination.Easy
Show Answer

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther of a flower to the stigma of the same or another flower of the same species.
Distinguish between self-pollination and cross-pollination.Easy
Show Answer

Self-pollination: pollen transfers from the anther to the stigma of the same flower or another flower on the same plant. Cross-pollination: pollen transfers from the anther of one plant to the stigma of a different plant of the same species.
Name two agents of pollination.Easy
Show Answer

Biotic: insects (bees, butterflies) — entomophily. Abiotic: wind — anemophily. Others include water, birds, bats.
What is the function of the ovary in a flower?Easy
Show Answer

The ovary contains ovules (each with a female gamete/egg cell). After fertilisation, the ovary develops into a fruit and each ovule develops into a seed.
What happens after pollen lands on the stigma?Easy
Show Answer

The pollen grain germinates and grows a pollen tube down through the style to the ovary. The male gamete travels through this tube to the ovule and fuses with the egg cell (fertilisation).

Set B Medium — Flower Structure & Pollination

Compare the structural adaptations of wind-pollinated and insect-pollinated flowers.Medium
Show Answer

Wind-pollinated (e.g., grass): small/no petals, no scent/nectar, large feathery stigma to catch pollen, light and abundant pollen, anthers dangle outside flower. Insect-pollinated (e.g., rose): large colourful petals, scent, nectar, sticky stigma, less pollen (but carried precisely). Wind flowers use quantity; insect flowers use quality.
What is double fertilisation and in which plant group does it occur?Medium
Show Answer

Double fertilisation occurs in angiosperms (flowering plants). Two male gametes travel down the pollen tube: one fuses with the egg cell → zygote (2n); the other fuses with two polar nuclei → endosperm (3n) providing nutrition for the developing embryo.
Explain why cross-pollination is genetically more advantageous than self-pollination.Medium
Show Answer

Cross-pollination combines genetic material from two different individuals, creating offspring with greater genetic variation. This variety allows natural selection to act, improving adaptation, disease resistance, and long-term survival. Self-pollination produces near-clones and accumulates harmful recessive mutations (inbreeding depression).
What role do petals and sepals play in the reproductive success of a flower?Medium
Show Answer

Petals: attract pollinators with colour and pattern (especially UV patterns visible to bees), and may guide them to nectar. Sepals: protect the developing flower bud before it opens. Neither is directly involved in fertilisation, but petals are crucial for ensuring cross-pollination in insect-pollinated species.
Describe the process of pollen tube growth and the journey of the male gamete to the ovule.Medium
Show Answer

After pollen lands on the stigma, it absorbs moisture and germinates. The pollen tube grows through the style, guided by chemical signals (chemotropism) secreted by the ovule. The tube nucleus leads the way; two male gametes follow. The tube enters the ovule through the micropyle, and the gametes are released for fertilisation.
How does the structure of the anther facilitate pollen release?Medium
Show Answer

The anther consists of two lobes, each with two pollen sacs (microsporangia). When pollen matures, the anther wall dries and splits open (dehiscence) along a line of weakness. This releases pollen into the air or onto visiting insects. The anther is raised on the filament to maximise pollen dispersal.
What is a bisexual (hermaphrodite) flower? Give one example and one advantage of this arrangement.Medium
Show Answer

A bisexual flower has both stamen (male) and carpel (female) in the same flower. Example: rose, hibiscus. Advantage: self-pollination is possible as a backup if pollinators are absent, ensuring reproduction even in isolated locations or poor pollinator years.
Trace the development of the ovary and ovule after fertilisation.Medium
Show Answer

After fertilisation: ovule → seed (contains embryo + food reserve). Ovary wall → fruit (pericarp). Sepals may persist or fall. The seed contains the embryo (new plant) protected by the seed coat (testa). The fruit aids dispersal of seeds away from the parent plant.

Set C Hard — Flower Structure & Pollination

Analyse how flower morphology has co-evolved with specific pollinators, giving two detailed examples.Hard
Show Answer

(1) Bee orchid (Ophrys): flower mimics the shape, colour, and scent of a female bee. Male bees attempt to mate with the flower (pseudocopulation), picking up pollen. No nectar is offered — pure deception. (2) Red tubular flowers (e.g., salvia) co-evolved with hummingbirds: deep tube matches the beak length, red colour (birds see red well, bees cannot), no scent (birds rely on vision), positioned to dust pollen on the bird's head. Each partnership evolved mutual adaptations over millions of years.
Explain the genetic consequence of self-pollination over many generations using the concept of heterozygosity.Hard
Show Answer

Each generation of self-pollination increases homozygosity by 50%. After n generations, heterozygosity = (1/2)ⁿ of original. Harmful recessive alleles (normally hidden in Aa heterozygotes) become expressed in aa homozygotes, causing inbreeding depression — reduced growth, fertility, and disease resistance. After many generations, populations become nearly fully homozygous, vulnerable to any environmental change. Plants counteract this with self-incompatibility mechanisms (chemically rejecting own pollen).
How do plants with self-incompatibility prevent self-fertilisation at the molecular level?Hard
Show Answer

The S-locus encodes proteins in pollen and stigma. If pollen S-protein matches the stigma S-protein (same plant), the stigma activates a rejection pathway — either pollen tube growth is arrested or RNA is degraded (RNase-based system in Solanaceae). Only pollen with a different S-allele combination succeeds. This molecular recognition system enforces obligate cross-pollination at the cellular level, maintaining genetic diversity.
Compare the life cycles of a moss (bryophyte) and a flowering plant (angiosperm) in terms of the dominant generation.Hard
Show Answer

In mosses: the gametophyte (n, haploid green plant) is dominant; the sporophyte (2n) is dependent on it. In angiosperms: the sporophyte (2n, the visible plant) is dominant; the gametophyte is reduced to microscopic pollen grains and embryo sac. Angiosperms shifted dominance to 2n because larger diploid plants are more complex, adaptive, and efficient. Water is needed for fertilisation in mosses (swimming sperm), while angiosperms use pollen tubes — enabling reproduction on dry land.
Why is global bee decline a critical threat to food security? Quantify the proportion of crops that depend on bee pollination.Hard
Show Answer

Approximately 75% of the world's flowering crop species (and ~35% of global food production volume) depend on animal pollinators, primarily bees. This includes fruits (apples, mangoes), vegetables (cucumbers, tomatoes), nuts (almonds), and oil crops (sunflower). Without bees, hand pollination is too expensive at scale. Bee decline (from pesticides, habitat loss, varroa mites) directly reduces yields, increases food prices, and threatens nutritional diversity — especially micronutrient-rich fruits and vegetables.
Explain why some plants produce nectar guides (UV patterns) invisible to humans but critical for bees.Hard
Show Answer

Bees have trichromatic colour vision extending into UV (300–400 nm) — humans cannot see UV. Flowers with UV-absorbing pigments in specific patterns (e.g., dark bulls-eye around nectar) guide bees precisely to nectar and pollen. The flower appears uniformly yellow to humans but shows a landing guide to bees. This co-evolved precision increases pollination efficiency: bees visit faster, collect more pollen, and cross-pollinate more reliably than with random flower visits.
Compare pollination and fertilisation, clearly distinguishing where each occurs and what genetic change each produces.Hard
Show Answer

Pollination: transfer of pollen from anther to stigma (outside the plant's reproductive organs). No genetic change — pollen only arrives at the stigma surface. Fertilisation: fusion of male gamete (from pollen tube) with female gamete (egg cell in ovule) — occurs inside the ovary. Genetic change: two haploid (n) gametes fuse to form a diploid (2n) zygote with a unique genetic combination. Pollination is a prerequisite for fertilisation in flowering plants but does not itself create new genetic combinations.
A flower has its petals removed. Predict the effect on (i) self-pollination, (ii) cross-pollination, and (iii) seed production. Justify each prediction.Hard
Show Answer

(i) Self-pollination: minimal effect — petals are not needed for pollen to fall from anther to stigma within the same flower. Many self-pollinating flowers (e.g., pea) have petals that trap pollen, so removal may slightly reduce self-pollination. (ii) Cross-pollination: severely reduced — petals attract pollinators with colour/scent. Without petals, insect visitors decrease dramatically, so pollen transfer between plants drops. Wind-pollinated plants are less affected. (iii) Seed production: reduced (especially in insect-pollinated species) due to decreased cross-pollination, though self-fertilisation may still produce some seeds.

Set A Easy — Human Reproduction

Where does fertilisation occur in the human female?Easy
Show Answer

In the fallopian tube (oviduct), usually in the upper third, near the ovary. The sperm meets the egg here after ovulation.
How many chromosomes does a human zygote contain?Easy
Show Answer

46 chromosomes (2n = 46). Sperm contributes 23 (n) and egg contributes 23 (n), fusing to restore the diploid number.
Where does the fertilised egg (zygote) implant?Easy
Show Answer

In the endometrium (lining of the uterus/womb) approximately 6–10 days after fertilisation.
Name the organ that provides nutrition and oxygen to the developing foetus.Easy
Show Answer

The placenta — a disc-shaped organ attached to the uterine wall, connected to the foetus by the umbilical cord.
What is the function of the testes?Easy
Show Answer

Testes produce sperm cells (spermatogenesis) and secrete the male sex hormone testosterone.
Name the female gamete and state where it is produced.Easy
Show Answer

The female gamete is the egg cell (ovum). It is produced in the ovaries through a process called oogenesis.
What is the role of the umbilical cord?Easy
Show Answer

The umbilical cord connects the foetus to the placenta. It carries oxygenated blood and nutrients from the placenta to the foetus, and returns deoxygenated blood and waste products from the foetus to the placenta.
Define gestation period in humans.Easy
Show Answer

The gestation period is the time from fertilisation (or last menstrual period) to birth — approximately 38–40 weeks (about 9 months) in humans.

Set B Medium — Human Reproduction

Describe the journey of the sperm from the testes to the egg, naming all structures passed through.Medium
Show Answer

Testes → Epididymis (stored and matured) → Vas deferens → Seminal vesicles/prostate add fluid → Urethra (through penis) → Vagina → Cervix → Uterus → Fallopian tube → Egg (fertilisation).
Explain the functions of the placenta in detail.Medium
Show Answer

The placenta: (1) transfers oxygen and nutrients (glucose, amino acids) from mother's blood to foetal blood; (2) removes CO₂ and metabolic waste from foetal blood; (3) acts as a barrier against most pathogens; (4) secretes hormones (HCG, progesterone, oestrogen) to maintain pregnancy; (5) allows passive immunity via maternal antibodies. Blood of mother and foetus never mix directly.
Why is it important that the mother's blood and foetal blood do not mix in the placenta?Medium
Show Answer

Different blood groups: foetal blood may have a different group (e.g., Rh+) that would trigger an immune attack by the mother's antibodies. Different blood pressures: mother's higher pressure would damage fragile foetal capillaries. Exchange of gases and nutrients occurs by diffusion across the placental membrane, which acts as a semi-permeable barrier — efficient exchange without blood mixing.
Describe the structural adaptations of sperm cells for their function.Medium
Show Answer

Head: contains nucleus (23 chromosomes) and acrosome (enzymes to penetrate egg). Midpiece: packed with mitochondria to produce ATP for swimming. Tail (flagellum): whip-like movement for swimming. Streamlined shape reduces resistance. Small size: millions produced to increase chance of one reaching the egg. No cytoplasm: reduces weight/drag.
Why are testes located outside the body in the scrotum?Medium
Show Answer

Sperm production (spermatogenesis) requires a temperature about 2–3°C lower than core body temperature (37°C). The scrotum's position outside the body maintains ~34–35°C, optimal for sperm development. The cremaster muscle raises or lowers the testes to regulate temperature.
What is ectopic pregnancy and why is it dangerous?Medium
Show Answer

Ectopic pregnancy occurs when the zygote implants outside the uterus — most commonly in the fallopian tube. The tube cannot expand like the uterus; as the embryo grows, it ruptures the tube, causing severe internal bleeding (haemorrhage). It is life-threatening and requires immediate surgical intervention. It cannot develop into a viable pregnancy.
How does the amniotic fluid protect the developing foetus?Medium
Show Answer

Amniotic fluid: (1) cushions the foetus from physical shocks and blows; (2) maintains constant temperature; (3) allows free movement — important for limb and lung development; (4) prevents the foetus from sticking to the amniotic sac; (5) provides some nutrition and antimicrobial protection.
Compare oogenesis and spermatogenesis in terms of number of gametes produced and timing.Medium
Show Answer

Spermatogenesis: one primary spermatocyte → four functional sperm. Begins at puberty, continuous throughout adult life, producing millions daily. Oogenesis: one primary oocyte → one functional egg + three polar bodies (discarded). Begins before birth but pauses; one egg released per cycle (monthly) from puberty to menopause. Males produce far more gametes continuously; females have a fixed, finite supply.

Set C Hard — Human Reproduction

Explain why only one sperm fertilises the egg despite millions being released, describing the zona reaction.Hard
Show Answer

When the first sperm penetrates the egg's zona pellucida using acrosomal enzymes and fuses with the egg membrane, the egg immediately undergoes the zona reaction: cortical granules beneath the egg membrane release enzymes that harden the zona pellucida into an impenetrable fertilisation membrane. This "slow block" and an immediate electrical "fast block" prevent polyspermy. Polyspermy would result in a triploid zygote (incompatible with normal development).
Trace the hormonal changes from fertilisation to successful implantation.Hard
Show Answer

After fertilisation, the embryo travels to the uterus (3–5 days). The corpus luteum in the ovary continues secreting progesterone (maintaining the endometrium). At implantation (~day 6–10), the embryo secretes human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) — this signals the corpus luteum to keep producing progesterone instead of degenerating. hCG is the hormone detected by pregnancy tests. Without hCG, progesterone drops, the endometrium sheds (menstruation), and pregnancy fails.
Describe the three stages of labour and the hormonal triggers involved.Hard
Show Answer

Stage 1 (dilation): oxytocin triggers uterine contractions; cervix dilates to 10 cm. Positive feedback: more contractions → more oxytocin release. Stage 2 (expulsion): baby pushed out through birth canal by contractions. Stage 3 (placental stage): placenta and membranes expelled. Oxytocin causes postpartum contractions. Progesterone drops sharply before labour, removing inhibition of contractions. Prostaglandins also promote labour onset.
Explain why identical (monozygotic) twins are genetically identical while fraternal (dizygotic) twins are not.Hard
Show Answer

Identical twins: one zygote splits into two embryos (around days 3–8). Both embryos have exactly the same DNA from the original sperm+egg fusion. Fraternal twins: two separate eggs are ovulated simultaneously and fertilised by two different sperm. Each zygote has a unique genetic combination (different sperm chromosomes). Fraternal twins share ~50% DNA on average — like ordinary siblings born at the same time. Only identical twins are true clones.
How do the placenta's roles as a nutritional organ, endocrine organ, and immune barrier each contribute to foetal survival?Hard
Show Answer

Nutritional: large surface area of villi maximises diffusion of glucose, amino acids, O₂ — fuelling foetal growth at 100× the rate possible from diffusion alone. Endocrine: secretes HCG (maintains corpus luteum), progesterone (prevents menstruation), and oestrogen (prepares mammary glands for lactation) — without these hormones pregnancy terminates. Immune barrier: most bacteria and large antibodies cannot cross, protecting from maternal immune rejection of the foetus (which carries foreign paternal antigens). Maternal IgG antibodies do cross, conferring passive immunity to the newborn for the first months of life.
Discuss IVF (in vitro fertilisation): describe the procedure, success rates, and one ethical concern from an IB MYP Global Context perspective.Hard
Show Answer

Procedure: woman given hormone therapy to stimulate multiple egg production; eggs surgically retrieved; fertilised with sperm in laboratory dish; embryos cultured 3–5 days; 1–2 selected embryos transferred to uterus. Success rates: ~30–40% per cycle for women under 35, declining with age. Ethical concern: surplus embryos are created and often frozen or discarded — raising questions about the moral status of embryos. From a Fairness and Development Global Context: IVF is unaffordable for most people in lower-income countries, creating unequal access to reproductive technologies.
Explain the difference between the embryonic period and foetal period of development, describing key events in each.Hard
Show Answer

Embryonic period (weeks 1–8): Zygote → morula → blastocyst → gastrulation (three germ layers). All major organs form (organogenesis): neural tube (brain/spinal cord) by week 4; heart beating by week 5; limb buds by week 6; all organ systems present by week 8. Highly vulnerable to teratogens (alcohol, drugs). Foetal period (weeks 9–40): Rapid growth and maturation of organs already formed. Face develops, movements begin (week 16), viability outside womb achievable from ~24 weeks with NICU support. Less vulnerable to structural defects (organogenesis complete), but brain development continues throughout.
Why do premature babies often struggle to breathe independently? Relate your answer to organ development timing.Hard
Show Answer

Lungs are the last major organs to fully mature. Pulmonary surfactant (a lipoprotein mixture produced by Type II pneumocytes) is not produced in sufficient quantities until approximately 34–36 weeks gestation. Surfactant reduces surface tension in alveoli, preventing collapse after each breath. Without it, babies develop Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) — each breath requires enormous effort. Treatment: antenatal steroids (accelerate lung maturation) and exogenous surfactant administration via endotracheal tube. This is why 28-week premature infants require mechanical ventilation.

Set A Easy — Menstrual Cycle

What is the average length of the menstrual cycle?Easy
Show Answer

Approximately 28 days, though normal cycles range from 21 to 35 days. Day 1 is the first day of menstruation.
On which day of a 28-day cycle does ovulation typically occur?Easy
Show Answer

Around day 14 — the midpoint of the cycle. This is triggered by a surge in Luteinising Hormone (LH).
What hormone causes the uterine lining (endometrium) to thicken in the first half of the cycle?Easy
Show Answer

Oestrogen — secreted by the developing follicle in the ovary. It stimulates proliferation (rebuilding) of the endometrium after menstruation.
What happens to the uterine lining if fertilisation does not occur?Easy
Show Answer

Progesterone and oestrogen levels fall, the endometrium breaks down and is shed as menstrual flow (menstruation), lasting approximately 3–7 days.
What is the function of progesterone in the menstrual cycle?Easy
Show Answer

Progesterone (secreted by the corpus luteum after ovulation) maintains and thickens the endometrium, making it receptive to a fertilised egg for implantation.
What is the corpus luteum?Easy
Show Answer

The corpus luteum is the ruptured follicle left in the ovary after ovulation. It secretes progesterone (and some oestrogen) to prepare the uterus for implantation.
Name the four phases of the menstrual cycle.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Menstrual phase (days 1–5): shedding of endometrium. (2) Follicular/proliferative phase (days 1–13): follicle grows, oestrogen rises. (3) Ovulation (day 14): egg released. (4) Luteal/secretory phase (days 15–28): corpus luteum secretes progesterone.
What hormone triggers ovulation?Easy
Show Answer

A surge of Luteinising Hormone (LH) from the pituitary gland around day 13–14 triggers ovulation — the release of the mature egg from the Graafian follicle.

Set B Medium — Menstrual Cycle

Describe the hormonal sequence that controls the menstrual cycle from day 1 to day 28.Medium
Show Answer

Days 1–5: Low oestrogen/progesterone → FSH rises → follicle development begins. Days 6–13: Oestrogen rises (from follicle) → endometrium thickens; oestrogen peaks → LH surge. Day 14: LH surge → ovulation. Days 15–28: Corpus luteum secretes progesterone + oestrogen → endometrium maintained. If no fertilisation: corpus luteum degenerates → both hormones fall → menstruation begins (day 28/1).
Why is the fertile window not simply day 14 but spans several days?Medium
Show Answer

Sperm can survive in the female reproductive tract for up to 5 days. The egg remains fertilisable for only 12–24 hours after ovulation. Therefore the fertile window spans about days 10–17 — from when sperm deposited can survive until ovulation, to when the egg is still viable. Ovulation timing also varies between women and cycles, widening uncertainty.
Explain the negative feedback mechanism by which oestrogen controls FSH secretion.Medium
Show Answer

Rising oestrogen (from the developing follicle) feeds back to the hypothalamus and pituitary gland, inhibiting FSH release. This prevents multiple follicles from maturing simultaneously (usually only one follicle completes development per cycle). This negative feedback is a key homeostatic control, analogous to a thermostat reducing heating when temperature reaches target.
What is the difference between the follicular phase and the luteal phase in terms of dominant hormone and uterine events?Medium
Show Answer

Follicular phase (days 1–13): dominant hormone = oestrogen (from maturing follicle); uterine event = endometrium rebuilds (proliferates). Luteal phase (days 15–28): dominant hormone = progesterone (from corpus luteum); uterine event = endometrium thickens further and becomes glandular/secretory, ready for implantation.
What happens to the corpus luteum if fertilisation occurs? What happens if it does not?Medium
Show Answer

If fertilisation occurs: the embryo secretes hCG, which signals the corpus luteum to continue producing progesterone — maintaining the endometrium for implantation and early pregnancy. If fertilisation does not occur: hCG is absent; the corpus luteum degenerates into the corpus albicans (white scar tissue) by day 28; progesterone/oestrogen fall; menstruation begins.
Why do combined oral contraceptive pills (containing oestrogen and progesterone) prevent ovulation?Medium
Show Answer

The pill maintains artificially elevated oestrogen and progesterone levels throughout the cycle. This constant high level exerts negative feedback on the hypothalamus and pituitary, suppressing FSH and LH release. Without the LH surge, no follicle fully matures and ovulation does not occur. The endometrium changes also make implantation less likely.
How does stress or illness affect the menstrual cycle?Medium
Show Answer

Stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, releasing cortisol and CRH. CRH suppresses GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) from the hypothalamus, reducing FSH and LH release. This delays or prevents follicle development and ovulation — causing irregular or missed periods. Severe under-eating (as in anorexia nervosa) similarly suppresses GnRH, causing amenorrhea (absence of menstruation).
What is PMS (premenstrual syndrome) and what hormonal changes cause it?Medium
Show Answer

PMS symptoms (bloating, mood changes, cramps, headaches) occur in the late luteal phase (days 21–28) as progesterone and oestrogen begin to fall. The drop in progesterone affects serotonin levels in the brain (influencing mood). Prostaglandins released during endometrial breakdown cause uterine muscle contractions (menstrual cramps/dysmenorrhea). Severity varies greatly between individuals.

Set C Hard — Menstrual Cycle

Explain the positive and negative feedback mechanisms involving oestrogen in controlling the LH surge at ovulation.Hard
Show Answer

Initially (days 1–12): rising oestrogen exerts negative feedback on pituitary, suppressing LH/FSH (prevents premature ovulation). When oestrogen exceeds a threshold (~200 pg/mL for ~36 hours): it switches to positive feedback — stimulating a massive LH surge from the pituitary. This is the exception to the usual negative feedback rule. The LH surge (day 13–14) triggers ovulation. After ovulation, oestrogen falls, corpus luteum progesterone rises, and negative feedback resumes. This switching between negative and positive feedback is unique to the preovulatory period.
Compare the hormonal profiles of a cycle with pregnancy vs. a cycle without fertilisation from days 14–28.Hard
Show Answer

Without fertilisation: progesterone peaks around day 21, then falls as corpus luteum degenerates → menstruation. Oestrogen also falls. With fertilisation: embryo implants ~day 20–21 and secretes hCG, rescuing corpus luteum → progesterone remains high (prevents menstruation). hCG is detectable by day 10–12 post-fertilisation and peaks at week 8–10. After week 10, the placenta takes over progesterone/oestrogen production and corpus luteum is no longer essential.
Analyse how the menstrual cycle is an example of a biological feedback system, using the hypothalamus-pituitary-ovarian (HPO) axis.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothalamus: secretes GnRH in pulses → stimulates pituitary. Pituitary: secretes FSH (follicle growth) and LH (ovulation trigger). Ovaries: respond by secreting oestrogen (negative feedback to pituitary/hypothalamus suppressing FSH) and progesterone (after ovulation, negative feedback suppressing LH). Exception: preovulatory oestrogen peak → positive feedback → LH surge. This three-tier axis with dual-mode feedback (mostly negative, briefly positive) produces the precisely timed 28-day cycle. Disruption at any level (stress → hypothalamus, PCOS → ovary, pill → pituitary) breaks the cycle.
Why do women approaching menopause experience irregular periods before cessation? Explain the hormonal mechanism.Hard
Show Answer

As ovarian follicle reserve depletes (typically after age 45), fewer follicles respond to FSH. Less oestrogen is produced. The pituitary compensates by increasing FSH secretion (FSH blood levels rise — a diagnostic marker of perimenopause). With insufficient oestrogen, the LH surge may not occur consistently, causing anovulatory cycles (no ovulation) — irregular timing and flow. Eventually (menopause), no follicles remain, oestrogen drops to very low levels permanently, and menstruation ceases.
How do fertility drugs (e.g., clomiphene/Clomid) work to stimulate ovulation in women with PCOS?Hard
Show Answer

PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome) involves elevated androgens and insulin resistance — disrupting GnRH pulsatility and oestrogen feedback, preventing regular ovulation. Clomiphene is a selective oestrogen receptor modulator (SERM) — it blocks oestrogen receptors in the hypothalamus/pituitary, preventing oestrogen from exerting negative feedback. The brain "thinks" oestrogen is low → releases more GnRH → more FSH/LH → follicle development stimulated → ovulation induced. Risk: multiple follicles may mature simultaneously → multiple births.
Evaluate the evidence for menstrual synchrony (the idea that women living together align their cycles) — what does current science say?Hard
Show Answer

Original claim (McClintock, 1971): women in dorms showed cycle synchrony attributed to pheromones. However, multiple rigorous re-analyses with larger samples and controlled statistics found no consistent evidence of synchrony beyond what chance would produce. Human vomeronasal organ (pheromone detector) is vestigial. Perceived synchrony may reflect statistical artifacts (cycles that are similar in length will periodically align randomly). Current scientific consensus: menstrual synchrony in humans is not a reliable, reproducible phenomenon — the original study had methodological flaws in data analysis.
Explain how emergency contraception (morning-after pill, levonorgestrel) prevents pregnancy. Is it an abortifacient?Hard
Show Answer

Levonorgestrel (high-dose progestogen) works primarily by delaying or inhibiting ovulation — preventing egg release so fertilisation cannot occur. If taken before the LH surge, it is ~95% effective. It may also affect cervical mucus (reducing sperm penetration) and, to a lesser degree, implantation (secondary mechanism, less certain). It does NOT terminate an established pregnancy (confirmed by hCG/implantation). Therefore, medically and legally it is NOT an abortifacient — it prevents fertilisation or implantation; it cannot disrupt an already implanted embryo.
Why is the 28-day cycle sometimes called "lunar" and what is the evolutionary basis for this connection, if any?Hard
Show Answer

The lunar month is ~29.5 days, close to the average menstrual cycle (~28 days). Some researchers suggest this reflects ancestral synchronisation with moonlight-regulated circadian cycles. However, modern evidence shows: (1) cycle length varies greatly (21–35 days is normal); (2) there is no causal mechanism linking moonlight to HPO axis hormones in humans; (3) other primates with similar cycles show no lunar correlation. The similarity is likely coincidental. Some marine organisms (e.g., Platynereis worms) genuinely synchronise reproduction with the lunar cycle via melatonin — but robust evidence for this in humans is lacking.

What is contraception?Easy
Show Answer

Contraception (birth control) refers to methods used to prevent pregnancy by stopping fertilisation or implantation of a fertilised egg.
Name the only contraceptive method that also protects against STIs.Easy
Show Answer

Barrier methods — specifically the male condom (and female condom). Condoms physically prevent exchange of bodily fluids, protecting against sexually transmitted infections.
How does a condom work as a contraceptive?Easy
Show Answer

A condom is a thin barrier (usually latex) worn over the penis during intercourse. It physically prevents sperm from entering the vagina and reaching the egg, blocking fertilisation.
What is the mechanism of action of the combined oral contraceptive pill?Easy
Show Answer

The pill contains synthetic oestrogen and progesterone that suppress FSH and LH secretion, preventing ovulation. It also thickens cervical mucus (blocking sperm) and alters the endometrium (reducing implantation chances).
What does IUCD stand for, and how does it work?Easy
Show Answer

Intrauterine Contraceptive Device. A small T-shaped device (copper or hormonal) inserted into the uterus by a doctor. Copper IUCDs: copper ions are toxic to sperm. Hormonal IUCDs: release progestogen to thicken cervical mucus and thin the endometrium.
What is vasectomy?Easy
Show Answer

Vasectomy is a permanent surgical contraceptive procedure for males in which the vas deferens (the tube carrying sperm from testes to urethra) is cut, tied, or sealed, preventing sperm from being ejaculated.
What is tubectomy (tubal ligation)?Easy
Show Answer

Tubectomy is a permanent surgical contraceptive procedure for females in which the fallopian tubes are cut, tied, or sealed — preventing eggs from reaching the uterus and blocking sperm from reaching eggs.
List one advantage and one disadvantage of surgical sterilisation over hormonal contraception.Easy
Show Answer

Advantage: permanent (one-time procedure), no ongoing use required, near 100% effective. Disadvantage: generally irreversible; does not protect against STIs; surgical risks (infection, anaesthesia).

Set B Medium — Contraception Methods

Compare barrier, hormonal, and surgical contraceptive methods across effectiveness, reversibility, and STI protection.Medium
Show Answer

Barrier (condom): ~87% typical use effectiveness; fully reversible (stop using = restored fertility); only method protecting against STIs. Hormonal (pill): ~91% typical use; fully reversible; no STI protection. IUCD: >99% effective; reversible (remove device); copper IUCD can be emergency contraceptive too; no STI protection. Surgical: >99.5% effective; permanent (rarely reversible); no STI protection. STI protection is unique to barrier methods.
Explain why "typical use" effectiveness of contraceptives is lower than "perfect use" effectiveness, with the condom as an example.Medium
Show Answer

Perfect use: method used correctly and consistently every time. Condom perfect use: ~98% effective. Typical use: accounts for human error — forgetting, incorrect application, breakage, not using it every time. Condom typical use: ~87% effective. The gap reflects real-world behaviour. Hormonal methods have smaller gaps (pill perfect: 99.7%; typical: 91%) because most failures come from forgetting pills, not incorrect use. IUCD and implants have near-zero gap — user cannot make errors once inserted.
Why might hormonal contraceptives be medically contraindicated for some women?Medium
Show Answer

Combined pill containing oestrogen is contraindicated for: women over 35 who smoke (increased blood clot/stroke risk); history of deep vein thrombosis, stroke, or heart disease; severe migraines with aura; breast cancer history; hypertension. In these cases, progesterone-only pills or non-hormonal methods (condom, copper IUCD) are safer alternatives. Each woman's medical history must be assessed before prescription.
How does the copper IUCD work as emergency contraception when inserted within 5 days of unprotected sex?Medium
Show Answer

Copper ions released by the IUCD are toxic to sperm — reducing sperm motility and viability even after they have entered the uterus. If sperm has already fertilised an egg, copper ions alter the uterine environment, making it hostile to implantation. The copper IUCD is >99% effective as emergency contraception and simultaneously provides ongoing long-term contraception for up to 10 years.
Describe the natural family planning (rhythm/calendar) method and explain its limitations.Medium
Show Answer

The method tracks the menstrual cycle to identify fertile days (around ovulation) and avoids unprotected sex during that window. Also uses temperature (basal body temperature rises after ovulation) and cervical mucus changes (becomes clear/stretchy at ovulation). Limitations: requires regular cycles; cycles vary (illness, stress); sperm can survive 5 days; method relies on accurate tracking and high partner cooperation. Typical use failure rate: ~24% per year — much higher than barrier/hormonal methods.
From an IB MYP perspective, discuss why access to contraception is an issue of both health and social equity.Medium
Show Answer

Health: access to contraception reduces unintended pregnancies, unsafe abortions, and maternal mortality. Women with access can plan families, improving maternal and child health outcomes. Social equity: in many low-income countries, poverty, gender inequality, lack of education, and religious restrictions limit access — disproportionately affecting women. Unintended pregnancy can trap women in cycles of poverty, limit education, and reduce economic participation. Equal access to contraception is therefore both a health right (WHO declares it essential medicine) and a gender equality issue (UN SDG 3 and 5).
What is the sub-dermal implant (e.g., Nexplanon) and how does it differ from the pill in terms of user compliance?Medium
Show Answer

The sub-dermal implant is a 4 cm rod inserted under the skin of the upper arm by a clinician. It slowly releases etonogestrel (progestogen) over 3 years, preventing ovulation and thickening cervical mucus. Unlike the daily pill, it requires zero user action once inserted — eliminating compliance failures. Effectiveness: >99.9% (near-perfect real-world use). It is one of the most effective reversible contraceptives (LARC — Long-Acting Reversible Contraception), removed when the woman wants to conceive.
Why is it important for sex education programmes to teach contraception even in communities with abstinence-based values?Medium
Show Answer

Evidence consistently shows that abstinence-only education does not reduce teenage sexual activity rates but does reduce use of contraception when sex does occur — leading to higher teenage pregnancy and STI rates compared to comprehensive sex education programmes. Knowledge of contraception does not increase sexual activity. It ensures that those who are sexually active — regardless of intention — can make informed choices to protect their health. WHO and UNICEF recommend comprehensive sex education as a public health standard.

Set C Hard — Contraception Methods

Evaluate the Pearl Index as a measure of contraceptive efficacy and explain its limitations as a comparative tool.Hard
Show Answer

Pearl Index = (number of pregnancies × 1200) / (total months of exposure). It gives pregnancies per 100 woman-years. Limitations: (1) It assumes constant failure rate over time — but some methods (condoms) have higher failure risk in early months when users are less skilled. (2) Populations in trials differ in age, coital frequency, and motivation — making cross-study comparison unreliable. (3) It confounds perfect and typical use. (4) It underestimates risk for long-term users (years 3–5) if failure rates are not time-stratified. Life-table analysis (cumulative failure rates per year) is a more rigorous alternative.
Explain the molecular mechanism by which progesterone-only pills (mini-pills) prevent pregnancy through multiple pathways.Hard
Show Answer

(1) Cervical mucus: progestogen thickens mucus to a viscous plug impenetrable to sperm — primary mechanism. (2) Endometrium: thins the endometrial lining, reducing glycogen stores needed for implantation. (3) Ovulation: at higher doses (desogestrel-containing pills), LH surge is suppressed → anovulatory in ~97% of cycles. (4) Tubal motility: may slow fallopian tube cilia, reducing egg transport. Progesterone-only pills avoid oestrogen-related cardiovascular risks, making them suitable for breastfeeding mothers, smokers over 35, and those with migraine.
From a sociological and demographic perspective, evaluate India's historical family planning programmes and their ethical concerns.Hard
Show Answer

India launched family planning in 1952 — the world's first national programme. Early phases (1960s–70s): voluntary education and spacing. 1975–77 Emergency: mass forced sterilisation targets led to coerced vasectomies of millions — a major human rights violation, causing public backlash and programme collapse. Post-Emergency: shifted to voluntary, incentive-based, female-focused tubectomy — raising concerns of gender inequality and socioeconomic coercion of poor women. Modern policy (National Health Mission): rights-based, informed consent, spacing methods prioritised. Lesson: demographic goals cannot ethically override individual bodily autonomy — coercive programmes are both unethical and counterproductive (undermine public trust).
Why is male hormonal contraception (testosterone-based) still not on the market despite decades of research? Discuss biological and social barriers.Hard
Show Answer

Biological barriers: (1) Men produce millions of sperm daily — suppression requires complete azoospermia (not just reduction); achieving this reliably is harder than suppressing one egg per month. (2) Exogenous testosterone suppresses endogenous production but causes side effects (mood changes, acne, reduced libido in some). (3) Recovery of fertility after cessation is slower and less predictable than female contraceptives. (4) A WHO trial (2016) was halted due to mood-related adverse events — though notably, similar effects in female pill trials were historically accepted. Social barriers: pharmaceutical investment is less for male products as women bear pregnancy risk (flawed market logic); regulatory thresholds for male contraceptive side effects are perceived as stricter by researchers, reflecting gender bias in clinical standards.
Compare the mechanisms of reversible contraception (IUCD, implant, pill) vs. permanent sterilisation (vasectomy, tubectomy) for a couple who have completed their family.Hard
Show Answer

Reversible LARCs: IUCD (10-year copper or 5-year hormonal) and implant (3-year) are >99% effective without ongoing effort; fertility returns within weeks of removal — appropriate if any chance of changing plans. Tubectomy: laparoscopic procedure under general anaesthesia; ~0.5% failure rate; reversal (reanastomosis) has <50% success — practically permanent; no hormonal effects; risk of ectopic pregnancy post-failure. Vasectomy: simpler, cheaper, lower surgical risk than tubectomy; same near-permanent outcome; reversal success declines with time (~90% if within 3 years, ~30% after 15 years). For a couple certain of family completion: vasectomy offers the least risk (outpatient, local anaesthetic) at equivalent efficacy to tubectomy with much lower surgical burden on the female partner.
What is the vaccine-based contraception concept (immunocontraception) and how could it work? What ethical issues does it raise?Hard
Show Answer

Immunocontraception: vaccines stimulate the immune system to produce antibodies against reproductive hormones or gamete proteins. Examples: (1) Anti-hCG vaccine (India's ICMR): antibodies against hCG prevent the corpus luteum rescue → no implantation. (2) Anti-zona pellucida vaccine: antibodies block sperm-egg binding. Potential: long-lasting, reversible when antibody levels wane, injectable (no daily compliance). Ethical issues: (1) If used covertly, violates bodily autonomy — "covert sterilisation" risk in populations with limited agency. (2) Cross-reactivity concerns (antibodies targeting reproductive hormones could disrupt hormonal cycles). (3) Equity: primarily researched for wildlife population control (deer, horses) — raises concern about application to marginalised human groups without consent, echoing eugenic history.
Analyse why STI rates among young people in India are rising despite increased access to contraceptives, using a systems-thinking approach.Hard
Show Answer

Systems factors: (1) Condom use rates remain low among young people — social stigma (buying condoms implies sexual activity), gender power imbalance (women cannot always negotiate condom use), and partner resistance. (2) Sex education is inadequate or absent — many young people lack accurate knowledge of STI transmission routes and condom effectiveness. (3) Rising sexual activity due to urbanisation, digital media influence, and changing social norms without corresponding behaviour change programmes. (4) Healthcare access: fear of stigma deters testing and treatment, allowing silent spread. (5) Misconception that contraceptives = STI protection (e.g., the pill does not protect against STIs). Intervention requires addressing all layers: education, healthcare access, gender equality, and destigmatisation simultaneously.
Evaluate the claim: "The introduction of the contraceptive pill was the most important factor in women's liberation in the 20th century." Do you agree? Use evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Supporting evidence: The pill (approved USA 1960, India 1965) gave women unprecedented control over reproduction for the first time in history. Studies show direct correlation between pill availability and women's increased education, delayed marriage, and career participation. Harvard economists Goldin and Katz (2002) found pill access in US states correlated with significantly higher female professional attainment. Counter-arguments: (1) Legal equality, voting rights, and labour rights preceded the pill and were won through political struggle. (2) The pill was initially available only to married women (unmarried access in US only after 1972). (3) Structural barriers (glass ceiling, pay gap) persisted long after pill availability. (4) Access inequities: pill was/is unaffordable for many women globally. Balanced conclusion: the pill was one critical enabling technology among multiple factors; its importance was mediated by legal, economic, and social changes occurring simultaneously.

How can STIs be prevented?Easy

What is body autonomy and why is it important in reproductive health?Easy

What does "informed consent" mean in the context of sexual health?Easy

Why is sex education important for adolescent health?Easy

Name two changes that occur during puberty in females and two in males.Easy

What is HIV and how is it different from AIDS?Easy

Why are adolescents particularly vulnerable to reproductive health risks? Discuss biological and social factors.Medium

Discuss the importance of HPV vaccination for adolescent girls (and boys) in India.Medium

Explain the concept of "safe sex" and why it goes beyond just preventing pregnancy.Medium

What are the social barriers to accessing reproductive healthcare in India, particularly for young women?Medium

How does HIV affect the immune system at a cellular level, and why does this make AIDS so dangerous?Medium
Show Answer

HIV specifically targets CD4+ helper T-cells using its gp120 protein to bind CD4 receptors. The virus inserts its RNA into the cell's DNA (via reverse transcriptase), hijacking the cell to produce more HIV. Over years, CD4 count declines from normal (>500/mm³) toward <200/mm³ (AIDS diagnosis). Without helper T-cells, the immune system cannot coordinate responses — B-cells cannot make antibodies effectively, cytotoxic T-cells cannot be activated. Opportunistic infections (Pneumocystis pneumonia, toxoplasmosis, Kaposi's sarcoma) that healthy immune systems control become lethal.
Discuss the MYP Global Context "Fairness and Development" in relation to reproductive health equity globally.Medium

Reproductive health disparities mirror global inequity: 99% of maternal deaths occur in low/middle-income countries (WHO). Women in sub-Saharan Africa face a 1-in-40 lifetime risk of maternal death vs. 1-in-5,600 in high-income countries. Access to contraception, skilled birth attendance, safe abortion, and STI treatment is limited by poverty, weak health systems, conflict, and gender inequality. SDG 3 (Good Health) and SDG 5 (Gender Equality) explicitly address reproductive health. From a Fairness and Development perspective: reproductive health inequity is not merely a medical issue but a justice issue — reflecting structural inequalities in wealth, gender, and power.
Why is menstrual health management (MHM) considered a public health and human rights issue in India?Medium
Show Answer

Approximately 23% of girls in India drop out of school after their first period due to lack of sanitation facilities, menstrual products, and education. Women who cannot manage menstruation hygienically face infections, pain, and shame. The 2017 removal of GST on sanitary pads and government distribution programmes reflect recognition as a public health issue. As a human rights issue: the inability to manage menstruation with dignity prevents girls' access to education and economic participation — violating rights to health, education, and equality.
Explain how antiretroviral therapy (ART) for HIV works and why it must be taken lifelong.Medium
Show Answer

ART combines drugs targeting multiple HIV replication stages: reverse transcriptase inhibitors (prevent RNA→DNA conversion), protease inhibitors (prevent viral assembly), integrase inhibitors (prevent DNA insertion), and entry inhibitors (block cell entry). Combination therapy (HAART) prevents resistance. ART reduces viral load to undetectable levels — restoring immune function and eliminating transmission risk (U=U: Undetectable=Untransmittable). It must be lifelong because HIV integrates into the host genome; stopping treatment allows the virus to rebound from latent reservoirs (memory T-cells). No cure currently exists — ART controls, not eliminates, HIV.

Critically evaluate the argument that comprehensive sex education "encourages" sexual activity among adolescents, using evidence-based counterarguments.Hard
Show Answer

Claim: teaching contraception normalises and increases teen sexual activity. Evidence against: (1) A systematic review of 83 studies (Kirby 2007) found comprehensive programmes do not accelerate sexual debut and often delay it. Analyse the epidemiology of HIV in India — which populations are most affected, and what structural factors drive transmission?Hard

Discuss the concept of gender power imbalance and how it affects reproductive health outcomes for women in heterosexual relationships.Hard

Compare the pathophysiology of bacterial STIs (e.g., chlamydia) vs. viral STIs (e.g., herpes) and explain why one is curable and the other is not.Hard

Chlamydia (bacterial): Chlamydia trachomatis is an intracellular bacterium. It infects epithelial cells of the genital tract, causing PID (pelvic inflammatory disease) if untreated → scarring → infertility. Curable with antibiotics (azithromycin, doxycycline) because: bacteria are free-living organisms with their own ribosomes and metabolic machinery susceptible to antibiotics; they do not integrate into host DNA. Herpes (viral HSV-2): DNA virus that, after primary infection, enters sensory neurons and establishes latency in dorsal root ganglia — inserting into the neuron's genome. Explain the concept of "syndemic" as applied to HIV, STIs, and mental health in vulnerable populations.Hard
Show Answer

A syndemic (synergistic epidemic) is two or more epidemics that co-occur in the same population and interact to worsen health outcomes. The HIV-STI-mental health syndemic: (1) STIs increase HIV transmission risk by causing genital ulcers (disrupted barrier). (2) Depression and substance use increase high-risk sexual behaviour (reduced condom use, multiple partners). (3) HIV diagnosis increases depression and anxiety. (4) Stigma and discrimination reduce healthcare utilisation for all three conditions. (5) Poverty underlies all three (limits healthcare access, increases vulnerability, reduces social support). The synergistic interaction means the combined burden is greater than the sum of individual epidemics. Effective intervention must address social determinants (poverty, stigma, mental health access) simultaneously — treating only HIV without addressing depression and STIs will not break the syndemic cycle.
What is the right to reproductive self-determination and how does it intersect with cultural, religious, and state interests? Use examples from at least two countries.Hard
Show Answer

Reproductive self-determination: the right of individuals to make free decisions about reproduction without coercion. International recognition: UN ICPD (1994, Cairo), CEDAW. Tensions with state interests: (1) China's one-child policy (1980–2015) — state penalised additional births; forced sterilisations and abortions documented; violated reproductive self-determination. (2) Romania (Ceausescu era): banned contraception and abortion to increase population — women subjected to forced pregnancies; maternal mortality spiked. (3) USA (Dobbs v. Jackson 2022): overturned Roe v. Wade, allowing states to ban abortion — pits state interest in foetal life against women's bodily autonomy. (4) India: sterilisation camps with incentives in rural areas disproportionately pressure poor women. Tensions with religious values: Catholic Church bans contraception; some Muslim scholars restrict certain methods. International human rights framework holds that reproductive autonomy takes precedence over state demographic goals and religious imposition on public policy — though this is contested in practice across political systems.
