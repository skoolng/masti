Fossil Fuel Formation

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What are fossil fuels? Name three examples.Easy
Show Answer

Fossil fuels are fuels formed from the remains of ancient organisms over millions of years under heat and pressure. Examples: coal, petroleum (crude oil), and natural gas.

Why are fossil fuels called "non-renewable" resources?Easy
Show Answer

Fossil fuels take millions of years to form. We are consuming them much faster than they can be replaced, so once used they cannot be replenished within a human timescale.

From what kind of ancient organisms is coal formed?Easy
Show Answer

Coal is formed from ancient land plants (forests, ferns, mosses) that lived and died in swampy environments hundreds of millions of years ago, primarily during the Carboniferous period.

State the two main conditions required for fossil fuel formation.Easy
Show Answer

Fossil fuels form under high heat (from depth underground) and high pressure (from the weight of sediment and rock layers above) over millions of years.

What is carbonisation?Easy
Show Answer

Carbonisation is the slow process by which plant/animal matter is converted to carbon-rich materials (coal, oil) over millions of years under heat and pressure without access to oxygen.

Name the geological era when most coal deposits were formed.Easy
Show Answer

Most coal deposits were formed during the Carboniferous period (approximately 300–360 million years ago) when vast swampy forests covered much of the Earth.

What element is most abundant in fossil fuels?Easy
Show Answer

Carbon is the most abundant element in fossil fuels. Coal is mostly carbon; petroleum and natural gas are hydrocarbons (carbon and hydrogen compounds).

What is a "sedimentary basin" and why are fossil fuels found there?Easy
Show Answer

A sedimentary basin is a large depression where sediment layers accumulated over time. Organic matter gets buried and compressed within these layers, creating the conditions for fossil fuel formation.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain the sequence of events that converts a swampy forest into a coal deposit over millions of years.Medium
Show Answer

Plants die in swamp → buried under sediment without full decomposition (anaerobic conditions) → peat forms → more sediment presses down → pressure increases temperature → peat compresses to lignite → further burial converts to bituminous coal → extreme pressure/temperature converts to anthracite.

Why does fossil fuel formation require the absence of oxygen?Medium
Show Answer

In the presence of oxygen, dead organisms are fully decomposed by bacteria and fungi, releasing CO₂ and water. Without oxygen, decomposition is incomplete — organic carbon is preserved and becomes the building material for fossil fuels.

Compare the timescale of fossil fuel formation to the rate of human consumption. What does this tell us?Medium
Show Answer

Fossil fuels took 100–400 million years to form. Global coal reserves may last ~130 years; oil ~50 years; gas ~55 years at current rates. We are consuming in centuries what took geological ages to form — confirming they are irreplaceable on any practical timescale.

Explain why petroleum is found in porous rock layers between impermeable rock "cap rocks."Medium
Show Answer

Petroleum forms from marine organisms buried in sediment. Being a liquid (less dense than water), it migrates upward through porous rock. When it reaches an impermeable "cap rock" (like shale), it is trapped beneath, accumulating in a reservoir. This geological structure is why oil drilling is targeted at anticlines (upward folds) beneath cap rocks.

Why does coal have different carbon percentages in its different grades?Medium
Show Answer

Carbon percentage increases with depth of burial and age. Peat (~55% C) undergoes progressively greater heat and pressure, driving off hydrogen, oxygen, and water molecules. Anthracite (95%+ C) has undergone the most transformation — nearly pure carbon with almost no other elements.

Why are some regions of India (e.g., Jharkhand, Odisha) particularly rich in coal?Medium
Show Answer

These regions contain the Gondwana sedimentary basins formed 250–300 million years ago when ancient forests covered the Indian subcontinent. The organic matter from these forests was buried and compressed into the Gondwana coal seams that now underlie Jharkhand, Odisha, West Bengal, and Chhattisgarh.

How does the organic matter in petroleum differ from that which forms coal?Medium
Show Answer

Coal forms mainly from land plant material (cellulose, lignin — complex plant polymers). Petroleum forms mainly from marine organisms — tiny plankton, algae, and zooplankton (rich in lipids). The lipid-rich marine origin explains why petroleum is a liquid mixture of hydrocarbons rather than a solid carbon mass.

Describe how seismic surveys help locate underground fossil fuel deposits without drilling.Medium
Show Answer

Seismic surveys send sound waves into the ground (from explosions or vibrators). Waves reflect off different rock layers at different speeds. By analysing reflection patterns, geologists map subsurface rock structures (anticlines, faults) that trap oil and gas, allowing targeted drilling to minimise cost and environmental disturbance.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the ethical principle of "intergenerational equity" in the context of fossil fuel consumption — are we acting fairly towards future generations?Hard
Show Answer

Intergenerational equity (central to sustainable development) requires that current generations meet needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs. Consuming non-renewable fossil fuels destroys a resource permanently. Climate damage from combustion imposes costs (sea level rise, extreme weather) on future generations who made no decision to emit. On this principle, current fossil fuel use is ethically indefensible without rapid transition to renewables.

Analyse how the discovery of fossil fuels triggered the Industrial Revolution and transformed human civilisation — was it overall positive or negative?Hard
Show Answer

Positive: coal-powered steam engines enabled mass manufacturing, transportation, and urbanisation; lifted billions from subsistence poverty; enabled modern medicine, communications, and agriculture. Negative: created colonial extraction economies, severe pollution, climate change, and may now threaten the civilisation it built through ecological collapse. Net assessment: transformatively positive in the short run (200 years) but potentially catastrophic in the long run if energy transition is not achieved.

Using the carbon cycle concept, explain why burning fossil fuels is fundamentally different from burning wood, even though both release CO₂.Hard
Show Answer

Burning wood re-releases carbon absorbed from the atmosphere within decades (if the forest is replanted — net zero new carbon). Burning fossil fuels releases carbon sequestered from the atmosphere 100–400 million years ago — carbon that had been removed from the active carbon cycle. This adds genuinely "new" carbon to the active atmosphere-ocean system, increasing total atmospheric CO₂ beyond the Holocene equilibrium.

Critically assess the claim that "the world has more fossil fuel reserves than we can safely burn" in the context of the 1.5°C climate target.Hard
Show Answer

The carbon budget for 1.5°C is ~420–570 Gt CO₂. Known fossil fuel reserves contain approximately 2,800+ Gt CO₂ if fully burned. This means 80%+ of known reserves must remain unburned to meet climate targets. This creates a "stranded assets" problem — fossil fuel companies have reserves on their books that cannot be monetised without destroying the climate. The claim is accurate and has profound economic and geopolitical implications.

Evaluate the concept of "peak oil" and discuss whether it remains a relevant concern in the era of renewable energy transition.Hard
Show Answer

Peak oil theory: conventional oil production peaks when half the recoverable resource is used, after which production declines. Conventional oil peaked around 2005–2008 in many fields. Relevance today: shale oil and deep-sea extraction extended supply, delaying geological peak. However, climate policy and EV adoption may cause a "demand peak" before supply peak, making conventional peak oil less economically relevant than the energy transition it is being superseded by.

Propose a framework for a national government deciding how quickly to phase out fossil fuel extraction, balancing economic, social, and environmental considerations.Hard
Show Answer

Framework: 1) Climate commitment (align phase-out with Paris Agreement nationally determined contribution). 2) Just transition fund (retrain fossil fuel workers, support affected communities with 10-year social programmes). 3) Economic diversification (replace fossil fuel revenue with renewable energy exports and manufacturing). 4) Technology readiness (accelerate grid storage, offshore wind to ensure energy security during transition). 5) International equity (higher-income countries phase out faster, compensate lower-income countries for foregone development). No single speed fits all countries.

Analyse the geopolitical implications of concentrated fossil fuel reserves — why has the geography of oil and coal shaped global power and conflicts?Hard
Show Answer

Oil is concentrated in the Middle East (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Iran), Russia, and Venezuela — regions that control global energy prices and leverage foreign policy through supply manipulation (OPEC). This concentration has driven colonialism (British in Middle East), military interventions (Gulf War, Iraq War), and economic dependency. Coal geography drove 19th century European imperial power. Renewable energy is geographically distributed (sun and wind everywhere), which could fundamentally democratise energy geopolitics by 2050.

Design a scientific investigation to estimate the energy content of two different grades of coal (lignite and anthracite) using calorimetry.Hard
Show Answer

Equipment: bomb calorimeter, 1g samples each of lignite and anthracite (precisely weighed). Procedure: combust each in oxygen atmosphere within the calorimeter; measure temperature rise in known mass of water (q = mcΔT). Calculate energy per gram. Repeat 3 times for reliability. Expected: anthracite has higher calorific value (~35 MJ/kg) vs lignite (~10–15 MJ/kg) due to higher carbon content. Control: same mass of fuel, same water volume, same thermometer calibration.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

List the four types of coal in order from lowest to highest carbon content.Easy
Show Answer

Peat (lowest, ~55% C) → Lignite (~70% C) → Bituminous coal (~80% C) → Anthracite (highest, ~95% C).

Which type of coal is used in thermal power stations and why?Easy
Show Answer

Bituminous coal is most commonly used in thermal power stations because it is abundant, has high energy content, and burns efficiently to produce steam for electricity generation.

What is coke and how is it different from coal?Easy
Show Answer

Coke is produced by heating bituminous coal in the absence of air (destructive distillation). It is almost pure carbon, burns at higher temperatures than coal, and is used in steelmaking (in blast furnaces).

State the approximate percentage of India's electricity generated from coal.Easy
Show Answer

Approximately 70% of India's electricity is generated from coal-fired thermal power plants, making India one of the world's largest coal consumers.

What is coal tar and what is it used for?Easy
Show Answer

Coal tar is a thick black liquid produced as a byproduct during destructive distillation of coal. It is used to make dyes, medicines, perfumes, synthetic fibres, and as a road surfacing material.

Name the gas produced during destructive distillation of coal that is used as a fuel.Easy
Show Answer

Coal gas (mainly hydrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide) is produced during destructive distillation and was historically used as a fuel for street lights and cooking.

Which type of coal has the highest heating value and is considered the best quality coal?Easy
Show Answer

Anthracite has the highest heating value (~35 MJ/kg), the highest carbon content (~95%), produces the least smoke, and is considered the highest quality coal.

Name two major coal-producing states in India.Easy
Show Answer

Jharkhand and Odisha are two of the largest coal-producing states in India. Chhattisgarh and West Bengal (Jharia coalfield) are also major producers.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Explain why coke is preferred over coal in steel-making blast furnaces.Medium
Show Answer

Coke is ~90–95% pure carbon and is physically strong (can support tonnes of iron ore in a blast furnace without crumbling). It burns at the very high temperatures (1800°C+) needed to melt iron ore, and its carbon monoxide gas (produced in the furnace) acts as the reducing agent that removes oxygen from iron ore to produce pure iron.

Describe what happens during destructive distillation of coal and name three products obtained.Medium
Show Answer

Coal is heated strongly in the absence of air (in a closed retort). Volatile compounds are driven off and collected, while a solid carbon residue remains. Products: 1) Coke (solid residue). 2) Coal tar (thick liquid, collected on cooling). 3) Coal gas (gaseous mixture used as fuel). 4) Ammonia (water-soluble gas used for fertilisers).

Compare lignite and anthracite in terms of formation conditions, carbon content, and energy output.Medium
Show Answer

Lignite: formed at relatively shallow depths, mild pressure, younger age; ~70% C; low calorific value (~10–15 MJ/kg); brown, soft, crumbly; high moisture. Anthracite: deep burial, intense pressure and temperature, very old; ~95% C; high calorific value (~35 MJ/kg); black, hard, shiny; burns with little smoke.

How does coal gas differ from coal tar in terms of physical state, composition, and use?Medium
Show Answer

Coal gas: gaseous mixture (H₂, CH₄, CO); lighter than air; used as a fuel (high energy). Coal tar: viscous black liquid; complex mixture of organic compounds (benzene, naphthalene, phenols); used for chemicals, dyes, medicines, and road surfacing — not primarily as a fuel.

Explain why India's dependence on coal for electricity is a challenge for meeting its climate commitments.Medium
Show Answer

India pledged to reach net-zero emissions by 2070 and achieve 50% renewable electricity by 2030. With 70% coal-dependent electricity generation, rapid transition requires massive investment in solar, wind, and grid infrastructure while managing the economic and social impact of closing coal plants that employ millions in mining communities.

What products derived from coal tar are used in medicine? Explain one example.Medium
Show Answer

Coal tar derivatives include carbolic acid (phenol) used as a disinfectant/antiseptic (Lister's antiseptic surgery pioneer). Aspirin (salicylic acid) and many pharmaceutical compounds were originally derived from coal tar chemistry, forming the foundation of the 19th-20th century pharmaceutical industry.

Why is open-cast coal mining more damaging to the environment than underground mining?Medium
Show Answer

Open-cast (strip) mining removes all overlying vegetation, soil, and rock layers to reach coal seams, destroying entire landscapes. It causes massive habitat loss, erosion, water table disruption, dust pollution, and acid mine drainage. Underground mining has a smaller surface footprint but causes subsidence and occupational health risks.

A student claims "burning anthracite is cleaner than burning lignite." Evaluate this claim with scientific reasoning.Medium
Show Answer

Largely true: anthracite has very high carbon content (~95%), low sulphur and moisture, burns nearly completely producing mainly CO₂ and H₂O. Lignite has higher moisture and sulphur content, producing more SO₂ (acid rain), unburnt particles, and less energy per tonne burned. However, both produce significant CO₂; "cleaner" refers to particulates and SO₂, not carbon emissions.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the just transition challenge facing coal-dependent communities in India as the country moves toward renewable energy.Hard
Show Answer

Jharkhand, Odisha, and Chhattisgarh coal communities employ millions in mining, power plants, and related industries. Rapid coal closure would eliminate livelihoods, collapse local tax bases, and destroy social infrastructure (hospitals, schools funded by mining revenues). A just transition requires: 10-year advance notice, retraining programmes, renewable energy factories sited in mining districts, pension guarantees, and environmental remediation employment. Without this, energy transition creates inequality between those who benefit from renewables and those who bear the transition costs.

Critically assess Coal India Limited's role as the world's largest coal mining company in the context of India's climate commitments.Hard
Show Answer

Coal India (CIL) produces ~80% of India's coal and contributes significantly to government revenue. Increasing CIL production contradicts India's 2030 renewable energy targets and 2070 net-zero commitment. Paradox: India needs coal for energy security and poverty alleviation short-term, but expanding coal mines now means stranded assets when climate policy forces closures. CIL needs a managed decline with diversification into solar manufacturing and coal to chemicals rather than continued expansion of extraction capacity.

Analyse why coal's role in the global economy is unlikely to end quickly despite the climate imperative, using systems thinking.Hard
Show Answer

Coal systems are deeply entrenched: power infrastructure (coal plants have 25–40 year lifespans), supply chains (railways, ports, logistics), political economy (coal lobbying, employment in swing states/regions), and developing country energy access needs. Feedback loops: cheap coal makes renewables relatively less competitive in price-sensitive markets; coal employment creates political resistance; stranded asset fear makes utilities resist transition. Systems change requires simultaneously addressing all feedback loops — technology, finance, policy, and behaviour.

Evaluate "clean coal" technology (carbon capture and storage, CCS) as a genuine climate solution versus greenwashing.Hard
Show Answer

CCS theoretically captures 85–90% of CO₂ from power plant flue gas and stores it geologically. Reality: only 0.1% of global coal power has CCS. CCS adds 25–40% to electricity costs, makes coal power uncompetitive versus solar/wind. Geological storage sites are limited and leak risks are unproven at scale. CCS has been used primarily by fossil fuel industries to extend coal's social licence. Genuine climate scientists favour rapid renewable deployment as faster, cheaper, and more certain than CCS-enabled coal.

Design a comparative lifecycle assessment to determine the true environmental cost of coal electricity versus solar electricity per kilowatt-hour.Hard
Show Answer

LCA boundaries: from resource extraction to decommissioning. Coal: mining (land destruction, dust, water), transport (rail diesel emissions), combustion (CO₂, SO₂, NOₓ, ash), ash disposal (heavy metals). Solar: silicon mining, panel manufacturing (energy-intensive, toxic chemicals), installation, operation (zero emissions), panel recycling. Key metric: carbon footprint (coal ~820 g CO₂e/kWh; solar ~20–50 g CO₂e/kWh). Solar is 20–40× lower carbon even accounting for manufacturing. Coal wins on grid reliability (continuous generation); solar requires battery storage accounting.

Critically evaluate whether India should export its coal reserves to fund its renewable energy transition ("burn coal to build solar").Hard
Show Answer

Argument for: coal export revenue (~$3–5 billion/year) could fund solar subsidies, grid infrastructure, and just transition programmes. Economic pragmatism: if coal will be burned anyway (global demand), better India earns the revenue than loses it to other producers. Argument against: enabling others to burn coal still produces global climate damage India is partly responsible for; carbon border taxes may undermine export viability; India's climate reputation damaged in UNFCCC negotiations; renewable investment better funded through international climate finance than coal exports. Net: morally complex, economically tempting, long-term counterproductive.

Analyse how the coal industry's political lobbying power has delayed climate action globally, using specific examples.Hard
Show Answer

Coal companies (Peabody Energy, Arch Coal, Australian coal lobby) funded climate denial campaigns in the 1980s–2000s. In Australia, coal lobbying contributed to the repeal of the Carbon Price Mechanism (2014). In the US, coal-state senators blocked Obama's Clean Power Plan. In India, coal industry interests have influenced delay of carbon pricing. Unlike tobacco (which harmed only consumers), coal's harm is diffused globally, making its political power proportionally greater — those who benefit from coal are concentrated and organised; those harmed are diffused and global.

Propose how coal-derived chemicals (coal tar products) could continue to provide economic value even after coal combustion is phased out.Hard
Show Answer

Coal tar and derivatives (benzene, naphthalene, phenanthrene) are feedstocks for pharmaceuticals, dyes, plastics, and carbon fibre. As combustion declines, gasification technology can convert coal to syngas (H₂ + CO) used for chemical manufacturing without burning. Coal-to-chemicals is 3–5× higher value use than combustion. Countries could maintain limited coal production for chemical feedstocks while phasing out electricity combustion, preserving some economic value and employment while dramatically reducing carbon emissions from coal use.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What organisms formed petroleum?Easy
Show Answer

Petroleum formed from the remains of microscopic marine organisms — mainly plankton, algae, and small sea creatures — that lived in ancient oceans millions of years ago.

What is crude oil and how is it found underground?Easy
Show Answer

Crude oil (petroleum) is a thick, dark, flammable liquid mixture of hydrocarbons found trapped in porous rock layers between impermeable cap rocks underground or under the sea floor.

Name the technique used to drill for oil and bring it to the surface.Easy
Show Answer

Rotary drilling is used, where a drill bit bores through rock layers. Oil under high underground pressure flows naturally to the surface initially; later, pumps extract remaining oil from the reservoir.

What does "crude" mean in "crude oil"?Easy
Show Answer

"Crude" means raw or unprocessed. Crude oil is petroleum as it comes out of the ground — a mixture of many different hydrocarbons that must be refined to produce useful fuels and chemicals.

Where are petroleum deposits found in India?Easy
Show Answer

Major petroleum deposits in India are found in Mumbai High (offshore, Arabian Sea), Assam (northeast India), Gujarat, and Rajasthan (Barmer district) — and in the Krishna-Godavari basin offshore.

Why is petroleum called a "fossil fuel" even though it comes from tiny marine organisms rather than large fossilised bones?Easy
Show Answer

The term "fossil fuel" refers to fuels formed from the buried, compressed remains of ancient living organisms. The organisms need not be large; the defining feature is formation from organic matter through geological processes over millions of years.

Name the two main elements found in petroleum hydrocarbons.Easy
Show Answer

Carbon (C) and Hydrogen (H) are the two main elements in petroleum hydrocarbons. Petroleum is largely a mixture of hydrocarbons (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ type compounds).

What is an oil spill and name one major oil spill incident in history.Easy
Show Answer

An oil spill is the accidental release of crude oil or petroleum products into the environment, especially into water bodies. Example: Deepwater Horizon (2010) in the Gulf of Mexico released 4.9 million barrels of crude oil.

Set BMedium – Understanding & Application

Describe the geological process by which marine organisms become crude oil over millions of years.Medium
Show Answer

Marine organisms die and sink → buried under sediment → anaerobic bacteria partially decompose organic matter → further burial increases temperature and pressure → "oil window" (60–120°C) converts organic matter (kerogen) into liquid hydrocarbons → crude oil migrates upward through porous rock until trapped by impermeable cap rock.

Why is crude oil a mixture of different hydrocarbons rather than a single compound?Medium
Show Answer

The original organic matter contains many different types of molecules (lipids, proteins, carbohydrates) from diverse marine organisms. Under heat and pressure, these break down into a wide range of hydrocarbon chain lengths (C₁ to C₄₀+). No single chemical transformation produces one compound — the geological process creates a complex mixture.

Explain why petroleum is found in anticlines (upward-folded rock structures) rather than in flat or downward-folded rock layers.Medium
Show Answer

Oil and gas are less dense than water in porous rock. They migrate upward through permeable rock layers. An anticline is an arch-shaped fold — oil migrates to the crest and is trapped when it reaches the impermeable cap rock at the top. Flat or downward-folded structures allow oil to continue migrating rather than accumulating in concentrated reservoirs.

Differentiate between onshore and offshore oil drilling in terms of technology, cost, and environmental risk.Medium
Show Answer

Onshore: simpler infrastructure, lower cost (~$30–60/barrel production cost), lower accident risk, easier spill clean-up. Offshore: complex platforms/floating rigs, high cost (~$60–100/barrel), risk of well blowouts (Deepwater Horizon), oil spills damage marine ecosystems and are extremely difficult to clean up in deep water.

How does hydraulic fracturing (fracking) extract oil and gas from shale rock and what are the associated environmental concerns?Medium
Show Answer

Fracking injects high-pressure water, sand, and chemicals into shale rock, creating fractures that release trapped oil and gas. Environmental concerns: groundwater contamination from fracking fluids, induced seismic activity (small earthquakes), methane leakage, massive water use, and surface disturbance. It has unlocked vast US shale reserves but at significant environmental cost.

Explain how natural gas and oil are often found together in the same reservoir.Medium
Show Answer

Both oil and gas form from similar organic material under heat/pressure. Lighter hydrocarbons (C₁–C₄) become gas; heavier ones become oil. In a reservoir, gas (least dense) sits above oil, which sits above formation water. This three-layer structure (gas cap, oil zone, water zone) is typical in conventional oil and gas reservoirs.

What is a "petrostate" and what economic risks does dependence on oil revenue create?Medium
Show Answer

A petrostate is a country heavily economically dependent on oil exports (e.g., Saudi Arabia, Nigeria, Venezuela). Risks: "resource curse" — oil wealth can undermine other industries (manufacturing declines as exchange rates rise), create corruption, fund authoritarian governance, and leave the economy vulnerable to oil price crashes. When oil prices fall, petrostates face budget crises with no alternative revenue base.

Describe the environmental impact of an oil spill on marine ecosystems, explaining the mechanisms of harm.Medium
Show Answer

Oil spill forms a slick blocking sunlight to phytoplankton. Hydrocarbons are toxic to fish gills and larval development. Oil coats seabird feathers, destroying waterproofing and insulation (birds drown or freeze). Benthic organisms (seabed animals) smothered. PAH (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons) accumulate in food chains. Recovery takes 5–15 years for severely affected ecosystems.

Set CHard – MYP Analysis & Evaluation (Criteria B/D)

Evaluate the claim that the discovery of shale oil has made peak oil irrelevant and that we should not worry about running out of oil.Hard
Show Answer

Shale oil (unconventional) extended US production and global supply, delaying geological peak. However: shale wells decline rapidly (50–70% first year), requiring constant new drilling to maintain output; shale oil is more expensive and more carbon-intensive than conventional. Climate constraints mean 80% of known reserves cannot be burned. "Running out" is no longer the key constraint — burning too much is. Peak oil as a supply problem is less relevant; peak oil as a climate and economic question remains critical.

Analyse how the formation environment of petroleum (marine) versus coal (terrestrial swamp) leads to their different chemical compositions and physical properties.Hard
Show Answer

Marine organisms: rich in lipids (fatty acids — long hydrocarbon chains) and with minimal structural carbohydrates. Under heat they produce liquid hydrocarbons (oils) with low melting points. Terrestrial plants: rich in cellulose and lignin (aromatic, oxygen-containing polymers). Under pressure they produce solid, aromatic carbon-rich coal. The oxygen content of the source material explains why coal has higher oxygen content than petroleum; the lipid-rich marine origin explains petroleum's liquid, non-polar hydrocarbon nature.

Design an investigation to model the migration of oil through porous rock and its trapping in an anticline structure.Hard
Show Answer

Model: construct a transparent tank. Layer: bottom — clay (impermeable), middle — sand (permeable porous rock), top — another clay layer folded into an arch (anticline). Introduce coloured cooking oil mixed with water at one end of the sand layer. Observe: oil migrates upward through sand; water stays lower (density difference); oil accumulates at anticline crest; clay cap blocks further migration. Measure: time to accumulate, volume trapped. Variable: test different anticline angles and sand grain sizes on trapping efficiency.

Critically assess whether Arctic oil drilling should be permitted given the need for energy security and the ecological sensitivity of the region.Hard
Show Answer

For: Arctic holds ~13% of undiscovered oil (90 billion barrels), energy security for consuming nations. Against: fragile ecosystem (unique species, slow recovery from spills in cold water), accelerated Arctic ice melt from drilling infrastructure, shipping accidents impossible to contain in extreme cold, indigenous community rights, and any new development is incompatible with 1.5°C targets. Scientific consensus: Arctic drilling makes no climate sense. Energy security arguments are better addressed through demand reduction and renewables than opening the world's most sensitive frontier.

Evaluate the "resource curse" hypothesis — that oil wealth makes developing countries worse off — using evidence from Nigeria and Norway as contrasting cases.Hard
Show Answer

Nigeria: vast oil wealth concentrated in Niger Delta, corruption endemic, oil revenues fund elite not development, environmental devastation by spills, regional conflict (Biafra-era, Niger Delta militants). Norway: oil wealth managed via sovereign wealth fund ($1.4 trillion), reinvested for future generations, transparent governance, high human development index. Same resource, opposite outcomes. Difference: institutions, governance quality, and decision to save vs spend. Resource curse is not inevitable — it is a governance and institutional failure.

Propose a scientific research programme to develop biological systems that replicate petroleum formation (biopetroleum) as a sustainable alternative.Hard
Show Answer

Research programme: 1) Identify and engineer algae species with maximum lipid output (synthetic biology). 2) Design photobioreactor systems for dense algae cultivation using waste CO₂ from industrial sources. 3) Develop thermochemical conversion (hydrothermal liquefaction) to convert wet algae to crude-oil substitute at pilot scale. 4) Lifecycle analysis to confirm net CO₂ benefit. 5) Scale-up economics assessment. Current challenge: energy for growth exceeds energy output in most current systems. Targeted genetic engineering of lipid biosynthesis pathways is the key bottleneck to solve.

Analyse the geopolitical implications of the world transitioning away from oil — which countries benefit and which face existential economic threats?Hard
Show Answer

Benefit: oil-importing countries (India, Japan, Germany) reduce energy import bills; countries with lithium, cobalt, rare earths (Congo, Chile, Australia) gain strategic importance for EV supply chains; renewable energy exporters (Morocco solar, Australia hydrogen). Threat: Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, UAE), Russia, Nigeria, Venezuela face revenue collapse if demand falls. Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 is a partial response. Countries that diversify early survive; petrostates locked into oil dependence face potential state failure. Energy transition reshapes global power as significantly as oil's discovery did in the 20th century.

Using the principles of green chemistry, evaluate whether petroleum-based plastics can be fully replaced by bioplastics derived from renewable sources.Hard
Show Answer

Current bioplastics (PLA from corn, PHA from bacteria) can replace some single-use packaging. Limitations: PLA requires industrial composting (not home composting), PHA production costs are 3–5× petroleum plastic, and properties (heat resistance, durability) don't match all applications. Full replacement requires: cost reductions in bio-fermentation, development of high-performance bio-based polymers, and systemic infrastructure for collection/composting. Green chemistry principles (renewable feedstocks, biodegradability, reduced toxicity) are met in principle; economic and technical scale-up remains the barrier, not chemistry.

Set AEasy – Knowledge & Recall

What is fractional distillation of crude oil?Easy
Show Answer

Fractional distillation is the process of separating crude oil into its component fractions (products) by heating it and allowing different hydrocarbons to condense at different temperatures in a tall fractionating column.

Name the four main petroleum products and their primary uses.Easy
Show Answer

LPG (cooking and heating), Petrol/Gasoline (cars), Kerosene (aviation fuel, cooking stoves), Diesel (trucks, buses, trains), and Bitumen (road surfacing and waterproofing).

What does LPG stand for and what are its main component gases?Easy
Show Answer

LPG = Liquefied Petroleum Gas. It mainly consists of propane (C₃H₈) and butane (C₄H₁₀), stored under pressure as liquid in cylinders for use as cooking fuel.

Why does petrol evaporate more easily than diesel?Easy
Show Answer

Petrol contains shorter carbon chain hydrocarbons (C₅–C₁₀) with lower boiling points, so they evaporate easily at room temperature. Diesel has longer carbon chains (C₁₂–C₂₀) with higher boiling points and lower volatility.

What is bitumen and what is its most common use?Easy
Show Answer

Explain how the height of the fractionating column relates to the properties of different petroleum fractions collected at different levels.Medium
Show Answer

The column is hottest at the bottom (~350°C) and cooler near the top (~25°C). Heavy fractions (bitumen, fuel oil) with high boiling points condense near the bottom.
