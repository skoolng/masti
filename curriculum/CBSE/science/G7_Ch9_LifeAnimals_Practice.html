SET A Recall & Recognition Easy

What is an autotroph? Give one example.
Show Answer

An autotroph is an organism that makes its own food using sunlight through photosynthesis. Example: green plants (or algae, cyanobacteria).

What is a heterotroph? Give two examples.
Show Answer

A heterotroph cannot make its own food and must eat other organisms. Examples: cow (herbivore), lion (carnivore).

Define the term herbivore and name one animal that is a herbivore.
Show Answer

A herbivore is an animal that feeds only on plants. Example: rabbit, cow, or deer.

What do carnivores eat? Give one example.
Show Answer

Carnivores eat only the flesh of other animals. Example: lion, eagle, or shark.

Name one omnivore and explain why it is classified as an omnivore.
Show Answer

Bear (or human). It eats both plant material (fruits, grains) and animal flesh, so it belongs to both herbivore and carnivore feeding categories.

What is a saprophyte? Give one example.
Show Answer

A saprophyte feeds on dead and decaying organic matter. Example: mushroom (fungus) or certain bacteria.

What is a parasite? Name one animal parasite.
Show Answer

A parasite lives on or inside a host organism and feeds at the host's expense, harming the host. Example: tapeworm or mosquito.

Through which process do autotrophs produce food?
Show Answer

Photosynthesis – they use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make glucose.

List the five types of heterotrophic nutrition mentioned in Chapter 9.
Show Answer

Herbivore, carnivore, omnivore, saprophyte (saprotroph), and parasite.

Is a cow an autotroph or a heterotroph? Justify your answer.
Show Answer

A cow is a heterotroph (specifically a herbivore) because it cannot make its own food and must eat grass and other plants to obtain energy.

SET B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain the difference between a parasite and a saprophyte in terms of the food source they use.
Show Answer

A parasite feeds on living organisms (the host) and causes harm, while a saprophyte feeds on dead or decaying organic matter and does not harm any living organism.

Why are autotrophs called producers in a food chain?
Show Answer

Autotrophs produce (manufacture) their own organic food from inorganic substances using sunlight, so they form the first link of every food chain and are the primary source of energy for all other organisms.

A lion eats a zebra which eats grass. Identify the autotroph and all heterotrophs in this food chain and classify each heterotroph.
Show Answer

Autotroph: grass. Heterotrophs: zebra (herbivore) and lion (carnivore).

Compare the nutrition of a tapeworm (parasite) with that of a mushroom (saprophyte). Include at least two points.
Show Answer

1) Tapeworm feeds on living host tissue/digested food inside a living animal; mushroom feeds on dead/decaying matter. 2) Tapeworm harms the host; mushroom does not harm any living host. 3) Both are heterotrophs that cannot photosynthesize.

Human beings are described as omnivores. What does this mean and why is it an advantage?
Show Answer

Omnivores can eat both plant and animal food. This is advantageous because they can survive on a wider variety of food sources, helping them adapt to different environments where either plant or animal food may be scarce.

A plant absorbs water and carbon dioxide from the environment and releases oxygen. What type of nutrition is this? Write the word equation for this process.
Show Answer

Autotrophic nutrition (photosynthesis). Word equation: Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen (in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll).

Why do saprophytes play an important role in an ecosystem?
Show Answer

Saprophytes break down dead organic matter into simpler inorganic substances, recycling nutrients back into the soil. This decomposition is essential for nutrient cycling and keeping ecosystems clean.

A student says: "All parasites are also heterotrophs." Is this statement correct? Explain.
Show Answer

Yes, the statement is correct. Parasites cannot make their own food; they depend on a living host for nutrition. Since they obtain food from another organism, they are classified as heterotrophs.

Explain why a venus fly trap, which traps and digests insects, is still classified as an autotroph.
Show Answer

A venus fly trap has chlorophyll and can perform photosynthesis, so it produces its own food. It traps insects only to supplement minerals (especially nitrogen) not to get energy, so it is still primarily an autotroph.

Construct a table comparing autotrophs and heterotrophs under three headings: food source, energy source, and example.
Show Answer

Autotroph — food source: self-made via photosynthesis; energy source: sunlight; example: grass. Heterotroph — food source: other organisms; energy source: chemical energy in food consumed; example: rabbit.

SET C MYP Analysis & Extended Response Hard

Evaluate the ecological importance of each type of heterotrophic nutrition (herbivore, carnivore, omnivore, saprophyte, parasite) in maintaining a balanced ecosystem.
Show Answer

Herbivores control plant populations; carnivores control herbivore populations, preventing overgrazing; omnivores add flexibility to food webs; saprophytes decompose dead matter, returning nutrients to the soil; parasites regulate host population sizes and can drive evolutionary adaptations. Together they maintain balance and energy flow through the ecosystem.

A scientist discovers a deep-sea organism that has no access to sunlight but synthesises its own organic compounds using chemical energy from hydrothermal vents. How should this organism be classified? Justify using your knowledge of nutrition types.
Show Answer

This organism is a chemoautotroph – a type of autotroph. Like photosynthetic autotrophs it makes its own food from inorganic substances, but instead of sunlight it uses chemical energy (chemosynthesis). It is not a heterotroph because it does not consume other organisms.

Using MYP Criterion B (Communication in Science), draw and annotate a food web with at least five organisms that illustrates all five types of heterotrophic nutrition.
Show Answer

Accept any well-labelled food web, e.g.: Grass (autotroph) → Rabbit (herbivore) → Fox (carnivore) → Tapeworm in fox (parasite); Grass → Bear (omnivore); Dead rabbit → Mushroom (saprophyte). Full marks for correct arrows showing energy direction, correct labels for each nutrition type, and a brief written explanation.

Analyse how removing all saprophytes from an ecosystem would affect the autotrophs and heterotrophs in that system. Use scientific reasoning.
Show Answer

Without saprophytes, dead matter would accumulate and not decompose. Nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon) would be locked in dead organisms and unavailable to plants. Autotrophs would suffer mineral deficiency, reducing photosynthesis and plant growth. This would reduce food for herbivores, which in turn reduces food for carnivores. The entire ecosystem would collapse due to disrupted nutrient cycling.

A parasite is often described as "successful but harmful." Discuss this statement, explaining how being a parasite is an effective survival strategy and what long-term evolutionary trade-off it creates.
Show Answer

Parasites benefit from a constant, sheltered food source (the host) without expending energy to hunt. This makes them highly successful reproductively. However, killing the host removes their food source, so natural selection favours parasites that weaken but do not kill the host. Over time, many parasite-host relationships evolve toward a less harmful balance (commensalism or even mutualism), illustrating co-evolution.

Compare and contrast autotrophic and heterotrophic nutrition at the cellular level. In your answer, refer to which organelle is involved in autotrophy and which organelle releases energy in heterotrophs.
Show Answer

Autotrophy occurs in the chloroplast, where chlorophyll captures light energy to convert CO₂ and H₂O into glucose. Heterotrophy relies on the mitochondria, where organic molecules obtained from food are broken down via cellular respiration to release ATP energy. Both processes occur inside cells, but autotrophs can build their own substrate while heterotrophs must ingest theirs.

Design a classification key (dichotomous) to distinguish between the five types of heterotrophs. Present it as a series of yes/no questions.
Show Answer

Q1: Does it feed on dead/decaying matter? Yes → Saprophyte. No → Q2. Q2: Does it live on/inside a living host? Yes → Parasite. No → Q3. Q3: Does it eat only plants? Yes → Herbivore. No → Q4. Q4: Does it eat only animals? Yes → Carnivore. No → Omnivore.

Evaluate the claim: "As the human population grows, humanity's shift from carnivore-heavy to more plant-based diets is ecologically essential." Use your knowledge of food chains and energy transfer.
Show Answer

Only about 10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels. Eating plants (lower trophic level) is more energy-efficient than eating animals that ate plants. A plant-based diet supports more people per unit of land. Reducing meat consumption would lower pressure on land, water, and greenhouse gas emissions (from livestock), making it ecologically beneficial as populations grow.

A new disease kills all carnivores in a grassland ecosystem. Predict and explain the chain of ecological consequences using your understanding of nutrition types.
Show Answer

With no carnivores, herbivore populations explode. Overgrazing strips the land of autotrophs (plants). Without plants, herbivores eventually starve, causing their populations to crash. Saprophytes temporarily flourish on dead matter. The ecosystem loses biodiversity and stability, illustrating why each nutrition type is necessary for balance.

Critique this student's statement: "Since fungi break down dead things, they are carnivores." Correct any errors and explain the proper classification with scientific evidence.
Show Answer

The statement is incorrect. Carnivores actively hunt and eat living animals. Fungi feed on dead and decaying organic matter and are therefore saprophytes (saprotrophs), not carnivores. Fungi secrete extracellular digestive enzymes onto dead substrate and absorb the digested products — they never ingest living prey.

SET A Recall & Recognition Easy

List the organs of the human digestive system in the correct order from mouth to anus.
Show Answer

Mouth → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small intestine → Large intestine → Rectum → Anus.

What is the function of the mouth in digestion?
Show Answer

The mouth begins mechanical digestion by chewing (mastication) food into smaller pieces, and begins chemical digestion through saliva (which contains the enzyme amylase).

What does the oesophagus do?
Show Answer

The oesophagus is a muscular tube that pushes chewed food (bolus) from the mouth down to the stomach using wave-like muscle contractions called peristalsis.

Name the two types of digestion that occur in the human body.
Show Answer

Mechanical (physical) digestion — breaking food into smaller pieces by chewing and churning. Chemical digestion — breaking food into small molecules using enzymes.

Where does most nutrient absorption take place in the digestive system?
Show Answer

Most nutrient absorption takes place in the small intestine.

What is the function of the large intestine?
Show Answer

The large intestine absorbs water from undigested food waste, forming faeces (solid waste) ready for elimination.

What is peristalsis?
Show Answer

Peristalsis is the rhythmic, wave-like contraction of muscles in the walls of the digestive tract that pushes food along the digestive system.

What is the role of the rectum?
Show Answer

The rectum stores faeces (waste material) temporarily before it is expelled from the body through the anus.

Name three accessory organs that help in digestion but are not part of the main digestive tube.
Show Answer

Liver, pancreas, and gall bladder (or salivary glands).

What is the bolus?
Show Answer

The bolus is the ball of chewed, moistened food formed in the mouth that is swallowed and moves down the oesophagus.

SET B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain why digestion is necessary. Why can't the body just absorb food directly without breaking it down?
Show Answer

Food molecules (proteins, fats, complex carbohydrates) are too large to pass through the walls of the intestine into the blood. Digestion breaks them into small, soluble molecules (amino acids, fatty acids, glucose) that are small enough to be absorbed into the bloodstream and used by cells.

Describe the journey of a piece of bread from the mouth to absorption in the small intestine, mentioning mechanical and chemical digestion at each stage.
Show Answer

Mouth: teeth chew bread (mechanical); salivary amylase starts breaking starch into maltose (chemical). Oesophagus: peristalsis moves bolus down (mechanical only). Stomach: churning (mechanical); no significant starch digestion. Small intestine: pancreatic amylase completes starch digestion to maltose, then maltase breaks it to glucose; glucose is absorbed through villi into the blood.

Why is the small intestine much longer than the large intestine if absorption is its main job?
Show Answer

A longer tube provides more surface area for absorption. The small intestine also has villi and microvilli (brush border) that further increase surface area, ensuring maximum absorption of nutrients into the blood before the waste reaches the large intestine.

Compare the functions of the stomach and the small intestine.
Show Answer

Stomach: churns food, secretes HCl (kills bacteria, creates acidic pH), secretes pepsin (protein digestion begins), stores food temporarily. Small intestine: receives bile and pancreatic enzymes, completes digestion of all food types, absorbs nutrients into the blood and lymph through villi.

What happens if a person's large intestine does not absorb enough water? What condition might result?
Show Answer

If the large intestine does not absorb enough water, the faeces remain loose and watery, resulting in diarrhoea. This can also cause dehydration if fluids are not replaced.

The epiglottis is a flap at the back of the throat. What is its function during swallowing?
Show Answer

The epiglottis covers the opening of the trachea (windpipe) during swallowing, preventing food from entering the respiratory system (lungs). It directs food into the oesophagus instead.

Explain what villi are and why they are important.
Show Answer

Villi are tiny, finger-like projections on the inner wall of the small intestine. They massively increase the surface area available for nutrient absorption. Each villus contains blood capillaries (for absorbing glucose and amino acids) and lacteal vessels (for absorbing fats).

A person's oesophagus is blocked by a tumour. Describe the effect on digestion and suggest which part of digestion could still work normally.
Show Answer

Food could not pass from the mouth to the stomach, so all digestion from the stomach onwards would be impossible. Only the mechanical and chemical digestion that occurs in the mouth (chewing and salivary amylase on starch) could still function normally.

Explain the difference between egestion and excretion in the context of the digestive system.
Show Answer

Egestion (defecation) is the elimination of undigested food waste (faeces) through the anus — this material was never absorbed into the body. Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste products (e.g., urea, CO₂) produced inside body cells after absorption.

Why do we say digestion involves both physical and chemical processes? Give one example of each.
Show Answer

Physical: chewing in the mouth breaks food into smaller pieces, increasing surface area for enzymes — but does not change the chemical structure. Chemical: amylase in saliva breaks the chemical bonds in starch, converting it to maltose (changes chemical composition). Both types are needed for complete digestion.

SET C MYP Analysis & Extended Response Hard

Evaluate how the structure of the small intestine is adapted for maximum nutrient absorption. Refer to at least three structural features.
Show Answer

1) Long length (~6 m) provides extended contact time. 2) Villi (finger-like projections) vastly increase surface area. 3) Microvilli (brush border) on each epithelial cell further multiply surface area. 4) Rich capillary network in each villus ensures rapid transport of absorbed nutrients into blood. 5) Thin epithelial layer allows short diffusion distance. Together these adaptations maximise absorption efficiency.

A patient is diagnosed with coeliac disease, which destroys the villi of the small intestine. Predict and explain the consequences for nutrition, health, and body functions.
Show Answer

Without villi, the surface area for absorption is drastically reduced. The patient would experience malnutrition: insufficient glucose causes fatigue; lack of amino acids impairs growth and repair; lack of fats reduces hormone production and cell membrane integrity; vitamin and mineral deficiencies cause further problems (anaemia from lack of iron, weakened bones from lack of calcium). Weight loss and developmental problems would occur, especially in children.

Design a controlled experiment to test whether saliva is necessary for starch digestion. Include hypothesis, variables, method, and expected results.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Saliva (amylase) is required to break down starch. IV: Presence/absence of saliva. DV: Presence of starch (tested with iodine). Control: tube with starch + water only. Method: Tube A — starch solution + saliva, 37°C, 5 min. Tube B — starch solution + water (control), 37°C, 5 min. Test both with iodine solution. Expected: Tube A turns yellow/orange (starch digested); Tube B turns blue-black (starch present). This supports the hypothesis.

Compare the digestive systems of a cow (ruminant) and a human, explaining how structural differences relate to their different diets.
Show Answer

Cows are herbivores that eat grass (cellulose), which humans cannot digest. Cows have a four-chambered stomach (rumen, reticulum, omasum, abomasum) and host cellulose-digesting bacteria in the rumen; they also regurgitate and re-chew food (rumination). Humans have a single-chambered stomach and lack cellulase enzymes. These differences reflect dietary adaptation: cows extract nutrients from tough plant material while humans rely on more easily digestible cooked foods.

Using MYP Criterion D (Reflecting on the Impacts of Science), discuss how understanding the digestive system has led to improvements in medical treatments for digestive disorders.
Show Answer

Understanding digestion has enabled: antacid medications to treat excess stomach acid (ulcers); enzyme replacement therapy for pancreatic insufficiency; probiotic therapies to restore gut microbiome; surgical interventions like bowel resection; dietary management of conditions like coeliac disease. This shows how scientific knowledge directly improves human health and quality of life, with ethical implications for access to healthcare.

Explain how the concept of surface area to volume ratio applies to the design of the digestive system. Why is this ratio important?
Show Answer

As volume increases, the internal surface area must increase proportionally to serve it. The digestive system maximises SA:V ratio through folding (rugae in stomach), villi, and microvilli, allowing a compact organ system to have an enormous absorptive surface (~200 m²). A high SA:V ratio means more absorption sites per unit volume, making digestion efficient despite the body's compact size.

A student claims: "The large intestine is not important because digestion is already finished by the time food reaches it." Critique this statement using scientific evidence.
Show Answer

The statement is partly correct (most digestion is complete) but greatly understimates the large intestine's importance. It: 1) absorbs water, preventing dehydration; 2) absorbs electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺); 3) houses trillions of beneficial gut bacteria that produce vitamins K and B12; 4) stores and compacts faeces; 5) its failure causes diarrhoea and dehydration. Therefore it is critically important to health.

Trace the fate of a protein molecule from a chicken breast through the entire digestive system, specifying the enzyme and product at each stage, until the amino acids enter a muscle cell.
Show Answer

Mouth: no protein digestion. Stomach: pepsin (activated by HCl) hydrolyses protein into peptides. Small intestine: pancreatic proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin) break peptides further; peptidases on villus surface produce amino acids. Absorption: amino acids cross villus epithelium into blood capillaries. Blood transport: portal vein → liver (regulates amino acid levels) → general circulation → muscle cells. In muscle: amino acids are used for protein synthesis (muscle growth/repair).

Evaluate the effect of a high-fibre diet on the function of the digestive system, using your knowledge of the large intestine and gut motility.
Show Answer

Dietary fibre (cellulose) is not digested by human enzymes but absorbs water, softening and bulking faeces. This stimulates peristalsis, speeding transit time through the colon. Benefits include: prevention of constipation, reduced risk of colon cancer (waste moves through quickly, limiting contact of carcinogens with colon wall), improved gut microbiome diversity. A low-fibre diet slows transit, increases cancer risk, and causes constipation.

The digestive system is sometimes called a "tube within a tube." Explain this analogy and describe what happens to food that is never actually "inside" the body in the true biological sense.
Show Answer

The digestive tract is a continuous hollow tube that runs from mouth to anus, passing through the body. Food inside this tube is technically outside body cells — it is in the external environment of the gut lumen. Only once nutrients are absorbed across the intestinal wall do they truly enter the body's internal environment (blood, cells). Undigested matter that passes through without being absorbed is egested, never having entered the body at the cellular level. This illustrates that absorption, not digestion, constitutes entry into the body.

SET A Recall & Recognition Easy

What enzyme is found in saliva, and what does it digest?
Show Answer

Salivary amylase. It begins the digestion of starch (a complex carbohydrate), breaking it down into maltose.

Name the enzyme produced in the stomach that begins protein digestion.
Show Answer

Pepsin. It breaks proteins into shorter polypeptide chains.

What acid is produced in the stomach and what is its approximate pH?
Show Answer

Hydrochloric acid (HCl). The stomach has a pH of approximately 2, making it strongly acidic.

What organ produces bile and where is bile stored?
Show Answer

Bile is produced in the liver and stored in the gall bladder before being released into the small intestine (duodenum).

What does bile do to fats?
Show Answer

Bile emulsifies fats — it breaks large fat droplets into smaller droplets, increasing the surface area for lipase enzymes to act on.

Name two enzymes produced by the pancreas.
Show Answer

Pancreatic amylase (digests starch) and pancreatic lipase (digests fats). Also acceptable: trypsin (digests proteins).

What are the final products of carbohydrate digestion?
Show Answer

Simple sugars (monosaccharides) — mainly glucose.

What are the final products of protein digestion?
Show Answer

Amino acids.

What are the final products of fat (lipid) digestion?
Show Answer

Fatty acids and glycerol.

Why does the stomach need to be acidic (pH 2)?
Show Answer

The acidic environment activates pepsin (which works best at low pH), kills harmful bacteria and pathogens in food, and begins the denaturation of food proteins to make them easier to digest.

SET B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain why pancreatic juice is described as a "complete digestive fluid."
Show Answer

Pancreatic juice contains enzymes for all three food types: amylase (carbohydrates), lipase (fats), and proteases such as trypsin (proteins). It also contains sodium bicarbonate to neutralise stomach acid, creating the slightly alkaline pH needed for these enzymes to work in the small intestine.

Describe the role of HCl in the stomach beyond creating an acidic environment for pepsin.
Show Answer

HCl: 1) activates the inactive enzyme pepsinogen into active pepsin; 2) kills most bacteria and pathogens ingested with food; 3) partially denatures protein, making it more accessible to enzymes; 4) stops salivary amylase activity (which works optimally at neutral pH).

Explain why bile is not classified as an enzyme, yet it is essential for fat digestion.
Show Answer

Bile is a detergent-like substance (emulsifier), not an enzyme — it does not break chemical bonds. Instead it physically breaks fat globules into tiny droplets (emulsification), vastly increasing the surface area exposed to lipase. Without bile, lipase can only act on fat surfaces, making digestion very slow and inefficient.

A student eats a meal containing starch, protein, and fat. For each nutrient, name the first enzyme that acts on it and where this occurs.
Show Answer

Starch: salivary amylase in the mouth. Protein: pepsin in the stomach. Fat: lingual/pancreatic lipase — significant fat digestion begins in the small intestine (minimal in mouth); bile emulsification occurs in the small intestine before lipase acts.

What is chyme? Where is it produced and where does it go?
Show Answer

Chyme is the thick, semi-liquid mixture of partially digested food and gastric juice formed in the stomach. It is released in small amounts through the pyloric sphincter into the duodenum (first part of the small intestine) for further digestion.

Why must the stomach be protected from its own acid? How does it protect itself?
Show Answer

The stomach secretes HCl and pepsin which would digest its own walls. It is protected by a thick mucus layer secreted by goblet cells lining the stomach wall. This mucus acts as a barrier between the acidic contents and the stomach epithelium. If this protection fails, gastric ulcers result.

Why does digestion in the small intestine occur at a slightly alkaline pH rather than the acidic pH of the stomach?
Show Answer

The enzymes in the small intestine (pancreatic amylase, lipase, trypsin) work optimally at slightly alkaline pH (around 7–8). The pancreas releases sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃) into the duodenum to neutralise the acidic chyme from the stomach, creating the correct environment for these enzymes.

Describe what happens when someone has gallstones that block the release of bile. What digestive problems would result?
Show Answer

Without bile reaching the small intestine, fat emulsification does not occur. Lipase has very little fat surface to work on, so fats are largely undigested. The person would have fatty, greasy stools (steatorrhoea), deficiency in fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), and abdominal pain. Jaundice may also occur as bile backs up into the blood.

How are glucose and amino acids absorbed across the wall of the small intestine and transported to the liver?
Show Answer

Glucose and amino acids are absorbed by active transport and diffusion across the epithelial cells of the villi into the blood capillaries within each villus. They travel via the hepatic portal vein to the liver, where they are processed before entering the general circulation.

How do fatty acids and glycerol differ from glucose in their absorption pathway?
Show Answer

Fatty acids and glycerol are absorbed into the lacteals (lymph vessels) inside villi, not into blood capillaries directly. They re-combine into triglycerides, enter the lymphatic system, and are transported through the thoracic duct before entering the bloodstream near the heart — bypassing the portal vein and liver initially.

SET C MYP Analysis & Extended Response Hard

Design an experiment to investigate the effect of pH on amylase activity. Include hypothesis, independent variable, dependent variable, and controls.
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Amylase works optimally at pH 7 (neutral). IV: pH of buffer solution (e.g., pH 2, 4, 7, 9, 11). DV: Time for starch to be completely digested (tested with iodine every 30 s). Controls: constant temperature (37°C), same concentration of amylase and starch, same volume. Expected: fastest digestion at pH 7; slowest at extremes (pH 2 or 11) as enzyme denatures. This tests the enzyme's optimal pH.

Pepsinogen is the inactive form of pepsin. Explain why enzymes are sometimes secreted in an inactive form and the advantage this provides to the stomach.
Show Answer

Secreting pepsin in inactive form (pepsinogen) prevents the enzyme from digesting the protein-rich cells of the stomach wall before secretion. HCl activates pepsinogen by cleaving a portion of the molecule, exposing the active site only when it is safely in the acidic stomach lumen. This is a safety mechanism called zymogen activation, protecting the secretory cells from self-digestion.

Compare the digestion of a carbohydrate vs. a fat in terms of enzymes involved, location of digestion, products formed, and absorption pathway.
Show Answer

Carbohydrate (starch): Amylase (saliva + pancreatic) → maltose; maltase (brush border) → glucose. Absorbed into blood capillaries of villi → portal vein → liver. Fat: Emulsification by bile (duodenum); lipase (pancreatic) → fatty acids + glycerol. Absorbed into lacteals of villi → lymphatic system → bloodstream via thoracic duct. Key difference: glucose enters bloodstream directly; fats enter lymph first.

A patient's pancreas is surgically removed. Predict in detail how each type of nutrient (carbohydrate, protein, fat) would be digested and what deficiencies the patient would develop.
Show Answer

Carbohydrate: salivary amylase still acts in mouth, but without pancreatic amylase only partial digestion occurs. Protein: stomach pepsin begins digestion, but without trypsin/chymotrypsin proteins are incompletely digested → amino acid deficiency → poor growth/repair. Fat: bile still emulsifies fat, but without pancreatic lipase fats are largely undigested → fatty stool, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K), essential fatty acid deficiency. Patient would need enzyme replacement therapy and dietary supplements.

Evaluate the importance of enzyme specificity in digestion. What would happen if digestive enzymes were not specific?
Show Answer

Enzyme specificity (lock-and-key model) means each enzyme only digests one type of substrate (amylase only starch, lipase only fats, etc.). This is crucial for: 1) controlled digestion — each nutrient type is broken down by its dedicated enzyme at the right time and place; 2) preventing enzymes from digesting inappropriate substrates or body structures; 3) efficiency — enzymes are not "wasted" on wrong substrates. Without specificity, uncontrolled digestion of body tissues could occur.

Using enzyme theory (active site, substrate, enzyme-substrate complex), explain how amylase digests starch and why this process is affected by high temperature.
Show Answer

Starch (substrate) fits into the active site of amylase (specific complementary shape) forming an enzyme-substrate complex. The enzyme catalyses hydrolysis, breaking glycosidic bonds to release maltose. The enzyme is unchanged and can be reused. High temperature (above ~45°C for human amylase): increased kinetic energy distorts the active site through excessive vibration, changing its shape so starch can no longer fit → enzyme is denatured → digestion stops irreversibly.

Explain the role of the liver in nutrient metabolism after absorption from the small intestine, covering at least three functions.
Show Answer

1) Glucose regulation: converts excess glucose to glycogen (glycogenesis) for storage; converts glycogen back to glucose when blood sugar is low (glycogenolysis). 2) Amino acid processing: deaminates excess amino acids, converting the nitrogen portion to urea (sent to kidneys) and the carbon skeleton to glucose or fat. 3) Fat metabolism: synthesises lipoproteins for fat transport, produces cholesterol, and generates ketone bodies during fasting. 4) Produces bile from broken-down haemoglobin. The liver is the central metabolic hub for all absorbed nutrients.

Some diet plans advocate "food combining" — not eating carbohydrates and proteins at the same meal, claiming enzymes cannot work on both simultaneously. Using your scientific knowledge, evaluate this claim.
Show Answer

The claim is scientifically unfounded. The digestive system simultaneously secretes carbohydrase (amylase) and protease (pepsin, trypsin) enzymes. Each enzyme is specific — amylase only acts on starch and pepsin only on proteins — so there is no competition for substrates. The stomach, small intestine, and pancreas produce both sets of enzymes during every meal. Food combining has no physiological basis; mixed meals are digested efficiently due to enzyme specificity.

Helicobacter pylori is a bacterium that causes stomach ulcers by breaking down the protective mucus layer. Using your knowledge of stomach physiology, explain the cascade of damage that results.
Show Answer

H. pylori secretes urease (breaks down mucus barrier) and produces ammonia, which neutralises local acid. Without mucus: HCl and pepsin directly contact the stomach wall → protein digestion of epithelial cells → erosion of stomach lining → ulcer formation. Damaged blood vessels bleed → anaemia. Nerve endings exposed → severe pain. If untreated, the ulcer can perforate the stomach wall → peritonitis (life-threatening). Treatment: antibiotics + antacids to heal the lining.

Critically evaluate why the small intestine, not the stomach, is the key organ for both digestion and absorption, considering structural and chemical adaptations.
Show Answer

Structural: villi and microvilli increase SA to ~200 m²; long length (~6 m); rich capillary and lymphatic supply in villi. In contrast, the stomach only partially digests proteins and has no absorptive structures. The small intestine's combination of complete digestion AND specialised absorption structures makes it the functional centre of nutrition.

Arteries, veins, and capillaries.

Arteries carry blood away from the heart.

Veins carry blood back to the heart.

What is the aorta?

The aorta is the largest artery in the body. It carries oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the rest of the body.

What is blood pressure?

Compare the structure and function of arteries and veins. Include wall thickness, presence of valves, and blood pressure.
Show Answer

Arteries: thick muscular elastic walls, no valves, high blood pressure, carry blood away from heart. Veins: thin walls, have valves (prevent backflow), low blood pressure, carry blood to heart. Both have three layers (tunica intima, media, externa) but arteries have thicker tunica media due to higher pressure.

Explain why the left ventricle has a thicker muscular wall than the right ventricle.
Show Answer

The left ventricle must pump blood to the entire body (systemic circulation), a much longer distance requiring higher pressure. The right ventricle only pumps blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation), which is nearby and requires lower pressure. Greater pumping force needs thicker muscle walls.

What is the role of capillaries in the circulatory system? Why are they adapted for this role?
Show Answer

Capillaries are the site of exchange between blood and body cells — they deliver O₂ and nutrients and collect CO₂ and waste. They are adapted by: being only one cell thick (thin wall for short diffusion distance), forming dense networks (short distance to every cell), and having a very large combined surface area for efficient exchange.

What are valves in the heart and why are they necessary?
Show Answer

Valves are flap-like structures between the heart chambers and at the base of major arteries. They open to let blood flow in one direction and close to prevent backflow. Without valves, blood would flow backwards, making the heart inefficient — like a pump with no one-way mechanism.

What is a "pulse" and where on the body can it be felt? What does it indicate?
Show Answer

The pulse is the rhythmic expansion and recoil of artery walls each time the heart beats. It can be felt where arteries pass close to the skin surface, such as the wrist (radial artery) or neck (carotid artery). The pulse rate equals the heart rate and indicates the number of times the heart beats per minute.

Explain what happens to heart rate during exercise and why this is necessary.
Show Answer

Heart rate increases during exercise because muscles need more oxygen and glucose for increased aerobic respiration. A faster heart rate pumps more blood per minute (increases cardiac output), delivering more O₂ and nutrients to working muscles and removing CO₂ and lactic acid more quickly.

A person has high blood pressure (hypertension). Explain two long-term risks to health that this creates.
Show Answer

1) Atherosclerosis: high pressure damages artery walls, promoting fatty plaque (atheroma) deposits, narrowing arteries and increasing risk of heart attack or stroke. 2) Aneurysm: weak spots in artery walls may balloon outward and rupture, causing internal bleeding. Both conditions can be fatal if untreated.

What is the coronary circulation, and why is it essential for the heart?
Show Answer

The coronary arteries supply the heart muscle (myocardium) itself with oxygenated blood. The heart cannot extract oxygen from the blood inside its chambers, so it needs its own dedicated blood supply. Blockage of coronary arteries (coronary artery disease) causes a heart attack (myocardial infarction) because heart cells are deprived of oxygen and die.

Explain why the circulatory system is described as a "transport system" for the body.
Show Answer

The circulatory system transports: O₂ from lungs to cells; CO₂ from cells to lungs; nutrients (glucose, amino acids, fats) from intestines to cells; waste products (urea) from liver to kidneys; hormones from endocrine glands to target organs; heat to regulate body temperature; immune cells and antibodies to sites of infection. It is the body's primary distribution and collection network.

Show Answer

Show Answer

Arteries are under high pressure from the heart's pumping — blood moves forward forcefully with no risk of backflow. Veins have low pressure (especially in the legs, below the heart) and rely on skeletal muscle contractions and breathing movements to push blood upward. Valves prevent backflow under gravity. The sinoatrial (SA) node is called the "pacemaker" of the heart. Explain its role, and describe what happens when it malfunctions and an artificial pacemaker is fitted.
Show Answer

The SA node in the right atrium generates regular electrical impulses (~70/min) that spread across both atria (causing contraction), then to the atrioventricular (AV) node, then down the bundle of His and Purkinje fibres to the ventricles (causing ventricular contraction). This coordinates the heartbeat. SA node malfunction → irregular or too-slow heartbeat (bradycardia/arrhythmia) → insufficient cardiac output → dizziness, fainting. Analyse the differences between the circulatory systems of insects (open) and humans (closed), and evaluate the advantages of a closed system for large, active organisms.
Show Answer

Insect open system: haemolymph flows freely in body cavity (haemocoel); heart pumps fluid but no capillaries deliver directly to cells; O₂ delivered by separate tracheal system. Human closed system: blood stays within vessels at all times; capillaries reach every cell; high pressure maintained; rapid, directed delivery possible. Advantages for large animals: 1) higher pressure allows delivery to distant cells; 2) precise routing of blood to active organs; 3) rapid response to exercise demands; 4) efficient O₂ delivery (impossible by diffusion alone in a large body). Insects' open system works only because they are small and trachea deliver O₂ directly.

Why do red blood cells lack a nucleus?

Red blood cells lose their nucleus during maturation to make room for more haemoglobin, maximising oxygen-carrying capacity. The biconcave disc shape also increases surface area for gas diffusion.

What colour is oxygenated blood and why?

Oxygenated blood is bright red because haemoglobin combined with oxygen (oxyhaemoglobin) has a bright red colour. Deoxygenated blood is dark red/maroon.

Where are red blood cells produced in the adult human body?

Explain how the biconcave disc shape of red blood cells is an adaptation for efficient oxygen transport.
Show Answer

The biconcave disc shape: 1) increases surface area relative to volume, allowing more O₂ to diffuse in/out quickly; 2) creates a thin centre so no haemoglobin molecule is far from the cell surface (short diffusion distance); 3) makes cells flexible enough to squeeze through narrow capillaries. Distinguish between phagocytes and lymphocytes in terms of how they combat infection.

Phagocytes (e.g., neutrophils, macrophages) engulf and digest pathogens by phagocytosis — a non-specific response. Lymphocytes produce specific antibodies that bind to antigens on pathogens, neutralising them or marking them for destruction; they also produce memory cells for long-term immunity. Phagocytosis is immediate; lymphocyte response takes days but provides lasting protection.

Explain what anaemia is, what causes it, and how it affects the body.
Show Answer

Causes: iron deficiency (haemoglobin needs iron), vitamin B12 deficiency, blood loss, or destruction of RBCs (e.g., sickle cell anaemia). Effects: fatigue, shortness of breath, pale skin, dizziness — all because body tissues receive inadequate O₂.

How does the immune system produce "memory" after an infection, and why does this mean a second infection is less severe?
Show Answer

During the first infection, B-lymphocytes produce antibodies against the specific pathogen. Some B-cells become long-lived memory cells. During a second infection with the same pathogen, memory cells recognise the antigen immediately and rapidly produce large quantities of antibodies — much faster and in greater amounts than the first response. This clears the infection before symptoms become severe (secondary immune response).

Plasma makes up 55% of blood volume. List five different substances carried in plasma and state where each is going.
Show Answer

1) Glucose — from intestine to all body cells for respiration. 2) Urea — from liver to kidneys for excretion. 3) Hormones (e.g., insulin) — from pancreas to target cells. 4) Carbon dioxide — from body cells to lungs for excretion. 5) Amino acids — from intestine to cells for protein synthesis.

Show Answer

A high WBC count indicates the body is fighting an infection (bacterial or viral) or experiencing an inflammatory response. In some cases it may indicate leukaemia (blood cancer), in which abnormal WBCs multiply uncontrollably. Doctors use WBC count to diagnose infections and monitor disease progression.

Explain what happens to haemoglobin at high altitudes where oxygen levels are lower, and how the body adapts.
Show Answer

At high altitude, partial pressure of O₂ is lower, so haemoglobin picks up less O₂ in the lungs. This means less O₂ is delivered to tissues → fatigue, headache (altitude sickness). The body adapts by: increasing breathing rate, increasing RBC production (via erythropoietin hormone), and producing haemoglobin with higher O₂ affinity. Over weeks, blood carries more RBCs to compensate for lower O₂ availability.

Analyse the structure of haemoglobin and explain precisely how it loads oxygen in the lungs and unloads it in active tissues. Refer to the concept of oxygen dissociation.

Which gas is breathed in and which gas is breathed out during gas exchange?

What is the role of the diaphragm in breathing?

The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle below the lungs. When it contracts it flattens, increasing the volume of the chest cavity and causing air to rush in (inhalation). When it relaxes it domes upward, decreasing chest volume and pushing air out (exhalation).

What are the cilia and mucus in the trachea for?

Mucus traps dust, bacteria, and other particles from inhaled air. Cilia (tiny hair-like projections) sweep the mucus and trapped particles upward toward the throat to be swallowed or expelled, keeping the lungs clean.

What is the difference between breathing and respiration?
Show Answer

Breathing (ventilation) is the physical process of moving air in and out of the lungs. Respiration is the chemical process in cells where oxygen is used to release energy from glucose (cellular respiration). Breathing supplies the oxygen for respiration.

Name the two main bronchi and which organ do they lead to?

The right bronchus leads to the right lung and the left bronchus leads to the left lung. They branch from the trachea.

What are the intercostal muscles and where are they located?

Intercostal muscles are muscles located between the ribs. They help expand and contract the rib cage during breathing — external intercostals lift the ribs during inhalation; internal intercostals lower them during exhalation.

Why is the trachea reinforced with rings of cartilage?
Show Answer

The C-shaped cartilage rings prevent the trachea from collapsing when air pressure inside drops during inhalation. They keep the airway permanently open while allowing flexibility for swallowing.

Describe the mechanism of inhalation (breathing in), referring to the diaphragm, intercostal muscles, and rib cage.

Diaphragm contracts and flattens; external intercostal muscles contract, pulling ribs upward and outward. Both actions increase the volume of the thoracic cavity. Explain how alveoli are adapted for efficient gas exchange. Give at least four structural features.

Explain the difference between respiration and breathing. Why is it incorrect to say "we breathe to get energy"?

Breathing is the mechanical process of ventilating the lungs (physical movement of the chest). Respiration is the chemical process inside cells where glucose is broken down using oxygen to release ATP. Explain why aerobic respiration releases more energy than anaerobic respiration from the same amount of glucose.
Show Answer

Aerobic respiration completely oxidises glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) to CO₂ and H₂O, breaking all chemical bonds and releasing ~38 ATP per glucose molecule. Anaerobic respiration only partially breaks down glucose (to lactic acid or ethanol + CO₂), releasing only 2 ATP per glucose. The incomplete breakdown means most of the energy remains locked in the partially oxidised products.

Why do cells need a continuous supply of glucose and oxygen for aerobic respiration? What happens if either runs out?
Show Answer

Glucose and O₂ are consumed in every cycle of respiration to produce ATP. Cells must continuously make ATP (it cannot be stored in large quantities) for ongoing activities. If glucose runs out → cells switch to breaking down fats, then proteins → starvation. Explain why the mitochondria are described as "the powerhouse of the cell" and describe their structure briefly.
Show Answer

Mitochondria are the primary sites of ATP production via aerobic respiration, generating nearly all usable energy for the cell — hence "powerhouse." Structure: double membrane (outer and inner); inner membrane is highly folded into cristae (greatly increasing surface area for enzyme reactions); central fluid-filled matrix contains enzymes for the Krebs cycle. This elaborate structure maximises the rate of ATP production.

How is the CO₂ produced by respiration removed from body cells and eventually exhaled?
Show Answer

Blood carries CO₂ to the lungs, where it diffuses from blood into the alveoli down the concentration gradient, then is exhaled.

Show Answer

The statement is incorrect. Plants carry out BOTH photosynthesis AND cellular respiration. Photosynthesis occurs only in green parts in the presence of light. Explain, step by step, the three main stages of aerobic respiration (glycolysis, Krebs cycle, oxidative phosphorylation) and where each occurs in the cell.
Show Answer

Evaluate the biological importance of ATP as an energy carrier compared with simply releasing energy directly from glucose. Why is ATP a better energy currency?
Show Answer

Biofuels: bioethanol from sugar/maize fermentation — used as petrol substitute; reduces fossil fuel dependence and CO₂ emissions. Industrial enzymes (proteases, amylases) produced by fermentation for detergents and food industry. Fermentation is arguably the most economically important biotechnological process in human history.

Some organisms can switch between aerobic and anaerobic respiration depending on O₂ availability — they are called facultative anaerobes. Evaluate the survival advantage this flexibility provides and give two examples.
Show Answer

Facultative anaerobes (e.g., yeast, E. coli, some muscle cells) can survive in both O₂-rich and O₂-poor environments. Survival advantages: 1) can exploit anoxic (no-oxygen) niches unavailable to obligate aerobes (e.g., deep in soil, inside fermentation vessels, in waterlogged environments); 2) can maintain ATP production when O₂ is temporarily unavailable (e.g., during peak exercise); 3) competitive advantage — they can outcompete obligate anaerobes when O₂ arrives by switching to more efficient aerobic respiration. Examples: Yeast (aerobic in presence of O₂, ferments when O₂ is absent); Saccharomyces cerevisiae; human skeletal muscle (aerobic normally, anaerobic during sprinting).

Evaluate the ethical and environmental implications of large-scale bioethanol production from crops such as maize or sugarcane.
Show Answer

Benefits: renewable energy source; reduces fossil fuel use; CO₂ released during combustion was recently absorbed by the crop (carbon neutral in theory); creates rural employment. Problems: food vs fuel debate — cropland for fuel competes with food production → raises food prices, threatening food security especially in developing nations. Intensive farming causes: deforestation (for new farmland), pesticide use, water depletion, soil degradation. Energy balance: some studies show bioethanol production (fertiliser, harvesting, distillation) uses nearly as much energy as it produces. Ethical: when biofuel corporations buy land in Africa or Asia, local smallholders may be displaced. Second-generation biofuels from waste cellulose (not food crops) address some concerns but face technical barriers.

Compare the structure of the word equations for aerobic and anaerobic respiration in humans and yeast. What does the comparison reveal about the level of oxidation of glucose in each?
Show Answer

Aerobic (all): glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + 38 ATP. Glucose is completely oxidised (all carbons released as CO₂; all hydrogens combined with O₂ to form water). Human anaerobic: glucose → lactic acid + 2 ATP. Glucose partially oxidised; carbons and H atoms remain in lactic acid — energy still locked in molecule. Yeast anaerobic: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP. Partially oxidised; some C atoms released as CO₂ but carbons and H remain in ethanol. Comparison reveals: aerobic extracts 19× more ATP because complete oxidation releases all bond energy; anaerobic is inefficient because much energy remains trapped in the organic by-products.

Explain the physiological concept of the "lactate threshold" in endurance athletes, and evaluate how training above this threshold can shift it higher.
Show Answer

The lactate threshold (LT) is the exercise intensity at which lactic acid begins to accumulate in the blood because production exceeds the body's rate of clearance. Below LT: aerobic respiration dominates, lactate is cleared as fast as it is produced. Above LT: anaerobic contribution increases, lactate accumulates → fatigue. Elite endurance athletes have high LTs (up to 85% of VO₂max vs ~55% in untrained people). Training above LT (tempo training, interval training) shifts LT higher by: increasing mitochondrial density (more aerobic capacity); improving blood vessel density in muscles; increasing lactate clearance enzymes in liver and heart; improving O₂ delivery. A higher LT means the athlete can work harder before fatigue, directly improving endurance performance.

Analyse why some cancers switch to anaerobic-like respiration even in the presence of abundant oxygen (the Warburg effect). What does this suggest about the metabolic needs of rapidly dividing cells?
Show Answer

The Warburg effect: cancer cells preferentially use glycolysis followed by lactate production (aerobic glycolysis) even when O₂ is plentiful. This seems energetically inefficient. Why? Rapidly dividing cells need not just ATP but also biosynthetic building blocks: pyruvate and glycolytic intermediates are used to synthesise amino acids, nucleotides, and lipids for new cell membranes and DNA. Aerobic glycolysis generates these precursors faster than the full TCA cycle would allow while still producing sufficient ATP for the cell's needs. The Warburg effect is therefore a metabolic switch from energy maximisation to biosynthesis maximisation. This insight has led to anti-cancer drug targets (glycolysis inhibitors) and PET scan imaging (cancer cells take up more glucose, visible as hot spots).

Heart muscle cells (cardiomyocytes) are almost exclusively aerobic and have very limited anaerobic capacity. Explain why this adaptation, while usually advantageous, becomes fatal during a heart attack.
Show Answer

Cardiomyocytes have the highest mitochondrial density of any human cell (~35% cell volume = mitochondria) and very efficient aerobic respiration — the heart works continuously for 70+ years. This is advantageous because it avoids lactic acid accumulation in the heart (which would impair the electrical activity governing heartbeat). During a heart attack (coronary artery blockage), O₂ supply stops. Without aerobic ATP and with minimal anaerobic backup, cardiomyocytes rapidly run out of ATP (within minutes). Without ATP they cannot maintain ion gradients → electrical disturbances (arrhythmia) → cells die (infarction) within 20–40 minutes. The very adaptation that makes the heart reliable under normal conditions makes it uniquely vulnerable to ischaemia.

Explain how sports drinks containing carbohydrates and electrolytes help athletes maintain performance during prolonged anaerobic-aerobic mixed exercise. Refer to specific physiological mechanisms.
Show Answer

During prolonged exercise: liver glycogen stores deplete after ~90 min → blood glucose falls → brain and muscles starved of aerobic substrate → fatigue. Sports drinks containing glucose/maltodextrin rapidly replenish blood glucose → sustain aerobic and anaerobic respiration → maintain ATP production → delay fatigue. Electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺) replace those lost in sweat → maintain ion gradients in nerve and muscle cells → prevent muscle cramps and impaired nerve impulse transmission. Fluid replacement prevents dehydration → maintains plasma volume → sustains cardiac output and blood delivery to muscles. All three components (carbohydrate, electrolytes, fluid) work synergistically to maintain performance beyond what water alone can achieve.

Using your knowledge of fermentation, explain the complete process of making wine from grapes and account for what happens chemically at each stage from grape to bottled wine.
Show Answer

1) Crushing: grapes are crushed → juice (must) containing sugars (glucose, fructose), water, and wild yeast. 2) Early fermentation: with some O₂, yeast grows aerobically → CO₂ + H₂O. 3) Main fermentation: sealed vessel → anaerobic conditions → yeast ferments glucose: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂. Ethanol and CO₂ accumulate. 4) As ethanol reaches ~14%, it becomes toxic to yeast → fermentation stops. 5) Racking: wine is separated from sediment (dead yeast). 6) Fining/filtering: clarification. 7) Optional: malolactic fermentation by bacteria → converts malic acid to lactic acid → softer taste. 8) Ageing (barrel/bottle): slow oxidation → complex flavour development. 9) Bottling: airtight to prevent vinegar formation (Acetobacter oxidising ethanol to acetic acid).

What is excretion?

Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste products produced by chemical reactions inside body cells. These are toxic substances that must be eliminated to maintain homeostasis.

Name three organs of excretion and state what waste product each removes.
Show Answer

Kidneys: remove urea, excess water, and salts (as urine). Lungs: remove carbon dioxide and water vapour. Skin: removes some urea, excess water, and salts (as sweat).

What is urea and how is it formed in the body?
Show Answer

Urea is a nitrogenous waste product formed in the liver when excess amino acids are broken down (deamination). The amino group (–NH₂) is removed from amino acids, converted to toxic ammonia, and then combined with CO₂ to form less toxic urea.

Name the two kidneys' position in the body.
Show Answer

The kidneys are two bean-shaped organs located in the back of the abdominal cavity, one on either side of the spine (vertebral column), just below the ribcage.

What liquid is produced by the kidneys and where does it go after leaving the kidney?
Show Answer

The kidneys produce urine. Urine passes from each kidney through a ureter to the urinary bladder where it is stored temporarily, then expelled through the urethra.

What is the nephron?
Show Answer

The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains about one million nephrons. Each nephron filters the blood and produces urine through ultrafiltration and selective reabsorption.

Name the main components of urine.
Show Answer

Water (95%), urea, uric acid, creatinine, mineral salts (sodium chloride, potassium salts), and small amounts of other metabolic waste products.

Why is the liver also considered an excretory organ?
Show Answer

The liver excretes bile pigments (bilirubin and biliverdin) — produced from the breakdown of old red blood cells — into bile. These pigments leave the body in faeces and give faeces their brown colour.

What is the difference between excretion and egestion?
Show Answer

Excretion removes waste products of metabolism produced inside body cells (urea, CO₂). Egestion (defecation) eliminates undigested food waste that passed through the digestive tract without being absorbed — it was never actually inside body cells.

Which blood vessel supplies the kidney with blood to be filtered?
Show Answer

The renal artery supplies blood to each kidney. The renal vein carries filtered blood away from the kidney back to the general circulation.

SET B Understanding & Application Medium

Describe the two main processes by which kidneys form urine: ultrafiltration and selective reabsorption.
Show Answer

Ultrafiltration: blood is filtered under high pressure in the glomerulus (knot of capillaries) into the Bowman's capsule. Small molecules (water, glucose, urea, salts, amino acids) pass through. Blood cells and large proteins remain in blood. Selective reabsorption: as filtrate flows through the tubule, all glucose and amino acids, most water, and useful salts are actively reabsorbed back into the blood. Urea and excess water/salts remain → concentrated urine flows to the collecting duct.

Why is glucose not normally found in urine, but it is present in the urine of people with uncontrolled diabetes?
Show Answer

Normally, all glucose in the filtrate is actively reabsorbed from the tubule back into the blood (active transport via glucose transporters). In uncontrolled diabetes, blood glucose is extremely high → filtrate glucose concentration exceeds the maximum reabsorption capacity (renal threshold) → excess glucose remains in the urine (glycosuria). This is a classic diagnostic sign of diabetes.

How does the body regulate urine concentration on a hot day vs a cold day?
Show Answer

Hot day: more water lost through sweating → blood becomes more concentrated (higher osmolality) → hypothalamus detects this → pituitary gland releases more ADH (antidiuretic hormone) → kidneys reabsorb more water from tubules → concentrated, dark urine (small volume). Cold day: little sweating → blood is dilute → less ADH released → less water reabsorbed → dilute, pale urine (large volume). This is osmoregulation by negative feedback.

Explain the role of the liver in producing the waste product urea. Why can't the body simply excrete amino acids directly?
Show Answer

When protein intake exceeds the body's needs, excess amino acids cannot be stored. The liver removes the amino group (deamination): amino acid → keto acid (used for energy/glucose) + ammonia (NH₃). Ammonia is highly toxic even in tiny amounts. The liver immediately combines ammonia with CO₂ (urea cycle) → less toxic urea. Urea is safe to transport in blood at relatively high concentrations. Excreting amino acids directly would waste valuable nitrogen compounds and is physiologically impractical.

A person with kidney disease has protein in their urine (proteinuria). What does this indicate about the kidneys?
Show Answer

Protein is normally too large to pass through the glomerular filtration membrane and stays in the blood. Proteinuria indicates damage to the glomerular membrane, which has become leaky — large protein molecules are now passing into the filtrate and not being reabsorbed. This is a sign of glomerulonephritis (kidney inflammation) or other kidney disease and is used diagnostically.

What is kidney dialysis and why might a patient need it?
Show Answer

Kidney dialysis is a medical procedure that artificially filters the blood when the kidneys have failed. Blood is passed through a dialysis machine containing a semi-permeable membrane. Waste products (urea, excess salts) diffuse out into the dialysis fluid; essential substances (glucose, proteins) remain in the blood. Patients with chronic kidney disease or acute kidney failure need it to prevent toxic accumulation of urea and maintain fluid/electrolyte balance.

Why do patients with kidney failure develop a build-up of urea in the blood (uraemia)? What symptoms does this cause?
Show Answer

Failing kidneys cannot filter urea from the blood efficiently. Urea accumulates in the blood (uraemia). Effects: urea is converted partly to ammonia (toxic to brain cells) → encephalopathy (confusion, drowsiness, seizures); nausea, vomiting, decreased appetite; protein catabolism increases; immune suppression; eventually coma and death. Dialysis or transplant can substitute for kidney function.

Compare the roles of the kidneys and the lungs in excretion.
Show Answer

Kidneys: excrete nitrogenous waste (urea), excess water, salts, and drugs/toxins; regulate blood osmolality (water balance) and pH; produce urine. Lungs: excrete CO₂ (from aerobic respiration) and water vapour; also regulate blood pH by controlling CO₂ levels. Both are essential — kidneys handle non-gaseous waste; lungs handle gaseous waste. Together they maintain the chemical composition of blood within narrow homeostatic ranges.

How does the hormone ADH control water reabsorption in the kidney? Describe the negative feedback loop.
Show Answer

Stimulus: high blood osmolality (too little water). Detection: osmoreceptors in hypothalamus detect increased solute concentration. Response: pituitary gland releases ADH into bloodstream. Effect: ADH binds to collecting duct cells → inserts aquaporin (water channel) proteins into membrane → more water reabsorbed from urine back into blood. Result: blood osmolality falls back to normal → ADH secretion decreases → less water reabsorbed → normal urine volume restored. Classic negative feedback homeostatic loop.

Explain why drinking alcohol leads to increased urine production (diuresis). What longer-term effect does this have on hydration?
Show Answer

Alcohol inhibits ADH secretion from the pituitary gland. With less ADH, fewer aquaporin channels are inserted into the collecting duct → less water is reabsorbed from the filtrate → more water remains in the urine → large volumes of dilute urine are produced (diuresis). This removes more water than was consumed in the drink → dehydration → thirst, headache, and dry mouth (classic hangover symptoms). Drinking water alongside alcohol helps counteract this effect.

Describe in detail the structure of the nephron and explain the function of each region in the production of urine.
Show Answer

Bowman's capsule: surrounds glomerulus; receives filtrate from ultrafiltration. Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT): active reabsorption of all glucose, amino acids, ~65% of water and salts. Analyse the differences between the three types of nitrogenous waste (ammonia, urea, and uric acid) produced by different animal groups. Explain which is most efficient for each environment.

Ammonia: very toxic, very water-soluble → must be diluted immediately; excreted by aquatic animals (fish, most invertebrates) that can afford to lose lots of water. Explain the counter-current multiplier system in the Loop of Henle and explain how it enables the kidney to produce concentrated urine.
Show Answer

The Loop of Henle creates a concentration gradient in the kidney medulla through counter-current flow (fluid in the descending and ascending limbs flows in opposite directions). Descending limb: permeable to water; as filtrate descends into increasingly salty medulla, water leaves by osmosis → filtrate becomes more concentrated. Ascending limb: impermeable to water; actively pumps NaCl out into medulla, maintaining the salt gradient. This gradient means the deepest part of the medulla is very salty. Collecting duct passes through this gradient: when ADH is present, water leaves the duct by osmosis into the salty medulla → urine becomes highly concentrated. Longer Loops of Henle (desert animals) create a steeper gradient → more concentrated urine → more water conserved.

Analyse how the kidneys contribute to blood pressure regulation through the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
Show Answer

Low blood pressure → kidney cells (juxtaglomerular apparatus) detect low stretch in renal arteriole → secrete renin enzyme. Renin cleaves angiotensinogen (liver protein) → angiotensin I → converted by ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme, in lungs) → angiotensin II. Angiotensin II: 1) constricts arterioles → raises blood pressure directly; 2) stimulates adrenal cortex to release aldosterone; 3) stimulates ADH release. Aldosterone: collecting duct retains more Na⁺ → water follows by osmosis → increases blood volume → raises blood pressure. ACE inhibitors (common blood pressure drugs) block this pathway, lowering blood pressure. This shows the kidney is not only an excretory organ but a major blood pressure regulator.

Evaluate the evidence that the microbiome (gut bacteria) influences kidney stone formation. What type of scientific thinking is required to assess such a claim?

Discuss the impact of high-protein diets (e.g., Atkins, ketogenic) on kidney function, using your knowledge of urea production and kidney filtration.
Show Answer

High protein intake → increased deamination in liver → more urea produced → higher urea load delivered to kidneys. The statement is incorrect. What are the two main parts of the nervous system?
Show Answer

The Central Nervous System (CNS) — comprising the brain and spinal cord — and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) — comprising all the nerves that branch out from the CNS to the rest of the body.

What is a neuron?
Show Answer

A neuron (nerve cell) is the basic functional unit of the nervous system. It is a specialised cell that receives and transmits electrical signals (nerve impulses) from one part of the body to another.

Name the three types of neurons and state the role of each.
Show Answer

Sensory neurons: carry signals from sense organs/receptors to the CNS. Motor neurons: carry signals from the CNS to effectors (muscles/glands). Relay neurons (interneurons): found within the CNS, connect sensory and motor neurons and process information.

What is a reflex action? Give one example.
Show Answer

A reflex action is an automatic, involuntary, rapid response to a stimulus that does not require conscious thought from the brain. Example: withdrawing your hand from a hot object, blinking when something approaches the eye, or knee-jerk reflex.

What is the pathway of a reflex arc? List the components in order.
Show Answer

Receptor → Sensory neuron → Relay neuron (in spinal cord) → Motor neuron → Effector (muscle or gland).

What is the function of the brain in the nervous system?
Show Answer

The brain is the control centre of the nervous system. It processes information from sense organs, stores memories, coordinates movements, regulates body functions (breathing, heart rate), and produces conscious thought, language, and behaviour.

What is the spinal cord and what does it do?
Show Answer

The spinal cord is a long bundle of nerve fibres running down inside the vertebral column (backbone). It carries nerve signals between the brain and the rest of the body and is the coordination centre for reflex actions.

What is a synapse?
Show Answer

A synapse is the tiny gap (junction) between two neurons or between a neuron and a muscle/gland. Nerve signals cross this gap chemically: the first neuron releases neurotransmitter molecules that diffuse across the gap and bind to receptors on the next neuron, triggering a new electrical signal.

What is a receptor in the context of the nervous system?
Show Answer

A receptor is a specialised cell or sensory ending that detects a stimulus (e.g., light, touch, temperature, sound, chemical) and converts it into a nerve impulse. Examples: rod/cone cells in the eye, taste buds on the tongue, pain receptors in skin.

Name the neurotransmitter released at most synapses in the nervous system.
Show Answer

Acetylcholine (ACh) is the main neurotransmitter at neuromuscular junctions and many CNS synapses. Other important neurotransmitters include dopamine, serotonin, and noradrenaline (norepinephrine).

SET B Understanding & Application Medium

Explain why reflex actions are faster than voluntary actions. Refer to the nervous pathway used in each.
Show Answer

Reflex actions use a short pathway through the spinal cord only (receptor → sensory neuron → relay neuron in spinal cord → motor neuron → effector). Voluntary actions require the signal to travel all the way to the brain, be processed, and a response signal sent back down — a much longer, slower pathway. The shorter reflex path means the response occurs in milliseconds, often before the brain has even registered the stimulus.

Describe how a nerve impulse travels across a synapse from one neuron to the next.
Show Answer

1) Nerve impulse reaches the terminal (presynaptic) knob. 2) Ca²⁺ ions enter the terminal, triggering vesicles containing neurotransmitter to fuse with the presynaptic membrane. 3) Neurotransmitter molecules are released by exocytosis into the synaptic cleft. 4) Neurotransmitter diffuses across the gap and binds to complementary receptors on the postsynaptic membrane. 5) Binding opens ion channels → new electrical impulse generated in the next neuron. 6) Neurotransmitter is broken down by enzymes (or taken back up) to reset the synapse.

Explain the difference between the somatic nervous system and the autonomic nervous system within the PNS.
Show Answer

Somatic nervous system: controls voluntary movements of skeletal muscles (conscious control — e.g., moving your hand). Autonomic nervous system: controls involuntary functions of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands (unconscious control — heart rate, digestion, breathing). The autonomic system is further divided into sympathetic (fight-or-flight: accelerates heart, dilates pupils) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest: slows heart, increases digestion).

Describe the structure of a motor neuron and explain how each part is adapted for its function.
Show Answer

Cell body: contains nucleus and metabolic machinery — provides energy and proteins for the neuron. Dendrites: short, branching extensions that receive signals from other neurons — increase surface area for input. Axon: long fibre that carries the electrical impulse away from the cell body — length allows signal transmission over long distances. Myelin sheath: fatty insulation around the axon — speeds up signal transmission (saltatory conduction) and protects the axon. Synaptic knobs: at the end of the axon — release neurotransmitters to communicate with the next cell.

Explain the role of myelin in speeding up nerve impulse transmission. What condition results when myelin is destroyed?
Show Answer

Myelin (produced by Schwann cells) insulates the axon, forcing the electrical impulse to "jump" between gaps (nodes of Ranvier) — called saltatory conduction. This is far faster than the impulse travelling along the entire membrane. Multiple sclerosis (MS) is an autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks and destroys myelin sheaths. Without myelin, nerve impulses travel slowly and unreliably → muscle weakness, coordination problems, vision loss, and fatigue.

Why is it important that the response to a reflex happens before the brain processes the pain signal?
Show Answer

Reflexes evolved as protective mechanisms. If we had to consciously decide to withdraw from pain, the delay (signal travelling to brain + decision + response signal back) could allow serious tissue damage to occur. For example, if you touch a hot stove, the reflex arc pulls your hand away in <200 ms — before you even consciously feel pain. The brain processes the pain signal slightly later. This speed difference can be life-saving in avoiding burns, cuts, and falls.

Some drugs affect synaptic transmission. Using one example of a stimulant and one depressant, explain how each alters nervous system function.
Show Answer

Stimulant (e.g., caffeine): blocks adenosine receptors (which normally slow neural activity) → prevents inhibitory signalling → neurons fire more readily → increased alertness and heart rate. Or cocaine: blocks reuptake of dopamine at synapse → dopamine remains in cleft longer → prolonged stimulation of reward pathways → intense pleasure/dependency. Depressant (e.g., alcohol): enhances GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) receptors and blocks glutamate (excitatory) receptors → slows neural activity → reduced coordination, reaction time, and inhibition → sedation.

Compare the nervous system and the endocrine system as two methods of body communication and coordination.
Show Answer

Nervous system: uses electrical signals; very fast (milliseconds); short-lived responses; precise, specific pathway; involved in immediate responses (reflexes, movement). Endocrine system: uses chemical hormones in blood; slower (seconds to minutes); longer-lasting responses; affects multiple organs; controls growth, reproduction, metabolism, and slow homeostatic adjustments. Both systems work together (neuroendocrine integration) — e.g., during stress, the nervous system triggers adrenaline release from the adrenal gland (endocrine).

Explain what happens at the neuromuscular junction to cause a muscle to contract.
Show Answer

Motor neuron impulse reaches the neuromuscular junction → ACh released from synaptic knob → diffuses across cleft → binds to ACh receptors on the motor end plate of the muscle fibre → muscle cell membrane depolarises → action potential spreads through the muscle → calcium ions released from sarcoplasmic reticulum → Ca²⁺ binds to troponin → myosin heads attach to actin filaments → cross-bridge cycling → muscle shortens (contracts). ACh is then broken down by acetylcholinesterase to allow the muscle to relax.

A person suffers a spinal cord injury at the level of the thoracic vertebrae. Predict the effects on movement and sensation below the injury and explain why sensation is also affected, not just movement.
Show Answer

A complete spinal cord injury at the thoracic level severs all nerve pathways above and below: motor signals from the brain cannot reach muscles below the injury → paralysis of legs and lower trunk. Sensory signals from below the injury cannot reach the brain → loss of sensation (touch, pain, temperature) in the same areas. Both are affected because both ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) tracts run through the spinal cord. The result is paraplegia. Reflex arcs below the injury still work (they are coordinated by the spinal cord locally) but the person has no conscious control or awareness of them.

Explain the ionic basis of the resting membrane potential and the action potential. Why is sodium-potassium ATPase essential for maintaining neural function?
Show Answer

Resting potential (~-70 mV): the inside of the neuron is more negative. Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle (active transport), maintaining high K⁺ inside and high Na⁺ outside. K⁺ leaks out through channels → net negative charge inside. Action potential: stimulus → Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ rushes in (depolarisation, +40 mV). Then Na⁺ channels close, K⁺ channels open → K⁺ flows out (repolarisation). Refractory period prevents backward transmission. Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase is essential: without it, Na⁺/K⁺ gradients would dissipate → no further action potentials possible → all neural function ceases. This is why cyanide (blocks ATP production) and anaesthetics (block Na⁺ channels) eliminate nerve function.

Evaluate how neuroplasticity (the brain's ability to rewire itself) challenges the traditional view of the brain as a fixed organ. Give examples from learning, injury recovery, and development.
Show Answer

Traditional view: brain is hardwired after early childhood; adult brain cannot change. Neuroplasticity: synaptic connections are strengthened by use (long-term potentiation) and weakened by disuse → learning and memory are physically encoded in changed synaptic strengths. Examples: 1) Learning: taxi drivers develop larger hippocampi from spatial navigation training; musicians have enlarged motor cortex areas for their instrument. 2) Recovery: after stroke, neighbouring brain regions can partially take over functions of damaged areas — intensive rehabilitation drives this rewiring. 3) Development: young children's brains are especially plastic; critical periods exist for language acquisition (0–7 years). Evidence undermines the fixed-brain model; the brain is dynamic and adaptive throughout life, though plasticity decreases with age.

Most addictive drugs (opioids, cocaine, amphetamines, nicotine, alcohol) hijack the dopamine reward pathway (mesolimbic system). Normal: pleasurable stimuli → dopamine released in nucleus accumbens → rewarding feeling. Neurological changes: long-term structural changes in prefrontal cortex impair impulse control and decision-making. This makes addiction a brain disease, not merely a choice — explaining why willpower alone is usually insufficient without medical support.

Compare voluntary and involuntary actions using specific examples, explaining the neural pathways involved in each and why both types are necessary for survival.
Show Answer

Voluntary (e.g., reaching for a cup): stimulus detected → sensory neurons → brain (cerebral cortex) → decision made consciously → motor cortex → motor neurons → skeletal muscles. Involves awareness, learning, and planning. Involuntary (e.g., blinking reflex, heartbeat regulation): sensory neurons → spinal cord or brainstem (no cerebral cortex involvement) → motor neurons → effectors. Fast, automatic, no conscious effort. Necessity: voluntary actions allow complex, adaptable behaviour (tool use, language, problem-solving). Involuntary actions free up conscious attention — we cannot afford to consciously control heartbeat, breathing, blinking, digestion simultaneously. Both are required: voluntary enables intelligence; involuntary ensures survival without cognitive overload.

Explain how the brain is organised into functional regions. Describe the roles of the cerebrum, cerebellum, and medulla oblongata in coordinating body functions.
Show Answer

Cerebrum (cerebral cortex): largest part; divided into lobes: frontal (decision-making, personality, speech), parietal (sensory processing, spatial awareness), temporal (hearing, memory, language), occipital (vision). Controls all voluntary movement and higher cognition. Cerebellum: located at back; coordinates precise voluntary movements, balance, and posture. Receives input from muscles and joints → fine-tunes motor commands from cerebrum. Damage → ataxia (uncoordinated movement). Medulla oblongata (brainstem): controls vital involuntary functions: heart rate, breathing rhythm, blood pressure, swallowing, vomiting. Damage to medulla is immediately life-threatening. Also contains the cardiovascular and respiratory control centres. Together these regions are specialised and interconnected, allowing both automatic life-support and complex conscious behaviour.

Analyse the evidence for and against the hypothesis that consciousness is produced entirely by the brain, with no non-physical component.
Show Answer

Evidence FOR (neural basis of consciousness): brain damage produces specific, predictable changes in consciousness (e.g., Phineas Gage personality change after frontal lobe damage); anaesthesia eliminates consciousness by disrupting neural activity; neuroimaging shows specific brain regions activating during specific conscious experiences; split-brain patients show divided consciousness when corpus callosum is severed. Evidence AGAINST / limitations: the "hard problem of consciousness" (Chalmers): physical neural processes explain behaviour but not the subjective quality of experience (qualia — why does red look red?). No neuroscientific experiment has explained how neural activity gives rise to subjective experience. Some philosophers argue this gap cannot be bridged by physical science alone. Balanced MYP response: current scientific evidence strongly supports the neural basis of cognition and behaviour, but the nature of subjective experience remains an open philosophical and scientific question — honesty about the limits of current science is important.

Explain how the nervous system and endocrine system coordinate the body's "fight-or-flight" response. Which system acts first and why?
Show Answer

Threat detected → sensory organs → amygdala (threat assessment centre in brain). 1) Nervous system (immediate, seconds): hypothalamus activates sympathetic nervous system → directly increases heart rate, dilates pupils, stimulates sweat glands, redirects blood to muscles. Noradrenaline released at nerve endings. 2) Endocrine system (slightly slower, seconds-minutes): hypothalamus signals adrenal medulla via nerve → adrenal medulla secretes adrenaline and noradrenaline into blood → travel throughout body → sustain and amplify heart rate increase, increase blood glucose (liver glycogenolysis), dilate bronchioles (more O₂), reduce digestion (blood redirected to muscles). Nervous system acts first because electrical signals travel in milliseconds; hormones must travel via bloodstream (slower). Together they create an integrated response that prepares the whole body for rapid action.

Alzheimer's disease is characterised by the loss of acetylcholine (ACh)-producing neurons. Using your knowledge of synaptic transmission, explain the cognitive symptoms, and evaluate the rationale for current drug treatments (acetylcholinesterase inhibitors).
Show Answer

Normal memory formation requires repeated ACh signalling at hippocampal synapses to strengthen neural connections (long-term potentiation). In Alzheimer's: ACh-producing neurons in the basal forebrain die → less ACh released into hippocampus and cortex synapses → insufficient postsynaptic stimulation → synaptic weakening → impaired memory formation and retrieval → progressive dementia. Also: amyloid plaques and tau tangles disrupt neural circuits. Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil): block the enzyme that breaks down ACh in the synapse → ACh remains in the cleft longer → more stimulation of remaining receptors → partially compensates for reduced ACh levels. Evaluation: these drugs slow symptom progression in some patients but do not reverse neuron loss or cure the disease. They address the symptom (low ACh) not the cause (why neurons die). Research into amyloid-clearing therapies addresses the root cause but has had limited success in clinical trials to date.

Using your knowledge of the entire nervous system, evaluate the claim that "the brain is the most important organ in the body." Consider competing perspectives from other organ systems.
Show Answer

In favour: the brain controls every other organ, either directly (voluntary muscle) or indirectly (autonomic nervous system, endocrine regulation via hypothalamus/pituitary). Without brain function, all other organs ultimately fail. Consciousness, memory, personality, and all complex behaviours reside in the brain. Brain death is legally accepted as death in most countries even when other organs continue to function with support. Counter-perspectives: the heart pumps blood to the brain — without it, the brain dies in 4 minutes; the brain is dependent on the heart. Balanced conclusion: the brain is uniquely important as the coordinator and seat of consciousness, but it cannot function without the other organ systems supporting it. Organ importance is contextual — every major organ is essential to life.
