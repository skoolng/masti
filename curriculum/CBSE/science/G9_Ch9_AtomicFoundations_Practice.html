Elements, Compounds & Mixtures

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Elements, compounds and mixtures
CrashCourse Chemistry of Mixtures and Compounds

Set A Recall & Concept

Define element, compound, and mixture. Give one example of each. Easy
Show Answer

Element: a pure substance made of only one type of atom (e.g., oxygen, O&sub2;). Compound: a pure substance made of two or more elements chemically bonded in fixed ratios (e.g., water, H&sub2;O). Mixture: two or more substances physically combined, retaining individual properties and separable (e.g., salt water).

How does a compound differ from a mixture of the same elements? Easy
Show Answer

A compound has its elements bonded in fixed ratios; it has different properties from its elements. A mixture has elements/compounds in variable ratios; it retains the properties of its components. A compound can only be separated by chemical means; a mixture can be separated physically.

Classify: (a) air (b) iron (c) salt (NaCl) (d) sea water (e) diamond (f) blood. Easy
Show Answer

(a) Air: mixture. (b) Iron: element. (c) NaCl: compound. (d) Sea water: mixture. (e) Diamond: element (pure carbon). (f) Blood: mixture.

What distinguishes a homogeneous mixture from a heterogeneous mixture? Give an example of each. Medium
Show Answer

Homogeneous mixture: uniform composition throughout; cannot distinguish components visually (e.g., salt water, vinegar). Heterogeneous mixture: non-uniform; components are visible or separable (e.g., sand and gravel, salad).

Water (H&sub2;O) and hydrogen peroxide (H&sub2;O&sub2;) are both compounds of hydrogen and oxygen. Are they the same compound? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

No. Although made of the same elements, their atoms are in different fixed ratios (2:1 vs 1:1 H:O ratio per hydrogen). Different bonding arrangements give completely different properties: H&sub2;O is a liquid at room temperature and life-sustaining; H&sub2;O&sub2; is a bleaching agent and can be toxic in concentration.

Name five methods used to separate mixtures and state what property each exploits. Medium
Show Answer

1. Filtration: exploits particle size (solid vs. liquid). 2. Distillation: exploits different boiling points. 3. Chromatography: exploits solubility and molecular size. 4. Magnetic separation: exploits magnetic properties. 5. Crystallisation: exploits solubility differences at different temperatures.

Why does iron burning (rusting) produce a compound while mixing iron filings with sulphur produces a mixture? Medium
Show Answer

Rusting: iron reacts with oxygen, forming new chemical bonds to produce iron oxide (Fe&sub2;O&sub3;) — a new compound with different properties. Mixing iron and sulphur: no bonds are formed, each substance retains its identity, and they can be separated by a magnet (iron is attracted; sulphur is not).

An unknown substance has a fixed melting point, conducts electricity when dissolved in water, and is made of two elements. Is it likely to be a compound or mixture? Justify. Hard
Show Answer

Compound. Compounds have fixed melting points (pure substances); mixtures have variable/broad melting points. Two elements in fixed ratio = compound. Conducting electricity when dissolved suggests ionic compound (like NaCl). A mixture would have variable composition and no single melting point.

Set B Application & Analysis

A student heats a mixture of iron filings and sulphur. What happens? Is the product a compound or mixture? How can you tell? Medium
Show Answer

When heated, iron reacts with sulphur to form iron(II) sulphide (FeS) — a grey compound. It is a compound because: (1) it has different properties (no longer magnetic, different colour); (2) it cannot be separated by a magnet; (3) it has a fixed ratio of Fe:S = 1:1; (4) it requires a chemical reaction to separate.

Design an experiment to separate a mixture of sand, salt, and iron filings into three pure components. Medium
Show Answer

Step 1: Use a magnet to remove iron filings. Step 2: Add water to dissolve salt (sand remains insoluble). Step 3: Filter to separate sand from salt solution. Step 4: Evaporate/crystallise to obtain pure salt. Each step exploits a different physical property.

Explain why the properties of water are completely different from the properties of hydrogen and oxygen gases. Hard
Show Answer

In a compound, atoms are bonded in a new arrangement, creating a completely new substance. Water's properties (liquid at room temperature, high surface tension, polar solvent) emerge from the O-H covalent bond and the bent molecular shape, which are entirely different from H&sub2; (flammable, gaseous) and O&sub2; (supports combustion, gaseous).

A food label says "contains: water, sugar, salt, vinegar, spices." Classify each ingredient and the overall product. Medium
Show Answer

Water (H&sub2;O): compound. Sugar (C&sub1;&sub2;H&sub2;&sub2;O&sub1;&sub1;): compound. Salt (NaCl): compound. Vinegar (acetic acid solution): mixture. Spices: mixture. Overall product: a mixture (heterogeneous or homogeneous depending on processing).

Crude oil is a mixture. What advantage does this give refineries? How do they separate it? Medium
Show Answer

Being a mixture means components (petrol, diesel, kerosene, bitumen) can be separated by physical means without chemical reactions, preserving their value. Refineries use fractional distillation: crude oil is heated; different fractions condense at different temperatures (different boiling points) and are collected at different levels of the fractionating column.

Why is air a mixture and not a compound of its gases? Medium
Show Answer

Air: (1) has variable composition (% of N&sub2;, O&sub2;, CO&sub2; vary with altitude, pollution). (2) Components retain their individual properties (O&sub2; still supports combustion). (3) Can be separated physically (fractional distillation of liquid air). (4) No chemical bond is formed between N&sub2; and O&sub2;. These are all characteristics of mixtures, not compounds.

Distinguish between a pure substance and an impure substance. Why does impurity affect melting point? Hard
Show Answer

Pure substance: single element or compound, fixed melting/boiling point. Impure: mixture, broader/lower melting point. Impurities disrupt the regular crystal lattice of the pure solid, requiring less energy to break (lower melting point) and causing melting over a temperature range instead of a sharp point. This is used as a purity test.

Steel is iron mixed with carbon. Would you classify steel as a compound, mixture, or something in between? Explain. Hard
Show Answer

Steel is an alloy — a type of mixture (technically a solid solution). Carbon atoms fit interstitially into the iron crystal lattice without forming ionic or covalent bonds with specific iron atoms in fixed stoichiometry. The composition is variable (0.2–2% carbon). It is classified as a mixture (homogeneous), though its properties differ significantly from pure iron.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

A student mixes H&sub2; and O&sub2; gases in a jar. Another student sparks the same mixture. Compare the products and classify each situation. Hard
Show Answer

Just mixed: both gases retain their properties (H&sub2; is still flammable, O&sub2; still supports combustion) — this is a mixture. After the spark: chemical reaction produces water (2H&sub2; + O&sub2; → 2H&sub2;O) — this is a compound with completely different properties. The spark triggers bond-breaking and bond-forming, the hallmark of a chemical change vs a physical change.

Design a flowchart for classifying any substance as element, compound, or mixture. Include key tests. Medium
Show Answer

Step 1: Does it have a fixed, sharp melting/boiling point? YES → pure substance; NO → mixture. Step 2 (pure substance): Does it contain only one type of atom? YES → element; NO → compound. Tests for element: spectroscopy, cannot be decomposed chemically. Tests for compound: electrolysis/decomposition gives elements in fixed mass ratios.

Explain why purifying water is essential for health. Use the concept of mixtures and contaminants. Medium
Show Answer

Natural water is a mixture containing dissolved salts, microorganisms, heavy metals, organic pollutants. These contaminants cause disease (cholera, typhoid from microorganisms; lead poisoning from heavy metals). Purification (filtration, chlorination, reverse osmosis) removes contaminants to leave H&sub2;O in a concentration safe for drinking. Understanding mixtures is fundamental to water treatment science.

Compare physical and chemical changes. Classify: (a) melting ice, (b) burning wood, (c) dissolving sugar, (d) rusting iron. Medium
Show Answer

Physical change: no new substance formed; reversible (usually). Chemical change: new substances formed with different properties; usually irreversible. (a) Melting ice: physical (H&sub2;O changes state, not composition). (b) Burning wood: chemical (new substances CO&sub2;, H&sub2;O, ash formed). (c) Dissolving sugar: physical (can be recovered by evaporation). (d) Rusting iron: chemical (iron oxide Fe&sub2;O&sub3; forms, different from iron).

Why do countries spend billions on desalination plants rather than simply filtering sea water? What does this say about the nature of compounds vs mixtures? Hard
Show Answer

Sea water is a mixture — salt is dissolved in water. Simple filtration cannot separate dissolved substances because they are mixed at the molecular level. Desalination requires energy-intensive processes (reverse osmosis, evaporation/condensation) that effectively separate the mixture at molecular scale. This illustrates that even though salt water is "just a mixture," separating it at scale is technically challenging and expensive.

Gold jewellery is often 18-karat (75% gold, 25% other metals). Is this a compound or mixture? What are the practical advantages? Hard
Show Answer

It is a mixture (alloy). Pure gold (24k) is too soft for jewellery. The 18k alloy is: (1) harder and more durable; (2) variable composition (can be adjusted for hardness/colour); (3) retains gold's lustre and value; (4) separable by chemical means (unlike a compound). Mixing metals in variable ratios = mixture, even though properties are dramatically different from pure gold.

A scientist isolates a new substance from a plant. Describe the tests they would conduct to determine if it is a pure compound or a mixture. Hard
Show Answer

Tests: (1) Melting point: sharp/narrow range = pure; broad = mixture. (2) Thin layer chromatography: one spot = pure compound; multiple spots = mixture. (3) Elemental analysis: fixed mass ratios of elements = compound; variable = mixture. (4) Recrystallisation: if repeated recrystallisation changes melting point → impure mixture. (5) Mass spectrometry: single molecular mass peak = pure compound.

Explain how the concept of compounds underpins medicine: why must pharmaceutical drugs be pure compounds rather than mixtures? Hard
Show Answer

Drugs must be pure compounds because: (1) dosage requires exact composition; (2) impurities may cause side effects or toxicity (e.g., thalidomide's two isomers had opposite effects); (3) regulatory approval requires confirmed fixed molecular structure; (4) shelf life and stability depend on purity. A mixture would have variable composition, making consistent dosing impossible and dangerous.

Chemical Formulae — Subscripts and Ratios

Set A Recall & Concept

For H&sub2;O, state the number of each type of atom and the total atoms per molecule. Easy
Show Answer

H&sub2;O: 2 hydrogen atoms + 1 oxygen atom = 3 atoms per molecule. The subscript 2 after H means 2 hydrogen atoms; no subscript after O means 1 oxygen atom.

Interpret the formula CO&sub2;. How many atoms of each element? What does the subscript mean? Easy
Show Answer

CO&sub2; = 1 carbon atom + 2 oxygen atoms = 3 atoms per molecule. The subscript 2 after O means there are 2 oxygen atoms bonded to 1 carbon atom in every CO&sub2; molecule.

What information does the formula C&sub6;H&sub1;&sub2;O&sub6; convey? Name this compound. Easy
Show Answer

C&sub6;H&sub1;&sub2;O&sub6; = 6 carbon + 12 hydrogen + 6 oxygen atoms per molecule (24 atoms total). Ratio C:H:O = 1:2:1. This is glucose (a simple sugar).

Explain the difference between H&sub2;O and H&sub2;O&sub2; using subscripts. Medium
Show Answer

H&sub2;O: 2 H atoms and 1 O atom (water). H&sub2;O&sub2;: 2 H atoms and 2 O atoms (hydrogen peroxide). The subscript 2 after O in H&sub2;O&sub2; doubles the oxygen atoms, creating a different compound with different properties (H&sub2;O&sub2; is a bleaching/disinfecting agent).

What is the difference between a molecular formula and an empirical formula? Give an example. Medium
Show Answer

Molecular formula: actual number of atoms per molecule (e.g., C&sub6;H&sub1;&sub2;O&sub6; for glucose). Empirical formula: simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (e.g., CH&sub2;O for glucose). The empirical formula may not represent a real molecule; the molecular formula does.

Write the correct formula for these compounds: (a) sodium chloride (1 Na, 1 Cl) (b) calcium oxide (1 Ca, 1 O) (c) aluminium oxide (2 Al, 3 O). Easy
Show Answer

(a) NaCl. (b) CaO. (c) Al&sub2;O&sub3;. Subscripts show the number of each atom in the formula unit.

Explain what the coefficient "3" means in the expression 3H&sub2;O. Medium
Show Answer

The coefficient 3 means 3 molecules of water. Total atoms: 3 × 2H = 6 H atoms + 3 × 1O = 3 O atoms. Coefficients (in front) multiply the entire formula; subscripts (inside) define the fixed composition of each molecule.

What is the total number of atoms in one formula unit of Ca(OH)&sub2;? Hard
Show Answer

Ca(OH)&sub2;: 1 Ca + 2×(1 O + 1 H) = 1 Ca + 2 O + 2 H = 5 atoms total. The brackets with subscript 2 mean the entire (OH) group is doubled: 2 OH groups.

Set B Application & Calculation

Calculate the number of atoms of each element in 2 molecules of H&sub2;SO&sub4;. Easy
Show Answer

H&sub2;SO&sub4; per molecule: 2H + 1S + 4O. For 2 molecules: 4H + 2S + 8O = 14 atoms total.

Determine the empirical formula of a compound with 40% C, 6.7% H, 53.3% O. Hard
Show Answer

Moles: C = 40/12 = 3.33; H = 6.7/1 = 6.7; O = 53.3/16 = 3.33. Ratio: C:H:O = 3.33:6.7:3.33 = 1:2:1. Empirical formula: CH&sub2;O.

How many atoms are in one formula unit of Al&sub2;(SO&sub4;)&sub3;? Medium
Show Answer

Al: 2. SO&sub4; group × 3: 3S + 12O. Total = 2 Al + 3 S + 12 O = 17 atoms.

Write the formula for these ionic compounds: (a) magnesium chloride (Mg²⁺, Cl⁻) (b) iron(III) oxide (Fe³⁺, O²⁻). Medium
Show Answer

(a) MgCl&sub2;: Mg²⁺ needs 2 Cl⁻ to balance charges. (b) Fe&sub2;O&sub3;: 2 Fe³⁺ (+6 total) balanced by 3 O²⁻ (−6 total).

A compound has the molecular formula C&sub2;H&sub6;O. What is its empirical formula? Medium
Show Answer

C:H:O = 2:6:1. GCD of 2,6,1 = 1. Empirical formula = C&sub2;H&sub6;O (same as molecular formula since no simpler whole-number ratio is possible keeping O as 1).

Count all atoms in 3 molecules of Ca(HCO&sub3;)&sub2;. Hard
Show Answer

Ca(HCO&sub3;)&sub2; per molecule: 1 Ca + 2(1H + 1C + 3O) = 1 Ca + 2H + 2C + 6O = 11 atoms. For 3 molecules: 3 Ca + 6H + 6C + 18O = 33 atoms.

What is the percentage by mass of oxygen in SO&sub3;? (Ar: S=32, O=16) Medium
Show Answer

Molar mass of SO&sub3; = 32 + 3×16 = 80 g/mol. Mass of O = 48 g/mol. % O = (48/80) × 100 = 60%.

A hydrocarbon has molecular formula C&sub4;H&sub1;&sub0;. What is its empirical formula and % C by mass? (Ar: C=12, H=1) Hard
Show Answer

Empirical formula: C&sub4;H&sub1;&sub0; → divide by 2 → C&sub2;H&sub5;. Molar mass = 4×12 + 10×1 = 58 g/mol. % C = (48/58) × 100 = 82.8%.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

Explain the law of constant composition (law of definite proportions) using the formula of water. Medium
Show Answer

The law states that a compound always contains the same elements in the same mass ratio regardless of its source. Water is always 88.9% O and 11.1% H by mass (2:1 atom ratio), whether from a river, laboratory synthesis, or outer space. This distinguishes compounds from mixtures, which can have variable composition.

How did Dalton use chemical formulae to propose the law of multiple proportions? Give an example. Hard
Show Answer

Dalton observed that when two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in simple whole-number ratios. Example: CO (1:1.33) and CO&sub2; (1:2.67) — the ratio of O masses for the same C mass is 1:2, a simple ratio. This pointed to discrete atoms combining in whole numbers, supporting atomic theory.

Two compounds have the same empirical formula CH&sub2;O but different molecular masses (30 g/mol and 180 g/mol). What are their molecular formulas and names? Hard
Show Answer

Empirical CH&sub2;O has mass = 12+2+16 = 30 g/mol. At 30 g/mol: n=1, molecular formula = CH&sub2;O = formaldehyde. At 180 g/mol: n = 180/30 = 6, molecular formula = C&sub6;H&sub1;&sub2;O&sub6; = glucose. Same empirical formula, completely different compounds with vastly different properties.

A student changes the subscript in H&sub2;O to get H&sub2;O&sub2;. Is this the same compound? What does this illustrate about the importance of subscripts? Medium
Show Answer

No — completely different compound (hydrogen peroxide vs water). H&sub2;O&sub2; is a bleaching agent and can be toxic, while water is essential for life. Subscripts define the exact atomic composition and bonding ratio of a compound — changing them creates an entirely new substance. This shows why the formula must be written precisely.

Explain the concept of structural isomers. Why do C&sub2;H&sub5;OH and CH&sub3;OCH&sub3; have the same molecular formula but different properties? Hard
Show Answer

Structural isomers: same molecular formula but different connectivity of atoms. C&sub2;H&sub5;OH (ethanol): O bonded to C and H at end. CH&sub3;OCH&sub3; (dimethyl ether): O bonded between two C atoms. Different bonding arrangements → completely different physical/chemical properties: ethanol boils at 78°C; dimethyl ether at −24°C. Both have C&sub2;H&sub6;O.

How does a chemical formula encode the concept of valency? Explain using MgCl&sub2; and AlCl&sub3;. Hard
Show Answer

Valency is the combining power of an element (number of bonds formed). Mg has valency 2 (forms 2 bonds), Cl has valency 1 (forms 1 bond). MgCl&sub2;: 2 Cl atoms satisfy Mg's valency of 2. Al has valency 3; AlCl&sub3;: 3 Cl atoms satisfy Al's valency of 3. The subscripts in ionic formulas directly encode the valency balance between cation and anion.

Why do ionic compounds like NaCl use a formula unit (not a molecular formula), while covalent compounds like H&sub2;O have true molecular formulas? Hard
Show Answer

NaCl exists as an extended 3D ionic lattice (not discrete molecules). The formula unit NaCl represents the simplest ratio of ions. H&sub2;O exists as discrete molecules (2H + 1O covalently bonded). The formula H&sub2;O represents the actual molecule. Ionic compounds have no molecules; covalent compounds do. This distinction is fundamental to understanding solid vs molecular structures.

Calculate the % by mass of each element in glucose (C&sub6;H&sub1;&sub2;O&sub6;, Ar: C=12, H=1, O=16). Which element has the highest mass fraction? Hard
Show Answer

Molar mass = 6(12) + 12(1) + 6(16) = 72 + 12 + 96 = 180 g/mol. % C = 72/180 × 100 = 40%. % H = 12/180 × 100 = 6.7%. % O = 96/180 × 100 = 53.3%. Oxygen has the highest mass fraction (53.3%) despite having the same number of atoms as carbon, because each O atom is 16 times heavier than H and 4/3 heavier than C.

The Mole Concept — Avogadro's Number

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy The mole and Avogadro's number Molar mass
CrashCourse The Mole

Set A Recall & Concept

Define one mole. State Avogadro's number. Easy
Show Answer

One mole is the amount of substance that contains 6.02 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, or ions). Avogadro's number N_A = 6.02 × 10²³ mol²¹.

Define molar mass and explain how to find it from the periodic table. Easy
Show Answer

Molar mass is the mass of 1 mole of a substance, in g/mol. For an element, it equals its relative atomic mass in g/mol (e.g., O = 16 g/mol). For a compound, add the molar masses of all atoms in the formula (e.g., H&sub2;O = 2(1) + 16 = 18 g/mol).

Why is the mole concept useful in chemistry? Medium
Show Answer

Atoms are too small to count individually. The mole connects microscopic atoms to macroscopic (lab-scale) masses we can measure. Using moles, chemists can calculate reacting ratios, predict product amounts, and scale reactions precisely.

Calculate the molar mass of: (a) CO&sub2; (b) NaOH (c) H&sub2;SO&sub4;. (Ar: H=1, C=12, N=14, O=16, Na=23, S=32) Easy
Show Answer

(a) CO&sub2; = 12 + 2(16) = 44 g/mol. (b) NaOH = 23+16+1 = 40 g/mol. (c) H&sub2;SO&sub4; = 2(1)+32+4(16) = 98 g/mol.

How many molecules are in 1 mole of water? How many atoms? Easy
Show Answer

Molecules = 6.02 × 10²³. Atoms per molecule of H&sub2;O = 3. Total atoms = 3 × 6.02 × 10²³ = 1.806 × 10²&sup4; atoms.

Why is Avogadro's number such a large number? What does this tell us about atom sizes? Medium
Show Answer

Avogadro's number is ~6 × 10²³ because atoms are incredibly small. Fitting 18 g of water (1 mole) requires 6 × 10²³ molecules. This enormous number reflects how many microscopic particles fit into a gram-scale mass, telling us atoms have masses of ~10²²³ g each.

What is the mass of 1 mole of carbon-12 atoms? Why is this significant? Easy
Show Answer

1 mole of C-12 = exactly 12 g (by definition). This is significant because the mole was originally defined based on C-12: 1 mole of C-12 = exactly 12 g, which contains exactly 6.022 × 10²³ atoms. It links atomic mass units to grams.

Explain the term "molar volume" at STP for a gas. What is its value? Hard
Show Answer

Molar volume is the volume occupied by 1 mole of any ideal gas at STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure: 0°C, 101.3 kPa). Value = 22.4 L/mol. This is the same for all ideal gases because gas volume depends on number of particles, not type (Avogadro's law).

How many moles are in 36 g of water? (Molar mass H&sub2;O = 18 g/mol) Easy
Show Answer

n = mass/molar mass = 36/18 = 2 mol.

What is the mass of 3 moles of CO&sub2;? (Molar mass = 44 g/mol) Easy
Show Answer

Mass = n × molar mass = 3 × 44 = 132 g.

How many molecules are in 4.4 g of CO&sub2;? (Molar mass = 44 g/mol) Medium
Show Answer

n = 4.4/44 = 0.1 mol. Molecules = 0.1 × 6.02×10²³ = 6.02×10²² molecules.

What mass of NaCl contains 1.204 × 10²&sup4; formula units? (Molar mass NaCl = 58.5 g/mol) Medium
Show Answer

n = 1.204×10²&sup4; / 6.02×10²³ = 2 mol. Mass = 2 × 58.5 = 117 g.

How many atoms of hydrogen are in 0.5 mol of H&sub2;SO&sub4;? (H&sub2;SO&sub4; has 2 H atoms per formula unit) Medium
Show Answer

Molecules = 0.5 × 6.02×10²³ = 3.01×10²³. H atoms = 2 × 3.01×10²³ = 6.02×10²³ atoms.

Calculate the molar mass of glucose C&sub6;H&sub1;&sub2;O&sub6; and the number of molecules in 90 g. (Ar: C=12, H=1, O=16) Medium
Show Answer

Molar mass = 6(12)+12(1)+6(16) = 72+12+96 = 180 g/mol. n = 90/180 = 0.5 mol. Molecules = 0.5 × 6.02×10²³ = 3.01×10²³.

If 1 molecule of H&sub2;O has mass 3 × 10²²³ g, calculate Avogadro's number using molar mass = 18 g/mol. Hard
Show Answer

N_A = molar mass / (mass per molecule) = 18 / (3×10²²³) = 6 × 10²³ mol²¹. This confirms Avogadro's number from the mass of a single molecule.

How many moles and molecules of nitrogen gas (N&sub2;) are in 56 g? (Ar N=14) Medium
Show Answer

Molar mass N&sub2; = 2 × 14 = 28 g/mol. n = 56/28 = 2 mol. Molecules = 2 × 6.02×10²³ = 1.204×10²&sup4; molecules.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

If you stacked 1 mole of A4 paper sheets, how tall would the stack be? (Each sheet is 0.1 mm thick) Is this number comprehensible? Hard
Show Answer

Height = 6.02×10²³ × 0.0001 m = 6.02×10¹&sup9; m. The Milky Way is ~10²¹ m across — the stack would be ~60 Milky Way diameters. Avogadro's number is incomprehensibly large, which is why we need the mole as a counting unit rather than counting atoms individually.

A student says "2 moles of H&sub2; and 1 mole of O&sub2; react to give 2 moles of H&sub2;O, so 2 g of H&sub2; and 16 g of O&sub2; give 18 g of H&sub2;O." Find the error. Hard
Show Answer

The mole ratio is correct (2:1:2) but the masses are wrong. Molar mass of H&sub2; = 2 g/mol (not 1). 2 mol H&sub2; = 4 g (not 2 g). Molar mass O&sub2; = 32 g/mol (not 16). 1 mol O&sub2; = 32 g (not 16 g). Correct: 4 g H&sub2; + 32 g O&sub2; → 36 g H&sub2;O (conservation of mass: 4+32=36).

How does the mole concept enable stoichiometry? Why can't we just use grams directly for ratio calculations? Hard
Show Answer

Chemical reactions occur in particle ratios (2H&sub2; + O&sub2; = ratio 2:1). Grams alone don't reflect particle ratios: 2 g H&sub2; ≠ 2 moles of H&sub2; and doesn't relate to 1 mole O&sub2;. The mole converts gram-scale masses to particle ratios, enabling correct stoichiometric calculations. Without moles, we couldn't scale reaction ratios to practical quantities.

The molar mass of haemoglobin is 68 000 g/mol. How many molecules are in 1 g? What does this suggest about haemoglobin's structure? Hard
Show Answer

n = 1/68000 = 1.47×10²&sup5; mol. Molecules = 1.47×10²&sup5; × 6.02×10²³ = 8.8×10¹&sup5; molecules. Relatively few molecules in 1 g means each haemoglobin molecule is enormous (hundreds of thousands of atoms). This suggests it is a very large, complex protein with thousands of amino acids — consistent with its 4 protein subunits and 4 iron-containing haem groups.

Why is it important that all atoms of the same element have (approximately) the same mass? How does this underpin the mole? Hard
Show Answer

If atoms of the same element had variable masses, we couldn't define a consistent molar mass. The mole works because 1 mole of carbon always has the same mass (12 g for C-12), allowing predictable, reproducible calculations. Isotope averaging gives the relative atomic mass used in mole calculations, which is constant for natural samples of each element.

Calculate how many breaths of air you take in a lifetime (assume 15 breaths/min, 80 years, 500 mL/breath, air = 78% N&sub2;). Find moles of N&sub2; inhaled. Hard
Show Answer

Total breaths = 15 × 60 × 24 × 365 × 80 = 6.3×10&sup8;. Volume = 6.3×10&sup8; × 0.5 L = 3.15×10&sup8; L. N&sub2; volume = 0.78 × 3.15×10&sup8; = 2.46×10&sup8; L. At STP (22.4 L/mol): n = 2.46×10&sup8;/22.4 ≈ 1.1×10&sup7; moles of N&sub2; in a lifetime.

Avogadro's number was originally determined experimentally. Describe one method (e.g., oil drop experiment, X-ray diffraction). Hard
Show Answer

Oil drop (Langmuir): a drop of oil spreads on water to a single molecule thick. Measure drop volume (V) and slick area (A). Molecule diameter d = V/A ≈ 10²¹&sup0; m. Knowing molar volume and molecule size gives N_A. Modern value uses X-ray diffraction of crystals: measure atomic spacing, calculate atoms per unit cell, relate to crystal volume → N_A = 6.02214076 × 10²³ mol²¹ (SI-defined since 2019).

If you had 1 mole of £1 coins, how long would it take to spend them at £1 million per second? Compare to the age of the universe. Hard
Show Answer

Coins = 6.02×10²³. At £10&sup6;/s: time = 6.02×10²³/10&sup6; = 6.02×10¹&sup7; seconds = ~1.9×10¹&sup0; years ≈ 19 billion years. The universe is ~13.8 billion years old — you couldn't spend them even in the lifetime of the universe. This illustrates why Avogadro's number is essential: it's the only unit that makes counting atoms practical.

Set A Recall & Concept

State the law of conservation of mass. What does it mean for chemical equations? Easy
Show Answer

The law of conservation of mass states that mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. For equations, this means the total number of each type of atom must be the same on both sides of the arrow (reactants = products).

What is the golden rule of balancing equations? What can you change and what must you NOT change? Easy
Show Answer

You may ONLY change coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) to balance an equation. You must NEVER change subscripts (numbers inside formulas) as that would change the identity of the substances.

Balance: H&sub2; + O&sub2; → H&sub2;O. Show your working. Easy
Show Answer

Unbalanced: H&sub2; + O&sub2; → H&sub2;O. Count O: 2 on left, 1 on right → add coefficient 2 to H&sub2;O: H&sub2; + O&sub2; → 2H&sub2;O. Now H: 2 on left, 4 on right → add coefficient 2 to H&sub2;. Balanced: 2H&sub2; + O&sub2; → 2H&sub2;O. Check: H: 4=4 ✓, O: 2=2 ✓.

Why can't you balance an equation by changing subscripts? Give an example of what goes wrong. Medium
Show Answer

Changing subscripts changes the substance entirely. E.g., changing H&sub2;O to H&sub2;O&sub2; to "balance" an equation means you're now dealing with hydrogen peroxide (a different, toxic compound), not water. The equation would no longer represent the intended reaction.

What do the state symbols (s), (l), (g), (aq) mean? Give an example equation using all four. Medium
Show Answer

(s) = solid, (l) = liquid, (g) = gas, (aq) = aqueous (dissolved in water). Example: CaCO&sub3;(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl&sub2;(aq) + H&sub2;O(l) + CO&sub2;(g).

Balance: Fe + O&sub2; → Fe&sub2;O&sub3;. Medium
Show Answer

Unbalanced: Fe + O&sub2; → Fe&sub2;O&sub3;. Balance Fe: 4Fe + O&sub2; → 2Fe&sub2;O&sub3;. Balance O: 4Fe + 3O&sub2; → 2Fe&sub2;O&sub3;. Check: Fe: 4=4 ✓, O: 6=6 ✓.

What information does a balanced equation provide beyond just identifying reactants and products? Medium
Show Answer

A balanced equation provides: (1) identity of reactants and products; (2) mole ratios of each substance; (3) state symbols; (4) conservation of mass (atoms balanced). It enables stoichiometric calculations for real reactions.

Explain what "stoichiometric coefficients" tell us about a reaction. Hard
Show Answer

Stoichiometric coefficients (the numbers in front of formulas in a balanced equation) give the mole ratio of reactants and products. E.g., in 2H&sub2; + O&sub2; → 2H&sub2;O: 2 mol H&sub2; reacts with 1 mol O&sub2; to give 2 mol H&sub2;O. These ratios are used to calculate how much of each substance reacts and is produced.

Chemical Bonding — Ionic and Covalent

📺 Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Ionic and covalent bonds Ionic bonding in NaCl
CrashCourse Chemical Bonding Overview

Set A Recall & Concept

Describe how an ionic bond forms. Give one example. Easy
Show Answer

An ionic bond forms when a metal atom transfers one or more electrons to a non-metal atom. This creates oppositely charged ions (cation and anion) that attract each other by electrostatic force. Example: Na + Cl → Na⁺Cl⁻ (sodium chloride).

Describe how a covalent bond forms. Give one example. Easy
Show Answer

A covalent bond forms when two non-metal atoms share one or more pairs of electrons to achieve full outer shells. Example: H&sub2; — two hydrogen atoms share 1 electron each to form a single covalent bond (H—H).

State four properties of ionic compounds. Easy
Show Answer

1. High melting and boiling points (strong ionic lattice forces). 2. Solid at room temperature. 3. Conduct electricity when dissolved in water or when molten (ions free to move). 4. Often dissolve in water (polar solvent). 5. Hard but brittle.

State four properties of covalent compounds (molecular). Easy
Show Answer

1. Generally low melting/boiling points (weak intermolecular forces). 2. Often liquid or gas at room temperature. 3. Do NOT conduct electricity (no ions). 4. May be soluble in organic solvents. 5. Softer than ionic solids.

Why do ionic compounds conduct electricity when dissolved in water but not when solid? Medium
Show Answer

In solid state, ions are fixed in the crystal lattice — they cannot move, so no current flows. When dissolved, water molecules surround and separate the ions, allowing them to move freely through the solution and carry charge. When molten (liquid), ions can also move freely.

Why do ionic compounds have high melting points? Medium
Show Answer

Ionic compounds form a giant 3D lattice of alternating positive and negative ions held by strong electrostatic attractions in all directions. Breaking this lattice requires a large amount of energy, resulting in high melting points. E.g., NaCl melts at 801°C.

What determines whether an element forms an ionic or covalent bond in a compound? Medium
Show Answer

Electronegativity difference: if the difference is large (typically >1.7, between metals and non-metals), an ionic bond forms (electron transfer). If small (between non-metals), a covalent bond forms (electron sharing). General rule: metal + non-metal → ionic; non-metal + non-metal → covalent.

Draw a dot-and-cross diagram for the formation of Na⁺Cl⁻. Medium
Show Answer

Na: [2,8,1] → Na⁺ [2,8] (loses 1 electron, shown leaving). Cl: [2,8,7] → Cl⁻ [2,8,8] (gains 1 electron, shown entering). Both ions now have full outer shells like noble gases. The transferred electron is shown in the Cl⁻ outer shell.

Show Answer

(a) MgO: ionic (metal + non-metal). (b) H&sub2;O: covalent (non-metal + non-metal). (c) KBr: ionic. (d) CO&sub2;: covalent. (e) CaCl&sub2;: ionic.

Explain the difference in melting points: NaCl (801°C) vs HCl (−114°C). Medium

NaCl: ionic compound with strong electrostatic forces in a 3D lattice → high MP. HCl: covalent molecule; H—Cl bond is strong but the intermolecular forces between HCl molecules (dipole-dipole) are weak → very low MP. Bond type and structure determine melting points.

Draw a dot-and-cross diagram for H&sub2;O showing the covalent bonds and lone pairs. Medium
Show Answer

O atom has 6 valence electrons: shares 1 with each H atom, forming 2 O—H bonds. Remaining 4 electrons on O form 2 lone pairs. Each H shares its 1 electron with O, completing H's shell of 2. H—O—H with O having 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs.

Why does solid aluminium conduct electricity but solid aluminium oxide (Al&sub2;O&sub3;) does not? Medium
Show Answer

Aluminium metal: electrons are delocalised (free to move) throughout the metallic lattice → conducts electricity. Al&sub2;O&sub3;: ionic compound; ions are fixed in the solid lattice and cannot move → does not conduct. When Al&sub2;O&sub3; is molten, ions can move and it then conducts.

Account for why water (covalent) dissolves ionic compounds like NaCl but does not dissolve covalent compounds like wax. Medium
Show Answer

"Like dissolves like." Water is a polar molecule; it is attracted to the charged ions in NaCl (ion-dipole interaction), pulling them into solution (hydration). Wax (covalent, non-polar) has no charges for water to interact with. Non-polar substances dissolve in non-polar solvents (e.g., wax in petrol).

Compare single, double, and triple covalent bonds using examples from O&sub2;, N&sub2;, and CO&sub2;. Hard
Show Answer

Single bond: 1 shared electron pair (e.g., H—H). Double bond: 2 shared pairs (e.g., O=O in O&sub2;; O=C=O in CO&sub2;). Triple bond: 3 shared pairs (e.g., N≡N in N&sub2;). More shared pairs = stronger, shorter bond. N≡N (triple) is very strong, making N&sub2; very unreactive.

Explain why diamond (covalent) and graphite (covalent) have such different properties despite both being pure carbon. Hard
Show Answer

Diamond: each C bonded to 4 others in 3D tetrahedral network (giant covalent lattice) → very hard, does not conduct electricity, extremely high melting point. Graphite: C bonded in 2D hexagonal layers (3 bonds per C), with delocalised electrons between layers → soft (layers slide), conducts electricity, high melting point but lower than diamond. Same atoms, different bonding structure = completely different properties.

Predict whether MgCl&sub2; or CCl&sub4; would (a) have higher melting point (b) conduct electricity in solution. Justify. Hard
Show Answer

(a) MgCl&sub2; (ionic) has much higher MP (714°C) than CCl&sub4; (covalent, −23°C) because ionic lattice forces > intermolecular forces between CCl&sub4; molecules. (b) MgCl&sub2; conducts in solution (Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻ ions). CCl&sub4; does not dissolve in water well and produces no ions either way → does not conduct.

Set C Critical Thinking & Extended Response

Explain why ionic bonds always involve transfer of electrons, not sharing. Use energy arguments. Hard
Show Answer

Ionic bonds form between elements with very different electronegativities. The more electronegative non-metal "pulls" electrons completely from the less electronegative metal. This is energetically favourable because: (1) the metal lowers its energy by achieving noble gas configuration; (2) the energy released by the electrostatic attraction between ions (lattice energy) more than compensates for the ionisation energy spent. Sharing would not achieve stable octets as efficiently.

Why is water's covalent bonding crucial for life? Discuss polarity, hydrogen bonding, and high boiling point. Hard
Show Answer

Water (H—O—H) is a polar covalent molecule (O is more electronegative, causing δ− on O, δ⁺ on H). This polarity enables hydrogen bonding between water molecules, which: (1) gives water an unusually high boiling point (100°C) for its molecular mass, keeping it liquid at life temperatures; (2) makes it an excellent polar solvent for ions and biomolecules; (3) gives water high surface tension for biological processes. Without covalent polarity, none of these properties would exist.

Compare the electrical conductivity of NaCl solid, NaCl solution, NaCl molten, and HCl gas. Explain each case. Hard
Show Answer

NaCl solid: NO — ions fixed in lattice, cannot move. NaCl solution: YES — Na⁺ and Cl⁻ free to move in water. NaCl molten: YES — ions free to move as liquid. HCl gas: NO — covalent molecules, no ions present. HCl dissolved in water: YES — fully ionises to H⁺ + Cl⁻. This illustrates that conductivity requires free-moving charged particles.

Silicon dioxide (SiO&sub2;) is a covalent compound but has a melting point of 1710°C, much higher than most covalent compounds. Explain why. Hard
Show Answer

SiO&sub2; is a giant covalent (macromolecular) structure — every Si is covalently bonded to 4 O atoms in a 3D network. To melt it, you must break covalent bonds throughout the entire structure, requiring enormous energy. This is unlike simple covalent molecules (H&sub2;O, CO&sub2;) where only weak intermolecular forces between molecules must be overcome to melt/boil.

Why does the pharmaceutical industry prefer manufacturing drugs as covalent organic compounds rather than ionic salts? What are the tradeoffs? Hard
Show Answer

Covalent organic drugs: (1) more structurally diverse (can have specific shapes to fit protein receptors); (2) often cross cell membranes (non-polar varieties are lipid-soluble); (3) can be designed for specific metabolic stability. Ionic salt forms of drugs: dissolve faster (better bioavailability), more crystalline (easier to formulate/store). Trade-off: drug developers often make both forms and test which gives better absorption, stability, and selectivity.

Electrolysis of molten NaCl produces Na metal and Cl&sub2; gas. Write the half-equations and explain what happens at each electrode. Link to ionic bonding. Hard
Show Answer

Cathode (negative): Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na (reduction — Na⁺ gains electron to become Na metal). Anode (positive): 2Cl⁻ → Cl&sub2; + 2e⁻ (oxidation — Cl⁻ loses electrons to form Cl&sub2; gas). Ionic bonding is reversed — the NaCl ionic lattice is split back into its elements. This requires molten NaCl (so ions can move) and energy (reverse of the exothermic bond formation).

Design an experiment to distinguish an ionic compound from a covalent compound using three different tests. Medium
Show Answer

Test 1: Electrical conductivity in solution — dissolve in water, connect electrodes/bulb. Ionic: bulb lights (ions conduct). Covalent: no light. Test 2: Melting point — heat sample. Ionic: high MP (>300°C typically). Covalent: low MP. Test 3: Solubility — test in water and in organic solvent (e.g., hexane). Ionic: dissolves in water. Non-polar covalent: dissolves in hexane. Each test provides independent evidence.

Explain how metallic bonding differs from ionic and covalent bonding. Why does this make metals good conductors and ductile? Hard
Show Answer

Metallic bonding: positive metal ions in a "sea" of delocalised electrons. Unlike ionic (complete transfer) or covalent (specific sharing), metallic electrons are free to move throughout the entire metal lattice. Conductivity: delocalised electrons carry charge freely (no heating needed). Ductility: metal ions can slide past each other without breaking bonds (the electron sea flows around them), allowing metals to be drawn into wires or hammered into sheets without fracturing.
