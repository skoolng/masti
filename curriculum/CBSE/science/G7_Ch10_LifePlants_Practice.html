Photosynthesis

Set A Easy – Recall & Identify

Write the word equation for photosynthesis.Easy
Show Answer

Carbon dioxide + Water + Light energy → Glucose + Oxygen

Write the chemical equation for photosynthesis.Easy
Show Answer

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Where in the plant cell does photosynthesis occur?Easy
Show Answer

In the chloroplasts, which contain the green pigment chlorophyll that absorbs light energy.

List three factors that affect the rate of photosynthesis.Easy
Show Answer

Light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature (and water availability).

What is chlorophyll and what is its role in photosynthesis?Easy
Show Answer

Chlorophyll is a green pigment in chloroplasts that absorbs light energy (mainly red and blue wavelengths) to power the photosynthesis reaction.

What two raw materials do plants need for photosynthesis and where does each come from?Easy
Show Answer

CO₂ – absorbed from air through stomata in leaves. Water – absorbed by roots from soil, transported up via xylem.

What are the two products of photosynthesis and how is each used by the plant?Easy
Show Answer

Glucose – used for energy (respiration), growth, stored as starch. Oxygen – released through stomata (most is released; some used in plant's own respiration).

Why do plants appear green?Easy
Show Answer

Chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light but reflects green light – the reflected green light reaches our eyes, making plants appear green.

Set B Medium – Understand & Apply

Explain what is meant by a "limiting factor" in photosynthesis. Give an example.Medium
Show Answer

A limiting factor is the factor in shortest supply that prevents the rate of photosynthesis from increasing further, even if other factors are optimal. Example: on a bright day, if CO₂ is low, increasing light does not increase the rate – CO₂ is the limiting factor.

A plant is placed in a greenhouse with high CO₂ and strong light but kept at 5°C. Explain what limits its photosynthesis rate.Medium
Show Answer

Temperature is the limiting factor. Photosynthesis depends on enzymes (e.g., RuBisCO) that slow down at low temperatures. Even with optimal light and CO₂, cold conditions reduce enzyme activity and the rate of photosynthesis.

Describe an experiment to show that light is needed for photosynthesis using a variegated leaf (part green, part white).Medium
Show Answer

Use a variegated leaf. Remove leaf, decolorise in hot ethanol, wash with water. Add iodine solution. Green areas (with chlorophyll) turn blue-black = starch present (photosynthesis occurred). White areas (no chlorophyll) remain orange-brown = no starch. Conclusion: chlorophyll is needed for photosynthesis.

Why do plants carry out both photosynthesis and respiration, while animals only respire?Medium
Show Answer

Plants are autotrophs – they make their own food (glucose) via photosynthesis using sunlight. They then use this glucose in respiration to release energy for growth and other processes. Animals cannot photosynthesise (no chloroplasts) – they must eat to obtain glucose, then respire it.

Explain the compensation point in photosynthesis.Medium
Show Answer

The compensation point is the light intensity at which the rate of photosynthesis exactly equals the rate of respiration – no net gas exchange. Below this point, respiration exceeds photosynthesis; above it, net photosynthesis occurs and the plant gains organic matter.

Predict how a plant would respond if CO₂ levels in its environment doubled, assuming light and temperature are not limiting.Medium
Show Answer

The rate of photosynthesis would increase (more CO₂ substrate available for carbon fixation). The plant would produce more glucose, allowing faster growth. Eventually another factor (light or temperature) would become limiting and the rate would plateau.

A student tests a pond plant (Elodea) by counting oxygen bubbles per minute at different distances from a lamp. What variable is being changed and what results would you expect?Medium
Show Answer

Variable changed: light intensity (distance from lamp – closer = more intense light). Expected: as distance decreases (light intensity increases), bubble rate increases. At very close distances, another factor (CO₂ or temperature) may limit rate and the count plateaus.

Why is photosynthesis described as an energy conversion process?Medium
Show Answer

Photosynthesis converts light energy (from the sun) into chemical energy stored in the bonds of glucose molecules. This energy can later be released by respiration. It transforms one form of energy (light) into another (chemical) – hence an energy conversion process.

Set C Hard – MYP Criterion B & D

Design a full experiment to investigate how light intensity affects the rate of photosynthesis in Elodea. Include hypothesis, method, data analysis strategy. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: as light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis (O₂ bubble count per minute) increases until a limiting factor is reached. Method: place Elodea sprig in NaHCO₃ solution (ensures constant CO₂), position lamp at 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 cm. Count bubbles per minute for 3 minutes at each distance. Average results. Controlled: same sprig, same water temperature (water bath), same CO₂ level. Analysis: plot bubble rate vs. 1/d² (inverse square of distance = light intensity). Expect positive correlation, then plateau.

Critically evaluate: "Increasing CO₂ levels in the atmosphere will indefinitely increase global plant growth." (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Partially supported: CO₂ is a reactant in photosynthesis; higher CO₂ can increase photosynthesis rates (CO₂ fertilisation effect). However: (1) temperature increase (climate change) may exceed enzyme optima, reducing rates; (2) droughts reduce water availability; (3) other nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus) may become limiting; (4) some plants are C4 (more efficient) vs C3 – effects differ by species. Overall plant growth response is complex and non-linear.

Analyse the differences between C3 and C4 photosynthesis and explain why C4 plants (e.g., maize, sugarcane) are more efficient in hot, dry climates. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

C3 plants (most crops) use a 3-carbon compound first; when it's hot, stomata close to prevent water loss → less CO₂ enters → photorespiration (wasteful process). C4 plants have a special CO₂ concentration mechanism: CO₂ is fixed in outer mesophyll cells and pumped to inner bundle sheath cells in high concentration, suppressing photorespiration. This allows C4 plants to photosynthesise efficiently even with stomata partly closed → less water loss and higher efficiency in hot/dry conditions.

A student's data shows photosynthesis rate increases from 10°C to 30°C but drops sharply at 40°C. Explain the molecular reason for each part of this graph. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

10°C–30°C: enzyme activity (Calvin cycle enzymes, e.g., RuBisCO) increases with temperature → more substrate-enzyme collisions per second → faster rate. Above 35–40°C: enzymes begin to denature; active sites change shape → cannot catalyse reactions → rate falls sharply. The temperature optimum is ~25–35°C for most plant enzymes. Note: the light reactions are less temperature-sensitive as they are photochemical.

Evaluate the potential of artificial photosynthesis as a technology for tackling climate change and food security. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Artificial photosynthesis replicates the light-capture and water-splitting reactions using semiconductor catalysts to produce hydrogen or organic compounds from CO₂ and sunlight. Potential: removes CO₂ (climate benefit), produces clean fuel (H₂), could create food molecules independently of farmland. Limitations: current efficiency very low (~1% vs. plants' 1–3%); expensive materials; not yet scalable. It holds promise but is not yet a practical solution – requires significant research investment.

Plan an investigation to compare photosynthesis rates in high-altitude mountain plants vs. lowland plants. What variables would differ and why? (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: plant type (high-altitude vs. lowland same species if possible). DV: O₂ production per gram of leaf per hour under same laboratory conditions. Key variables that differ in their natural environment: UV intensity (higher at altitude), lower atmospheric CO₂ pressure, lower temperatures, higher light scatter. Standardise lab conditions for fair test. Predict: mountain plants may have higher chlorophyll density per unit area (adaptation to lower light/CO₂) or different enzyme temperature optima.

Using atomic tracking of radioactive ¹⁴CO₂, how would you prove that the carbon in glucose comes from CO₂ and not water? (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

Supply plant with ¹⁴CO₂ (radioactively labelled carbon dioxide) while using normal H₂O. After photosynthesis, extract glucose and test for radioactivity. If glucose is radioactive → carbon came from ¹⁴CO₂. Repeat with ¹⁸O-labelled water – the ¹⁸O appears in oxygen gas released (not in glucose), proving oxygen in O₂ product comes from water, not CO₂. Calvin's experiment using ¹⁴CO₂ in Chlorella algae confirmed glucose carbon originates from CO₂.

Critically discuss whether deforestation in tropical regions has a greater negative impact on the global carbon cycle than fossil fuel combustion. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Fossil fuels: currently contribute ~37 Gt CO₂/year globally – the primary driver of rising atmospheric CO₂. Deforestation: contributes ~4–8 Gt CO₂/year directly (burning/decay) PLUS removes carbon sink (tropical forests absorb ~2 Gt CO₂/year). So total deforestation impact is ~6–10 Gt CO₂/year. Conclusion: fossil fuels are currently the larger source, but deforestation has a double impact (source + sink removal). Both must be addressed; they are not mutually exclusive problems.

Leaf Structure

Set A Easy – Recall & Identify

Name four adaptations of a leaf for photosynthesis.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Broad and flat – large surface area to catch light. (2) Thin – short diffusion distance for CO₂. (3) Contains chlorophyll – absorbs light. (4) Many stomata – gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ out).

What are stomata and where are they found on a leaf?Easy
Show Answer

Stomata are tiny pores on the leaf surface (mainly the lower epidermis) through which CO₂ enters, O₂ and water vapour exit. Each stoma is surrounded by two guard cells that control its opening.

What is the function of the palisade mesophyll layer?Easy
Show Answer

The palisade mesophyll is the main photosynthesis layer – packed with chloroplasts near the top of the leaf to absorb maximum sunlight.

What do leaf veins contain and what is their function?Easy
Show Answer

Veins contain xylem (water and mineral transport upward) and phloem (sugar transport). Veins also provide structural support to the leaf.

What is the function of the waxy cuticle on the upper leaf surface?Easy
Show Answer

The waxy cuticle is waterproof and transparent – it reduces water loss through the upper leaf surface while allowing light to pass through to the palisade cells below.

Name the two types of mesophyll and their positions in the leaf.Easy
Show Answer

Palisade mesophyll – upper layer, columnar cells packed with chloroplasts, main site of photosynthesis. Spongy mesophyll – lower layer, loosely packed cells with air spaces for gas diffusion.

What gas enters a leaf during the day through stomata?Easy
Show Answer

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) enters for photosynthesis. Oxygen (O₂) and water vapour exit. At night, CO₂ exits and O₂ enters (respiration only, no photosynthesis).

Why are stomata mostly on the lower (underside) surface of leaves?Easy
Show Answer

The lower surface is shaded and cooler, reducing evaporative water loss through open stomata. The upper surface faces direct sun and would lose too much water if covered with stomata.

Set B Medium – Understand & Apply

Explain how guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata.Medium
Show Answer

Guard cells absorb water by osmosis when light is available (daytime) → cells swell and their thickened inner wall bows outward → stoma opens. At night or during drought, water leaves guard cells → cells become flaccid → stoma closes. This regulates gas exchange and water loss.

Compare the palisade and spongy mesophyll in terms of structure and function.Medium
Show Answer

Palisade: columnar cells, tightly packed, many chloroplasts, near top of leaf – maximises light absorption for photosynthesis. Spongy: irregularly shaped, loosely packed with air spaces, fewer chloroplasts – air spaces allow CO₂ and O₂ to diffuse easily to and from cells.

Explain why leaves are thin and why this is an advantage for photosynthesis.Medium
Show Answer

Thinness means: (1) CO₂ from stomata only needs to diffuse a short distance to reach mesophyll cells; (2) light penetrates to lower cell layers. Both increase the efficiency and rate of photosynthesis.

A student notices that aquatic plants (like water lilies) have stomata on the UPPER surface of leaves, unlike land plants. Explain this.Medium
Show Answer

Water lily leaves float on water – the lower surface is submerged. Stomata on the lower surface would be blocked by water and unable to allow gas exchange. Placing stomata on the upper surface (exposed to air) allows efficient CO₂ uptake and O₂ release.

What would happen to photosynthesis if all stomata were permanently closed? Explain using the equation.Medium
Show Answer

CO₂ could not enter the leaf. Without CO₂ as a reactant, photosynthesis (6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂) would stop. The plant would use up stored sugars, then be unable to produce new ones, eventually dying. O₂ also could not escape.

Explain how the leaf is adapted to receive maximum light even when sun angle changes during the day.Medium
Show Answer

Many plants have petioles (leaf stalks) that twist and orient the leaf blade to face the sun throughout the day (heliotropism). The flat broad surface maximises light interception. In forest canopy, leaves are arranged in a mosaic pattern to minimise overlap (leaf mosaic), ensuring all leaves receive adequate light.

Why do cacti have reduced leaves (spines) and carry out photosynthesis mainly in their thick green stems?Medium
Show Answer

In desert environments, leaves lose too much water. Cacti evolved spines (modified leaves) that have minimal surface area → minimal water loss. The thick green stem takes over photosynthesis and also stores water. This is a trade-off: less photosynthesis surface but far greater water conservation for survival.

What is the role of the air spaces in the spongy mesophyll and how do they aid photosynthesis?Medium
Show Answer

Air spaces create a large internal surface area for gas exchange. CO₂ diffuses through stomata → air spaces → dissolves in moist cell walls → enters spongy mesophyll cells. O₂ produced in photosynthesis follows the reverse path. The interconnected air spaces act as a gas distribution system inside the leaf.

Set C Hard – MYP Criterion B & D

Evaluate the claim: "Sun leaves and shade leaves of the same tree species are identical because they share the same DNA." (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

False. Sun leaves and shade leaves show phenotypic plasticity (same genotype, different phenotype due to environment). Sun leaves: thicker, more palisade layers, more chloroplasts per cell, smaller. Shade leaves: thinner, larger surface area, fewer chloroplasts but more spread out to capture available light. DNA is identical but gene expression differs in response to light environment during leaf development.

Design an experiment to test whether the number of stomata per cm² differs between the upper and lower surfaces of a leaf. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

Method: Apply clear nail varnish to both upper and lower surfaces of the same leaf. Allow to dry. Peel off thin strips. Mount on microscope slides. View under microscope at same magnification. Count stomata in a defined field of view (e.g., 1mm²). Repeat for 5 different areas per surface. Average counts. Compare. Controlled: same leaf, same species, same magnification. Prediction: lower surface has significantly more stomata.

Analyse the evolutionary and functional reasons why rainforest leaves have drip tips (elongated pointed leaf tips) while desert plants have succulent round leaves. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Rainforest drip tips: allow rapid drainage of excess rainwater from leaf surface (water creates fungal/algae growth, blocks light to stomata, adds weight). Desert succulents: round shape minimises surface area:volume ratio → minimises water loss through cuticle and reduces heat absorption. Both are evolutionary adaptations maximising photosynthesis efficiency within their specific environmental constraints.

Explain why autumn leaf colour change occurs and link it to chlorophyll degradation and nutrient recovery. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

As days shorten and temperatures drop, deciduous trees prepare to shed leaves. Chlorophyll (green) is broken down and its nitrogen is recovered and stored in the stem (efficient nutrient recycling). As green colour disappears, yellow/orange carotenoid pigments (always present but masked by chlorophyll) become visible. Some trees also produce red anthocyanin pigments (protecting recovering nutrients from UV damage). Leaf drop reduces water loss during winter drought when soil water is frozen.

Critically evaluate the use of leaf wax coatings as an agricultural intervention to reduce water loss in drought-prone regions. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Thicker cuticle/wax reduces transpiration water loss → conserves water in drought. However: wax also reduces CO₂ diffusion into leaf → potential reduction in photosynthesis rate → lower crop yield. Trade-off: water conservation vs. photosynthesis rate. Genetic modification for thicker wax has shown mixed results. Some crops (e.g., jojoba) naturally produce heavy wax – could provide gene donor. Environmental concerns about GM crops apply. Mulching and irrigation may be more practical alternatives.

Plan an investigation comparing CO₂ uptake rates in leaves of different ages (young vs. old leaves) on the same plant. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: leaf age (young new leaves vs. mature mid-leaves vs. old leaves – identified by position on branch). DV: rate of CO₂ absorption (use CO₂ probe in sealed chamber or IRGA – infrared gas analyser). Controlled: same leaf area (cut standard size discs), same light intensity, same temperature, same plant species. Measure CO₂ concentration change per minute. Predict: young/mature leaves photosynthesise fastest; old leaves may have damaged chloroplasts → lower rate.

Explain why aquatic plants that are fully submerged (e.g., Elodea) do not need a waxy cuticle or stomata. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Waxy cuticle: land plants need it to prevent water loss. Elodea is surrounded by water, so water loss through leaves is not a problem – no cuticle needed. Stomata: land plants need stomata to obtain CO₂ from air. Elodea is surrounded by water containing dissolved CO₂, which diffuses directly through the thin cell walls across the entire leaf surface. No specialised pores are needed. The entire submerged surface acts as a gas exchange surface.

Evaluate the impact of air pollution (e.g., particulate matter blocking stomata) on urban tree health and ecosystem services. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Particulate matter deposits on leaf surfaces, blocking stomata → reduced CO₂ entry → reduced photosynthesis → weaker trees with lower growth and carbon storage. Also blocks light to chloroplasts. Long-term: reduced immune response → more susceptible to pests and disease. Ecosystem services affected: reduced carbon sequestration (worsens air quality), reduced oxygen production, reduced shade/cooling effect (urban heat island worsens). Urban tree selection should favour species with waxy/hairy leaves that trap or shed particles more effectively (e.g., London Plane tree).

Transport in Plants

Set A Easy – Recall & Identify

What does xylem transport and in which direction?Easy
Show Answer

Xylem transports water and dissolved minerals from roots upward to leaves. Movement is one-directional (upward only).

What does phloem transport and in which direction?Easy
Show Answer

Phloem transports dissolved sugars (sucrose) and other organic compounds made during photosynthesis. Movement is bidirectional – from leaves (source) to any organ that needs food (sink): roots, fruits, stems.

What is translocation?Easy
Show Answer

Translocation is the movement of dissolved sugars and other organic solutes through the phloem from areas of production (leaves/source) to areas of use or storage (roots, fruits/sinks). It requires energy (active transport).

Why is xylem tissue dead at maturity?Easy
Show Answer

Xylem vessels are dead, hollow tubes with no cell contents (no nucleus, no cytoplasm). This creates an unobstructed channel for water flow under pressure. Living cells would block the movement of water.

What is osmosis in the context of water entering root hair cells?Easy
Show Answer

Osmosis is the movement of water molecules from high water concentration (dilute soil water) to low water concentration (more concentrated cell sap in root hair cells) across a semi-permeable membrane.

What are root hair cells and why are they important for water absorption?Easy
Show Answer

Root hair cells are elongated cells on roots that greatly increase surface area for water and mineral absorption from soil. Their large surface area maximises the rate of osmosis and active uptake of minerals.

What force drives water upward through xylem vessels?Easy
Show Answer

Transpiration pull (suction): water evaporating from leaves creates negative pressure that pulls water up through xylem like a straw. Root pressure (osmotic push from roots) and capillarity also contribute.

Name two minerals absorbed by plant roots and why each is needed.Easy
Show Answer

Nitrates – needed for protein synthesis and growth. Magnesium – needed for chlorophyll production. (Also: phosphates for DNA/ATP; potassium for enzyme activation.)

Set B Medium – Understand & Apply

Explain the cohesion-tension theory of water movement in xylem.Medium
Show Answer

Water molecules are cohesive (attracted to each other via hydrogen bonds), forming a continuous column in xylem. As water evaporates from leaves (transpiration), it creates tension (negative pressure/suction) at the top. This tension pulls the water column upward – like pulling the top of a chain pulls the whole chain. Adhesion to xylem walls prevents the column breaking. This works even to heights of 100+ m in tall trees.

Why does phloem require energy (ATP) for transport while xylem does not?Medium
Show Answer

Phloem transport (translocation) moves sugars against or without a driving physical force – companion cells actively load sucrose into phloem sieve tubes using ATP-powered pumps. Xylem transport is passive – driven by transpiration pull (physical evaporation), requiring no metabolic energy from the plant.

A farmer applies excess nitrogen fertiliser and notices plants wilt. Explain why using osmosis.Medium
Show Answer

Excess fertiliser makes soil water more concentrated (high solute). By osmosis, water moves from cells (less concentrated) into soil (more concentrated) – the opposite of normal. Root cells lose water and become flaccid → plant wilts. This is plasmolysis/osmotic stress caused by fertiliser overdose.

Compare the structure of xylem and phloem cells with relation to their function.Medium
Show Answer

Xylem: dead, hollow, thick lignified walls (structural strength), no end walls → continuous tube for water flow. Phloem: living, sieve tube elements (perforated end walls = sieve plates), companion cells provide energy for active loading → enables active transport of organic solutes in both directions.

Explain what happens to water transport if a ring of bark (containing phloem) is removed from a tree trunk (ring barking).Medium
Show Answer

Xylem (inside the bark) is not cut, so water and mineral transport from roots upward continues. However, phloem (in the bark) is severed – sugars cannot move downward to roots. Roots are starved of sugars → cannot respire → die. Without roots, water uptake stops → eventually the whole tree dies. This is why ring barking kills trees.

How do plants absorb mineral ions against their concentration gradient?Medium
Show Answer

Root cells use active transport – protein pumps in the cell membrane use ATP energy to move mineral ions (e.g., nitrates, potassium) from low concentration in soil water into the root cell where concentration is already higher. This process requires respiration to produce ATP.

Why do plants wilt when the soil is dry even though the air is humid?Medium
Show Answer

Water enters roots by osmosis from soil water. In dry soil, there is insufficient water for osmosis → root cells cannot take in water → xylem has no water to transport → leaf cells lose turgor pressure → leaf becomes flaccid → wilts. Air humidity does not help as water must enter through roots, not leaves.

What is turgor pressure and why is it important for plant support?Medium
Show Answer

Turgor pressure is the pressure of water inside a plant cell pushing against the cell wall. When cells are full of water (turgid), they press against each other, making the plant firm and upright. Loss of turgor (plasmolysis due to water loss) causes wilting. Plants without woody support (herbaceous plants) rely entirely on turgor for structural support.

Set C Hard – MYP Criterion B & D

Design an experiment to demonstrate that xylem is the water transport tissue using a celery stalk and coloured water. State method, expected results, and controls. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

Method: place celery stalk in red-dyed water for 24 hours. Cut cross-sections at different heights. Observe coloured sections under magnification. Expected: red colour appears only in xylem vessels (visible as dots in the vascular bundles). Control: celery in plain water – no red vessels. This demonstrates xylem's role in upward water transport.

Critically analyse the cohesion-tension theory. What evidence supports it, and what is its main limitation? (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Evidence: (1) Pressure bomb measurements show negative pressure in xylem consistent with tension; (2) dye experiments confirm continuous water column; (3) cutting xylem causes air locks that stop water flow; (4) transpiration rate correlates with water transport rate. Limitation: the water column under tension is theoretically unstable – air bubbles (cavitation) should break it. Plants have mechanisms to refill embolised vessels, but the theory doesn't fully explain how tall trees maintain columns during drought. Some debate remains about exact mechanism in tallest trees.

Evaluate the impact of soil salinity (salt-contaminated soil) on the water transport system of a rice plant. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

High soil salinity increases soil water potential (concentrated salt solution). Water in root cells is more dilute → by osmosis, water leaves root cells into soil (reverse osmosis) → cells plasmolase → wilting and ion toxicity. Xylem cannot function without a water source. Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions may also be taken up and cause enzyme disruption. Salt-tolerant rice cultivars (developed through breeding/GM) use ion compartmentalisation and compatible solutes to counter osmotic stress.

Using your understanding of transport systems, predict the effect of waterlogged (flooded) soil on mineral ion uptake by roots. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Waterlogged soil has no air spaces → root cells cannot get oxygen → root cells cannot carry out aerobic respiration → no ATP produced → active transport of mineral ions (nitrates, phosphates) cannot occur. Plants show deficiency symptoms (e.g., yellowing from nitrogen deficiency). Water uptake may still occur by osmosis but mineral starvation harms the plant. This is why fields need drainage.

Plants in mangrove forests grow in seawater. Explain the osmotic challenge and the adaptations that allow water uptake. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Challenge: seawater is more concentrated than cell sap → water should leave cells by osmosis. Adaptations: (1) mangroves concentrate solutes in root cells above seawater concentration → maintain osmotic gradient for water uptake; (2) salt glands on leaves actively excrete excess salt; (3) ultrafiltration in roots blocks salt entry while allowing water through (salt exclusion). These mechanisms maintain the water potential gradient needed for water absorption.

A plant biologist injects a radioactive ¹⁴C-sucrose solution into a leaf and detects it 30 minutes later in both the roots and a developing fruit. What does this prove about phloem, and how does it relate to the source-sink model? (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

The bidirectional movement of ¹⁴C-sucrose to both roots (below leaf) and fruit (above leaf) proves: (1) phloem can transport in both directions (up and down) simultaneously; (2) the source-sink model is confirmed – photosynthesising leaf (source) exports sugars to both the sink below (roots, for storage/respiration) and the sink above (growing fruit, for development). Multiple sinks compete for phloem sugars, with stronger sinks receiving more.

Evaluate the use of plant grafting (joining two plants) as evidence for phloem transport across graft union. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Grafting joins a scion (top) to a rootstock (bottom). If phloem connections re-establish across the graft union, sugars produced by the scion's leaves travel through the graft to nourish the rootstock's roots, and vice versa. Grafted plants thrive only when vascular connections form. Girdling above the graft (cutting phloem) starves roots. This provides direct evidence that phloem carries organic nutrients across long distances, even across genetically different tissues.

Plan an investigation to compare the rate of water uptake via xylem in a plant stem at two different temperatures (10°C vs. 30°C). Include prediction based on viscosity principles. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: water temperature (10°C vs. 30°C). DV: water uptake rate (cm³/hour) measured by bubble movement in a potometer capillary tube. Method: set up potometer with fresh leafy shoot, fill with water at different temperatures in a water bath, measure bubble movement distance per minute. Controlled: same shoot species and size, same leaf area, same light intensity, same air humidity. Prediction: 30°C water has lower viscosity → flows more easily → higher uptake rate. Also: stomata may open more at 30°C, increasing transpiration pull.

Set A Easy – Recall & Identify

Define transpiration.Easy
Show Answer

Transpiration is the process by which water vapour is lost from plant leaves (and other aerial parts) through stomata into the atmosphere.

List four factors that increase the rate of transpiration.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Higher temperature, (2) lower humidity, (3) stronger wind, (4) higher light intensity (stomata open wider). Also: larger leaf surface area.

Why does transpiration rate increase in windy conditions?Easy
Show Answer

Wind blows away the humid air layer that builds up around the leaf (boundary layer). This maintains a steep concentration gradient for water vapour between inside and outside the leaf, increasing the rate of diffusion out.

What instrument is used to measure the rate of transpiration?Easy
Show Answer

A potometer measures the rate of water uptake by a cut leafy shoot, which approximates transpiration rate. The movement of an air bubble along a capillary tube is measured.

Why does transpiration rate decrease in high humidity?Easy
Show Answer

High humidity means the air outside the leaf already contains a lot of water vapour. The concentration gradient between inside the leaf (high water vapour) and outside is smaller → slower diffusion → lower transpiration rate.

Give two benefits and one disadvantage of transpiration for a plant.Easy
Show Answer

Benefits: (1) creates transpiration pull that drives water and mineral transport up the plant; (2) evaporation cools the leaf surface. Disadvantage: if water loss exceeds uptake, plant wilts and may die (especially in heat/drought).

How do guard cells respond to bright light and how does this affect transpiration?Easy
Show Answer

Bright light causes guard cells to absorb water (swell) → stomata open → more water vapour exits → transpiration rate increases during daytime.

Why do plants lose most water through leaves rather than stems?Easy
Show Answer

Leaves have a large surface area and many stomata for gas exchange. Stems are mostly covered by bark (waterproof) with few stomata. The large leaf surface maximises photosynthesis but also maximises water loss.

Set B Medium – Understand & Apply

Explain how transpiration creates a "transpiration pull" that moves water up the stem.Medium
Show Answer

As water evaporates from leaf cells, water concentration in those cells drops. Water from xylem in the leaf veins moves into these cells by osmosis. This creates tension/suction in the xylem that pulls the water column upward from the roots. Water cohesion maintains the continuous column.

Describe how a potometer experiment works. What does it actually measure?Medium
Show Answer

A cut leafy shoot is inserted airtight into a capillary tube filled with water. As the plant transpires, water is taken up, drawing an air bubble along the calibrated capillary. The distance the bubble moves per minute = rate of water uptake. This approximates (but is not exactly equal to) transpiration rate, as a very small amount of water is used in photosynthesis.

A student measures transpiration rate in a potometer at 20°C and 35°C. Predict the result and explain why.Medium
Show Answer

Prediction: higher rate at 35°C. Reason: higher temperature → water molecules have more kinetic energy → evaporate from mesophyll cells faster → concentration gradient between leaf interior and exterior increases → faster diffusion of water vapour through stomata → faster bubble movement in potometer.

Why might a plant close its stomata on a very hot day even though this reduces photosynthesis?Medium
Show Answer

Survival trade-off: on a very hot day, transpiration rate may exceed water uptake rate → the plant risks wilting and cell damage. The plant closes stomata (via ABA hormone signal) to reduce water loss. This sacrifices CO₂ uptake (reducing photosynthesis) but preserves cell hydration and prevents death. Short-term growth is sacrificed for survival.

Explain why deforestation of large forest areas can reduce local rainfall.Medium
Show Answer

Forests release enormous amounts of water vapour through transpiration (plus evaporation from soil/water bodies). This water vapour condenses to form clouds and falls as rain (the water cycle). Removing forests reduces this moisture source → fewer clouds → less precipitation → local climate becomes drier (as seen in parts of the Amazon).

Compare transpiration rates in a cactus and a deciduous tree in summer. Explain the difference.Medium
Show Answer

Cactus: very low transpiration – spines (modified leaves) have very small surface area; stomata open only at night (CAM photosynthesis). Deciduous tree: high transpiration in summer – broad leaves, many stomata, open during day for photosynthesis. The cactus is adapted for minimal water loss; the tree prioritises maximum photosynthesis and accepts water loss as the cost.

What is guttation and how does it differ from transpiration?Medium
Show Answer

Guttation is the exudation of liquid water droplets from leaf tips/edges through hydathodes, occurring at night or in high humidity when transpiration is low but root pressure is high. Transpiration: loss of water vapour through stomata driven by evaporation. Guttation: loss of liquid water driven by root pressure. Guttation water contains dissolved minerals; transpiration water is pure vapour.

Explain why transpiration rate is highest at midday and lowest at midnight. Link to two factors.Medium
Show Answer

Midday: (1) maximum light intensity → stomata fully open; (2) highest temperature → maximum evaporation rate → peak transpiration. Midnight: (1) no light → stomata close; (2) low temperature → minimal evaporation; transpiration is essentially zero or near zero.

Set C Hard – MYP Criterion B & D

Design a complete experiment to test how wind speed affects transpiration rate using a potometer. Include full methodology and safety considerations. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: wind speed (calm, slow fan, fast fan). DV: distance bubble moves per minute in potometer. Method: set up potometer with same species/size leafy shoot. Start with calm air → measure 5×1 min bubble movement → average. Position electric fan on slow, then fast setting, same distance. Allow 2 minutes for conditions to stabilise before each reading. Controlled: light, temperature, humidity, same shoot. Safety: keep fan away from water; fix potometer stand to prevent tipping. Prediction: bubble moves faster with increasing wind speed.

Critically evaluate the statement: "Transpiration is wasteful and plants should evolve to have no stomata." (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Incomplete reasoning. Transpiration is a necessary consequence of having stomata for gas exchange. Without stomata: no CO₂ uptake → no photosynthesis → plant death. Also: transpiration drives mineral transport and provides evaporative cooling. Plants have evolved compromises: desert plants minimize stomata/timing (CAM, C4 pathways). "No stomata" would eliminate photosynthesis itself. Evolution cannot produce a solution that removes a survival-critical process – any adaptation must balance water loss with CO₂ needs.

Analyse the role of transpiration in the global water cycle and explain what would happen to rainfall patterns if all terrestrial plants died. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Terrestrial plants contribute ~40% of land surface water vapour to the atmosphere via transpiration. This drives rainfall, especially in continental interiors (recycled rainfall). Without plants: (1) transpiration stops → reduced atmospheric water vapour over land; (2) soil becomes bare → more evaporation but less vegetation-driven rainfall recycling; (3) inland areas become deserts (no moisture recycling); (4) coastal areas may maintain some rainfall from ocean evaporation. Global rainfall patterns shift dramatically drier inland.

A forest scientist measures transpiration at ground-level plants as 0.5 mm/day and at canopy trees as 3.5 mm/day. Explain this difference using three factors. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

(1) Light intensity: canopy trees receive full sunlight → stomata fully open → maximum transpiration. Ground plants are shaded → stomata partially closed → lower rate. (2) Wind: canopy is exposed to wind → boundary layer constantly removed → steep concentration gradient maintained. Ground-level plants are sheltered. (3) Temperature: canopy temperatures higher (direct solar radiation) → faster evaporation from leaf cells → higher transpiration rate.

Evaluate the use of anti-transpirant sprays in agriculture. What are their benefits and potential drawbacks? (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Anti-transpirants coat leaf surfaces to reduce water vapour loss (waxy sprays or kaolin particles). Benefits: reduce water stress in drought; allow transplanting in hot conditions; used on cut flowers to extend vase life. Drawbacks: also reduce CO₂ entry → lower photosynthesis → potential yield reduction; some compounds may block stomata permanently if applied in excess; may alter leaf temperature. Beneficial in short-term stress situations but not as a permanent solution – net effect on plant growth must be weighed.

Plan an investigation to determine whether stomatal density on a leaf correlates with the plant's native habitat (desert vs. tropical rainforest species). (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

Collect leaves from: (1) cactus/desert succulent, (2) tropical rainforest tree (e.g., rubber plant). Use nail varnish peel method to make leaf surface replicas. Observe under microscope. Count stomata in 5 × 1mm² fields. Average per mm². Also measure stomatal aperture size. Predict: desert plants have fewer, smaller stomata to minimise water loss; rainforest plants have more, larger stomata to maximise CO₂ uptake (water is not limiting). Control: same leaf position (same-age leaves), same surface (lower epidermis).

In the context of climate change (rising temperatures and longer droughts), critically evaluate the adaptive capacity of different plant photosynthesis pathways (C3, C4, CAM) to survive future conditions. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

C3 (wheat, rice): most vulnerable – photorespiration increases with temperature; stomata must stay open for CO₂ → high water loss. C4 (maize, sugarcane): intermediate resilience – CO₂ concentrating mechanism reduces photorespiration even when stomata partially closed; better water use efficiency in heat. CAM (cactus, pineapple): most resilient to drought – stomata open only at night when temperatures are lower → minimal water loss; can survive extended drought. Climate change will likely favour C4 and CAM-adapted crops. Breeding C3 crops with C4 traits is an active research goal (e.g., C4 rice project).

Using the concept of water potential, explain how water moves from soil → root → stem → leaf → air during transpiration. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Water moves from high water potential to low water potential: Soil water (high Ψ, dilute) → Root cells (lower Ψ, concentrated solutes) by osmosis → Xylem vessels (lower Ψ due to tension) → Leaf mesophyll cells (even lower Ψ, concentrated due to water evaporation) → Air (very low Ψ, low humidity outside). Each step has a more negative water potential than the previous, creating a continuous gradient. Transpiration at the leaf end maintains the gradient by lowering leaf water potential, pulling the entire chain.

Set A Easy – Recall & Identify

Define pollination and state two methods by which it occurs.Easy
Show Answer

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther (male) to the stigma (female) of a flower. Methods: insect pollination (entomophily) and wind pollination (anemophily).

What is fertilisation in flowering plants?Easy
Show Answer

Fertilisation is the fusion of the male gamete (pollen tube delivers sperm nucleus) with the female gamete (egg cell in the ovule) to form a zygote, which develops into a seed.

List four methods of asexual reproduction in plants.Easy
Show Answer

Runners (stolons) – e.g., strawberry. Bulbs – e.g., onion. Tubers – e.g., potato. Cuttings (artificial). Also: grafting, layering, rhizomes.

What are the advantages of asexual reproduction for a plant?Easy
Show Answer

(1) Faster reproduction – no need to wait for pollination. (2) Produces genetically identical offspring (clones) with the same favourable traits as the parent. (3) No pollinator required.

Name the male and female reproductive parts of a flower.Easy
Show Answer

Male: stamen (anther produces pollen + filament). Female: pistil/carpel (stigma – receives pollen, style – pollen tube grows through, ovary – contains ovules/eggs).

What is a seed and what does it contain?Easy
Show Answer

A seed contains an embryo plant (formed from the fertilised egg), a food store (cotyledon/endosperm), and a seed coat (testa) for protection. It is the dispersal unit of flowering plants.

Name two differences between insect-pollinated and wind-pollinated flowers.Easy
Show Answer

Insect-pollinated: colourful petals, scent, nectar, sticky/spiky pollen, enclosed anthers. Wind-pollinated: small dull petals, no scent, light/smooth pollen (lots of it), exposed dangling anthers, feathery stigmas.

What is the difference between self-pollination and cross-pollination?Easy
Show Answer

Self-pollination: pollen transfers from anther to stigma of the same flower or same plant. Cross-pollination: pollen transfers from one plant to a different plant of the same species – increases genetic diversity.

Set B Medium – Understand & Apply

Compare sexual and asexual reproduction in plants using three criteria: genetic diversity, speed, and independence from other organisms.Medium
Show Answer

Genetic diversity: sexual = high (variation through fertilisation); asexual = none (clones). Speed: sexual = slow (pollination, seed development, germination); asexual = fast (runners, bulbs produce plants quickly). Independence: sexual = depends on pollinator or wind; asexual = entirely independent. Sexual is better for adaptation; asexual is better for rapid colonisation of favourable environments.

Explain how a fruit develops after fertilisation and its role in seed dispersal.Medium
Show Answer

After fertilisation: the ovary wall develops into the fruit (e.g., fleshy for animal dispersal, winged for wind). Ovules → seeds. Fruit functions: protects seed; assists dispersal. Fleshy fruits attract animals → seeds pass through gut undamaged and deposited elsewhere. Dry winged fruits (maple) spin in wind for distance. This moves seeds away from the parent, reducing competition.

Explain why potato farmers prefer to grow potatoes from tubers rather than from seeds.Medium
Show Answer

Tubers produce genetically identical plants (clones) with the same desirable characteristics (flavour, disease resistance, yield) as the parent. Seeds from sexual reproduction may produce variable offspring. Growing from tubers is also faster – new plants emerge within weeks, not months. Commercial farmers need uniformity and predictability.

Describe the process of germination, listing three conditions needed.Medium
Show Answer

Germination is the growth of a seed into a seedling. Conditions: (1) water – activates enzymes, hydrates cells; (2) warmth – enables enzyme activity for respiration and growth; (3) oxygen – needed for aerobic respiration to fuel growth. (Light not always needed at germination stage.) Radicle (root) emerges first, then shoot.

Explain why cross-pollination produces more genetically diverse offspring than self-pollination.Medium
Show Answer

Cross-pollination combines genetic material from two different parent plants. The offspring receive different allele combinations from each parent, creating genetic variation. Self-pollination uses the same plant's pollen – offspring inherit less variation. Genetic diversity helps populations adapt to changing environments and resist diseases.

How does the pollen tube grow and achieve fertilisation?Medium
Show Answer

Pollen grain lands on stigma → germinates → pollen tube grows down the style towards the ovule. Male gamete (sperm nucleus) travels through the tube to the ovule. The tube penetrates the ovule → sperm nucleus fuses with egg nucleus → fertilisation → zygote → embryo. This is an evolutionary adaptation: male gametes travel to female without swimming (unlike animals/non-vascular plants).

Explain two disadvantages of asexual reproduction for a plant species facing a new disease.Medium
Show Answer

(1) No genetic variation – all clones have the same genotype. If one individual is susceptible to a disease, all are susceptible → entire population could be wiped out (e.g., banana Cavendish blight). (2) Cannot adapt through natural selection over generations because there is no variation for selection to act on.

A rose grower uses grafting to produce new plants. Explain this process and its advantage over growing from seed.Medium
Show Answer

Grafting: a scion (shoot from desired rose variety) is joined to a rootstock (a different rose with strong roots). They fuse and grow as one plant. Advantage: scion clones the desired flower traits (colour, fragrance); rootstock provides disease resistance or vigour. Much faster than growing from seed; produces exact replicas. Used extensively in horticulture and fruit production.

Set C Hard – MYP Criterion B & D

Evaluate the ethical implications of cloning crop plants (asexual reproduction) vs. allowing genetic diversity through sexual reproduction. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Cloning: ensures uniform, high-yield crops; predictable quality for food industry; faster production. However: monocultures of clones are vulnerable to single disease/pest wiping out entire harvest (Irish potato famine, banana crisis). Reduced genetic diversity = reduced resilience. Sexual reproduction: slower, less predictable, but generates variation – natural buffer against disease. Ethical consideration: prioritising profit (cloning) vs. food security (diversity). Responsible agriculture should maintain seed banks and variety conservation alongside commercial cloning.

Design an experiment to investigate whether temperature affects the germination rate of bean seeds. Include IV, DV, hypothesis, method, and expected graph shape. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: temperature (5°C, 15°C, 25°C, 35°C, 45°C). DV: percentage of seeds germinated after 5 days (radicle > 2mm visible). Hypothesis: germination rate highest at ~25–30°C; very low below 10°C (enzymes inactive) and above 40°C (denaturation). Method: 10 seeds per Petri dish with moist cotton wool at each temperature in incubators. Count germinated seeds at day 5. Controlled: seed batch, water amount, darkness (light not needed). Graph: inverted U-shape (bell curve) peaking at optimum temperature.

Critically evaluate the decline of bee populations globally and its predicted impact on food security. Link to pollination. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

~75% of flowering crop plants rely on animal pollination (mainly bees). Bee decline (due to pesticides, habitat loss, parasites) reduces pollination efficiency → lower fruit and seed set → lower crop yields. Economic impact estimated at $235–577 billion/year globally. Crops affected: apples, almonds, coffee, cocoa, vegetables. Some crops (rice, wheat) are wind-pollinated and less affected. Mitigation: reducing pesticide use, planting wildflower corridors, protecting wild bee habitats. Food security is directly threatened – not just honey production.

Explain using genetics why offspring from cross-pollination are more likely to survive changing environments than those from self-pollination. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Cross-pollination combines alleles from two parents → offspring have more varied genotypes. Some variants may carry alleles conferring resistance to new pests, drought tolerance, or other advantageous traits. Natural selection can act on this variation, increasing population fitness over generations. Self-pollination maintains homozygosity → less variation → less raw material for selection → slower/no adaptation to new challenges. Inbreeding depression also reduces survival fitness of highly self-pollinated populations.

Analyse seed dormancy as an evolutionary adaptation. Why is it advantageous for seeds to delay germination? (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Dormancy allows seeds to survive unfavourable conditions (winter, drought, fire) without germinating. Advantages: (1) germination occurs only when conditions are right for seedling survival (spring warmth, adequate moisture); (2) seeds can be dispersed far before germinating; (3) some seeds require fire (smoke triggers germination) – ensures post-fire colonisation with reduced competition. Dormancy evolved because seeds that germinated immediately in autumn would die in winter – those that waited survived → natural selection favoured dormancy.

Compare the adaptations of wind-dispersed seeds (e.g., dandelion, maple) with animal-dispersed seeds (e.g., mango, berry). Explain the evolutionary logic. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Wind-dispersed: light (small mass), wings or parachute structures (dandelion pappus, maple samara), produced in huge numbers (compensate for low success rate), no investment in nutrition. Animal-dispersed: fleshy nutritious fruit (attracts animals), tough seed coat (survives gut), sweet/colourful (visual signal to animals), fewer seeds but higher success rate per seed. Evolutionary logic: both strategies maximise gene dispersal. Animal dispersal achieves longer distances with higher placement accuracy; wind dispersal is cheaper (no fruit production) but unpredictable. Both reduce parent-offspring competition.

Plan an investigation comparing the germination success rate of seeds with and without their fruit coat/seed coat. Include a hypothesis and variables. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: seed coat intact vs. scarified (scratched/removed). DV: germination percentage (radicle emergence) after 7 days. Hypothesis: scarified seeds may germinate faster (coat removed = water enters more easily); however some seeds require chemical signals from the coat breaking down. Method: 10 seeds per condition on moist Petri dishes at 25°C in dark. Count radicle emergence daily. Controlled: same seed batch, same water volume, same temperature, same light conditions. Note: for some species (hard seeds) scarification speeds germination; for others the coat contains germination inhibitors that need time/conditions to break down.

Evaluate the development of seedless fruits (e.g., seedless grapes, bananas) through plant breeding. What are the trade-offs from an ecological and agricultural perspective? (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Agricultural benefit: consumer preference → higher market value; no seeds to remove when eating. Ecological trade-off: seedless fruits rely entirely on asexual reproduction → genetic uniformity → vulnerability to disease (all plants share same susceptibility). No seeds = cannot reproduce sexually → wild populations cannot sustain themselves; completely dependent on human propagation (cuttings, grafting). Loss of genetic diversity = loss of adaptability. From an evolutionary standpoint, seedless fruits are a dead end for the species in the wild. Agricultural convenience comes at the cost of long-term resilience.

Set A Easy – Recall & Identify

What is the carbon cycle?Easy
Show Answer

The carbon cycle is the continuous movement of carbon atoms between the atmosphere (as CO₂), living organisms, soil, and oceans through processes like photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, and combustion.

Name two processes that remove CO₂ from the atmosphere.Easy
Show Answer

Photosynthesis (plants absorb CO₂) and dissolution in oceans (seawater absorbs CO₂, forming carbonic acid).

Name three processes that add CO₂ to the atmosphere.Easy
Show Answer

Respiration (by all living organisms), combustion (burning fossil fuels/wood), decomposition (saprophytes breaking down dead matter).

Why are plants called carbon stores (carbon sinks)?Easy
Show Answer

Plants absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis and store carbon in their tissues (as glucose, cellulose, starch). Forests store enormous quantities of carbon, keeping it out of the atmosphere for decades or centuries.

What are fossil fuels and how were they formed?Easy
Show Answer

Fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) are ancient carbon stores formed over millions of years from the compressed remains of dead plants and marine organisms. Burning them releases CO₂ that was stored millions of years ago.

What is the greenhouse effect and which gas causes most of it?Easy
Show Answer

The greenhouse effect is the warming of Earth's surface when greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapour) trap outgoing infrared radiation. CO₂ is the main anthropogenic greenhouse gas, though methane is more potent per molecule.

How does deforestation affect the carbon cycle?Easy
Show Answer

Deforestation releases stored carbon (burning or decomposing trees → CO₂) and removes photosynthesis capacity (less CO₂ absorbed in future) → double increase in atmospheric CO₂.

What is decomposition and how does it return carbon to the atmosphere?Easy
Show Answer

Decomposition is the breakdown of dead organic matter by decomposers (bacteria, fungi). They respire aerobically, releasing CO₂ back into the atmosphere as they break down carbon-containing compounds in dead tissues.

Set B Medium – Understand & Apply

Explain how burning fossil fuels disrupts the natural carbon cycle.Medium
Show Answer

Natural cycle: carbon cycles through photosynthesis/respiration/decomposition over decades. Fossil fuels contain carbon stored over millions of years. Burning them releases this "old" carbon as CO₂ rapidly (centuries vs. millions of years). This overwhelms the natural absorption capacity of plants and oceans → net increase in atmospheric CO₂ → enhanced greenhouse effect → climate change.

Explain how the ocean acts as a carbon sink and what risk this poses at high CO₂ levels.Medium
Show Answer

Oceans dissolve atmospheric CO₂: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate). This absorbs ~25–30% of human CO₂ emissions. Risk: as more CO₂ is absorbed, ocean pH falls (ocean acidification). Lower pH dissolves calcium carbonate shells of marine organisms (corals, molluscs, plankton) → ecosystem collapse. Additionally, warmer oceans absorb less CO₂, reducing sink capacity.

Describe the fast and slow carbon cycles with examples of each.Medium
Show Answer

Fast carbon cycle: photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition – cycles carbon within years to decades. Example: a tree grows (absorbs CO₂), dies, decomposes (releases CO₂) over 50–100 years. Slow carbon cycle: geological processes – carbon locked in fossil fuels, limestone (shells → rock), released by volcanic eruptions or combustion over millions of years. Human burning of fossil fuels has artificially sped up the slow cycle.

Calculate: If a forest absorbs 2 kg CO₂/m²/year and covers 10,000 m², how many kg CO₂ does it absorb in 5 years? What does this represent?Medium
Show Answer

Per year: 2 × 10,000 = 20,000 kg CO₂. Over 5 years: 20,000 × 5 = 100,000 kg = 100 tonnes CO₂. This represents the carbon sequestration value of that forest – equivalent to removing ~20 cars from the road per year. This illustrates why forest conservation is crucial for climate change mitigation.

Explain how peatlands store carbon and why draining them for agriculture is harmful.Medium
Show Answer

Peatlands are wetland ecosystems where dead plant material accumulates in waterlogged, low-oxygen conditions (preventing decomposition). Carbon builds up over thousands of years. Peatlands store ~twice as much carbon per unit area as forests. Draining peat for farming introduces oxygen → decomposers become active → peat decomposes rapidly → large CO₂ release → massive carbon pulse to atmosphere. Drained peatlands become carbon sources rather than sinks.

How do plants contribute to both sides of the carbon cycle (both removing and adding CO₂)?Medium
Show Answer

Remove CO₂: photosynthesis – plants absorb CO₂ to build glucose. Add CO₂: (1) respiration – all plant cells respire 24/7, releasing CO₂; (2) when plants die and decompose, their stored carbon is released as CO₂ by decomposers; (3) burning plants (combustion) releases stored carbon. Net carbon removal only occurs when photosynthesis > respiration (during daytime/growing season).

Explain why methane (CH₄) is a concern for the carbon cycle even though CO₂ is more abundant.Medium
Show Answer

Methane has a global warming potential 28× higher than CO₂ over 100 years (84× over 20 years). Sources: rice paddies, cattle digestion, landfills, melting permafrost. Although less abundant, its potency makes it a significant driver of near-term warming. Also: methane eventually oxidises to CO₂ in the atmosphere, contributing to the long-term CO₂ increase.

Describe carbon sequestration and give two natural and one artificial example.Medium
Show Answer

Carbon sequestration is the long-term capture and storage of CO₂ from the atmosphere. Natural: (1) forests absorbing CO₂ into wood (biomass); (2) marine phytoplankton dying and sinking to ocean floor, forming sediments. Artificial (technological): (1) carbon capture and storage (CCS) – capturing CO₂ from power plant chimneys and pumping it underground into geological formations; (2) biochar burial (charcoal from burned plant matter added to soil).

Set C Hard – MYP Criterion B & D

Evaluate whether reforestation alone is a sufficient strategy to combat rising atmospheric CO₂. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Reforestation helps but is insufficient alone. Benefits: each hectare of new forest sequesters 2–5 tonnes CO₂/year; restores biodiversity and watershed. Limitations: (1) human emissions are ~37 Gt CO₂/year; reforestation could only absorb ~2–3 Gt/year even if entire suitable land area reforested; (2) trees take decades to reach full carbon storage capacity; (3) warming itself threatens forests (wildfires, pest outbreaks). Conclusion: reforestation is necessary but must be combined with emissions reduction to be effective.

Design an investigation to measure the rate of CO₂ release during decomposition of leaves at different temperatures. Include full methodology. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: temperature (10°C, 20°C, 30°C, 40°C). DV: CO₂ concentration (ppm) in sealed chamber after 48 hours (measured with CO₂ probe/sensor). Method: equal mass of freshly fallen leaves in identical sealed containers with same moisture content. Place containers in water baths/incubators at each temperature. Measure CO₂ every 12 hours. Controlled: same leaf species and mass, same moisture level, same container size, same initial CO₂ concentration. Expected: CO₂ release increases with temperature up to ~35°C, then levels off as enzyme activity is affected at higher temperatures.

Critically analyse: "Planting trees is more ethical and effective than carbon capture technology for addressing climate change." (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Trees: ethical (enhances biodiversity, natural process, relatively cheap, co-benefits like watershed, habitat). Limitations: slow, requires land (competes with agriculture/housing), vulnerable to climate change itself, cannot capture enough to offset current emissions. CCS technology: faster carbon removal per unit area, works at emission source (power plants). Limitations: expensive (~$50–300/tonne CO₂), requires infrastructure, risk of CO₂ leaking from storage, diverts investment from renewable energy. "More ethical" is subjective – depends on whether you weigh biodiversity, cost, effectiveness, and justice for communities displaced for tree planting. Both approaches are needed.

Analyse how melting Arctic permafrost creates a positive feedback loop in climate change. Link to the carbon cycle. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Permafrost contains frozen organic matter (dead plants, animals). As Arctic temperatures rise (climate change), permafrost thaws → organic matter decomposes → releases CO₂ and methane → these greenhouse gases warm the atmosphere further → more permafrost melts → more GHGs released (feedback loop). This is a "tipping point": once enough permafrost melts, the process becomes self-sustaining regardless of human emissions reductions. Arctic permafrost stores ~1.5 trillion tonnes carbon – roughly double all CO₂ currently in the atmosphere.

Using the concept of the carbon cycle, critically evaluate the claim that burning biomass (wood pellets) is "carbon neutral." (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

The claim: trees absorb CO₂ as they grow → burning releases the same CO₂ → net zero. Reality: (1) timescale mismatch: burning releases CO₂ now; trees take 50–100 years to reabsorb it; climate damage happens in the interim; (2) forest ecosystem services lost (biodiversity, water cycle); (3) transport and processing of pellets add additional CO₂; (4) old-growth forest carbon stores are irreplaceable. The EU subsidises biomass as "renewable" – many scientists argue this is misleading. Carbon neutrality requires replacement trees to fully reabsorb emissions within relevant climate timescales, which often doesn't happen.

Explain how ocean acidification, caused by increased CO₂, threatens marine photosynthesis and the global carbon cycle. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

CO₂ dissolved in seawater forms carbonic acid → lowers pH. Effects on carbon cycle: (1) Coral reefs dissolve → loss of reef ecosystem that supports 25% of marine species; (2) calcifying phytoplankton (coccolithophores) shells weaken → these organisms perform ~50% of ocean photosynthesis AND sink to seafloor (biological pump), sequestering carbon. Their decline reduces both ocean CO₂ absorption and carbon burial → less carbon removed from cycle. Positive feedback: acidification reduces ocean carbon sink capacity → more CO₂ stays in atmosphere → more warming → more acidification.

Critically evaluate the concept of "net zero emissions by 2050" in terms of what changes to the global carbon cycle would be required to achieve it. (MYP-D)Hard
Show Answer

Net zero = emissions added = emissions removed from atmosphere. Required changes: (1) Reduce: eliminate fossil fuel burning (energy transition to solar/wind), reduce deforestation and peatland drainage, reduce livestock methane; (2) Increase removal: afforestation, peatland restoration, ocean protection, CCS deployment, direct air capture; (3) Carbon cycle naturally: maintain and expand ocean and land carbon sinks. Challenges: developing nations' right to economic development; existing fossil fuel infrastructure; political will; equity (who bears costs). Current trajectory would reach net zero ~2080–2100, not 2050 without massive policy change. Biology of natural sinks is a limiting factor that cannot be easily engineered.

Plan an investigation to compare carbon sequestration rates between a grassland and a forested ecosystem using soil carbon measurements. (MYP-B)Hard
Show Answer

IV: ecosystem type (grassland vs. forest – same climate/soil type). DV: soil organic carbon content (g carbon/kg soil) measured at 0–10 cm, 10–30 cm, 30–50 cm depths. Method: take 10 soil cores per ecosystem using a soil auger at random locations. Dry samples, weigh, combust at 550°C (loss on ignition = organic matter). Calculate %C. Compare means using statistics. Controlled: same soil parent material (geology), same climate zone, no recent disturbance. Predict: forest has higher SOC due to larger root systems and more litter input. Also measure above-ground biomass for total carbon comparison.
