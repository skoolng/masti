SET A Recall – Natural Selection Basics (Easy)

Easy
What term describes the process by which organisms with favourable traits survive and reproduce more successfully than those without?
Show Answer

Natural selection. Organisms with traits that make them better adapted to their environment are more likely to survive, reproduce, and pass on those traits.

Easy
Darwin observed that organisms produce far more offspring than can possibly survive. What is this called, and why is it important for natural selection?
Show Answer

Overproduction (or over-reproduction). It creates competition for limited resources, meaning only the best-adapted individuals survive — this is the "struggle for existence."

Easy
List the four key observations Darwin used to build his theory of natural selection.
Show Answer

1. Overproduction — more offspring are born than survive. 2. Variation — individuals differ in their traits. 3. Competition — individuals struggle for limited resources. 4. Differential survival & reproduction — those with favourable traits survive and pass on genes.

Easy
What is "variation" in the context of natural selection, and what ultimately causes it at the molecular level?
Show Answer

Variation means individuals in a population differ in their characteristics (e.g., colour, size, speed). It is caused by random mutations in DNA and by genetic recombination during sexual reproduction.

Easy
Why must a trait be heritable (inherited) for natural selection to produce evolutionary change?
Show Answer

If a beneficial trait cannot be passed to offspring, it disappears when the individual dies. Only heritable traits can accumulate in the population over generations, driving evolution.

Easy
Define "adaptation" as used in evolutionary biology.
Show Answer

An adaptation is a heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness (ability to survive and reproduce) in its particular environment.

Easy
What does "survival of the fittest" actually mean? Does "fittest" mean the strongest?
Show Answer

No — "fittest" means best adapted to the current environment, not necessarily the strongest. A small camouflaged insect is "fitter" than a large visible one if predation is the main pressure.

Easy
What is a population in evolutionary terms, and why is evolution described as a change in a population rather than an individual?
Show Answer

A population is a group of individuals of the same species in the same area. Individuals cannot evolve — it is the frequency of alleles in the gene pool of the population that changes over generations.

Easy
State the difference between Lamarckian inheritance and Darwinian natural selection, using giraffe neck length as an example.
Show Answer

Lamarck: giraffes stretched their necks during life and passed on longer necks (acquired characteristics — incorrect). Darwin: random variation meant some giraffes were born with longer necks; these survived better and reproduced more, so long-neck alleles increased in frequency.

Easy
Over time, natural selection can cause a new species to form from an ancestral one. What is this process called?
Show Answer

Speciation. When populations become reproductively isolated and adapt to different environments, they can diverge until they can no longer interbreed — forming distinct species.

SET B Apply – Natural Selection Scenarios (Medium)

Medium
A population of mice lives in a snowy environment. Most are brown, but a few are white due to a mutation. Explain, step by step using natural selection, how the population could become predominantly white over many generations.
Show Answer

1. Overproduction: mice produce many offspring. 2. Variation: random mutation produces some white mice. 3. Selection pressure: owls are the predator — brown mice on snow are more visible. 4. Differential survival: white mice are better camouflaged, survive and reproduce more. 5. Inheritance: white coat allele passed to offspring. 6. Over generations, white allele frequency rises — population becomes predominantly white.

Medium
Explain why two populations of the same species, isolated on different islands with different food sources, might eventually become two separate species.
Show Answer

Geographic isolation prevents gene flow. Each population faces different selection pressures (different food → different beak shapes, etc.). Over many generations, each population accumulates different adaptations. Eventually, the two populations become so genetically different they can no longer interbreed successfully — they are now separate species (allopatric speciation).

Medium
A scientist claims: "The cheetah evolved to run fast because it wanted to catch prey." Identify the error in this thinking and restate it correctly using natural selection.
Show Answer

Error: evolution has no intention or "want" — this is teleological/Lamarckian thinking. Correct: Random variation meant some ancestral cheetahs were born faster. These caught more prey, survived better, and reproduced more. Faster cheetahs passed speed-related alleles to offspring. Over many generations, the population became faster.

Medium
Describe how the concept of "differential reproductive success" links variation to evolutionary change.
Show Answer

Different variants in a population reproduce at different rates. Individuals with favourable variants leave more offspring and therefore contribute more alleles to the next generation. Over generations, the frequency of favourable alleles increases. This shift in allele frequency IS evolution.

Medium
What is artificial selection? Give one example and explain how it relates to natural selection.
Show Answer

Artificial selection is when humans selectively breed organisms for desired traits (e.g., breeding dogs for small size → Chihuahua). It works by the same mechanism as natural selection — individuals with desired traits reproduce more — but the "selector" is human choice rather than environmental pressures.

Medium
A student says, "Because variation in a population is random, natural selection must also be random." Do you agree? Explain.
Show Answer

Disagree. Variation (mutation) is random and non-directional. However, natural selection is non-random — the environment consistently favours certain traits over others. The combination of random variation + non-random selection produces directional, adaptive change.

Medium
In a population of beetles, green beetles are eaten by birds more often than brown beetles. After 20 generations, 95% of beetles are brown. Identify which of Darwin's four mechanisms is illustrated at each step.
Show Answer

Overproduction: beetles reproduce abundantly. Variation: beetles exist in both green and brown forms (due to genetic differences). Competition/Selection pressure: birds preferentially eat green beetles. Differential survival: brown beetles survive and reproduce more. Inheritance: brown colouration alleles passed on. Result: brown frequency rises to 95%.

Medium
Why does natural selection act on phenotype rather than directly on genotype?
Show Answer

The environment interacts with an organism's observable traits (phenotype) — the physical form, behaviour, biochemistry. The underlying genotype is only "visible" to selection through its phenotypic expression. Recessive alleles hidden in heterozygotes are not directly selected against.

Medium
The peppered moth exists in a light form and a dark (melanic) form. Before industrialisation, light moths were common; after, dark moths dominated. Explain this using natural selection, then explain what happened when clean air acts were introduced.
Show Answer

Before: light moths camouflaged on lichen-covered bark; dark moths were eaten. After industrialisation: soot blackened trees, dark moths were camouflaged, light moths eaten → dark form increased. After clean air acts: lichen returned to pale bark → light moths again favoured → light form increased again. Shows natural selection reversing with changing selection pressures.

Medium
Why is sexual reproduction important for the pace of evolution compared to asexual reproduction?
Show Answer

Sexual reproduction produces new combinations of alleles through crossing over and independent assortment during meiosis. This generates far more variation than mutation alone (which is the only source in asexual reproduction). More variation means more "raw material" for natural selection, potentially accelerating adaptation.

SET C Advanced – MYP/DP Bridge (Hard)

Hard
Distinguish between stabilising, directional, and disruptive natural selection. Give one biological example of each and predict how each affects the bell-curve distribution of a trait.
Show Answer

Stabilising: selects against extremes, favouring intermediate phenotype → bell curve narrows (e.g., human birth weight — very large or very small babies have lower survival). Directional: favours one extreme → bell curve shifts (e.g., bacteria becoming resistant to antibiotic). Disruptive: favours both extremes, selects against intermediate → bell curve becomes bimodal (e.g., beak size in finches when medium-sized seeds disappear, favouring both large and small beaks).

Hard
Explain the concept of fitness in quantitative terms. How would a biologist calculate the relative fitness of two genotypes in a population?
Show Answer

Fitness (w) = relative reproductive success. Calculated as: w = average number of offspring that survive to reproduce for genotype X ÷ maximum average offspring among all genotypes. The genotype with highest reproductive output has w=1.0; others are expressed relative to this. Selection coefficient s = 1 − w. A genotype with s=0 is neutral; s=1 is lethal.

Hard
What is genetic drift, and how does it differ from natural selection as a mechanism of evolution? In what type of population is drift most significant?
Show Answer

Genetic drift = random changes in allele frequency due to chance sampling events (not due to fitness advantage). Unlike natural selection (which is non-random and adaptive), drift is random and can fix harmful alleles or remove beneficial ones. Most significant in small populations (e.g., founder effect, bottleneck effect) where chance events have proportionally large effects on allele frequencies.

Hard
Explain why natural selection cannot produce "perfect" organisms, referencing constraints such as historical baggage, trade-offs, and developmental constraints.
Show Answer

Historical baggage: evolution modifies existing structures rather than designing from scratch (e.g., recurrent laryngeal nerve takes a long detour in mammals). Trade-offs: improving one trait may reduce another (e.g., wider pelvis for larger-brained babies vs. efficient bipedal walking). Developmental constraints: some mutations are lethal or produce non-viable phenotypes. Time: selection operates generation by generation; rapid environmental change may outpace adaptation.

Hard
The Hardy-Weinberg principle states that allele frequencies remain constant unless acted upon by certain forces. List five conditions required for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium and explain what each violation implies for evolution.
Show Answer

1. No mutation (violation → new alleles introduced). 2. No gene flow/migration (violation → alleles enter or leave population). 3. Random mating (violation → sexual selection or assortative mating). 4. No genetic drift/very large population (violation → allele frequencies change by chance). 5. No natural selection (violation → differential survival changes allele frequency). Any violation means the population is evolving.

Hard
Evaluate whether natural selection is the only mechanism of evolution. Construct an argument referencing at least three other mechanisms.
Show Answer

Natural selection is the primary adaptive mechanism but not the only one. 1. Mutation: ultimate source of new alleles — without mutation, no variation for selection to act on. 2. Genetic drift: random fixation or loss of alleles, especially in small populations — can produce non-adaptive evolution. 3. Gene flow: movement of alleles between populations can introduce or spread alleles irrespective of fitness. 4. Sexual selection: mate choice drives traits that may reduce survival fitness but increase reproductive success (e.g., peacock tail). 5. Non-random mating: assortative mating alters genotype frequencies. Modern synthesis integrates all these mechanisms.

Hard
Distinguish between microevolution and macroevolution. How does accumulation of microevolutionary changes lead to macroevolutionary outcomes over geological time?
Show Answer

Microevolution: changes in allele frequency within a species over short time scales (e.g., beak size shift in finches over decades). Macroevolution: large-scale changes producing new species, genera, or higher taxa over millions of years (e.g., evolution of tetrapods from fish). Macroevolution occurs through the accumulation of many microevolutionary changes, combined with speciation events. Over geological time, geographic isolation, repeated speciation, and mass extinctions produce the diversity of life.

Hard
Analyse the statement: "If natural selection is true, we should never observe vestigial structures in organisms." Do you agree? Justify with examples.
Show Answer

Disagree. Vestigial structures are expected under evolution — they are remnants of structures that were functional in ancestors but are now reduced or non-functional because selection pressure for them was removed (not reversed). Example: human appendix (reduced cecum from herbivorous ancestors), whale pelvis (remnant hindlimb bones), flightless bird wings. Selection only removes structures if they impose a significant fitness cost; otherwise they persist neutrally through many generations.

Hard
Design a thought experiment to distinguish Lamarckian inheritance from Darwinian natural selection using bacteria grown in the presence of an antibiotic. How did the Luria-Delbrück experiment settle this debate?
Show Answer

Lamarckian prediction: all bacteria exposed to antibiotic would acquire resistance at a similar, low rate → similar, small number of resistant colonies on each replicate plate. Darwinian prediction: random mutations occurred before antibiotic exposure → some cultures (lucky early mutations) would have many resistant colonies; others, none (high variance between replicates). Luria-Delbrück (1943) fluctuation test: variance between replicate plates was far higher than Lamarck predicted, supporting pre-existing random mutations. This proved mutations are random and pre-adaptive, not directed by the environment.

Hard
Critically evaluate: "Natural selection always leads to increased complexity in organisms." Use specific examples to support or refute this claim.
Show Answer

Refute: natural selection produces adaptation to environment, not necessarily complexity. Counter-examples: 1. Parasites often evolve simplicity — tapeworms lost entire organ systems (eyes, digestive tract) because these were unnecessary in their host environment. 2. Cave fish lost eyes through relaxed selection and genetic drift. 3. Endosymbiotic bacteria became mitochondria by gene loss. Complexity can evolve (e.g., eye evolution from photoreceptor to camera eye) but only when it confers fitness advantage. The trend is toward fitness, not complexity.

SET A Recall – Evidence Basics (Easy)

Easy
What is the fossil record, and how does it provide evidence for evolution?
Show Answer

The fossil record is the collection of preserved remains or traces of past organisms. It provides evidence for evolution by showing a sequence of increasingly complex organisms over geological time and documenting transitional forms linking major groups.

Easy
Define homologous structures and give one example showing evolutionary relationships between species.
Show Answer

Homologous structures are body parts with the same underlying anatomy (same bones, same developmental origin) in different species, even if used for different functions. Example: the pentadactyl limb — human arm, whale flipper, bat wing, and horse leg all share the same arrangement of humerus, radius/ulna, and digit bones, indicating a common ancestor.

Easy
The DNA of humans and chimpanzees is approximately 98.7% identical. What does this tell us about their evolutionary relationship?
Show Answer

It indicates humans and chimpanzees share a very recent common ancestor (about 6-7 million years ago). The high similarity in DNA sequence means both lineages have changed relatively little since diverging from the shared ancestor.

Easy
What are analogous structures? How do they differ from homologous structures?
Show Answer

Analogous structures perform the same function in different species but have different underlying anatomy and different evolutionary origins (convergent evolution). Example: bird wing vs. insect wing — both used for flight but built completely differently. Homologous structures share anatomy/origin; analogous structures share function only.

Easy
What is a transitional fossil? Give one example and explain why it is scientifically significant.
Show Answer

A transitional fossil shows intermediate characteristics between an ancestral group and a descendant group. Example: Tiktaalik — a fish with fin-like proto-limbs, showing the transition from fish to tetrapods. Significant because it fills gaps in the fossil record, providing direct evidence of evolutionary change.

Easy
What is biogeography, and how does the distribution of species on islands support evolution?
Show Answer

Biogeography is the study of the geographical distribution of organisms. Island species closely resemble those of the nearest mainland (not those of similar climate elsewhere), suggesting they descended from mainland colonisers who then adapted to island conditions — supporting evolution by natural selection.

Easy
What are vestigial structures? Name two examples in humans.
Show Answer

Vestigial structures are reduced or non-functional remnants of structures that were functional in ancestors. Human examples: 1. The coccyx (tailbone) — remnant of an ancestral tail. 2. The arrector pili muscles (cause goosebumps) — remnant of fur-raising response that was useful in furry ancestors.

Easy
How does comparative embryology provide evidence for evolution?
Show Answer

Early embryos of very different vertebrates (fish, humans, chickens) look strikingly similar — all have pharyngeal arches, tails, and similar body plans at early stages. This suggests all vertebrates share a distant common ancestor whose developmental programme is conserved.

Easy
Name two types of molecular evidence (beyond DNA sequence) that support evolution and common ancestry.
Show Answer

1. Protein sequence similarity — cytochrome c protein is almost identical across many species, with more differences in less closely related species. 2. Universal genetic code — all known life uses the same codons to specify the same amino acids, pointing to a single common ancestor.

Easy
Scientists compare the DNA sequences of the cytochrome c gene across 20 species. Explain how this data can be used to construct an evolutionary tree.
Show Answer

More similar cytochrome c sequences indicate more recent common ancestry (fewer mutations have accumulated). By counting sequence differences between pairs of species, scientists can group the most similar together, building a phylogenetic tree that represents the evolutionary relationships and branching points of all 20 species.

SET B Apply – Interpreting Evidence (Medium)

Medium
A whale has small, non-functional pelvic bones embedded in its body with no connection to the spine. Explain what type of evidence this is and what it reveals about whale evolution.
Show Answer

These are vestigial structures — remnants of the hindlimbs of the terrestrial ancestor of whales (Pakicetus). They provide evidence that whales evolved from four-limbed land mammals. The structures no longer have a function but were not completely eliminated because they impose minimal fitness cost.

Medium
Two species of cactus and two species of euphorbia look almost identical — both have thick water-storing stems and spines, but cacti are found only in the Americas and euphorbia in Africa. Are their similarities homologous or analogous? What does this tell us?
Show Answer

Analogous (convergent evolution). Their similar body plans evolved independently in response to similar arid environments, not from a recent common ancestor. This demonstrates that similar environments can produce similar adaptations through natural selection operating on different genetic starting points.

Medium
The fossil record is incomplete. A critic says: "If evolution is true, every species should have a corresponding fossil." Evaluate this argument.
Show Answer

The argument is flawed. Fossilisation is extremely rare — it requires specific conditions (rapid burial, mineral-rich water, absence of decomposers, suitable rock type). Soft-bodied organisms almost never fossilise. Despite being incomplete, the fossil record shows consistent patterns: simpler organisms appear in older layers, complex ones in newer, with transitional forms at expected points. The gaps do not disprove evolution; they reflect the difficulty of preservation.

Medium
Explain how the concept of "molecular clocks" uses DNA mutation rates to estimate when two species diverged.
Show Answer

DNA mutations accumulate at a roughly constant rate over time. By comparing the number of differences in a specific DNA sequence between two species and knowing the average mutation rate, scientists can estimate how long ago they shared a common ancestor. Example: if two species differ by 10 mutations and the rate is 1 mutation per million years, they diverged ~10 million years ago.

Medium
How do endogenous retroviruses (ERVs) in the human genome provide particularly compelling evidence for shared ancestry with other primates?
Show Answer

ERVs are remnants of ancient viral infections that integrated into host DNA. Humans, chimpanzees, and gorillas share many of the same ERV insertions at identical locations in their genomes. The probability of two species independently having identical viral insertions at the same genomic location is astronomically small — the only logical explanation is inheritance from a common ancestor in whom the original insertion occurred.

Medium
Darwin noticed that finches on the Galápagos Islands had very different beak shapes for different food sources, yet all resembled the finches on the nearby South American mainland. Explain this observation using evolutionary principles.
Show Answer

A single finch species from South America colonised the Galápagos. In the absence of competitors, descendants spread into different ecological niches. Natural selection favoured different beak shapes for different food sources (seeds, insects, nectar). Separated on different islands, populations diverged into 13+ species through adaptive radiation — they resemble mainland finches because they recently descended from them.

Medium
A phylogenetic tree shows Species A and B branching from a node 10 million years ago, while Species C branches from the same line 50 million years ago. Which pair — A&B or A&C — would be expected to have more similar DNA sequences? Explain.
Show Answer

A and B would have more similar DNA sequences. They share a more recent common ancestor (10 million years ago vs. 50 million years ago). Less time has elapsed for mutations to accumulate independently in each lineage, so their sequences diverge less.

Medium
Explain how the existence of Tiktaalik (a fossil fish with proto-limbs) was actually predicted before it was found, and what this prediction demonstrates about the scientific power of evolutionary theory.
Show Answer

Palaeontologists reasoned that if fish evolved into tetrapods ~375 million years ago, transitional fossils should exist in rocks of that age. They identified suitable 375 million-year-old Devonian rocks in Arctic Canada and specifically searched there — finding Tiktaalik in 2004. Predictive success demonstrates evolutionary theory has real explanatory power; it is not just descriptive but can generate testable, falsifiable hypotheses.

Medium
Compare the pentadactyl limb of a human, whale, bat, and mole. What does the shared bone structure suggest, despite the very different functions?
Show Answer

All four share the same basic arrangement: one proximal bone (humerus), two distal bones (radius/ulna), wrist bones (carpals), and five digits (metacarpals + phalanges). Functions differ: human — manipulation; whale — swimming; bat — flight; mole — digging. The shared underlying structure (homology) indicates descent from a common pentadactyl ancestor; evolution modified the same blueprint for different purposes.

Medium
An archaeologist discovers a layered rock formation with simple single-celled organisms in the deepest layer and complex mammals in the top layer. How does the principle of superposition support evolution?
Show Answer

The principle of superposition states that in undisturbed rock layers (strata), older layers are deeper and younger layers are on top. Finding simple organisms in old layers and complex ones in new layers matches the evolutionary prediction that life started simple and diversified over time. This stratigraphic pattern is consistent across thousands of sites worldwide.

SET C Advanced – MYP/DP Bridge (Hard)

Hard
Critically evaluate the claim that molecular evidence for evolution is stronger than morphological evidence. Consider strengths and limitations of each.
Show Answer

Molecular strengths: quantitative (countable differences), resistant to convergent evolution (same protein sequence unlikely to arise independently), applicable even to species with no fossil record. Morphological strengths: directly observable in fossils, shows functional adaptation, provides temporal information. Limitations of molecular: assumes constant mutation rate (clocks can vary), requires preserved DNA (ancient specimens). Limitations of morphological: convergent evolution can mislead, soft tissue not preserved, subjective interpretation. Neither alone is conclusive; consilience of both strengthens conclusions.

Hard
Explain how the concept of "deep homology" — finding the same genetic toolkit (e.g., Hox genes) in organisms as distantly related as flies and humans — provides evidence for universal common ancestry.
Show Answer

Hox genes control body axis development. Flies and mice share nearly identical Hox gene sequences; a mouse Hox gene can even function in a fly. If these species had independent origins, identical complex gene sequences would be vanishingly improbable. Instead, the shared Hox toolkit points to a very ancient common ancestor in whom this gene family evolved. Deep homology shows that the fundamental developmental genetic programme is conserved across the animal kingdom.

Hard
How does cladistics differ from traditional (Linnaean) taxonomy, and why is cladistics considered more evolutionarily accurate?
Show Answer

Traditional taxonomy groups organisms by overall similarity (which can be misleading when convergent evolution occurs). Cladistics groups organisms strictly by shared derived characters (synapomorphies) inherited from a common ancestor, producing clades (monophyletic groups). This is more accurate because it reflects true evolutionary relationships. Example: traditional taxonomy placed crocodiles with other "reptiles," but cladistics reveals crocodiles are more closely related to birds than to lizards.

Hard
The human vitamin C synthesis gene (GULO) is broken (a pseudogene) with exactly the same mutations in the same positions in humans, chimpanzees, and gorillas. Analyse why this is considered very strong evidence for common ancestry.
Show Answer

A functional gene becomes a pseudogene through random mutations. For exactly the same inactivating mutations to occur independently in three separate lineages is extraordinarily improbable. The identical pseudogene sequence (shared "errors") in all three species is best explained by a single mutational event in a common ancestor that was inherited by all three. This is called a "shared derived character" and is particularly compelling because there is no functional reason for three species to independently acquire identical non-functional sequences.

Hard
Design an experiment to test whether morphological similarity or molecular similarity better predicts true evolutionary relationships, using a group of organisms where the two methods give different results.
Show Answer

Use convergently evolved organisms (e.g., dolphins, ichthyosaurs, sharks) that are morphologically similar but molecularly distant. 1. Measure morphological similarity using multivariate analysis of body plan characters. 2. Sequence conserved genes (cytochrome b, 16S rRNA) and calculate molecular distance. 3. Build two separate trees — one morphological, one molecular. 4. Test which better predicts the established fossil-calibrated phylogeny. Prediction: molecular tree will better match true phylogeny because convergent evolution creates morphological similarity without genetic relatedness. Resolution: molecular evidence is more reliable for distantly related converging lineages.

Hard
Evaluate the strengths and limitations of using stratigraphy and radioactive decay for dating fossils, and explain why multiple dating methods are used together.
Show Answer

Stratigraphy: strengths — relative dating, widely applicable, supports superposition. Limitations — gives only relative age, tectonic events can disturb layers. Radioactive decay (e.g., potassium-argon, uranium-lead): strengths — absolute dates, consistent decay rates, applicable over millions/billions of years. Limitations — requires suitable igneous rocks near fossil site, contamination possible, large error margins for very old samples. Carbon-14: accurate for up to ~50,000 years only. Using multiple methods cross-validates results — agreement between independent methods increases confidence. Different isotopes work at different time scales, providing complementary coverage.

Hard
Explain how genome sequencing of the Neanderthal genome changed our understanding of human evolution, and what it tells us about the nature of evolutionary evidence.
Show Answer

Svante Pääbo's team sequenced Neanderthal DNA from 40,000-year-old bones (2010). Findings: 1-4% of non-African modern human DNA is Neanderthal in origin → interbreeding occurred. This revised the simple "out of Africa replacement" model to one of partial admixture. Methodologically significant: ancient DNA analysis opened an entirely new form of evolutionary evidence beyond morphology and modern genomics. It also showed evolution is not always clean species replacement — hybridisation between lineages occurred. This demonstrates how new technologies continuously refine evolutionary understanding.

Hard
How does the distribution of marsupials provide evidence for both continental drift and evolution? Construct an argument connecting plate tectonics to biogeography.
Show Answer

Marsupials are concentrated in Australia (and opossums in the Americas). This distribution is explained by: 1. ~180 million years ago, all landmasses formed Pangaea — ancestors of marsupials spread across connected continents. 2. Gondwana separated — Australia drifted away ~45 million years ago with marsupials already present, before placental mammals diversified in the northern hemisphere. 3. Isolated from competition with placentals, marsupials underwent adaptive radiation in Australia. 4. Biogeographic evidence: fossil marsupials in Antarctica (once between South America and Australia) support the dispersal route. This shows evolution and geology are interlinked sciences.

Hard
A student argues: "Because scientists occasionally revise evolutionary trees when new data are found, evolution is not a reliable theory." Construct a counter-argument addressing the nature of scientific theory and evidence.
Show Answer

This argument confuses theory revision with theory weakness. In science, revising a model when new evidence arrives is a strength, not a weakness. The core claim — that all species share common ancestors and have changed over time — is supported by convergent evidence from genetics, palaeontology, comparative anatomy, embryology, and biogeography. Revisions to specific branching patterns (which species is more related to which) do not undermine the central theory. This is like saying physics is unreliable because we revised Newtonian mechanics with relativity. Scientific theories that accommodate and incorporate new evidence are more reliable, not less.

Hard
Compare and contrast the evidence that would support convergent evolution versus common ancestry when two species share a trait. How would a scientist distinguish between the two explanations?
Show Answer

Common ancestry: same underlying anatomical structure (homology) at the cellular/genetic level; gene sequences for the trait are similar; trait appears at the same phylogenetic position. Convergent evolution: superficially similar but structurally different (e.g., dolphin fin built from modified fingers vs. shark fin from skin folds); genes involved differ; species are phylogenetically distant. Distinguishing method: 1. Build independent phylogeny using many unrelated traits/genes. 2. Map the trait on this phylogeny — if it appears only once (common ancestor node), it is homologous; if independently in multiple lineages, it is convergent. 3. Sequence the genes responsible — divergent sequences suggest independent origin.

SET A Recall – Antibiotic Resistance Basics (Easy)

Easy
What are antibiotics, and what types of organisms do they affect?
Show Answer

Antibiotics are chemicals that kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria. They do NOT affect viruses. They work by targeting structures unique to bacteria, such as cell walls (penicillin) or bacterial ribosomes (streptomycin).

Easy
What is antibiotic resistance, and why is it a major public health concern?
Show Answer

Antibiotic resistance is the ability of bacteria to survive and reproduce in the presence of antibiotics that would normally kill them. It is a major concern because it makes bacterial infections harder to treat, leading to longer illness, more deaths, and the potential for untreatable "superbugs."

Easy
Explain how a random mutation in a bacterium can lead to antibiotic resistance.
Show Answer

A random mutation in the bacterium's DNA may alter the shape of the antibiotic's target site, produce an enzyme that destroys the antibiotic, or create a pump that expels the antibiotic. The mutation occurs before any antibiotic is present — the antibiotic does not cause the mutation.

Easy
What does MRSA stand for, and why is it considered a "superbug"?
Show Answer

MRSA = Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus. It is resistant to methicillin and most other beta-lactam antibiotics, making it very difficult to treat. It is particularly dangerous in hospitals where patients are vulnerable and the bacterium spreads easily.

Easy
What does MDR-TB stand for, and what does it mean for the treatment of tuberculosis?
Show Answer

MDR-TB = Multi-Drug Resistant Tuberculosis. It means the TB bacterium (Mycobacterium tuberculosis) is resistant to at least the two most powerful first-line antibiotics (isoniazid and rifampicin). Treatment requires longer courses of more toxic, expensive second-line drugs with lower success rates.

Easy
Why does antibiotic resistance evolve faster when patients do not complete their full course of antibiotics?
Show Answer

If treatment stops early, the most susceptible bacteria are killed first but some bacteria with partial resistance may survive. These partially resistant bacteria reproduce in the absence of competition from susceptible bacteria, and continued low-dose antibiotic exposure selects further for fully resistant variants.

Easy
What is horizontal gene transfer (conjugation), and how does it accelerate the spread of antibiotic resistance?
Show Answer

Horizontal gene transfer (conjugation) is the direct transfer of DNA (often resistance genes on plasmids) between bacteria — even between different species — without reproduction. This allows resistance genes to spread rapidly through bacterial populations far faster than through vertical transmission (parent to offspring).

Easy
Give two reasons why overuse of antibiotics in livestock farming contributes to antibiotic resistance in humans.
Show Answer

1. Heavy antibiotic use in livestock selects for resistant bacteria in animals; these resistant bacteria can transfer to humans through food contamination or direct contact. 2. Resistance genes from livestock bacteria can transfer horizontally to human pathogens, spreading resistance into human bacterial populations.

Easy

How does antibiotic resistance illustrate that evolution is an ongoing process happening right now, not just something that occurred in the distant past?

Antibiotic resistance is observable, measurable evolution in real time. Resistant strains emerge within years or decades of new antibiotic introduction. Medium
A patient takes penicillin for a bacterial infection. On day 5 (out of 10), they feel better and stop taking the antibiotic. Explain, using natural selection, why this is dangerous and what may happen next.
Show Answer

On day 5, most susceptible bacteria are killed but some bacteria with slight resistance remain. Stopping treatment removes the selection pressure but also removes competition — the partially resistant survivors reproduce rapidly. Subsequent treatments may fail as the surviving bacteria are more resistant. Completing the full course ensures all bacteria, including the most resistant, are eliminated.

Medium
Using the four steps of natural selection, explain precisely how a population of bacteria evolves resistance to an antibiotic over multiple generations.
Show Answer

1. Variation: random mutations produce occasional bacteria with resistance (enzyme that degrades antibiotic, altered target site). 2. Selection: antibiotic kills susceptible bacteria; resistant mutants survive. 3. Differential reproduction: resistant bacteria reproduce freely in antibiotic-rich environment; susceptible bacteria cannot reproduce (killed). 4. Inheritance: resistant offspring inherit the resistance gene. Over many generations, resistant allele frequency increases until the entire population is resistant.

Medium
Why do bacteria evolve resistance much faster than, say, elephants would evolve immunity to a disease?
Show Answer

Bacteria have: 1. Very short generation times (E. coli divides every 20 min → thousands of generations per week, vs. elephant generation of ~20 years). 2. Enormous population sizes (millions to billions) → much greater chance of a rare resistance mutation occurring. 3. Horizontal gene transfer — can acquire resistance genes from entirely different species without reproduction. These factors mean natural selection can act much faster in bacteria.

Medium
Explain why antibiotic resistance mutations do NOT arise as a direct response to the presence of antibiotics. What does cause the mutations?
Show Answer

Mutations arise randomly during DNA replication errors, spontaneously, or from mutagens — not in response to antibiotic presence. The antibiotic acts as a selective filter after the fact: it eliminates bacteria that happen to lack resistance and allows pre-existing resistant mutants to survive and reproduce. The Luria-Delbrück experiment confirmed this — resistant bacteria exist in populations before any antibiotic exposure.

Medium
Describe the "evolution in action" MEGA plate experiment by Kishony's lab at Harvard. What did it demonstrate about the speed and mechanism of antibiotic resistance evolution?
Show Answer

The MEGA plate (2016): a large agar plate with bands of increasing antibiotic concentration (no antibiotic → low → medium → high → very high). Bacteria were seeded at the edges. Over ~2 weeks, bacteria colonised increasing concentrations: each time they reached a new band boundary, resistant mutants arose and moved forward. Scientists could observe stepwise acquisition of resistance in real time, demonstrating that multiple successive mutations can rapidly evolve resistance to extremely high antibiotic doses through sequential selection.

Medium
Compare the costs and benefits of antibiotic resistance. Why don't all bacteria in a non-antibiotic environment become resistant over time?
Show Answer

Resistance mechanisms often have metabolic costs: producing resistance enzymes or altered cell walls requires energy. In antibiotic-free environments, resistant bacteria may grow slower than susceptible ones. Without the selection pressure of antibiotics, susceptible bacteria outcompete resistant ones, and resistance allele frequency decreases. This is why antibiotic stewardship (reducing overuse) is important — it can allow resistant strains to decline in frequency.

Show Answer

The fitness cost of resistance — if resistant bacteria grow as fast as susceptible ones, resistance spreads rapidly; if resistance is costly, it spreads more slowly.

Show Answer

For a bacterium to survive combination therapy, it would need simultaneous resistance mutations to all drugs used. If the probability of resistance to Drug A is 1 in 10⁸ and to Drug B is 1 in 10⁷, the probability of simultaneous resistance to both is approximately 1 in 10¹⁵ — far below the number of bacteria in an infection. This is why TB, HIV, and cancer are treated with drug combinations.

It occurs between different species, meaning resistance in one type of bacterium can rapidly jump to important pathogens. 2. It does not require reproduction — can happen many times per generation. 3. Hard
Explain the "Red Queen Hypothesis" in the context of host-pathogen evolution, specifically antibiotic resistance.
Show Answer

The Red Queen Hypothesis (Van Valen, 1973) states that organisms must continuously evolve just to maintain their relative fitness against co-evolving antagonists — "running to stay in the same place" (like the Red Queen in Alice in Wonderland). In host-pathogen terms: as we develop new antibiotics, bacteria evolve resistance; we develop new antibiotics; bacteria evolve again. Neither side "wins" — there is a continuous evolutionary arms race. This explains why antibiotic development must be ongoing, and why we cannot rest on existing drugs. The hypothesis predicts that if we stop developing new antibiotics, bacteria will eventually have resistance to all existing ones while we have no new weapons.

Minimal DNA repair in some contexts — higher mutation rate. 5. Haploid genome — beneficial mutations expressed immediately (no masking by dominant alleles).

Show Answer

Strengths: antibiotic resistance provides direct, observable, measurable, repeatable evidence of natural selection in action. Counter: the same mechanism (mutation + selection + time + isolation) that produces resistance produces macroevolution; the difference is only time scale. SET A Recall – Enzyme Basics (Easy)

Easy
What is an enzyme? Give its chemical nature and its main function in living organisms.
Show Answer

An enzyme is a biological catalyst made of protein. Its main function is to speed up chemical reactions in living organisms without being consumed in the process. Without enzymes, most biochemical reactions would proceed too slowly to sustain life.

Easy
What is the active site of an enzyme, and what is its relationship to the substrate?
Show Answer

The active site is a specific region on the enzyme with a precisely shaped 3D pocket formed by certain amino acids. It is complementary in shape (and sometimes charge) to the substrate — the molecule the enzyme acts upon. The substrate fits into the active site like a key fits into a lock.

Easy
Explain the lock-and-key model of enzyme action in simple terms.
Show Answer

In the lock-and-key model, the enzyme is the "lock" and the substrate is the "key." The substrate has a specific shape that exactly fits the enzyme's active site. Only a substrate with the correct complementary shape can bind to that active site, explaining why enzymes are highly specific — each enzyme acts on only one type of substrate.

Easy
What is the enzyme-substrate complex?
Show Answer

The enzyme-substrate complex is the temporary structure formed when the substrate binds to the enzyme's active site. It is an intermediate stage before the reaction occurs. Once the reaction is complete, the product(s) are released and the enzyme returns to its original shape, ready to act again.

Easy
What does it mean to say an enzyme is "specific"? Give one example.
Show Answer

Specificity means each enzyme catalyses only one particular reaction or acts on only one type of substrate. Example: salivary amylase only breaks down starch (amylose), not proteins or fats — because only starch fits its active site.

Easy
Explain what is meant by the "activation energy" of a reaction, and how enzymes reduce it.
Show Answer

Activation energy is the minimum energy needed to start a chemical reaction. Enzymes lower activation energy by holding substrates in the correct orientation, stressing bonds, or creating a microenvironment that makes the reaction more likely to proceed — so reactions can occur at normal body temperatures without requiring extreme heat.

Easy
Is an enzyme consumed in the reaction it catalyses? Explain why or why not.
Show Answer

No — the enzyme is not consumed. After the reaction, the product(s) are released and the enzyme returns to its original shape with an unchanged active site. This means one enzyme molecule can catalyse the same reaction thousands to millions of times per second.

Easy
Name the two key components needed to identify an enzyme by its systematic name, and give an example.
Show Answer

Systematic enzyme names describe: 1. The substrate acted upon. 2. The type of reaction catalysed. The suffix "-ase" is added. Example: lactase (acts on lactose, breaking it down by hydrolysis). Amylase (acts on amylose/starch). Lipase (acts on lipids).

Easy
What is the induced fit model, and how does it improve on the lock-and-key model?
Show Answer

The induced fit model proposes that the active site is not rigidly pre-shaped like a lock; instead, it flexibly adjusts its shape as the substrate binds, creating a tighter, more precise fit. This is more accurate than lock-and-key because it explains how related (but not identical) substrates can still bind, and why the enzyme is more catalytically effective after conformational change.

Easy
State three examples of metabolic processes in the human body that depend on enzymes.
Show Answer

1. Digestion — amylase breaks down starch; pepsin breaks down proteins; lipase breaks down fats. 2. DNA replication — DNA polymerase builds new DNA strands. 3. Cellular respiration — hexokinase phosphorylates glucose in glycolysis.

SET B Apply – Enzyme Specificity & Mechanism (Medium)

Medium
Explain why amylase cannot digest proteins, using the lock-and-key model in your answer.
Show Answer

Amylase has an active site that is complementary in shape to starch (amylose) molecules. Proteins have a completely different molecular shape from starch. Protein molecules therefore cannot fit into amylase's active site — no enzyme-substrate complex forms, so no digestion occurs. Each enzyme's active site is uniquely shaped for its specific substrate.

Medium
A competitive inhibitor has a shape similar to the enzyme's substrate. Explain how it reduces enzyme activity using the lock-and-key model.
Show Answer

The competitive inhibitor's shape is similar enough to the substrate to fit into the active site. When it occupies the active site, the real substrate cannot bind — it competes for the same site. No enzyme-substrate complex forms with the actual substrate, so reaction rate decreases. The effect can be reduced by increasing substrate concentration (outcompeting the inhibitor).

Medium
A non-competitive inhibitor binds to an allosteric site (not the active site) and changes the shape of the active site. Explain why this reduces enzyme activity, and why increasing substrate concentration does NOT overcome it.
Show Answer

Non-competitive inhibitors bind elsewhere on the enzyme, causing a conformational change that alters the active site shape. Even if the substrate is present, it no longer fits the distorted active site — the enzyme-substrate complex cannot form properly. Since the active site is blocked by shape change rather than by occupancy, adding more substrate cannot displace the inhibitor or restore activity.

Medium
The drug ibuprofen inhibits the enzyme COX-2 (cyclooxygenase-2), reducing inflammation. Explain this pharmacological mechanism in terms of enzyme biochemistry.
Show Answer

COX-2 catalyses a step in the synthesis of prostaglandins (chemicals that cause inflammation and pain). Ibuprofen is a competitive inhibitor that fits into COX-2's active site, blocking arachidonic acid (the natural substrate) from binding. With COX-2 inhibited, prostaglandin synthesis decreases, reducing the inflammatory response. This demonstrates how drugs are designed to exploit enzyme specificity.

Medium
Sketch and label an energy diagram showing a reaction with and without an enzyme. Identify the activation energy in each case.
Show Answer

The diagram shows: X-axis = reaction progress; Y-axis = energy level. Reactants start at a higher energy than products (exergonic reaction). Without enzyme: large energy hill (activation energy Ea) between reactants and products. With enzyme: same starting and ending energy levels BUT the energy hill is much lower (lower Ea). The enzyme lowers activation energy — it does not change the energy of reactants or products, only the pathway. Products are released at the same energy level regardless of enzyme presence.

Medium
Explain how enzyme immobilisation is used in industrial processes. Give one specific example.
Show Answer

Enzyme immobilisation attaches enzymes to an inert support (beads, membranes, or gel) so they cannot wash away. The substrate solution flows past the immobilised enzymes, products are collected, and the enzyme is reused — reducing cost. Example: lactase is immobilised in dairy processing to convert lactose to glucose + galactose, producing lactose-free milk. The enzyme remains in the reactor; only the lactose-free milk product exits.

Medium
Why are enzymes described as "globular proteins"? How does the globular shape relate to their function?
Show Answer

Globular proteins fold into a roughly spherical (globular) 3D shape, with hydrophilic (water-attracting) amino acids on the outside and hydrophobic ones tucked inside. This folding creates the precise 3D active site cavity. The active site's shape, charge distribution, and chemical properties are entirely determined by how the protein folds — making the protein's 3D structure inseparable from its catalytic function.

Medium
Explain the concept of "turnover number" (kcat) and what it tells us about enzyme efficiency.
Show Answer

The turnover number (kcat) is the number of substrate molecules converted to product per enzyme active site per second when the enzyme is saturated with substrate. Example: carbonic anhydrase has a kcat of ~10⁶ s⁻¹ — it processes 1 million CO₂ molecules per second per active site. Higher kcat = more efficient enzyme. It demonstrates how a single enzyme molecule can catalyse an enormous number of reactions quickly.

Medium
Why are enzymes that catalyse the same reaction in different organisms often similar in structure at the active site, even if the rest of the enzyme differs?
Show Answer

The active site must be precisely shaped to bind the substrate and catalyse the reaction — any significant change in active site amino acids would destroy function. Strong purifying selection maintains active site residues across species (evolutionary conservation). The rest of the protein (scaffold) can vary more freely, as long as it correctly holds the active site in position. This is why the active sites of amylase in humans and bacteria are remarkably similar despite 500+ million years of evolution.

Medium
Compare and contrast catabolic and anabolic enzyme-catalysed reactions, giving one biological example of each.
Show Answer

Catabolic reactions: enzymes break down large molecules into smaller ones, releasing energy. Example: amylase breaks starch into maltose; lipase breaks fat into glycerol + fatty acids. Anabolic reactions: enzymes build large molecules from smaller ones, requiring energy. Example: DNA polymerase builds DNA from nucleotides; peptidyl transferase (ribosome) links amino acids into proteins. Both use the same enzyme-substrate mechanism but differ in direction: catabolism = degradation, anabolism = synthesis.

Hard
Compare the lock-and-key and induced fit models of enzyme action. Which is better supported by experimental evidence, and what types of experiments support the induced fit model?
Show Answer

Lock-and-key (Fischer, 1894): rigid active site, substrate fits exactly. Simple, explains specificity. Does not explain why similar substrates bind, or why active site is more reactive when substrate is bound. Induced fit (Koshland, 1958): active site flexibly adjusts shape upon substrate binding, creating tighter fit and more catalytically competent complex. Evidence for induced fit: X-ray crystallography shows conformational changes in enzymes (e.g., hexokinase closes around glucose, excluding water), nuclear magnetic resonance shows dynamic movement, isotope labelling shows different reaction rates for closely related substrates. Induced fit is better supported — modern structural biology confirms that essentially all enzymes show conformational changes upon substrate binding.

Hard
Explain Michaelis-Menten kinetics, defining Vmax and Km, and describe what a low Km value tells you about an enzyme's affinity for its substrate.
Show Answer

Michaelis-Menten kinetics describes how enzyme reaction rate (V) depends on substrate concentration [S]: V = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S]). Vmax = maximum rate achieved when all active sites are saturated with substrate. Km = substrate concentration at which V = ½Vmax (the Michaelis constant). Km reflects enzyme-substrate affinity: low Km means the enzyme reaches half-maximal rate at a low substrate concentration → high affinity for substrate (few substrate molecules needed to fill active sites). High Km means low affinity — many substrate molecules needed. Km is a measure of how tightly the enzyme binds its substrate.

Hard
Describe how enzyme activity is regulated allosterically, explaining the concepts of allosteric activators and inhibitors and how feedback inhibition maintains metabolic homeostasis.
Show Answer

Allosteric regulation: small molecules (allosteric effectors) bind to sites other than the active site, causing conformational changes that alter enzyme activity. Allosteric activators: bind and change active site shape to increase substrate affinity or catalytic rate. Allosteric inhibitors: bind and distort active site, reducing activity. Feedback inhibition: the end product of a metabolic pathway inhibits an enzyme early in that pathway. Example: high ATP levels inhibit phosphofructokinase (early glycolysis enzyme) — when energy is abundant, glucose breakdown slows automatically. This maintains metabolic homeostasis without requiring separate signalling systems — the pathway self-regulates.

Hard
Explain the role of cofactors and coenzymes in enzyme function. Why is it that some vitamins are essential partly because they serve as coenzymes?
Show Answer

Many enzymes require non-protein helpers for full activity. Cofactors: inorganic ions (Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺, Fe²⁺) that stabilise enzyme structure or participate in catalysis. Coenzymes: organic molecules (often derived from vitamins) that carry chemical groups between reactions. Example: NAD⁺ (from vitamin B3/niacin) accepts hydrogen in cellular respiration; coenzyme A (from pantothenic acid/vitamin B5) carries acetyl groups. Without the coenzyme, the enzyme (apoenzyme) is inactive. This is why vitamin B3 deficiency causes pellagra — without NAD⁺, cellular respiration enzymes cannot function, causing widespread metabolic failure.

Hard
Analyse how the structure of an enzyme's active site at the molecular level (specific amino acid residues, charges, polarity) enables catalysis to occur at body temperature without the extreme conditions (high heat, strong acid) required for non-enzymatic equivalents.
Show Answer

Active site residues are precisely positioned to: 1. Bind substrate via hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, and van der Waals forces — holds substrate in optimal orientation. 2. Apply mechanical strain to substrate bonds (distortion catalysis), lowering activation energy. 3. Provide general acid-base catalysis — histidine and glutamate residues act as proton donors/acceptors without requiring extreme pH. 4. Provide nucleophilic catalysis — serine OH group attacks substrate covalently (serine proteases). 5. Create a microenvironment with tailored polarity/charge that stabilises the transition state. Compare: hydrolysis of peptide bonds in a test tube requires boiling in concentrated HCl for hours; peptidases achieve the same in milliseconds at pH 7, 37°C.

Hard
Explain how Sanger's development of enzyme-linked techniques and Khorana's work on synthetic nucleotides illustrate that understanding enzyme function is central to molecular biology and biotechnology.
Show Answer

Sanger used proteases with known specificity (trypsin, chymotrypsin) to cleave insulin at specific sites, generating predictable fragments that could be sequenced — his method for determining the first protein sequence (insulin, 1951) depended entirely on enzyme specificity. This demonstrated enzymes as analytical tools. Khorana synthesised trinucleotides using DNA polymerase to crack the genetic code. Modern PCR uses Taq polymerase (thermostable DNA polymerase) from hot-spring bacteria — the entire biotechnology industry (cloning, gene therapy, forensics, diagnostics) is built on exploiting enzyme specificity and function. Understanding active site structure allows rational drug design and engineering of novel enzymes.

Hard
What is a zymogen (proenzyme)? Explain why some enzymes are synthesised in inactive form, using trypsinogen and chymotrypsinogen as examples.
Show Answer

A zymogen is an inactive enzyme precursor that requires specific chemical modification (usually cleavage of an inhibitory peptide) to become active. Trypsinogen is secreted by the pancreas; in the small intestine, enterokinase cleaves a peptide from trypsinogen, creating active trypsin. Trypsin then activates chymotrypsinogen → chymotrypsin. Biological rationale: these are powerful proteases — if synthesised active in the pancreas, they would digest the pancreas itself (causing pancreatitis). Storing them as inactive zymogens and activating only at the site of use (intestine) provides spatial control. This principle of spatial/temporal control of enzyme activity is fundamental to cell biology.

Hard
Enzyme immobilisation is used in biosensors. Explain the principle behind a glucose biosensor (such as used in diabetic blood glucose monitors), identifying the enzyme used, the reaction it catalyses, and how this produces an electrical signal.
Show Answer

Glucose oxidase (GOx) is immobilised on an electrode. Reaction: glucose + O₂ → gluconolactone + H₂O₂ (catalysed by GOx). Two detection methods: 1. Amperometric: H₂O₂ is electrochemically oxidised at the electrode (H₂O₂ → 2H⁺ + O₂ + 2e⁻), generating a current proportional to H₂O₂ concentration, which is proportional to glucose concentration. 2. Mediator-based: electron mediators (ferrocene derivatives) transfer electrons from the enzyme directly to the electrode without O₂. The electrical current output is calibrated to glucose concentration. The enzyme's specificity for glucose (not other sugars) ensures the sensor is selective. This is a direct application of the lock-and-key principle in medical diagnostics.

Hard
Compare proteases found in the stomach (pepsin, optimal pH 2) versus the small intestine (trypsin, optimal pH 8). Explain at the molecular level why each enzyme is adapted to its environment, and what happens to each enzyme when placed in the other's pH environment.
Show Answer

Pepsin (pH 2): evolved to function in the acidic stomach. At pH 2, acidic/basic amino acid residues (Asp, Glu at active site) are in the correct protonation state for catalysis. At pH 8, these residues become deprotonated, changing their charge and disrupting the active site geometry → enzyme denatured/inactive. Trypsin (pH 8): pancreatic enzyme adapted to alkaline intestine. Its active site (catalytic triad: Ser, His, Asp) requires His to be neutral (not protonated) at pH 8 to function. At pH 2, His is protonated, disrupting the catalytic mechanism → inactive. This pH adaptation arises from natural selection shaping the amino acid composition of each enzyme for its specific cellular microenvironment.

Hard
Explain how directed evolution of enzymes (as developed by Nobel Laureate Frances Arnold) allows scientists to engineer novel enzymes for industrial and medical applications that do not exist in nature.
Show Answer

Directed evolution mimics natural selection in the laboratory. Process: 1. Take a gene encoding an enzyme with desired starting activity. 2. Introduce random mutations throughout the gene (error-prone PCR or DNA shuffling). 3. Express all mutant enzymes. 4. Screen or select for variants with improved properties (higher activity at industrial temperatures, new substrate specificity, greater stability in organic solvents). 5. "Winners" are taken as the new starting point; the process is iterated. Over many rounds, enzymes accumulate beneficial mutations far beyond what evolution would achieve naturally in reasonable time. Applications: enzymes stable at 80°C for biofuel production, enzymes that make new C-Si bonds (not found in nature), improved lipases for detergents. Arnold won the 2018 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for developing this approach.

What is the optimum temperature for most human enzymes, and why is this significant?
Show Answer

The optimum temperature for most human enzymes is approximately 37°C (body temperature). This is significant because human metabolism evolved to function optimally at normal body temperature — enzymes work fastest and most efficiently at this point.

Easy
What happens to an enzyme's activity if the temperature rises significantly above the optimum? What term describes this change?
Show Answer

Activity rapidly decreases. At high temperatures, the enzyme denatures — excessive heat causes the bonds holding the 3D protein structure (particularly hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions) to break, permanently altering the shape of the active site. The active site no longer fits the substrate, so activity is lost. Denaturation is usually irreversible.

Easy
What happens to enzyme activity at temperatures well below the optimum (e.g., near 0°C)?
Show Answer

Activity decreases but the enzyme is NOT denatured. Low temperatures reduce the kinetic energy of molecules, so enzyme and substrate collide less frequently. Fewer enzyme-substrate complexes form per second, so rate drops. When temperature rises back to optimum, activity is restored — this is why freezing food preserves it (slows microbial enzyme activity).

Easy
State the optimum pH for pepsin (stomach protease) and for salivary amylase, and explain why these differ.
Show Answer

Pepsin: optimum pH ≈ 2 (highly acidic), matching the stomach environment. Salivary amylase: optimum pH ≈ 7 (neutral), matching the mouth. Each enzyme evolved to function optimally in the specific body compartment where it acts. The pH affects the ionisation state of amino acid residues in the active site — at non-optimal pH, these residues are incorrectly protonated, disrupting active site function.

Easy
What is pH, and why does extreme pH (very high or very low) denature enzymes?
Show Answer

pH is a measure of hydrogen ion concentration — pH 7 is neutral, below 7 is acidic, above 7 is alkaline. Extreme pH values alter the charges on amino acid residues (by adding or removing H⁺ ions), disrupting the ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds that maintain the enzyme's 3D structure and active site shape. This causes denaturation and loss of activity.

Easy
Describe what happens to enzyme reaction rate as substrate concentration increases from zero to saturation. What limits the rate at very high substrate concentrations?
Show Answer

As substrate concentration increases from zero, rate increases (more substrate molecules available to fill active sites). Rate increases rapidly at first, then more slowly, then levels off at a maximum (Vmax — saturation). At Vmax, all active sites are occupied — adding more substrate makes no difference. The number of enzyme molecules (active sites) is now the limiting factor.

Easy
How does increasing enzyme concentration affect the rate of reaction, assuming substrate is not limiting?
Show Answer

Increasing enzyme concentration increases the number of available active sites. With more active sites and sufficient substrate, more enzyme-substrate complexes can form per second, so reaction rate increases proportionally. The relationship is roughly linear until another factor becomes limiting.

Easy
State two factors (besides temperature and pH) that affect enzyme activity.
Show Answer

1. Substrate concentration — more substrate increases rate up to saturation. 2. Enzyme concentration — more enzyme increases rate if substrate is not limiting. Also acceptable: presence of inhibitors, presence of cofactors/coenzymes.

Easy
Why do thermophilic bacteria (which live in hot springs at 70-80°C) have enzymes that function at temperatures that would denature human enzymes?
Show Answer

Thermophilic bacteria evolved in high-temperature environments. Their enzymes are thermostable — they have more covalent disulfide bonds, more hydrophobic core interactions, and different amino acid compositions that maintain their 3D structure at high temperatures. Natural selection favoured enzymes that remain functional at the temperatures where those bacteria live.

Easy
What practical application do thermostable enzymes (e.g., from Thermus aquaticus) have in biotechnology?
Show Answer

Taq polymerase (from T. aquaticus) is used in the Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR). PCR requires repeated heating to 95°C to separate DNA strands — normal DNA polymerase would denature at this temperature. Taq polymerase remains active through the high-temperature cycles, making PCR possible. PCR is used in forensics, medical diagnosis, and genetic research.

SET B Apply – Interpreting Enzyme Data (Medium)

Medium
A student measures the rate of hydrogen peroxide decomposition by catalase at 10°C, 20°C, 37°C, 50°C, and 70°C. Predict and explain the pattern of results they would observe, including a description of the graph shape.
Show Answer

10°C: low rate (low kinetic energy, few collisions). 20°C: moderate rate (more kinetic energy). 37°C: maximum rate (optimum temperature — maximum collision frequency, enzyme not denatured). 50°C: rate begins to decrease (some denaturation — active sites distorting). 70°C: rate near zero (severe denaturation — most active sites destroyed). Graph shape: bell curve (inverted U-shape) with peak at 37°C. Rate rises steeply to 37°C, then falls steeply above this. At 70°C approaches the x-axis.

Medium
In an investigation, a student tests amylase activity at pH 3, 5, 7, 9, and 11. Results: starch was only digested at pH 7. Explain these results, and then explain what would happen if pepsin were tested under the same conditions.
Show Answer

Amylase has an optimum of ~pH 7. At pH 3, 5, 9, and 11, the H⁺/OH⁻ concentration alters amino acid charges in the active site, distorting its shape — substrate cannot bind. At pH 7, active site is in optimal shape → starch digested. Pepsin: optimum pH ≈ 2. In the same pH range, pepsin would digest protein substrate at pH 3 (near its optimum), and be inactive at pH 7, 9, 11 (too alkaline — denaturation of its active site). This contrast shows enzyme adaptation to specific physiological environments.

Medium
An experiment measures enzyme rate against substrate concentration. Rate doubles as [S] increases from 0.1 to 0.2 mM, but barely changes between 10 and 20 mM. Explain what is happening at the molecular level in each region of the curve.
Show Answer

At low [S] (0.1-0.2 mM): many active sites are empty. Doubling substrate doubles the chance of productive collisions → rate approximately doubles. Linear region. At high [S] (10-20 mM): virtually all active sites are occupied (enzyme is saturated). Adding more substrate cannot increase rate because there are no empty active sites to bind to. The enzyme is the limiting factor. Rate ≈ Vmax. This is the plateau (zero-order kinetics region).

Medium
Explain how a fever (high body temperature, e.g., 40-41°C) can be both beneficial and dangerous in terms of enzyme activity.
Show Answer

Beneficial: slightly elevated temperature increases enzyme reaction rates in immune cells (white blood cells) — immune responses work faster. Also inhibits many pathogen enzymes that are optimally adapted to 37°C. Dangerous: if fever exceeds ~41-42°C, human enzyme denaturation accelerates. Critical enzymes involved in neural function, respiration, and protein synthesis begin to lose activity. Severe fever can cause seizures (neural enzyme denaturation), brain damage, and death. The therapeutic value of moderate fever is why the body evolved this response; its danger explains why extreme fevers are medical emergencies.

Medium
Design a controlled experiment to investigate how pH affects the activity of amylase. State the independent variable, dependent variable, and three control variables.
Show Answer

Aim: to investigate the effect of pH on amylase activity. Independent variable: pH (use buffer solutions at pH 3, 5, 7, 9, 11). Dependent variable: rate of starch digestion (measured by time for iodine solution to change from blue-black to yellow/clear, OR by measuring reducing sugars produced using Benedict's test at set time intervals). Control variables: temperature (constant 37°C water bath), starch concentration (same for all), amylase concentration (same volume and concentration), reaction time before testing. Each pH tested at least 3 times (repeat trials).

Medium
When the enzyme lactase is absent or reduced, people develop lactose intolerance. Explain how this connects to enzyme concentration affecting digestion, and suggest a dietary or medical solution.
Show Answer

Without sufficient lactase, the substrate lactose cannot be hydrolysed into glucose + galactose. Undigested lactose passes to the large intestine, where bacteria ferment it, producing gas (bloating), osmotic changes (diarrhoea), and pain. This illustrates that reducing enzyme concentration below a threshold causes the reaction rate to fall too low for physiological needs. Solutions: 1. Dietary: avoid lactose-containing foods; consume lactose-free dairy (lactose pre-digested by added lactase). 2. Medical: take lactase enzyme tablets with meals to supplement reduced endogenous lactase.

Medium
Explain the Q₁₀ concept in enzyme kinetics. If a reaction has a Q₁₀ of 2, what does this mean, and why does this relationship break down above the optimum temperature?
Show Answer

Q₁₀ = ratio of reaction rate at temperature T+10°C to rate at temperature T°C. Q₁₀ = 2 means the reaction rate doubles for every 10°C increase in temperature. Below the optimum, Q₁₀ ≈ 2 because increasing temperature increases molecular kinetic energy and collision frequency. Above the optimum, Q₁₀ no longer applies — increasing temperature now denatures the enzyme, destroying active sites faster than kinetic energy gain increases rate. Rate decreases sharply, so Q₁₀ < 1 (rate actually decreases with temperature increase).

Medium
A student claims: "Boiling an enzyme solution and then cooling it back to 37°C will restore full enzyme activity." Evaluate this claim.
Show Answer

The claim is incorrect. Denaturation above ~60-70°C (for most human enzymes) is irreversible. While some denaturation involves non-covalent bond disruption (which could theoretically reverse), boiling disrupts so many bonds simultaneously — including disulfide bonds in some cases — that the protein cannot re-fold correctly into its native structure. Cooling restores kinetic conditions but the enzyme remains denatured. Some cold-adapted enzymes can re-nature after mild warming, but boiling-then-cooling restores activity in almost no cases. The correct term for reversible loss of activity with no structural change is inhibition, not denaturation.

Medium
Explain how the food industry uses low temperature (refrigeration and freezing) to preserve food, linking this to enzyme activity and microbial respiration.
Show Answer

Refrigeration (4°C): significantly below the optimum for both food enzymes (catalysing ripening/browning) and microbial enzymes (respiration, reproduction). Reaction rates slow ~2-3-fold per 10°C drop → food ripens and decomposes much more slowly. Microbial growth slows dramatically. Freezing (−18°C): enzyme activity approaches zero (molecules lack kinetic energy for productive collisions). Water becomes ice, removing the aqueous environment enzymes need. Microbial enzymes and reproduction essentially cease. Neither process denatures enzymes — thawing restores activity, which is why thawed food must be cooked promptly or re-frozen.

Medium
An industrial enzyme is needed to function in a washing machine at 60°C and pH 11 (alkaline detergent). What properties must this enzyme have, and where might scientists look for organisms that naturally produce such enzymes?
Show Answer

Required properties: thermostable (active at 60°C, resisting denaturation), alkaliphilic (optimum pH ≈ 10-11), must be a protease (to digest protein stains) or lipase (fat stains), stable in the presence of detergent chemicals. Source organisms: alkaliphilic bacteria from alkaline environments (soda lakes, e.g., Lake Natron, Kenya; Bacillus alcalophilus); thermophilic organisms from hot springs. Savinase (an alkaline protease from Bacillus licheniformis) is used in biological detergents. Scientists screen extremophile bacteria and use directed evolution to further improve properties.

SET C Advanced – MYP/DP Bridge (Hard)

Hard
Sketch and interpret a Lineweaver-Burk (double reciprocal) plot, explaining how it allows you to determine Vmax and Km more accurately than a standard Michaelis-Menten curve. How do competitive and non-competitive inhibitors affect this plot?
Show Answer

Lineweaver-Burk plot: 1/V (y-axis) vs 1/[S] (x-axis). Linear graph: y-intercept = 1/Vmax; x-intercept = −1/Km; slope = Km/Vmax. More accurate because it linearises the hyperbolic Michaelis-Menten curve, making extrapolation to Vmax and Km easier. Competitive inhibitor: increases apparent Km (inhibitor competes with substrate, so more substrate needed to achieve half-Vmax), but Vmax unchanged. Plot: same y-intercept (Vmax same), different x-intercept (lower −1/Km = higher Km), steeper slope. Non-competitive inhibitor: reduces Vmax (fewer functional active sites), Km unchanged (affinity unaffected). Plot: x-intercept unchanged, but y-intercept higher (lower Vmax = higher 1/Vmax).

Hard
Explain the molecular basis of cooperative binding in allosteric enzymes such as haemoglobin (using it as a model, though not an enzyme), and how this sigmoidal kinetics differs from simple Michaelis-Menten behaviour.
Show Answer

Cooperative binding: multi-subunit proteins where binding of one substrate molecule causes conformational changes that increase affinity for subsequent substrate binding. Haemoglobin model: binding of first O₂ to one haem group changes protein conformation, increasing affinity of remaining haem groups for O₂. Sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve vs. hyperbolic Michaelis-Menten curve: at low [S], rate is lower than Michaelis-Menten predicts; at intermediate [S], rate increases more steeply (cooperative effect); at high [S], plateaus at Vmax. This gives a "switch-like" response — enzyme is relatively inactive below a threshold [S] and rapidly activated above it. Allows precise metabolic regulation: enzyme only activates when substrate accumulates above a threshold.

Hard
Heavy metal ions (e.g., Hg²⁺, Pb²⁺) are enzyme inhibitors. Explain the molecular mechanism by which heavy metals denature enzymes, linking this to their toxicity in living systems.
Show Answer

Heavy metal mechanism: Hg²⁺ and Pb²⁺ have high affinity for thiol groups (−SH) of cysteine residues in proteins. They displace H⁺ from −SH groups, forming mercury-thiolate (Hg-SR) or lead-thiolate bonds. This: 1. Disrupts disulfide bonds critical for 3D protein structure. 2. May directly block active site cysteine residues (enzyme inhibition). 3. Causes widespread protein misfolding. Biological toxicity: heavy metals inhibit numerous critical enzymes simultaneously. Mercury inhibits sulfhydryl enzymes in nerve tissue → neurological damage (Minamata disease). Lead inhibits δ-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase → impaired haem synthesis → anaemia, and inhibits NMDA receptors → developmental neurotoxicity. The broad enzyme inhibition explains why heavy metal poisoning is so destructive to multiple organ systems.

Hard
Explain how protein folding is connected to enzyme function, and why misfolded proteins (as seen in prion diseases and Alzheimer's) cause disease despite the amino acid sequence being normal.
Show Answer

Enzyme function depends entirely on correct 3D folding — the active site's shape, charge distribution, and flexibility are determined by how the polypeptide chain folds. The amino acid sequence specifies the fold; molecular chaperones assist in correct folding. In prion diseases: the normal prion protein (PrPᶜ) is correctly folded; the disease form (PrPˢᶜ) has identical amino acid sequence but different fold — it accumulates in neural tissue, causing neural death. In Alzheimer's: amyloid-β peptides misfold into β-sheet aggregates (plaques) that are neurotoxic and also inhibit normal enzymes. Key insight: even with correct amino acid sequence, wrong folding = wrong function. This is why protein quality control (chaperones, proteasome) is essential for cellular health.

Hard
Some organisms (psychrophiles) live in Arctic/Antarctic environments at −2 to 10°C. Explain the molecular adaptations of their enzymes that allow function at these temperatures, contrasting with mesophilic (normal temperature) enzymes.
Show Answer

Psychrophilic enzyme adaptations: 1. More flexible tertiary structure at low temperatures — achieved by: fewer disulfide bonds, fewer salt bridges, more glycine residues (no side chain = more flexibility), longer surface loops, less compact hydrophobic core. 2. Higher proportion of α-helices relative to β-sheets in some regions. 3. Larger active site cavity — allows substrate to bind without the conformational rigidity needed for mesophilic enzymes. 4. More charged (ionised) surface residues prevent aggregation in cold water. Trade-off: psychrophilic enzymes denature at moderate temperatures (30-40°C) because their flexible structure cannot maintain integrity when there is sufficient thermal energy to disrupt the weaker interactions. Mesophilic enzymes would be too rigid at low temperatures to undergo the conformational changes needed for catalysis.

Hard
Analyse how the concept of "enzyme saturation" relates to metabolic bottlenecks in cells, using rate-limiting steps in a metabolic pathway as an example.
Show Answer

When all enzyme active sites are occupied (saturation), the enzyme operates at Vmax — adding more substrate has no effect. If one enzyme in a metabolic pathway reaches saturation while others are unsaturated, it becomes the rate-limiting step — the entire pathway cannot proceed faster than this single step, regardless of the abundance of all other components. Example: In glycolysis, phosphofructokinase (PFK) is tightly regulated and often the rate-limiting step. Even if all other glycolytic enzymes are unsaturated, the pathway flux equals PFK's maximum rate. Cells exploit this: by regulating only the rate-limiting enzyme (allosteric inhibition/activation of PFK), the entire pathway can be controlled efficiently. Pharmaceutical targets often aim at rate-limiting enzymes (e.g., statins target HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting step in cholesterol synthesis).

Hard
Compare the effects of a reversible competitive inhibitor vs. an irreversible inhibitor on enzyme kinetics and on clinical pharmacology, giving one example drug from each category.
Show Answer

Reversible competitive inhibitor: binds non-covalently to active site; can be displaced by increasing substrate concentration; effect is temporary and concentration-dependent. Clinical advantage: predictable dose-response, can be titrated. Example: statins (e.g., atorvastatin) compete reversibly with HMG-CoA for HMG-CoA reductase active site — reducing cholesterol synthesis. Irreversible inhibitor: forms covalent bond with enzyme (often active site residue); cannot be displaced; effect lasts until new enzyme is synthesised. Example: aspirin irreversibly acetylates Ser530 in COX-1's active site → permanent inhibition of thromboxane synthesis for platelet's lifespan (~10 days) — hence aspirin's long-lasting anti-platelet effect. Clinical advantage of irreversible: sustained effect without continuous dosing. Disadvantage: toxicity risk if over-dosed; cannot be reversed (requires enzyme resynthesis).

Hard
Explain how cells use post-translational modifications (phosphorylation, proteolytic cleavage) to regulate enzyme activity, and why this is more responsive than altering gene expression.
Show Answer

Post-translational modification (PTM) operates on pre-existing enzyme molecules — it can activate or inactivate an enzyme within milliseconds. Phosphorylation: protein kinases add a phosphate group to Ser, Thr, or Tyr residues → changes enzyme shape (can activate or inhibit). Phosphatases remove the phosphate to reverse the effect. Example: glycogen phosphorylase activated by phosphorylation during adrenaline response. Proteolytic cleavage: zymogens activated by cleavage (trypsinogen → trypsin) — irreversible but rapid. Advantage over gene expression changes: gene expression requires minutes to hours (transcription → translation → folding); PTM acts in milliseconds and can be rapidly reversed. This is why acute responses (stress, injury, blood sugar changes) use PTM to regulate enzymes, while longer-term adaptations use gene expression changes.

Hard
The enzyme urease (which breaks down urea) has a Km of 10 mM and a Vmax of 1000 μmol/min. Calculate the rate of reaction when [S] = 10 mM and when [S] = 100 mM using the Michaelis-Menten equation, then interpret what these numbers show about the enzyme's behaviour.
Show Answer

Michaelis-Menten: V = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S]). When [S] = 10 mM (= Km): V = 1000 × 10 / (10 + 10) = 10,000/20 = 500 μmol/min. This is exactly ½Vmax — confirming that Km is the [S] at which V = ½Vmax. When [S] = 100 mM: V = 1000 × 100 / (10 + 100) = 100,000/110 = 909 μmol/min. This is 90.9% of Vmax. Interpretation: even at 10x Km, the enzyme only reaches ~91% of Vmax — it approaches but never reaches Vmax. To double the rate from 500 to 909, we needed 10x more substrate. This shows diminishing returns as substrate concentration rises above Km — increasingly large amounts of substrate are needed for smaller and smaller gains in rate.

Hard
Evaluate the use of enzyme activity assays as diagnostic tools in medicine, explaining why the appearance of specific enzymes in blood plasma indicates tissue damage. Give two clinical examples.
Show Answer

In healthy tissue, intracellular enzymes are confined within cells. When cells are damaged or die, their contents (including enzymes) leak into the bloodstream. Plasma enzyme activity therefore indicates the location and extent of tissue damage (each tissue has characteristic enzyme "signatures"). Clinical examples: 1. Myocardial infarction (heart attack): troponin I/T and creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB) are released from damaged heart muscle cells into blood. Elevated plasma CK-MB within 3-6 hours of chest pain confirms cardiac muscle necrosis. 2. Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis): alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) are released from damaged hepatocytes. Elevated plasma ALT is highly specific for liver damage. Enzyme assay advantages: rapid, quantitative, can indicate organ-specific damage without invasive biopsy.

SET A Recall – Membrane Structure Basics (Easy)

Easy
What is the name of the current accepted model of cell membrane structure, and who proposed it?
Show Answer

The fluid mosaic model, proposed by Singer and Nicolson in 1972. "Fluid" refers to the phospholipids moving laterally within the bilayer; "mosaic" refers to the varied proteins embedded throughout the membrane.

Easy
Describe the structure of a phospholipid molecule and explain why it forms a bilayer in water.
Show Answer

A phospholipid has a hydrophilic (water-loving) head (glycerol + phosphate + charged group) and two hydrophobic (water-fearing) fatty acid tails. In water, phospholipids spontaneously arrange into a bilayer: heads face outward toward water, tails face inward away from water. This arrangement minimises hydrophobic tails' contact with water while keeping heads hydrated.

Easy
What does "selectively permeable" mean in the context of cell membranes?
Show Answer

Selectively permeable means the membrane allows some molecules to pass through freely while restricting others. Small non-polar molecules (O₂, CO₂) pass easily; large or charged/polar molecules (glucose, ions, proteins) cannot cross the lipid bilayer without protein carriers or channels.

Easy
Distinguish between integral (intrinsic) proteins and peripheral (extrinsic) proteins in the cell membrane.
Show Answer

Integral proteins: embedded within or spanning the phospholipid bilayer (transmembrane proteins cross the full bilayer). They cannot be easily removed without disrupting the membrane. Functions: channels, carriers, receptors. Peripheral proteins: loosely attached to the outer surface of the membrane (often to integral proteins). Easily removed. Functions: enzyme activity, cell signalling, structural support.

Easy
What is cholesterol, and what role does it play in the cell membrane?
Show Answer

Cholesterol is a lipid molecule embedded in the phospholipid bilayer of animal cell membranes. It moderates membrane fluidity: at high temperatures, it reduces fluidity (stabilises the membrane); at low temperatures, it prevents the membrane from becoming too rigid by preventing phospholipid tails from packing too tightly.

Easy
What are glycoproteins and glycolipids on the cell membrane surface, and what are their main functions?
Show Answer

Glycoproteins: proteins with carbohydrate (sugar) chains attached on the extracellular surface. Glycolipids: lipids with carbohydrate chains on the outer face. Functions: cell-cell recognition (immune system identifies self vs. non-self), cell signalling (hormone receptors), cell adhesion, forming the glycocalyx (protective coat).

Easy
Name three types of membrane proteins and describe one function for each.
Show Answer

1. Channel proteins — form water-filled pores through the membrane, allowing specific ions (Na⁺, K⁺) or water (aquaporins) to pass freely. 2. Carrier proteins — bind specific molecules and change shape to transport them across the membrane (e.g., glucose transporter). 3. Receptor proteins — bind specific signalling molecules (hormones, neurotransmitters) on the outside and trigger a cellular response.

Easy
Explain what is meant by the "fluid" part of the fluid mosaic model.
Show Answer

Phospholipid molecules are not fixed in position — they can move laterally (sideways) within their leaflet of the bilayer at high speed, and proteins can also drift laterally. This gives the membrane a fluid, dynamic character. Lateral movement is possible because the hydrophobic tails interact with each other by relatively weak van der Waals forces. Flip-flop movement (switching between layers) is very rare and requires enzyme assistance (flippases).

Easy
Why does the membrane appear as two dark lines under an electron microscope?
Show Answer

Under electron microscopy, heavy metal stains (osmium tetroxide) bind to the hydrophilic phospholipid heads (outer and inner surfaces). The two stained head layers appear as two dark lines, separated by the lighter, unstained hydrophobic tail core. This "unit membrane" appearance confirms the bilayer structure.

Easy
How does the cell membrane contribute to cell signalling?
Show Answer

Receptor proteins on the membrane's extracellular surface bind specific signalling molecules (ligands) such as hormones, growth factors, and neurotransmitters. Binding triggers a conformational change in the receptor, initiating an intracellular signal cascade that alters cell behaviour. The membrane thus acts as the interface between the cell and its external information environment.

SET B Apply – Membrane Function (Medium)

Medium
Explain why oxygen and carbon dioxide can cross the cell membrane easily, while glucose and sodium ions cannot cross unaided. Refer to membrane structure in your answer.
Show Answer

O₂ and CO₂: small, non-polar molecules. The hydrophobic lipid bilayer core is permeable to non-polar molecules. They dissolve in the lipid layer and diffuse through. Glucose: a large, polar molecule (−OH groups). The hydrophobic core repels polar molecules. Glucose needs specific carrier proteins (GLUT transporters) to cross. Na⁺ ions: charged (ionic). Charges cannot pass through the hydrophobic core. Na⁺ requires specific protein channels (sodium channels) or active transport (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) to cross. This selective permeability allows the cell to control its internal environment.

Medium
How does temperature affect membrane fluidity, and what are the biological consequences of the membrane becoming too fluid or too rigid?
Show Answer

High temperature: increased phospholipid movement → membrane too fluid → may leak, membrane proteins may not function correctly; cell contents may escape. Low temperature: phospholipid tails solidify → membrane too rigid → transport proteins cannot change conformation (no transport), membrane may crack. Cholesterol buffers these extremes (prevents over-fluidity at high temperature and over-rigidity at low temperature). Organisms living in cold environments increase the proportion of unsaturated fatty acids in their membranes — kinked unsaturated tails maintain fluidity at low temperature.

Medium
Explain how the ABO blood group system relates to the glycoproteins and glycolipids on red blood cell membranes, and why incompatible blood types cause a dangerous immune reaction.
Show Answer

ABO blood groups are determined by the type of carbohydrate chain added to glycoproteins/glycolipids on red blood cell surfaces. Blood type A: A-antigen chains. Blood type B: B-antigen chains. Blood type AB: both. Blood type O: neither (H-antigen only). The immune system produces antibodies against foreign antigens. Type A blood has anti-B antibodies; Type B has anti-A. If incompatible blood is transfused, antibodies bind the foreign antigens on red blood cells → agglutination (clumping) → haemolysis (bursting) → potentially fatal transfusion reaction. Cell surface glycoproteins are thus critical for cell recognition.

Medium
Explain how lipid-soluble (fat-soluble) drugs and toxins can disrupt cell membrane function, giving two specific examples.
Show Answer

Lipid-soluble substances dissolve into the phospholipid bilayer. 1. General anaesthetics (chloroform, ether): dissolve in neuronal membranes, increasing membrane fluidity and disrupting ion channel function → loss of consciousness. 2. Ethanol: inserts between phospholipid molecules, fluidises the membrane, disrupts receptor protein conformation (GABA receptors, NMDA receptors) → impaired neural signalling → intoxication. Also: detergents/solvents dissolve the lipid bilayer entirely, destroying membrane integrity → cell death. This is why detergents are effective disinfectants against lipid-enveloped viruses (COVID-19, influenza).

Medium
How did the Frye-Edidin experiment (1970) provide direct evidence for membrane fluidity? Describe the experiment and its conclusion.
Show Answer

Frye and Edidin fused a human cell and a mouse cell. Before fusion, mouse surface proteins were labelled with red fluorescent antibodies; human proteins with green. Immediately after fusion, red and green labels were on separate halves of the fused cell. After 40 minutes at 37°C, the colours were completely intermixed. Conclusion: membrane proteins are not fixed in position — they drift laterally. Lateral diffusion of proteins within the fluid lipid bilayer was directly demonstrated. At low temperature, mixing was prevented, confirming fluidity depends on temperature.

Medium
Explain how the cell membrane is involved in receptor-mediated endocytosis, using LDL (low-density lipoprotein) uptake as an example.
Show Answer

LDL (cholesterol carrier) binds to specific LDL receptor proteins in the cell membrane. The membrane region containing receptor-LDL complexes invaginates (folds inward), pinching off to form an endosome (vesicle) inside the cell — this is receptor-mediated endocytosis. Inside the cell, LDL is processed and cholesterol released. LDL receptors return to the membrane surface for reuse. Significance: specific — only LDL (not random molecules) is taken up. Familial hypercholesterolaemia is caused by defective LDL receptors → LDL cannot be internalised → builds up in blood → atherosclerosis.

Medium
Why do plant cells, fungal cells, and bacteria not require cholesterol to maintain membrane stability? What do they use instead?
Show Answer

Plants: do not have cholesterol but have cell walls (cellulose) outside the membrane providing structural support; plant membranes contain sterols such as sitosterol and stigmasterol serving similar membrane-stabilising roles. Fungi: use ergosterol instead of cholesterol (this is why antifungal drugs like amphotericin B and azoles target ergosterol synthesis — they are specific to fungi). Bacteria: most lack sterols; their membranes are stabilised by hopanoids (triterpenoid molecules) and the surrounding peptidoglycan cell wall. These alternatives fulfil the membrane stabilisation function but differ structurally from cholesterol.

Medium
The antibiotic amphotericin B works by binding to ergosterol (the equivalent of cholesterol in fungi) and forming pores in the fungal membrane. Explain why this selectively kills fungi but not human cells.
Show Answer

Amphotericin B has much higher affinity for ergosterol than for cholesterol. Human cell membranes contain cholesterol, not ergosterol. The drug preferentially inserts into ergosterol-containing fungal membranes, forming channels that allow K⁺, Mg²⁺, and small molecules to leak out — disrupting the electrochemical gradient and killing the cell. Human cells are far less affected because the drug binds poorly to cholesterol. This demonstrates how differences in membrane composition between host and pathogen can be exploited in drug design — a key principle of selective toxicity in pharmacology.

Medium
Explain why the cell membrane is described as asymmetric, and give one functional consequence of this asymmetry.
Show Answer

Asymmetric means the two leaflets (inner = cytoplasmic; outer = extracellular) have different phospholipid and protein compositions. Outer leaflet: phosphatidylcholine, sphingomyelin, glycolipids, glycoproteins. Inner leaflet: phosphatidylserine (negatively charged), phosphatidylinositol (signalling lipid), phosphatidylethanolamine. Functional consequence: 1. Phosphatidylserine on the outer leaflet is an "eat me" signal — during apoptosis, it flips to the outer surface, signalling macrophages to phagocytose the cell. 2. Phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP₂) on the inner leaflet is a substrate for phospholipase C in signal transduction. The asymmetry is maintained by energy-consuming flippase enzymes.

Medium
Compare the structure of the plasma membrane with the nuclear envelope, explaining why the nuclear envelope has two membrane layers and what this implies for transport.
Show Answer

Plasma membrane: single phospholipid bilayer. Nuclear envelope: double membrane (two phospholipid bilayers) — inner and outer nuclear membrane, with a perinuclear space between them. The outer nuclear membrane is continuous with the rough ER. Nuclear pore complexes (NPC) span both membranes, providing the only regulated passage between nucleus and cytoplasm. NPCs are large protein structures (~120 nm) that allow selective transport of proteins (transcription factors, RNA polymerase) into the nucleus and mRNA, ribosomes, and tRNA out. The double membrane and selective NPCs protect the genome and control which proteins access DNA — critical for gene regulation.

SET C Advanced – MYP/DP Bridge (Hard)

Hard
Critically evaluate the fluid mosaic model. What experimental evidence led to its development, and what refinements have been made since Singer and Nicolson (1972)?
Show Answer

Evidence for: Frye-Edidin experiment (lateral protein mobility); freeze-fracture electron microscopy (proteins embedded in lipid, not just on surface); X-ray diffraction (bilayer arrangement). Refinements: 1. Lipid rafts: cholesterol and sphingolipid-rich microdomains with restricted lateral diffusion — membrane is not uniformly fluid but organised into functional platforms. 2. Membrane protein movement is often restricted by the cytoskeleton picket fence (corralled movement). 3. Some proteins are stationary (anchored by cytoskeleton). 4. Asymmetry of the two leaflets. 5. The glycocalyx (extracellular carbohydrate coat) plays a major role in cell communication not included in the original model. Current "updated fluid mosaic model" acknowledges these heterogeneities while retaining the core bilayer/mobile protein framework.

Hard
Explain the concept of membrane potential and how the differential distribution of ions across the cell membrane creates an electrical gradient crucial for nerve function.
Show Answer

Resting membrane potential (approx. −70 mV in neurons): inside of cell is negative relative to outside. Maintained by: 1. Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase actively pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle (creates a net positive charge outside). 2. Membrane is more permeable to K⁺ (more K⁺ channels open at rest) → K⁺ diffuses out down its concentration gradient, leaving negative charges behind. The resulting electrochemical gradient stores potential energy. Explain the chemiosmotic theory (Peter Mitchell's Nobel-winning work), describing how proton gradients across membranes drive ATP synthesis in mitochondria and chloroplasts.
Show Answer

Chemiosmosis (Mitchell, 1961; Nobel 1978): the cell membrane is not just a barrier but an energy transducer. In mitochondria: electron transport chain pumps H⁺ (protons) from matrix to intermembrane space across the inner mitochondrial membrane → creates electrochemical H⁺ gradient (proton motive force: concentration gradient + charge gradient). ATP synthase (complex V): H⁺ flow back down the gradient through ATP synthase's proton channel → the flow drives rotation of ATP synthase's F₀ subunit → mechanical rotation powers phosphorylation of ADP to ATP in the F₁ subunit. Similarly in chloroplasts: light reactions pump H⁺ into the thylakoid lumen → gradient drives ATP synthesis through ATP synthase. Key insight: the membrane's selective impermeability to H⁺ (except through ATP synthase) is essential — without the bilayer barrier, the gradient would collapse and no ATP could be synthesised.

Phospholipids self-assemble into bilayer vesicles spontaneously in water — no biological machinery required. This property means that the primordial cell membrane could have arisen abiotically. Without the membrane's selective permeability, metabolism, signalling, reproduction, and heredity cannot occur — the membrane is the boundary condition for life itself.
