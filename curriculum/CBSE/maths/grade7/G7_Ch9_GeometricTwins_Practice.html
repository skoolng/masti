Set A Foundation

What does it mean for two figures to be congruent?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Two figures are congruent if they have exactly the same shape AND the same size.

Method: Definition
Congruent figures are identical in all respects — you could lift one and place it exactly on the other (allowing rotation, reflection, and translation).
Symbol: ≅
Key Rule: Congruent = same shape AND same size. NOT just same shape (that would be similar).
What is the symbol for congruence?
Easy
Answer

Answer: ≅

Method: Notation
We write △ABC ≅ △PQR to mean triangles ABC and PQR are congruent.
The symbol ≅ combines = (equal in size) and ~ (similar in shape).
Key Rule: Use ≅ for congruence; use ~ for similarity; use = for equality of measure.
Can two congruent figures be mirror images of each other?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — reflected figures are still congruent.

Method: Transformation Types
Congruence allows: translations (slides), rotations (turns), and reflections (flips).
A reflected figure has the same shape and size — it is congruent.
Key Rule: Congruent figures can be obtained by any combination of translations, rotations, and reflections.
Is similarity the same as congruence? Explain.
Easy
Answer

Answer: No — similar figures have the same shape but may differ in size. Congruent figures have BOTH the same shape and the same size.

Method: Distinction
Similar (~): same angles, sides proportional. Example: a photo enlarged to 2× size — similar but not congruent.
Congruent (≅): same angles, corresponding sides EQUAL. Example: two identical stamps — congruent.
Key Rule: All congruent figures are similar, but not all similar figures are congruent.
If △ABC ≅ △PQR, which vertex corresponds to which?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A↔P, B↔Q, C↔R (ORDER matters in the congruence statement)

Method: Correspondence
In △ABC ≅ △PQR: the letters are listed in corresponding order.
First vertex of first ↔ first vertex of second: A↔P; B↔Q; C↔R.
Key Rule: The ORDER of vertices in a congruence statement specifies the correspondence.
Name three real-life examples of congruent objects.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Identical coins, machine-cut tiles, postage stamps from the same sheet.

Method: Application
1. Coins minted from the same die: identical size and shape.
2. Floor tiles from the same batch: same shape and size.
3. Postage stamps on a sheet: each is a congruent copy.
4. Identical windows in a building; identical machine parts; photocopies at 100%.
Key Rule: Congruence in manufacturing ensures interchangeability of parts.
A square of side 5 cm. Is it congruent to another square of side 5 cm?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes

Method: Check
Both squares have sides of 5 cm and all angles of 90°. Same shape, same size → congruent.
Key Rule: Two squares with the same side length are always congruent.
Can two circles be congruent? Under what condition?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — when they have the same radius.

Method: Circle Congruence
A circle is completely determined by its radius. Two circles are congruent if and only if they have the same radius.
Key Rule: Circles are congruent ↔ equal radii.
If you rotate a triangle by 90°, is the rotated triangle congruent to the original?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — rotation preserves shape and size.

Method: Transformation
Rotation is a rigid motion — it moves the figure without changing its size or shape. Therefore, a rotated triangle is always congruent to the original.
Key Rule: Rigid motions (translation, rotation, reflection) preserve congruence.
True or False: If two triangles have the same area, they are congruent.
Medium
Answer

Answer: False

Method: Counterexample
A right triangle with legs 3 and 4 (area=6) is NOT congruent to a triangle with base 4 and height 3 (area=6) that has different side lengths.
Example: right triangle (3,4,5) and isoceles triangle (3.5,3.5, base) with area 6.
Key Rule: Equal area is necessary but NOT sufficient for congruence.

Set B Practice

△PQR ≅ △XYZ. Name all corresponding sides and angles.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Sides: PQ=XY, QR=YZ, PR=XZ. Angles: ∠P=∠X, ∠Q=∠Y, ∠R=∠Z.

Method: Correspondence by Order
P↔X, Q↔Y, R↔Z. Each vertex maps to its counterpart in the same position in the statement.
Key Rule: Corresponding parts: match letters in the same position of the congruence statement.
If △ABC ≅ △DEF, AB = 5 cm, BC = 7 cm, and AC = 6 cm, what is DE?
Easy
Answer

Answer: DE = 5 cm

Method: Correspondence
A↔D, B↔E, C↔F. So AB↔DE. AB=5 cm → DE=5 cm.
Key Rule: Corresponding sides of congruent triangles are equal.
How many pairs of corresponding parts are there in two congruent triangles?
Easy
Answer

Answer: 6 pairs (3 pairs of sides + 3 pairs of angles)

Method: Count
Each triangle has 3 sides and 3 angles. In congruent triangles, all 6 parts match: 3 side pairs + 3 angle pairs = 6 corresponding pairs.
Key Rule: CPCT: all 6 corresponding parts (3 sides + 3 angles) are equal in congruent triangles.
Is it possible for two triangles to have equal areas AND equal perimeters but NOT be congruent?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Yes — different triangles can share both area and perimeter without being congruent.

Method: Counterexample
This is non-trivial — though examples exist mathematically, they involve irrational numbers. In general, equal area + equal perimeter does not guarantee congruence.
A simpler counter: two triangles with sides (3,4,5) and (2.5,5,5) might have equal perimeter 12 but different areas. To have BOTH equal is rare but possible.
Key Rule: No finite set of measurements (except all 6 parts) guarantees congruence unless specific rules are met.
△ABC ≅ △PQR, ∠A = 50°, ∠B = 70°. Find ∠R.
Medium
Answer

Answer: ∠R = 60°

Method: Corresponding Angles + Angle Sum
∠A=50°, ∠B=70°, ∠C=180°−50°−70°=60°. A↔P, B↔Q, C↔R. ∠R=∠C=60°.
Key Rule: Angle sum in triangle = 180°. Corresponding angles in congruent triangles are equal.
Why is the ORDER of vertices important in writing a congruence statement?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Because the order specifies which vertex corresponds to which — without order, we don't know which parts match.

Method: Conceptual
△ABC ≅ △PQR means A↔P, B↔Q, C↔R.
△ABC ≅ △QPR would mean A↔Q, B↔P, C↔R — completely different correspondence!
Same triangles, different statements — different implications for which sides and angles are equal.
Key Rule: Vertex ORDER in ≅ statement defines ALL correspondences; changing order changes the statement.
Two congruent rectangles each have length 8 cm and width 5 cm. What is the perimeter of each?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Perimeter = 26 cm

Method: Perimeter Formula
P = 2(l+w) = 2(8+5) = 2×13 = 26 cm. Both rectangles have same perimeter since they are congruent.
Key Rule: Congruent figures have identical measurements — including perimeter and area.
Is an equilateral triangle with side 4 cm congruent to an equilateral triangle with side 5 cm?
Easy
Answer

Answer: No — different side lengths → not congruent (though they are similar).

Method: Size Check
Both are equilateral (all angles 60°, same shape). But sides differ (4≠5). Not same size → NOT congruent.
They are similar (same shape) but not congruent (different size).
Key Rule: Same shape but different size = similar, NOT congruent.
If △ABC ≅ △BAC, what can you conclude about triangle ABC?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Triangle ABC is isosceles (with AB = AB, but A↔B means AC=BC).

Method: Correspondence Analysis
△ABC ≅ △BAC: A↔B, B↔A, C↔C.
Corresponding sides: AB↔BA (same), BC↔AC, AC↔BC.
So AC=BC → the triangle is isosceles with the two sides from C being equal.
Key Rule: Self-congruence with swapped vertices reveals symmetry in the triangle.
Can two different quadrilaterals be congruent? Give an example.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Yes — two squares of the same size, or two rectangles with the same dimensions.

Method: Example
Two 4-cm squares: both have 4 sides of 4 cm, all angles 90°. Same shape, same size → congruent.
Two identical non-square rectangles (3×5): congruent even though not squares.
Key Rule: Congruent quadrilaterals: all 4 sides and all 4 angles must correspond and be equal.

∠P+∠Q+∠R=180°. ∠R=180°−45°−90°=45°. P↔S, Q↔T, R↔U. ∠U=∠R=45°.

Method: Application
If bolt heads are congruent (same shape and size), any standard wrench of the matching size will fit any bolt. Non-congruent bolts would require different tools for each bolt.

Describe the transformations that take △ABC to its congruent image △A'B'C'.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Any combination of translation (slide), rotation (turn), and reflection (flip).

Method: Rigid Motions
Translation: shift every point by the same vector.
Rotation: turn around a fixed point by an angle.
Reflection: mirror image across a line.
All three preserve shape and size → preserve congruence.
Key Rule: Rigid motions (isometries) preserve congruence.
Two congruent equilateral triangles are placed together along one side. What shape do they form?

Answer: A rhombus (diamond shape)

Method: Visualise
Two equilateral triangles sharing a side form a quadrilateral with 4 equal sides (since all sides of equilateral triangles are equal) → a rhombus. Specifically, it is a rhombus with all angles 60° and 120°.

Is it possible for two triangles to be congruent if one is equilateral and the other is scalene?
Medium
Answer

Answer: No — congruent triangles must have identical shapes. An equilateral triangle has all sides equal; a scalene has all sides different.

Method: Contradiction
Equilateral: all sides equal (a,a,a). Scalene: all sides different (p,q,r with p≠q≠r≠p). For congruence, all corresponding sides must match → a=p, a=q, a=r → p=q=r. But then it's equilateral, not scalene. Contradiction.
Key Rule: Congruent triangles have the same type (equilateral with equilateral, etc.).
Write the congruence statement correctly for two triangles where P↔L, Q↔M, R↔N.

Method: Write Statement from Correspondence
P↔L: P first in left, L first in right. Q↔M: second positions. R↔N: third positions.
Write: △PQR ≅ △LMN.
Key Rule: Match vertices in corresponding positions to write the congruence statement.

Set D Challenge

Prove that if △ABC ≅ △DEF and △DEF ≅ △GHI, then △ABC ≅ △GHI (transitivity of congruence).
Hard
Answer

Answer: Because congruence is an equivalence relation — it is transitive.

Method: Equivalence Relation Proof
△ABC ≅ △DEF: all corresponding parts equal (AB=DE, BC=EF, AC=DF, ∠A=∠D, ∠B=∠E, ∠C=∠F).
△DEF ≅ △GHI: all corresponding parts equal (DE=GH, EF=HI, DF=GI, ∠D=∠G, ∠E=∠H, ∠F=∠I).
By transitivity of equality: AB=GH, BC=HI, AC=GI, ∠A=∠G, ∠B=∠H, ∠C=∠I. So △ABC ≅ △GHI. ✓
Key Rule: Congruence is an equivalence relation: reflexive, symmetric, transitive.
Can two triangles be "partially congruent" — where some but not all corresponding parts are equal?
Medium
Answer

Answer: No — congruence requires ALL corresponding parts to be equal, not just some.

Method: Definition
"Partial congruence" is not a standard geometric concept. Either two triangles are congruent (all 6 parts match) or they are not.
However, triangles may satisfy some but not all conditions — this leads to "similar" (all angles equal, sides proportional) or merely "having equal area."
Key Rule: Congruence is all-or-nothing: all 6 corresponding parts must be equal.
If △ABC ≅ △ACB, what type of triangle is ABC?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Isosceles with AB = AC.

Method: Correspondence Analysis
△ABC ≅ △ACB: A↔A, B↔C, C↔B.
Corresponding sides: AB↔AC (so AB=AC); BC↔CB (same side, always equal); AC↔AB (same as AB=AC).
Corresponding angles: ∠A↔∠A (trivial); ∠B↔∠C (so ∠B=∠C).
AB=AC → isosceles. ✓
Key Rule: A triangle congruent to itself with a swapped correspondence reveals its symmetry.
Explain why congruence is reflexive: every figure is congruent to itself.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Because you can map every figure to itself using the identity transformation (no movement).

Method: Reflexivity
The identity transformation (f(x)=x for all points) maps every point to itself. It is a rigid motion (trivially preserving distances and angles). So every figure is congruent to itself.
Notation: △ABC ≅ △ABC always holds.
Key Rule: Reflexivity of congruence: △ABC ≅ △ABC (trivially true by identity transformation).
How does congruence differ from "equal" in geometry? (e.g., ∠A = ∠B vs ∠A ≅ ∠B)
Medium
Answer

Answer: "=" is used for equal measures (numbers); "≅" is used for figures that are congruent. Technically, ∠A ≅ ∠B means the angles are congruent figures; ∠A = ∠B means their measures in degrees are equal.

Method: Notation Distinction
Strictly: m∠A = m∠B means "the measure of angle A equals the measure of angle B" (number equality).
∠A ≅ ∠B means "angle A is congruent to angle B" (figure congruence).
In practice, both notations are used interchangeably at the Grade 7 level.
Key Rule: ≅ for congruent figures; = for equal numerical measures. Both are commonly used for angles and segments.
Three triangles: △ABC with sides 3,4,5; △PQR with sides 4,5,3; △XYZ with sides 5,3,4. Are they all congruent to each other?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Yes — all three have sides {3,4,5}; all are congruent (by SSS).

Method: SSS Congruence
All three triangles have the same set of side lengths {3,4,5} — just written in different orders.
By SSS congruence (once we establish the correct correspondence), all three are congruent to each other.
Key Rule: The SET of side lengths determines SSS congruence; ORDER in the congruence statement sets the correspondence.
Is congruence preserved when you scale a figure by a factor of 1? What about a factor of 2?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Scale factor 1: congruence preserved (trivially — same size). Scale factor 2: NOT congruent (size doubles) — only similar.

Method: Scaling
Scale factor 1: every distance multiplied by 1 = unchanged. Figure identical → congruent. ✓
Scale factor 2: all sides doubled, area quadrupled. Not same size → NOT congruent (only similar). ✗
Key Rule: Scale factor 1 preserves congruence; any other scale factor creates a similar (but not congruent) figure.
Prove that two congruent triangles must have the same area.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Congruent triangles have all corresponding sides and angles equal; therefore base and height are equal, giving equal areas.

Method: Area Proof
If △ABC ≅ △DEF: all sides equal (AB=DE, BC=EF, AC=DF). Take base BC=EF and corresponding heights h_A and h_D.
Since the triangles are congruent (rigid motion), h_A = h_D.
Area(△ABC) = ½×BC×h_A = ½×EF×h_D = Area(△DEF). ✓
Key Rule: Congruent figures have identical areas (and all other measurements).
Can two figures be congruent even if one is labelled and the other is not? Explain.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — labels (vertex names) are human-assigned; the geometric shapes themselves may still be identical.

Method: Conceptual
Labels like A, B, C are just names for vertices. The underlying geometric shape doesn't depend on labelling. If the shapes and sizes match, the figures are congruent regardless of whether they are labelled.
Key Rule: Congruence is a geometric property of the figures themselves, independent of labelling.
Is the set of all triangles congruent to a given triangle △ABC a finite or infinite set?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Infinite — you can translate, rotate, or reflect △ABC infinitely many ways, each producing a congruent triangle in a different position.

Method: Transformation Count
For each point P in the plane and each angle θ, there is a rotation of △ABC by θ about P — infinitely many choices. Plus reflections and translations. So infinitely many congruent copies of △ABC exist.
Key Rule: Any figure has infinitely many congruent copies (differing in position/orientation).

When are two line segments congruent?
Easy
Answer

Answer: When they have the same length.

Method: Definition
Two line segments are congruent if they have equal lengths. Example: AB = 5 cm and CD = 5 cm → AB ≅ CD.
Key Rule: Line segments: congruent ↔ equal length.
Are two angles of 75° each congruent?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — both angles measure 75°, so they are congruent.

Method: Angle Congruence
Two angles are congruent if they have the same degree measure. 75° = 75° → congruent.
Key Rule: Angles: congruent ↔ equal degree measures.
Mark the segment AB = 6 cm and CD = 6 cm. Write the congruence statement.
Easy
Answer

Answer: AB ≅ CD

Method: Notation
Since AB and CD have equal lengths (6 cm each), we write AB ≅ CD.
Key Rule: Use ≅ for congruent segments; use = only for their lengths (AB = CD = 6 cm).
Instrument used to copy a line segment of given length?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A compass and ruler (straightedge)

Method: Construction
To copy segment AB: use a compass to measure length AB; mark the same length on another line. The resulting segment is congruent to AB.
Key Rule: Compass copies lengths; straightedge draws straight lines — together used for all classical constructions.
What is a protractor used for in the context of congruent angles?
Easy
Answer

Answer: To measure angles in degrees, allowing us to check if two angles are congruent (have equal measures).

Method: Tool Use
Place protractor centre at vertex; align baseline with one ray; read the degree measure at the other ray. If two angles give the same reading, they are congruent.
Key Rule: Protractor: measure angles; compass: copy angles (by constructing congruent angles).
Are all right angles congruent to each other?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — all right angles measure 90°.

Method: Right Angle
A right angle is defined as an angle of exactly 90°. Since all right angles have the same measure, they are all congruent to each other.
Key Rule: All right angles are congruent (all equal 90°). This is Euclid's 4th postulate.
PQ = 8 cm and RS = 8 cm. Are PQ and RS congruent?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — PQ ≅ RS

Method: Length Check
PQ = RS = 8 cm → same length → congruent segments. PQ ≅ RS.
Key Rule: Equal lengths → congruent segments.
∠XYZ = 120° and ∠PQR = 2/3 of 180°. Are they congruent?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Yes — ∠PQR = (2/3)×180° = 120° = ∠XYZ → congruent.

Method: Calculate and Compare
∠PQR = (2/3)×180° = 120°. ∠XYZ = 120°. Equal measures → congruent. ∠XYZ ≅ ∠PQR.
Key Rule: Compute the measure; compare; equal measure = congruent angle.
How many tick marks do we use to show two pairs of congruent segments in a diagram?
Easy
Answer

Answer: One pair: one tick mark each. Second pair: two tick marks each. Different numbers of tick marks mean different (non-congruent) lengths.

Method: Diagram Convention
Convention: segments with the same number of tick marks are congruent.
First congruent pair: | (one tick). Second congruent pair: || (two ticks). Third pair: ||| (three ticks).
Key Rule: Tick marks in geometry diagrams indicate equal (congruent) segments.
Arc marks on angles in a diagram indicate what?
Easy
Answer

Answer: That those angles are congruent (equal in measure).

Method: Diagram Convention
Single arc: two angles with one arc each are congruent. Double arc: those are congruent to each other (but not necessarily to the single-arc angles).
Key Rule: Arc marks indicate equal angles — same number of arcs = congruent angles.

Explain how to construct an angle congruent to ∠ABC using only a compass and straightedge.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Draw ray; mark arc on ∠ABC; transfer the same arc; mark chord length; draw ray through mark.

Method: Construction Steps
1. Draw ray PX (the base of new angle).
2. With compass at B: draw arc cutting BA at D and BC at E.
3. With same radius at P: draw arc cutting PX at Q.
4. Set compass to chord DE. With centre Q: draw arc intersecting first arc at R.
5. Draw ray PR. ∠RPQ = ∠ABC (congruent). ✓
Key Rule: Angle copy: transfer arc radius and chord measurement using compass.
Co-interior (same-side interior) angles when a transversal cuts parallel lines sum to 180°. Are they congruent?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Only if each is 90°. In general, they are supplementary, not congruent.

Method: Supplementary vs Congruent
Co-interior angles sum to 180° (supplementary). They are congruent only if both = 90°.
Example: if one is 70°, the other is 110°. 70° ≠ 110° → not congruent.
Key Rule: Co-interior angles: supplementary (sum 180°); NOT congruent in general.

State the SAS congruence rule.
Easy
Answer

Answer: If two sides and the INCLUDED angle of one triangle are equal to two sides and the included angle of another, the triangles are congruent.

Method: SAS Definition
SAS = Side-Angle-Side.
The angle must be BETWEEN (included by) the two known sides.
If AB=DE, ∠B=∠E, BC=EF → △ABC ≅ △DEF by SAS.
Key Rule: SAS: two sides AND the angle BETWEEN them must be equal. The angle is "sandwiched" between the two sides.

Easy
Answer

Answer: The angle formed between the two given sides — the angle at the vertex where the two sides meet.

Method: Definition
If the two known sides are AB and BC, the included angle is ∠B (at vertex B, between sides AB and BC).
If the sides were AB and AC, the included angle would be ∠A.
Key Rule: Included angle = angle AT the vertex that both given sides share.

State the ASA congruence rule.
Easy
Answer

Answer: If two angles and the INCLUDED side of one triangle equal two angles and the included side of another, the triangles are congruent.

Method: ASA Definition
ASA = Angle-Side-Angle.
The side must be BETWEEN (included by) the two angles — the side connecting the two angle vertices.
If ∠A=∠D, AB=DE, ∠B=∠E → △ABC ≅ △DEF by ASA.
Key Rule: ASA: two angles AND the side BETWEEN them must be equal.
What is the "included side" in ASA?
Easy
Answer

Answer: The side that connects the vertices of the two known angles — it is "between" them.

Method: Definition
If the two angles are at vertices A and B, the included side is AB (connecting A to B).
The side "AT vertex A and vertex B" is the one between those two angles.
Key Rule: Included side in ASA = side connecting the two angle vertices.

Set A Foundation

State the AAS congruence rule.
Easy
Answer

Answer: If two angles and a non-included (any corresponding) side of one triangle equal the corresponding parts of another triangle, the triangles are congruent.

Method: AAS Definition
AAS = Angle-Angle-Side.
The side does NOT have to be between the two angles (unlike ASA).
If ∠A=∠D, ∠B=∠E, BC=EF → △ABC ≅ △DEF by AAS.
Key Rule: AAS: two angles (anywhere) and one corresponding side (not necessarily included) → congruent.
How is AAS different from ASA?
Easy
Answer

Answer: In ASA, the side is BETWEEN the two angles. In AAS, the side is NOT between the two angles (it can be any corresponding side).

Method: Comparison
ASA: ∠A, AB (included), ∠B — side connects the two angle vertices.
AAS: ∠A, ∠B, BC (non-included) — side is NOT between the two angles.
Both are valid congruence criteria and are logically equivalent.
Key Rule: ASA = included side; AAS = non-included side. Both give 2 angles + 1 side → congruent.

∠A=∠P, ∠B=∠Q (two pairs of angles), BC=QR (corresponding non-included side opposite ∠A). AAS → △ABC ≅ △PQR. ✓
Key Rule: AAS: verify the side corresponds correctly (same position relative to the angles).

State the RHS congruence rule.
Easy
Answer
