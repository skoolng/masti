Hard
Answer

Benefits: replace damaged heart/nerve tissue; treat diabetes/Parkinson's. Ethical concerns: embryonic stem cell sourcing destroys embryos; risk of immune rejection; tumour formation. Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSC) address some concerns. IB Criterion D: science-society interface.

A scientist claims sclerenchyma provides better support than collenchyma. Construct an argument for and against this claim.
Hard
Answer

For: sclerenchyma has thick lignified walls, dead cells, very rigid — superior in mature woody plants. Against: collenchyma is living, flexible, can grow — better in young growing regions. Context determines which is 'better'.

How does the structure of a neuron relate to its function? Evaluate how demyelination (loss of myelin) affects this relationship.
Hard
Answer

Dendrites: receive signals (large surface area). Axon: transmit impulses (length). Myelin sheath: insulates, speeds conduction via saltatory transmission. Demyelination (MS) slows/blocks impulse transmission → muscle weakness, vision problems, coordination loss.

Structure-function analysis with disease application
Compare plant and animal responses to injury in terms of tissue regeneration. Which has greater regenerative capacity and why?
Hard
Answer

Plants: meristematic tissue allows extensive regeneration (totipotency, callus formation, vegetative propagation). Animals: limited — liver regenerates; neurons cannot. Plant advantage: meristems remain active; animal differentiated cells lose division ability. Evolution: sessile plants need repair; mobile animals evolved immunity instead.

Cross-kingdom comparison with evolutionary reasoning
A company proposes using artificial cartilage scaffolds to replace damaged knee cartilage. Analyse the scientific basis and potential risks.
Hard
Answer

Scientific basis: cartilage has no blood vessels so heals poorly; scaffold (biodegradable polymer) seeded with chondrocytes grows in vitro then implanted. Risks: immune rejection, scaffold degradation before replacement tissue forms, mechanical failure. Benefit: avoids donor shortage of natural cartilage.

Biotechnology application with risk-benefit analysis
Investigate how the distribution of meristematic tissue in a plant relates to its growth pattern.
Hard
Answer

Apical meristems at root/shoot tips → primary growth (elongation). Lateral meristems (vascular/cork cambium) → secondary growth (girth) in dicots. Intercalary meristems in grasses → regrowth after grazing. Pattern: growth occurs at specific zones, not throughout body — unlike animals.

Spatial analysis of plant growth zones
Explain why cancer (uncontrolled cell division) is more dangerous in nervous tissue than in epithelial tissue.
Hard
Answer

Epithelial cells divide frequently → carcinomas are common but treatable (surgery/chemo). Neurons rarely divide in adults → brain tumours (gliomas) destroy irreplaceable cells; surgery risks vital function; limited drug delivery through blood-brain barrier. Loss of neurons = permanent functional deficit.

Applying cell biology to oncology and tissue regeneration limits
A researcher finds that a plant can photosynthesize, store starch, and support itself using the same tissue. Identify the tissue and evaluate whether this multifunctionality is advantageous.
Hard
Answer

Parenchyma: thin-walled, living, totipotent. Functions: photosynthesis (chloroplasts in mesophyll), storage (amyloplasts), packing/support (turgid cells). Advantage: economical — fewer tissue types needed in simple plants. Disadvantage: less efficient than specialised tissues; structural support weaker than sclerenchyma. Evolutionary stage: parenchyma is the ancestral ground tissue.

Set B — Apply
Why does the respiratory epithelium need to be ciliated?
Medium
Answer

Mucus traps dust and bacteria; cilia beat rhythmically to move mucus up to pharynx for swallowing — mucociliary escalator mechanism

Smoking destroys cilia, causing chronic bronchitis
Explain how the structure of intestinal epithelium maximises nutrient absorption.
Medium
Answer

Simple columnar cells with microvilli (brush border) increase surface area 600×; cells have many mitochondria for active transport; tight junctions between cells control what passes

Structural adaptations match digestive function
Compare simple squamous and simple columnar epithelium in terms of structure and function.
Medium
Answer

Squamous: flat, large surface area, suited for rapid passive diffusion (alveoli, capillaries). Columnar: tall, space for organelles, suited for secretion and absorption (intestine, stomach)

Shape directly reflects functional demands
Why must the skin (stratified squamous) be stratified rather than simple?
Medium
Answer

Constant abrasion, UV exposure, and pathogen contact require multiple layers; outer dead keratinised cells are shed and replaced from dividing basal layer — protection requires depth

Function drives tissue architecture
What would happen if goblet cells stopped producing mucus in the airways?
Medium
Answer

Cilia would beat without mucus transport; dust/pathogens accumulate in bronchi; increased infection risk; airway damage. Conditions like cystic fibrosis involve thick, sticky mucus (a related problem)

Mucus is essential to mucociliary defence
A biopsy shows cells with irregular shapes, multiple nuclei, and rapid division in the cervix. What does this suggest?
Medium
Answer

Abnormal epithelial cells suggest dysplasia or cervical cancer (carcinoma in situ → invasive cervical cancer). HPV is a major cause. Pap smear detects these changes early.

Epithelial cancers (carcinomas) are the most common cancer type
How does transitional epithelium (urothelium) in the bladder differ from other epithelia?
Medium
Answer

Transitional epithelium stretches when bladder fills (cells flatten) and returns to dome shape when empty; multiple layers; unique intermediate cell shapes

Stretching requirement requires a unique tissue type
Why are tight junctions particularly important in intestinal epithelium?
Medium
Answer

They prevent pathogens and undigested molecules from passing between cells (paracellular pathway); only selected molecules cross through regulated channels — maintains barrier function

Leaky gut syndrome results from tight junction breakdown
Predict what would happen to kidney function if cuboidal epithelium in tubules was replaced with squamous epithelium.
Medium
Answer

Squamous epithelium lacks the transport proteins, mitochondria, and surface area of cuboidal cells → impaired reabsorption of glucose, amino acids, water → loss in urine (similar to kidney disease)

Epithelial type determines organ function
How does the structure of glandular epithelium relate to its secretory function?
Medium
Answer

Glandular cells have extensive rough ER and Golgi apparatus for protein synthesis/packaging; secretory vesicles release products by exocytosis; organised in acini or tubules to direct secretion

Secretory organelles are abundant in glandular cells

A student proposes that all epithelial damage heals by stem cell division. Evaluate this claim with evidence.
Hard
Answer

Partially true: basal layer stem cells divide to replace shed/damaged surface cells (skin, gut). However: severe burns destroy basal layer → skin grafts needed. How might nanotechnology be used to deliver drugs through epithelial barriers? Analyse the risks.
Hard
Answer

Nanoparticles (<200nm) can cross tight junctions or be endocytosed by epithelial cells; used in inhaled asthma drugs, transdermal patches, oral chemotherapy. Risks: accumulation in organs, cytotoxicity, immune reactions, unknown long-term effects. Criterion D: emerging technology risk-benefit.

Biotechnology application with safety analysis
Compare the regeneration rates of different epithelia and explain the evolutionary advantage of high turnover in gut epithelium.
Hard
Answer

Gut epithelium: replaced every 2–4 days (high exposure to digestive enzymes, bacteria, mechanical abrasion). Skin: 2–4 weeks. Lung alveolar: slow. High gut turnover prevents cancer accumulation from DNA damage; eliminates infected cells quickly. Evolution favours rapid renewal in high-damage zones.

Investigate: how does the composition of the basement membrane affect epithelial cell behaviour in cancer?
Hard
Answer

Basement membrane contains collagen IV, laminin, fibronectin. In early cancer, cells breach basement membrane (invasion). Loss of E-cadherin weakens cell-cell adhesion; matrix metalloproteinases degrade ECM. Criterion B: propose experiment with transwell invasion assay measuring cells crossing a basement membrane matrix.

Cancer biology and experimental design
Evaluate the use of skin grafts from different sources: autograft, allograft, xenograft. What scientific and ethical issues arise?
Hard
Answer

Autograft (own skin): best outcome, no rejection, but limited supply. Allograft (donor skin): temporary coverage, immune rejection requires immunosuppression. Xenograft (pig skin): emergency coverage, highly antigenic. Ethical: pig welfare, donor consent. Criterion D: medical ethics + animal use in research.

Medical application with ethics
A pharmaceutical company wants to use an inhaled drug that must cross the alveolar epithelium to enter the bloodstream. What properties must the drug have?
Hard
Answer

Must be small enough to cross simple squamous epithelium by diffusion; lipid-soluble (crosses cell membrane) or small enough for facilitated transport; must not trigger immune response or surfactant disruption; particle size 1–5μm to reach alveoli without being trapped by mucus in larger airways.

Drug delivery pharmacology based on tissue structure
Compare the cancer risk in epithelial vs nervous tissue and evaluate why carcinomas are more common than neuromas.
Hard
Answer

Epithelial cells divide continuously → accumulate mutations → carcinomas (90% of all cancers). Neurons rarely divide → neuromas are rare but glial cells (which support neurons and do divide) can form gliomas. High division rate + exposure to carcinogens (UV, toxins, diet) = higher mutation probability.

Cell biology applied to oncology
Analyse the role of tight junctions in the blood-brain barrier and evaluate the challenges this creates for treating brain diseases.
Hard
Answer

Brain capillary endothelium + astrocyte end-feet form blood-brain barrier (BBB). Tight junctions allow only small lipid-soluble molecules through. Challenge: most drugs cannot cross BBB — limits treatment of brain tumours, Alzheimer's, meningitis. Solutions: nanocarriers, focused ultrasound to temporarily open BBB, intrathecal injection. Criterion D: patient welfare vs invasive delivery methods.

Set B — Apply
Explain why a fracture in the shaft of a long bone heals but damaged articular cartilage often does not.
Medium
Answer

Bone has blood supply (periosteum → osteoblasts repair fracture). Cartilage is avascular → no osteoblast delivery → limited healing. Cartilage damage is often permanent, requiring surgical intervention or joint replacement.

Vascularity determines healing capacity
How do osteoblasts and osteoclasts work together in bone remodelling?
Medium
Answer

Osteoclasts dissolve old/damaged bone (resorption); osteoblasts secrete new matrix (formation). Balance maintains bone density. Imbalance: osteoporosis (excess resorption). Regulated by PTH, calcitonin, vitamin D, and mechanical load.

Bone is dynamic — continuously remodelled throughout life
Why does compact bone have a Haversian system structure?
Medium
Answer

Blood vessels in central canals supply nutrients/oxygen to osteocytes; concentric lamellae maximise strength by distributing stress evenly. Canaliculi connect lacunae to each other and to the canal for nutrient transfer.

Structural design matches mechanical and metabolic demands
Compare hyaline cartilage and fibrocartilage in terms of structure and function.
Medium
Answer

Hyaline: fine collagen fibres, glassy matrix, covers joint surfaces and trachea. Fibrocartilage: dense collagen bundles, very strong, withstands compression — intervertebral discs, menisci of knee. Hyaline = smooth movement; fibrocartilage = shock absorption.

Different cartilage types serve different mechanical needs
Why does osteoporosis occur more commonly in post-menopausal women?
Medium
Answer

Oestrogen inhibits osteoclast activity; after menopause, oestrogen levels fall → osteoclasts become more active → net bone resorption → reduced bone density → fracture risk especially in vertebrae and hip.

Hormonal influence on bone remodelling
A child with rickets has soft, bowing bones. Explain the cause at the tissue level.
Medium
Answer

Vitamin D deficiency → insufficient calcium absorption → inadequate mineralisation of osteoid (organic bone matrix) → bones remain soft. Organic matrix present but mineral component absent → no rigidity.

Mineralisation is essential for bone function
How does the structure of spongy bone reduce weight without sacrificing strength?
Medium
Answer

Trabeculae (struts) are aligned along lines of stress (Wolff's law) → resist compressive forces efficiently. Air spaces between trabeculae reduce mass. Red marrow in spaces serves haematopoietic function.

Engineering principles in biological structures
Explain how a bone grows in length during childhood.
Medium
Answer

Epiphyseal growth plate (cartilage at bone ends): chondrocytes divide, expand → zone of ossification below replaces cartilage with bone → bone elongates. Growth plate fuses at puberty (epiphyseal closure) → growth stops.

Endochondral ossification = bone growth in length
Why is the periosteum important in bone fracture repair?
Medium
Answer

Periosteum contains osteoblast precursors and blood vessels → activated after fracture → form callus (soft cartilage → then hard bone) → remodelled to original shape over months.

Periosteum is the bone's repair tissue
Compare the extracellular matrix composition of bone vs cartilage.
Medium
Answer

Bone: collagen + hydroxyapatite (calcium phosphate crystals) → rigid. Cartilage: collagen + proteoglycans (attract water) → flexible, compressible. Matrix composition determines mechanical properties.

ECM composition = tissue mechanical properties

Explain why elite athletes can have higher bone density than non-athletes and evaluate the limits of this effect.
Hard
Answer

Wolff's Law: bone responds to mechanical stress by increasing density. Weight-bearing exercise stimulates osteoblast activity. Limits: genetic ceiling on density; excessive training in young females → low oestrogen → bone loss (female athlete triad); benefit plateaus; retired athletes lose density. Balance required.

Type II collagen is cartilage's structural fibre → mutation → weak, deformable cartilage → joint deformity (skeletal dysplasia like achondrogenesis). Investigate how microgravity affects bone density in astronauts and evaluate the implications for long space missions.
Hard
Answer

Microgravity removes mechanical load on bones → osteoclasts dominate → 1–2% density loss per month (lumbar spine, femur most affected). Countermeasures: 2h/day resistive exercise, vibration plates, bisphosphonates (debated). Implication: Mars mission (3 years) risks severe osteoporosis → fracture on arrival. Criterion D: space exploration ethics.

Space biology with real-world application
Compare stem cell therapy and joint replacement surgery for cartilage repair. Evaluate scientific, social, and ethical dimensions.
Hard
Answer

Stem cell: inject mesenchymal stem cells → differentiate into chondrocytes → biological repair; avoids surgery; early-stage only; expensive; unproven long-term. Joint replacement: reliable, proven, but irreversible, requires surgery, implant lifespan ~20 years. Social: age, activity level determine choice. Ethical: access equity (cost of stem cell therapy).

Medical decision-making with multiple perspectives
How does the structure of intervertebral discs protect the spinal cord, and what happens when a disc herniates?
Hard
Answer

Nucleus pulposus (gel-like fibrocartilage core) absorbs axial compression. Annulus fibrosus (concentric fibrocartilage rings) contains nucleus. Herniation: annulus tears → nucleus pulposus bulges → compresses spinal nerve roots → pain, numbness (sciatica if L4/L5). Prevention: core muscle strength, posture. Treatment: physiotherapy or surgery.

Applied anatomy with clinical relevance
Analyse the ethics of using animal bone grafts (xenografts) in human bone repair surgeries.
Hard
Answer

Bovine and porcine bone xenografts are used as scaffolds after demineralisation (removes cells, leaves mineral structure). Benefits: abundant supply, avoids donor site morbidity of autograft. Risks: disease transmission (BSE risk with bovine), immune rejection. Ethical: religious objections (pork), animal welfare. Regulatory: sterilisation protocols required. Criterion D.

Bioethics and biomaterials
Predict what would happen to the skeleton if the pH of body fluids dropped significantly (acidosis).
Hard
Answer

Bone acts as pH buffer: in acidosis, osteoclasts dissolve hydroxyapatite → releases carbonate/phosphate ions → neutralises acid. Consequence: chronic acidosis (kidney disease) → sustained bone resorption → osteomalacia/osteoporosis. Treatment: correct underlying acidosis (dialysis, bicarbonate). Shows bone's metabolic role beyond structure.

Set B — Apply
Explain why a person with anaemia feels tired and breathless.
Medium
Answer

Anaemia = low RBC count or haemoglobin → reduced oxygen-carrying capacity → tissues receive less O₂ → cells rely on anaerobic respiration → less ATP → fatigue. Breathlessness = compensatory increased breathing rate.

Oxygen delivery chain connects to symptoms
How does the absence of a nucleus in mature RBCs relate to their function and lifespan?
Medium
Answer

No nucleus → more space for haemoglobin → higher O₂ carrying capacity. Trade-off: cannot repair DNA or replace worn proteins → lifespan only 120 days → must be continuously replaced in red bone marrow.

Structural trade-off: function vs longevity
Compare the roles of neutrophils and lymphocytes in immunity.
Medium
Answer

Neutrophils: innate immunity, first responders, engulf and destroy bacteria by phagocytosis. Lymphocytes: adaptive immunity — B cells produce antibodies; T cells destroy infected cells. Neutrophils are non-specific; lymphocytes are highly specific.

Two-arm immune system: innate vs adaptive
Why is adipose tissue important beyond energy storage?
Medium
Answer

Thermal insulation (subcutaneous fat reduces heat loss); mechanical cushioning (protects kidneys, eyeballs); endocrine function (secretes leptin — regulates appetite; oestrogen conversion in post-menopausal women).

Adipose has metabolic and endocrine roles
Explain the role of fibrinogen in blood clotting.
Medium
Answer

Fibrinogen (soluble plasma protein) → activated by thrombin → forms insoluble fibrin threads → mesh traps platelets → clot forms over wound. Vitamin K needed for clotting factor synthesis.

Coagulation cascade: fibrinogen is the final substrate
How does plasma composition change during digestion compared to fasting?
Medium
Answer

After eating: elevated glucose, amino acids, triglycerides, insulin. Fasting: lower glucose, elevated glucagon, ketone bodies from fat metabolism. Plasma composition reflects metabolic state.

Plasma is a dynamic reflection of body metabolism
A patient has a white blood cell count of 50,000/μL (normal: 4,000–11,000). What might this indicate?

Leucocytosis suggests active infection, inflammation, or leukaemia (cancer of WBC precursors). Context and WBC differential (which type elevated) guides diagnosis.

Efficiency: one system serves all transport needs; blood reaches every capillary bed in the body; hormones require rapid systemic delivery; evolutionary simplicity. Separate systems would require additional energy and infrastructure.

Explain how areolar tissue connects epithelium to deeper structures.
