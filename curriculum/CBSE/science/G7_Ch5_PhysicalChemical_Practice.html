Set A Recall & Recognition — Easy

What is a physical change? Easy
Show Answer

A physical change alters the form or appearance of a substance but does not produce a new substance. The composition remains the same.
Is melting ice a physical or chemical change? Explain why. Easy
Show Answer

Physical change. Ice (H₂O solid) melts into water (H₂O liquid). No new substance is formed; only the state changes.
Name THREE examples of physical changes you can observe at home. Easy
Show Answer

Examples: melting butter, tearing paper, dissolving sugar in water, cutting vegetables, freezing juice into ice pops.
What does "reversible" mean in the context of physical changes? Easy
Show Answer

Reversible means the change can be undone and the original substance can be recovered. E.g., melted ice can refreeze back into ice.
Does the mass of a substance change during a physical change? Easy
Show Answer

No. The mass stays the same because no atoms are gained or lost; only the arrangement or state changes.
Which of the following is a physical change: (a) burning wood, (b) dissolving salt in water, (c) cooking an egg? Easy
Show Answer

(b) Dissolving salt in water is a physical change. The salt can be recovered by evaporating the water. No new substance is formed.
What happens to the chemical composition during a physical change? Easy
Show Answer

The chemical composition stays exactly the same. The molecules are rearranged or change state but are not converted into different molecules.
Is folding a piece of paper a physical change? Give a reason. Easy
Show Answer

Yes. Folding only changes the shape; no new substance is formed and the paper's chemical composition is unchanged.
Name the process by which a liquid changes to a gas. Is it a physical change? Easy
Show Answer

Evaporation (or boiling). Yes, it is a physical change because the chemical formula of the substance remains the same (e.g., H₂O).
Give one example of a physical change that is NOT easily reversible. Easy
Show Answer

Tearing paper or breaking glass. Although no new substance forms, it is difficult to perfectly restore the original shape.

Set B Application & Understanding — Medium

A student dissolves copper sulphate in water. Is this physical or chemical? How could she recover the copper sulphate? Medium
Show Answer

Physical change. Copper sulphate can be recovered by evaporating the water (evaporation/crystallisation), leaving the original salt behind.
Why is boiling water classified as a physical change even though it involves a lot of energy? Medium
Show Answer

Because the water molecules (H₂O) remain chemically identical. The energy breaks intermolecular bonds, not chemical bonds, so no new substance forms.
Compare the properties of a substance before and after a physical change. Use an example. Medium
Show Answer

Example – Ice vs Water: both are H₂O. Physical properties like shape and state change, but density, chemical formula, and reactivity remain the same substance.
A piece of gold is hammered into a thin sheet. Identify the type of change and justify your answer. Medium
Show Answer

Physical change. Hammering changes the shape of gold but does not alter its chemical identity. It is still gold (Au) after hammering.
Explain why dissolving is considered physical even though the solid seems to "disappear." Medium
Show Answer

The solute particles spread out among solvent particles but are still present as the same substance. Evaporating the solvent restores the original solute, proving no new substance was created.
During condensation, water vapour turns to liquid water. What kind of change is this? What property changes? Medium
Show Answer

Physical change. The state changes from gas to liquid, but the chemical composition (H₂O) is unchanged. The substance is the same.
Salt water was evaporated in a dish. White crystals remained. Is this evidence of a physical change? Explain. Medium
Show Answer

Yes. The salt (NaCl) was recovered unchanged, proving it was only dissolved (physical change) not chemically transformed. The original substance reappeared.
How does the particle arrangement differ between ice, water, and steam? Is there any chemical difference? Medium
Show Answer

Ice: fixed lattice; Water: close, mobile; Steam: far apart, random. There is NO chemical difference — all three are H₂O. Only the physical state and particle arrangement change.
A wire is bent into a coil. Later it is straightened. Classify each step and explain. Medium
Show Answer

Both steps are physical changes. The metal's chemical composition does not change during bending or straightening; only the shape changes. It is reversible.
Name two physical properties that can change without a chemical change occurring. Medium
Show Answer

Examples: shape (e.g., cutting), state of matter (e.g., melting), size (e.g., grinding), colour in some cases (e.g., mixing dyes).

Set C MYP Criterion B & D — Hard / Extended

Design an experiment to prove that dissolving sugar in water is a physical change. Include apparatus, method, and expected results. Hard
Show Answer

Apparatus: Sugar, water, beaker, evaporating dish, Bunsen burner/hot plate, balance.
Method: 1) Weigh sugar. 2) Dissolve in measured water. 3) Evaporate water over gentle heat. 4) Weigh remaining crystals.
Expected results: White sugar crystals reappear; mass equals original sugar mass. This proves no new substance formed — physical change confirmed.
Evaluate the claim: "All physical changes are reversible." Is this always true? Support with evidence. Hard
Show Answer

The claim is mostly but not entirely true. Most physical changes (melting, dissolving) are reversible. However, tearing paper or breaking a glass are physical changes (no new substance) that are practically irreversible. The key criterion for physical change is unchanged chemical composition, not reversibility.
A student claims that mixing flour and water is a physical change but baking the mixture is chemical. Analyse both steps and justify. Hard
Show Answer

Mixing flour and water: Physical — the ingredients retain their chemical identity and could theoretically be separated. Baking: Chemical — heat causes proteins (gluten) to denature, starch to gelatinise, and Maillard reactions to occur; new substances with different properties form and the process is irreversible.
Using particle theory, explain what happens to water molecules when water changes from liquid to steam. Hard
Show Answer

In liquid water, molecules are close together with moderate intermolecular forces. Adding heat increases kinetic energy. When sufficient energy is provided, molecules overcome attractive forces and escape as gas. The H–O–H covalent bonds are NOT broken; only intermolecular hydrogen bonds are overcome. Chemical identity is preserved.
MYP Criterion D: A factory grinds limestone rocks into powder to make cement filler. Is this a physical or chemical change? Discuss TWO implications for industrial use. Hard
Show Answer

Physical change — grinding changes size/shape but not CaCO₃ composition. Implications: 1) The powder has a much greater surface area, making it more reactive in subsequent chemical processes (e.g., cement reactions). 2) Since no new substance forms, the powder retains limestone's chemical properties and can be used as a filler or base for further processing.
Explain why the sublimation of dry ice (solid CO₂ → gas CO₂) is a physical change, even though it looks dramatic. Hard
Show Answer

During sublimation, CO₂ molecules transition directly from solid to gaseous state. The covalent bonds within CO₂ molecules are unbroken; only intermolecular forces are overcome. The chemical formula remains CO₂ throughout. The dramatic fog is condensed water vapour from the air — not a new substance from the CO₂.
Construct a table comparing physical changes across five criteria: new substance, reversibility, composition, energy, example. Hard
Show Answer

Physical Change Table:
New substance: No | Reversibility: Usually yes | Composition: Unchanged | Energy: Absorbed/released (state changes) | Example: Melting ice
These criteria collectively define physical change and distinguish it from chemical change where all criteria would differ.

A 100 g block of wax is melted completely. Predict the mass of liquid wax and explain using the law of conservation of mass. Hard
Show Answer

The mass of liquid wax = 100 g. During melting (physical change), no atoms are created or destroyed. The wax molecules (hydrocarbons) are still present in liquid form; only their arrangement changes. Mass is conserved.
IB MYP context: Discuss how separating a mixture of sand and salt uses physical changes. Name the techniques and explain why each is physical. Hard
Show Answer

1) Dissolving (physical) — salt dissolves in water; sand doesn't. 2) Filtration (physical) — sand is filtered out; no new substance. 3) Evaporation (physical) — water evaporates; salt crystals remain unchanged. Throughout, NaCl and SiO₂ retain their identities. No chemical reactions occur.
Analyse: Is dissolving chalk in acid a physical or chemical change? Compare with dissolving chalk in water. Hard
Show Answer

Dissolving chalk (CaCO₃) in acid is a chemical change — it reacts: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. New substances form and CO₂ gas is released. In contrast, chalk in water barely dissolves and no reaction occurs — closer to a physical change. The key difference is whether the chemical identity changes.

Set A Recall & Recognition — Easy

Define a chemical change in your own words. Easy
Show Answer

A chemical change is a process where one or more substances are converted into one or more new substances with different chemical properties.
Name THREE examples of chemical changes from everyday life. Easy
Show Answer

Burning wood, rusting iron, cooking food, souring of milk, digestion of food.
What are the substances called before a chemical reaction? And after? Easy
Show Answer

Before: Reactants. After: Products.
Is burning paper a physical or chemical change? Why? Easy
Show Answer

Chemical change. Burning paper produces ash, carbon dioxide, and water vapour — completely new substances different from paper. It is irreversible.
Are chemical changes usually reversible or irreversible? Easy
Show Answer

Usually irreversible. Once new substances are formed, they cannot easily be converted back to the original reactants under ordinary conditions.
What happens to atoms during a chemical change? Easy
Show Answer

Atoms are rearranged — old chemical bonds are broken and new bonds are formed. The same atoms are present, just linked differently to form new substances.
Is cooking an egg a chemical change? Give a reason. Easy
Show Answer

Yes. Heat causes proteins in the egg to permanently denature and coagulate, forming new structures. The process is irreversible — a cooked egg cannot become raw again.
What is produced when iron undergoes a chemical change with oxygen and water? Easy
Show Answer

Rust (iron oxide, Fe₂O₃·xH₂O) is produced — a new reddish-brown substance with properties different from iron.
In a chemical reaction, what is meant by the term "new substance"? Easy
Show Answer

A new substance has different chemical properties, different composition, and a different chemical formula from the original reactants.
Name one chemical change that is useful and one that is harmful. Easy
Show Answer

Useful: Baking bread (CO₂ makes it rise). Harmful: Rusting of iron structures (weakens buildings/bridges).

Set B Application & Understanding — Medium

Explain why rusting is a chemical change and not a physical change. Medium
Show Answer

Rusting produces iron oxide (rust), a new substance with a different chemical formula (Fe₂O₃) and different properties (brittle, reddish-brown). The original iron cannot be restored by simple physical means. New chemical bonds are formed.
A student heats baking soda (NaHCO₃) and observes bubbles and a white residue. Is this physical or chemical? Identify the evidence. Medium
Show Answer

Chemical change. Evidence: gas bubbles (CO₂ produced), white residue (Na₂CO₃ formed), and the original baking soda cannot be recovered. New substances are formed.
Compare the properties of iron and rust. How does this difference confirm a chemical change? Medium
Show Answer

Iron: grey, shiny, strong, magnetic, conducts electricity. Rust: reddish-brown, flaky, brittle, non-magnetic, poor conductor. Different chemical formula (Fe vs Fe₂O₃). Completely different properties confirm a chemical change.
Why is digestion of food a chemical change? Name two substances produced. Medium
Show Answer

Enzymes break down large food molecules into smaller molecules with different chemical compositions. E.g., amylase breaks starch → maltose/glucose. Proteins → amino acids. New substances with different properties are formed irreversibly.
A new smell is detected when onions are cut. Is cutting a physical or chemical change? What about when the smell forms? Medium
Show Answer

Cutting is physical (shape changes, composition unchanged). The new smell results from a chemical change — enzymes released when cells are damaged react with amino acid sulfoxides to produce new sulfur compounds (propanethial S-oxide). New substance = chemical change.
Give a word equation for the burning of wood. Classify the change and name the products. Medium
Show Answer

Wood + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Ash (+ energy)
Chemical change. Products (CO₂, H₂O, ash) are completely different substances from wood and oxygen.
How does souring of milk demonstrate a chemical change? Mention the reactants and products. Medium
Show Answer

Bacteria convert lactose (milk sugar) into lactic acid. The milk's pH decreases, proteins denature (curd forms), taste changes. Reactant: lactose; Product: lactic acid. New substance + irreversible = chemical change.
Explain the difference between reactants and products using the example of photosynthesis. Medium
Show Answer

Reactants (CO₂ + H₂O) are the starting materials; Products (glucose + O₂) are the new substances formed. In photosynthesis, simple inorganic molecules are chemically transformed into complex organic sugar and oxygen gas.
Why can't burnt toast be turned back into bread? What does this tell us about the type of change? Medium
Show Answer

Burning toast causes Maillard reactions and pyrolysis — carbohydrates and proteins are converted into carbon, CO₂, and other compounds. The original molecules are destroyed. This irreversibility confirms it is a chemical change.
A silver spoon turns black over time. Is this physical or chemical? What is the black substance? Medium
Show Answer

Chemical change (tarnishing). Silver reacts with hydrogen sulphide (H₂S) in air to form silver sulphide (Ag₂S), a new black substance. This is a chemical reaction producing a new compound.

Set C MYP Criterion B & D — Hard / Extended

Design an investigation to determine whether burning a candle is a physical or chemical change. State a hypothesis, method, and how to interpret results. Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Burning a candle is a chemical change because new substances will form.
Method: 1) Hold a cold dry glass over the flame — observe condensation (water). 2) Hold limewater test tube above flame — check if it turns milky (CO₂). 3) Note soot deposits (carbon).
Interpretation: Water, CO₂, and carbon are products not present in the original wax. New substances = chemical change confirmed. Irreversibility further supports this.
A classmate argues: "Melting chocolate is the same type of change as burning chocolate." Evaluate this argument scientifically. Hard
Show Answer

This argument is incorrect. Melting: physical change — chocolate changes state but retains its chemical composition; it re-solidifies on cooling. Burning: chemical change — organic molecules react with O₂ to produce CO₂, H₂O, and char; irreversible, new substances formed. The key distinction is whether chemical bonds within molecules are broken and rearranged.
Analyse the chemical changes that occur during cement setting. What substances react and what new substance forms? Hard
Show Answer

Cement (calcium silicates + aluminates) reacts with water in a hydration reaction. New calcium silicate hydrate crystals form, interlocking to create a hard solid. This is irreversible — cement cannot return to powder once set. New crystalline substances with different properties confirm a chemical change.
Compare burning and rusting of iron as chemical changes. How do they differ in rate and conditions required? Hard
Show Answer

Both are oxidation reactions producing new compounds. Burning: rapid, requires ignition temperature, produces Fe₂O₃ and releases heat/light. Rusting: slow (days-years), requires moisture + oxygen, produces Fe₂O₃·xH₂O. Both are chemical changes but differ in activation energy, rate, and conditions (moisture not needed for burning).
Using the concept of bond breaking and bond forming, explain why chemical changes release or absorb energy. Hard
Show Answer

Breaking existing bonds requires energy (endothermic). Forming new bonds releases energy (exothermic). If energy released (forming products) > energy absorbed (breaking reactants) → overall exothermic reaction (e.g., combustion). If energy absorbed > released → endothermic (e.g., photosynthesis). The energy difference drives the reaction.
MYP: Evaluate the environmental impact of ONE chemical change that occurs in industry. Consider reactants, products, and sustainability. Hard
Show Answer

Example — Combustion of coal: Coal (C) + O₂ → CO₂ + SO₂ + energy. CO₂ contributes to greenhouse effect/climate change; SO₂ causes acid rain. Sustainability concern: finite resource; products are pollutants. Alternative: renewable energy avoids this chemical change. IB link: responsible chemistry requires evaluating environmental consequences of industrial processes.
Write a word equation for the reaction of vinegar (acetic acid) with baking soda. Identify reactants, products, and signs of chemical change. Hard
Show Answer

Acetic acid + Sodium bicarbonate → Sodium acetate + Water + Carbon dioxide
Signs: vigorous bubbling (CO₂ gas), temperature drop (endothermic), fizzing. Reactants: CH₃COOH + NaHCO₃. Products: CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂. New substances formed = chemical change.
A student observes that after mixing two clear solutions, a yellow solid forms at the bottom and the temperature drops. Analyse all evidence of chemical change. Hard
Show Answer

Evidence: 1) Precipitate (yellow solid) = new insoluble substance formed. 2) Temperature drop = endothermic reaction (absorbs heat). 3) Colour change (clear → yellow solid). Multiple indicators confirm a chemical change. Likely reaction: e.g., Pb(NO₃)₂ + KI → PbI₂ (yellow precipitate) + KNO₃.
Discuss the difference between reversible and irreversible chemical reactions. Give one example of each and their industrial relevance. Hard
Show Answer

Irreversible: Combustion (wood + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O) — cannot be reversed under normal conditions. Industrial: energy production. Reversible: Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃) — conditions adjusted to favour product. Industrial: ammonia for fertilisers. Understanding reversibility allows chemists to optimise yield and efficiency.
Extended response: A banana ripens over several days and turns brown. Classify the changes occurring and explain the chemistry involved. Hard
Show Answer

Ripening involves multiple chemical changes: 1) Starch → sugars (enzyme amylase activity) — sweeter taste. 2) Chlorophyll breaks down → reveals yellow carotenoids — colour change. 3) Browning: enzyme polyphenol oxidase reacts phenolic compounds with O₂ → brown melanins. All steps produce new substances, are irreversible, and involve enzyme-catalysed chemical reactions. Physical changes (softening) also occur as cell walls break down.

List SIX signs that indicate a chemical reaction has occurred. Easy
Show Answer

1) Colour change, 2) Gas produced (bubbles), 3) Precipitate formed (solid from solution), 4) Temperature change (heat released or absorbed), 5) Light produced, 6) New smell/odour produced.
What is a precipitate? Give one example. Easy
Show Answer

A precipitate is an insoluble solid that forms when two solutions are mixed. Example: mixing lead nitrate and potassium iodide solutions produces a yellow precipitate of lead iodide (PbI₂).
When vinegar is added to baking soda, bubbles appear. What gas is produced? Is this a chemical reaction? Easy
Show Answer

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is produced. Yes, this is a chemical reaction — acetic acid reacts with sodium bicarbonate to produce CO₂, water, and sodium acetate (new substances).
A reaction releases heat and light. Name this type of reaction. Give an example. Easy
Show Answer

Combustion reaction. Example: burning a matchstick produces heat and light as wood reacts with oxygen.
What colour change occurs when iron rusts? Is this a sign of a chemical reaction? Easy
Show Answer

Iron turns from grey/silver to reddish-brown (rust/iron oxide). Yes, this colour change is a sign of a chemical reaction.
How does a temperature change indicate a chemical reaction has occurred? Easy
Show Answer

If the surroundings become warmer, the reaction released energy (exothermic). If the surroundings become cooler, the reaction absorbed energy (endothermic). Either way, an energy change indicates new bonds are forming/breaking.
A clear liquid turns blue when tested with iodine solution. What does this indicate? Is it a chemical reaction? Easy
Show Answer

The blue-black colour indicates the presence of starch. The colour change from clear to blue is a sign of a chemical reaction between iodine and starch molecules (forming a starch-iodine complex).
A new smell is noticed when leaves decompose in a compost bin. What type of change is this? Easy
Show Answer

Chemical change. New substances (organic acids, gases like ammonia and methane) are formed by microbial action, producing the distinctive smell.
Is all bubbling evidence of a chemical reaction? Give an example where bubbles appear but NO chemical change occurs. Easy
Show Answer

No. Boiling water produces bubbles (water vapour) but this is a physical change — no new substance forms. Chemical bubbling (e.g., acid + carbonate → CO₂) involves a new gas substance.
Name the sign of a chemical reaction seen when a photographic film is exposed to light. Easy
Show Answer

Light causes a colour change/darkening of the silver halide on film (Ag⁺ → Ag metal), which is a sign of a chemical reaction triggered by light (photochemical reaction).

A student mixes two colourless solutions and a white solid forms. Name the sign of chemical reaction and suggest what the white solid might be. Medium
Show Answer

Sign: precipitate formation. The white solid could be barium sulphate (BaSO₄), calcium carbonate (CaCO₃), or silver chloride (AgCl) depending on the reagents used. A precipitate is direct evidence of a new insoluble substance forming.

When copper carbonate is heated, it turns black. Identify the sign of chemical reaction and name the black product. Medium

Sign: colour change (green → black). Black product: copper oxide (CuO). Equation: CuCO₃ → CuO + CO₂. The colour change and gas evolution both confirm a chemical reaction.
A student adds zinc to hydrochloric acid. Describe what she would observe and name TWO signs of reaction. Medium

Observations: vigorous bubbling and warmth. Signs: 1) Gas produced (hydrogen — confirmed by squeaky pop test), 2) Temperature increase (exothermic). Equation: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂.
Explain why a colour change alone is not always sufficient proof of a chemical reaction. Medium
Show Answer

Colour changes can occur in physical processes too — e.g., mixing food dyes, dissolving coloured salts. To confirm a chemical reaction, at least one other sign should be present (e.g., gas, precipitate, irreversibility, or temperature change), confirming a new substance has formed.
A sparkler burns producing bright white light, heat, and white smoke. Identify THREE signs of chemical reaction from this observation. Medium
Show Answer

1) Light produced (combustion/oxidation of metals like magnesium/aluminium). 2) Heat produced (exothermic). 3) White smoke (solid metal oxide particles = new substance). All three confirm a chemical reaction.
How would you distinguish between a physical change and a chemical change using only the sign of gas production? Medium
Show Answer

Physical: Gas produced is the same substance as the starting material (e.g., steam from boiling water = H₂O). Chemical: Gas produced is a different substance (e.g., CO₂ from acid + carbonate). Test the gas with appropriate reagents (limewater for CO₂, burning splint for H₂) to confirm new substance.
Magnesium burns with a bright white flame. Name the product and the signs of reaction observed. Medium
Show Answer

Product: Magnesium oxide (MgO) — white powder. Signs: Bright white light, intense heat (temperature increase), white solid residue (new substance). Equation: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO.
A reaction produces a gas with a pungent smell. How would you test to confirm the gas is ammonia? Medium
Show Answer

Hold moist red litmus paper near the gas. Ammonia (basic) turns red litmus paper blue. Alternatively, damp pH paper would turn alkaline (pH > 7). The pungent smell itself is also characteristic of NH₃.

Evaluate: Can a chemical reaction occur without any visible signs? Give an example and scientific reasoning. Hard
Show Answer

Yes. Some reactions produce no visible precipitate, no gas, no colour change, and minimal temperature change. Example: neutralisation of a strong acid with a strong base (HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O). The temperature change is small and no visible signs appear. Confirmation requires pH measurement or conductivity testing. This shows that absence of visible signs ≠ absence of reaction.
A classmate says "bubbles always mean a chemical reaction." Construct a scientific argument to challenge this claim with two counter-examples. Hard
Show Answer

Counter-example 1: Boiling water — bubbles of water vapour (H₂O → H₂O gas) = physical change, same substance. Counter-example 2: Carbonated drinks fizzing when opened — dissolved CO₂ escaping = physical change (pressure reduction), no new substance. Bubbles in chemical reactions produce a chemically different gas from the reactants; bubbles in physical changes produce the same substance in gaseous form.
Using particle theory, explain why a precipitate forms when two ionic solutions are mixed. Hard
Show Answer

When two ionic solutions mix, certain ion combinations are insoluble in water. The attractive forces between these specific ions (e.g., Pb²⁺ and I⁻) exceed the hydration energy that keeps them dissolved. These ion pairs aggregate and settle as a solid crystalline precipitate. The new ionic lattice structure constitutes a new chemical substance.
MYP extended: A forensic scientist uses signs of chemical reactions to identify unknown substances at a crime scene. Describe THREE tests she might use and what each would reveal. Hard
Show Answer

1) Flame test — different metal ions produce characteristic colours (Na → yellow, K → lilac, Cu → green-blue), identifying metal content via light emission. 2) Acid test — adding HCl; carbonates bubble (CO₂), identifying carbonate minerals. 3) Biuret test — purple colour with protein-containing substances. Each test relies on specific chemical reactions producing observable signs (colour, gas, precipitate).
Analyse why a temperature increase does not always mean combustion is occurring. Name TWO other reactions that produce heat. Hard
Show Answer

Temperature increase indicates an exothermic reaction, not specifically combustion. Other exothermic reactions: 1) Neutralisation (acid + base → salt + water; releases ~57 kJ/mol). 2) Rusting (slow oxidation of iron — hand warmers use this). 3) Dissolving calcium chloride in water. Combustion specifically requires rapid oxidation with flame/light, while other exothermic reactions may have none of these signs.
A student observes these changes simultaneously: colour change, gas production, and temperature rise. What conclusion can she draw? Explain why multiple signs strengthen the conclusion. Hard
Show Answer

She can confidently conclude a chemical reaction has occurred. Multiple signs strengthen the conclusion because: each sign individually could have an alternative explanation, but multiple simultaneous signs dramatically reduce the probability of a non-chemical explanation. The convergence of evidence (colour change = new substance; gas = new substance; temperature rise = bond energy change) provides reliable confirmation.
Compare the signs of chemical reactions for: (a) combustion of magnesium, (b) decomposition of hydrogen peroxide, and (c) formation of a precipitate. Hard
Show Answer

(a) Mg combustion: bright white light, intense heat, white MgO solid formed — signs: light, temperature rise, new substance colour change. (b) H₂O₂ decomposition (with MnO₂): vigorous O₂ bubbling, slight warming — signs: gas, temperature change. (c) Precipitate: instant colour change, solid forms in clear solution — sign: precipitate, possibly colour change. Each reaction has a unique combination of signs.
IB MYP context: Explain how the signs of chemical reactions are exploited in a breathalyser test for alcohol. Hard
Show Answer

A breathalyser contains potassium dichromate (orange) in acidic solution. Alcohol (ethanol) in breath reacts: ethanol is oxidised to acetic acid, and dichromate (Cr₂O₇²⁻, orange) is reduced to chromium(III) (Cr³⁺, green). Sign used: colour change (orange → green). The degree of colour change is proportional to alcohol concentration, allowing quantitative measurement. This exploits a specific chemical reaction sign for practical detection.
Construct a flow chart showing how to systematically use signs of chemical reactions to determine if an unknown reaction is chemical or physical. Hard
Show Answer

Flow chart steps: Start → Observe colour change? (Yes = likely chemical) → Check for gas production? Test with limewater/splint. (Yes = chemical) → Check temperature change? Use thermometer. (Yes = likely chemical) → Check for precipitate? (Yes = chemical) → Check for new smell? (Yes = chemical) → If ALL No → Test reversibility: can original substance be recovered? (Yes = physical; No = chemical). Multiple signs increase confidence. Document all observations before concluding.

Set A Recall & Recognition — Easy

What is a combination reaction? Write its general form. Easy
Show Answer

Two or more substances combine to form one new substance. A + B → AB. Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.

What is a decomposition reaction? Write its general form. Easy
Show Answer

A single compound breaks into two or more simpler substances. AB → A + B. Example: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂.

In a displacement reaction, what happens to the more reactive element? Easy
Show Answer

It displaces the less reactive element from its compound. Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu (Zn displaces Cu).

What is a double displacement reaction? Give an example. Easy
Show Answer

Two compounds exchange ions: AB + CD → AD + CB. Example: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃.

Write the word equation for combustion of methane. Easy
Show Answer

Methane + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy. CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O.

Classify: Iron + sulphur → iron sulphide. Easy

What is an exothermic reaction? Give one example. Easy
Show Answer

An exothermic reaction releases energy (as heat) to the surroundings. The surroundings become warmer. Example: combustion of wood — wood + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + heat.

What is an endothermic reaction? Give one example. Easy
Show Answer

An endothermic reaction absorbs energy from the surroundings. The surroundings become cooler. Example: photosynthesis — CO₂ + H₂O + light energy → glucose + O₂.

In an exothermic reaction, is energy released to the surroundings or absorbed from them? Easy
Show Answer

Released to the surroundings. The system loses energy; surroundings gain energy (become warmer).

Name THREE exothermic reactions from everyday life. Easy

How can you tell if a reaction is exothermic or endothermic using a thermometer? Easy

Exothermic: temperature of the reaction mixture RISES (heat released to surroundings). Endothermic: temperature FALLS (heat absorbed from surroundings).

What is activation energy? Is it the same for exothermic and endothermic reactions? Easy

Activation energy is the minimum energy needed to start a reaction. Both exothermic and endothermic reactions require activation energy. The difference is in the overall energy change — not the initial energy needed to begin.

Is cellular respiration exothermic or endothermic? Why is this important for living organisms? Easy

Is photosynthesis exothermic or endothermic? Where does the energy come from? Easy

Endothermic. Energy from sunlight (light energy) is absorbed by chlorophyll and converted to chemical energy stored in glucose molecules.

Carbon cycle: Photosynthesis (endothermic, absorbs CO₂) ↔ Respiration/combustion (exothermic, releases CO₂). Natural balance: rates approximately equal → stable atmospheric CO₂ → stable greenhouse effect → stable temperature. Human disruption: fossil fuel combustion adds CO₂ faster than photosynthesis absorbs → CO₂ accumulation → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming. Deforestation reduces photosynthesis capacity further. Restoring balance requires both reducing combustion (exothermic CO₂ sources) and increasing photosynthesis/carbon capture (endothermic CO₂ sinks).

Extended: A student measures heat evolved in a neutralisation reaction using a polystyrene cup calorimeter. Initial temp 20°C, final temp 26.8°C, 100 mL solution used. Calculate heat evolved (assume density = 1 g/mL, specific heat capacity = 4.18 J/g°C). Hard
Show Answer

ΔT = 26.8 − 20 = 6.8°C. Mass = 100 g. q = mcΔT = 100 × 4.18 × 6.8 = 2842.4 J = 2.84 kJ. The reaction is exothermic (temperature rose). To find molar enthalpy: need to know moles of reactants used. If 0.05 mol HCl used: ΔH per mol = −2.84/0.05 = −56.8 kJ/mol (close to theoretical −57.1 kJ/mol for strong acid + strong base neutralisation). Negative sign: exothermic.

Discuss Hess's Law. How does it relate to the principle that enthalpy is a state function? Give a practical example. Hard
Show Answer

Hess's Law: total enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the route taken — depends only on initial and final states. This is because enthalpy is a state function (path-independent). Practical: cannot directly measure ΔH of C + ½O₂ → CO. Instead: measure ΔH for (1) C + O₂ → CO₂ (−393 kJ) and (2) CO + ½O₂ → CO₂ (−283 kJ). By Hess: ΔH(CO formation) = −393 − (−283) = −110 kJ/mol. Allows calculation of ΔH for reactions that cannot be directly measured.

IB MYP: Evaluate how understanding exo/endothermic reactions enables drug design for metabolic diseases. Hard
Show Answer

Many metabolic disorders involve dysregulated enzymatic reactions. Understanding ΔH of reactions allows: 1) Designing drugs that inhibit exothermic catabolic reactions (e.g., statins interfere with cholesterol biosynthesis by blocking an enzyme in the mevalonate pathway). 2) Designing drugs that enhance energy-requiring (endothermic) pathways. 3) Thermogenic drugs for obesity treatment target cellular respiration uncouplers to increase heat production. 4) Cancer metabolism: cancer cells preferentially use aerobic glycolysis (Warburg effect) — targeting this metabolic pathway requires understanding the energetics of glucose oxidation pathways.

Why do some exothermic reactions require heating to start, while others proceed spontaneously at room temperature? Explain using activation energy concept. Hard
Show Answer

All reactions require activation energy to initiate. Room temperature reactions (e.g., acid + zinc) have low activation energy — thermal energy at room temperature is sufficient for enough molecules to react. Reactions requiring heating (e.g., combustion of wood, decomposition of CaCO₃) have high activation energy — molecules need additional energy (from heating, sparks, etc.) to overcome the energy barrier. Once started, exothermic reactions often self-sustain (released energy provides activation energy for subsequent molecules). Endothermic reactions need continuous energy input.

Construct an energy level diagram for the reaction C + O₂ → CO₂ (ΔH = −393 kJ/mol). Indicate: reactants, products, activation energy, transition state, and ΔH. Explain each feature. Hard

Diagram: Y-axis = energy. Reactants (C + O₂) at a high energy position. Arrow up to transition state (highest point = activation energy). Arrow down to products (CO₂) at lower energy than reactants. ΔH = −393 kJ/mol shown as the energy difference between reactants and products (downward arrow). Features: 1) Activation energy = initial energy barrier (provided by spark/ignition). 2) Transition state = unstable intermediate at maximum energy. 3) Products lower than reactants = exothermic. 4) ΔH (negative) = energy released to surroundings.

Hydrolysis of starch → maltose → glucose. Enzyme: amylase (in saliva and pancreatic juice). Starch (polysaccharide) is broken into glucose monomers by adding water molecules across glycosidic bonds.

How does a battery produce electricity? What type of chemical reaction occurs? Easy

A battery converts chemical energy to electrical energy through redox (oxidation-reduction) reactions. The anode oxidises (loses electrons) and the cathode reduces (gains electrons). Electron flow through the external circuit = electrical current.

What type of chemical reaction occurs when a photograph is developed? Easy
Show Answer

Photochemical reaction. Light causes silver halide (AgBr) on film to decompose: AgBr → Ag (dark) + Br⁻. Areas exposed to more light have more Ag metal deposited = darker image. This is a photodecomposition reaction.

Is cooking food generally a reversible or irreversible chemical change? Give an example. Easy

Irreversible. Example: cooking an egg — proteins denature irreversibly; a cooked egg cannot become raw again. New substances form with permanently different properties.

What is fermentation? Write a word equation for fermentation of glucose by yeast. Easy

Fermentation is the anaerobic breakdown of sugars by yeast/bacteria. Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide (+ energy). C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂. Used in making bread, beer, wine, yoghurt.

Name THREE chemical reactions that occur in the human body daily. Easy

What type of chemical reaction charges and discharges a rechargeable battery? Easy
Show Answer

Reversible redox reactions. Discharging: spontaneous redox (chemical → electrical energy). Charging: non-spontaneous redox (electrical → chemical energy, reverse reaction). Example: Li-ion battery uses lithium intercalation reactions.

Set B Application & Understanding — Medium

Explain the chemistry of baking powder and why it makes cakes rise when heated. Write a chemical equation. Medium
Show Answer

Baking powder = sodium bicarbonate + cream of tartar (or sodium bicarbonate + citric acid). When heated: NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂. CO₂ gas produced expands the batter, causing the cake to rise. The porous structure sets when proteins/starches gelatinise, trapping air pockets permanently.

Why does caramelisation occur in cooking? What chemical changes happen to sugar when heated? Medium
Show Answer

Caramelisation: pyrolysis of sugars at 160-180°C. Sucrose decomposes, loses water (dehydration), and forms hundreds of new compounds including caramel (brown polymers of furanose rings) with complex sweet/bitter flavour. Chemical: C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ → caramel compounds + H₂O. Irreversible chemical change — new substances with different colour, smell, and taste.

Explain how digestion of proteins involves chemical reactions. Name the enzyme and the reaction type. Medium
Show Answer

Proteins = polymers of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. Digestion: hydrolysis (water added across peptide bonds). Enzymes (proteases — pepsin in stomach, trypsin/chymotrypsin in small intestine) catalyse: Protein + H₂O → amino acids. This is a decomposition/hydrolysis reaction — large molecule broken into monomers.

Describe the electrochemical reactions in a zinc-carbon battery during discharge. Which electrode is oxidised and which is reduced? Medium
Show Answer

Anode (zinc): Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidised). Cathode (carbon/MnO₂): MnO₂ + H₂O + e⁻ → MnOOH + OH⁻ (reduced). Electrons flow through external circuit (electrical current). NH₄Cl electrolyte allows ion flow. Overall: Zn provides electrons that reduce MnO₂.

How does souring of milk involve chemical reactions? What microorganism is responsible and what is produced? Medium
Show Answer

Lactobacillus bacteria convert lactose (milk sugar) to lactic acid (fermentation): C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ → 2C₃H₆O₃. Lactic acid lowers pH → milk proteins (casein) denature and coagulate (curd). Taste changes (sour). Chemical change: new substance (lactic acid) formed. This same process is controlled in making yoghurt and cheese.

Explain how antacid tablets work to neutralise stomach acid. Write a chemical equation. Medium
Show Answer

Stomach acid = hydrochloric acid (HCl). Antacids contain weak bases: CaCO₃, Mg(OH)₂, NaHCO₃. Neutralisation: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. pH increases (less acidic). Double displacement/neutralisation reaction — acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂. Burping is the CO₂ produced.

How do fireworks produce specific colours? Connect to what you know about flame tests in chemistry. Medium
Show Answer

Metal salts in fireworks are combusted at high temperatures. Metal ions absorb thermal energy, their electrons are excited to higher energy levels. When electrons return to ground state, they emit photons of specific wavelengths (colours). Same principle as flame tests: Na = yellow, K = lilac, Sr = red, Ba = green, Cu = blue. Different metals = different electron energy gaps = different photon energies = different colours.

Explain the chemistry of food preservation using vinegar (pickling). What chemical property of vinegar prevents microbial growth? Medium
Show Answer

Vinegar is acetic acid (CH₃COOH, pH ~2.4). Acid environment: 1) Denatures microbial enzymes (protein structure disrupted at low pH). 2) Disrupts cell membrane function. 3) Most bacteria cannot survive below pH 4. Pickling creates a chemically hostile environment through the sustained chemical property of acidity — a form of chemical food preservation.

Why do glow sticks produce cold light? What reaction is occurring inside? Medium
Show Answer

Chemiluminescence: chemical reaction produces excited electronic states that emit light without heat. Inside: hydrogen peroxide + diphenyl oxalate ester + fluorescent dye (in separate compartments). When mixed: H₂O₂ + phenyl oxalate → two phenol molecules + peroxyacid intermediate → decomposes, releasing energy. This energy excites dye molecules → emit light. No heat produced (cold light). Chemical energy → light energy directly.

Describe how the chemistry of photography has changed from film to digital cameras, focusing on the chemical reactions involved. Medium
Show Answer

Film photography: AgBr on film decomposes with light (AgBr → Ag + Br⁻). Chemical development amplifies the latent image using reducing agents. Digital cameras: no chemical reactions — photons hit silicon photodiodes, generating electron-hole pairs (photoelectric effect, physics not chemistry). Digital has replaced film because no chemicals needed, instant results, no processing. Film photography is a fascinating chemical-physical hybrid process now largely historical.

Explain the biochemistry of digestion and cellular respiration as a connected sequence of chemical reactions converting food to ATP energy. Hard
Show Answer

Digestion → Respiration pipeline: 1) Starch → glucose (amylase-catalysed hydrolysis). 2) Proteins → amino acids (protease hydrolysis). 3) Fats → fatty acids + glycerol (lipase hydrolysis). 4) Glucose absorbed into bloodstream. 5) Glycolysis: glucose → pyruvate + 2ATP (cytoplasm, anaerobic). 6) Krebs cycle: pyruvate → CO₂ + NADH (mitochondria). 7) Oxidative phosphorylation: NADH + O₂ → H₂O + ~34 ATP (electron transport chain). Analyse the photochemical reactions in the human eye that allow vision. What chemical changes enable you to see light? Hard
Show Answer

Rhodopsin (rod cells): protein opsin bound to 11-cis-retinal (vitamin A derivative). Light absorption: 11-cis-retinal undergoes photoisomerisation → all-trans-retinal (shape change). This conformational change activates opsin → triggers nerve impulse. Recovery: all-trans-retinal converted back to 11-cis-retinal by enzymes (requires energy). This light-triggered cis-trans isomerisation is the primary photochemical reaction of vision — one photon can trigger the detection of one molecule. This is why vitamin A deficiency causes night blindness — insufficient retinal for rhodopsin synthesis.

Discuss the chemistry of cooking meat and explain the Maillard reaction in molecular detail. Why does temperature matter? Hard

Extended: How does the chemistry of carbon dioxide in sparkling drinks relate to equilibrium? What happens when a bottle is opened? Hard
Show Answer

Dissolved CO₂ in water: CO₂(g) ⇌ CO₂(aq) ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Under pressure in sealed bottle: equilibrium favours dissolved CO₂ (Henry's law: solubility ∝ pressure). Opening bottle: pressure drops suddenly. Le Chatelier: equilibrium shifts LEFT (toward gas). CO₂ rapidly comes out of solution as bubbles (nucleation at glass surface imperfections). CO₂ also forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) giving acidic taste (pH ~3.5). This is why flat warm drink tastes different — lower CO₂ concentration (temperature decreases solubility) and different pH.
