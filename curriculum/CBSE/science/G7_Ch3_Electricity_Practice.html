Electric Charge and Current

Set A Fundamentals — Easy

What type of charge do protons carry?Easy
Show Answer

Protons carry a positive (+) charge.

What type of charge do electrons carry?Easy
Show Answer

Electrons carry a negative (−) charge.

Which particles move through a metal wire to create electric current?Easy
Show Answer

Free electrons move through metal wires to create current.

What is the SI unit of electric charge?Easy
Show Answer

The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C).

What is the SI unit of electric current?Easy
Show Answer

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A).

State the formula connecting current (I), charge (Q) and time (t).Easy
Show Answer

I = Q / t

In which direction does conventional current flow?Easy
Show Answer

Conventional current flows from the positive terminal to the negative terminal of a battery (outside the battery).

What instrument measures electric current in a circuit?Easy
Show Answer

An ammeter measures electric current.

An object gains electrons — does it become positively or negatively charged?Easy
Show Answer

It becomes negatively charged (excess electrons).

10 C of charge flows past a point in 2 s. What is the current?Easy
Show Answer

I = Q/t = 10/2 = 5 A

Set B Application — Medium

Distinguish between conventional current direction and actual electron flow direction.Medium
Show Answer

Electrons flow from negative to positive terminal. Conventional current was defined before electrons were discovered and flows positive to negative — the opposite direction.

A current of 3 A flows for 5 s. How much charge passes a point?Medium
Show Answer

Q = I × t = 3 × 5 = 15 C

60 C flows through a bulb with current 4 A. How long does this take?Medium
Show Answer

t = Q/I = 60/4 = 15 s

Why do metals conduct electricity well while rubber does not?Medium
Show Answer

Metals have free (delocalised) electrons; rubber has all electrons tightly bound, so no charge carriers are available to flow.

Two charges: +3 C and −3 C. Are they attracted or repelled?Medium
Show Answer

Opposite charges attract each other.

Explain what happens to charge when you rub a plastic rod with wool.Medium
Show Answer

Electrons transfer from wool to plastic. The rod becomes negatively charged; the wool becomes positively charged. Total charge is conserved.

A torch uses 0.5 A for 6 minutes. Calculate the total charge that flows.Medium
Show Answer

t = 360 s; Q = 0.5 × 360 = 180 C

Can electric charge be created or destroyed? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

No — the law of conservation of charge states charge can only be transferred, never created or destroyed.

1800 C flows through a wire in 30 minutes. What is the current?Medium
Show Answer

t = 1800 s; I = 1800/1800 = 1 A

Why must an ammeter be connected in series and not in parallel?Medium
Show Answer

An ammeter has very low resistance and must carry the full current to measure it. In parallel it would short-circuit the component and draw enormous current, destroying it.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Hard

[MYP B] Design an experiment to show charge transfer by friction. State hypothesis, variables, and expected results.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Rubbing ebonite with fur transfers electrons to the rod, making it negative.
IV: material used for rubbing. CV: same rod, strokes, humidity. DV: electroscope leaf divergence.
Expected: Ebonite + fur shows greater deflection than ebonite + silk, confirming greater charge transfer.

[MYP D] A student gets slightly different ammeter readings at two points in a series circuit. Evaluate possible sources of error.Hard
Show Answer

Causes: (1) ammeter's own resistance slightly reduces current; (2) poor contact resistance at connections; (3) parallax/zero error in analogue meters. In an ideal series circuit current is identical everywhere — differences indicate measurement error, not a real circuit difference. Digital ammeters with near-zero resistance minimise this.

Charge on one electron = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. How many electrons pass per second when I = 2 A?Hard
Show Answer

n = Q/e = 2 / 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ = 1.25 × 10¹⁹ electrons per second

[MYP D] Compare static electricity and current electricity — give one real-world application of each.Hard
Show Answer

Static: charges accumulate without continuous flow; short-lived discharge; application: photocopiers.
Current: continuous flow in a closed circuit; sustained; application: powering appliances. Key difference: static needs no circuit; current requires a complete closed path.

A thundercloud carries 5 C and discharges in 0.002 s. Calculate the average current and explain the danger.Hard
Show Answer

I = 5/0.002 = 2500 A. This is 25,000× the lethal threshold of ~0.1 A — causes severe burning, cardiac arrest, and instant death if it passes through a human body.

[MYP B] Sphere A: +8 C; Sphere B: neutral. They touch then separate. Predict the charge on each. Justify.Hard
Show Answer

Total charge = 8 C. By conservation of charge and symmetry (identical spheres), each gets +4 C.

I = 0.8 A flows for 1 hour. Calculate: (a) charge in coulombs, (b) number of electrons.Hard
Show Answer

(a) Q = 0.8 × 3600 = 2880 C
(b) n = 2880/1.6×10⁻¹⁹ = 1.8 × 10²² electrons

[MYP D] Why is conventional current defined opposite to electron flow? Is this a problem in modern physics?Hard
Show Answer

Franklin defined it before electrons were discovered. It was kept to avoid rewriting all equations. In modern physics it is not a problem — reversing charge sign and direction simultaneously leaves all observable quantities (force, power, fields) unchanged.

Wire A: 4 A for 10 s. Wire B: 2 A for 25 s. Which transfers more charge?Hard
Show Answer

A: Q = 40 C; B: Q = 50 C. Wire B transfers more charge.

[MYP B] Propose how you would safely measure current through a human body in a classroom demonstration. List all safety precautions.Hard
Show Answer

Setup: 1.5 V battery, current-limiting resistor (≥10 kΩ), digital microammeter, ESD wrist strap in series.
Safety: (1) Max 1.5–3 V; (2) Series resistor limits current below 1 mA; (3) Never connect to mains; (4) Dry hands only; (5) Teacher supervision; (6) Check all connections before switching on.

Voltage and Potential Difference

Set A Fundamentals — Easy

Define voltage (potential difference).Easy
Show Answer

Voltage is the work done per unit charge moving charge between two points: V = W/Q.

What is the SI unit of voltage?Easy
Show Answer

The volt (V).

Write the formula for voltage in terms of work (W) and charge (Q).Easy
Show Answer

V = W / Q

What instrument measures voltage in a circuit?Easy
Show Answer

A voltmeter.

Should a voltmeter be connected in series or in parallel?Easy
Show Answer

In parallel (across the component).

A 9 V battery does 9 J of work for every ___ C of charge.Easy
Show Answer

1 C

If voltage increases and resistance stays the same, what happens to current?Easy
Show Answer

Current increases.

20 J of work is done moving 4 C. What is the potential difference?Easy
Show Answer

V = 20/4 = 5 V

What is the standard mains voltage in India?Easy
Show Answer

230 V (at 50 Hz).

True or False: A higher voltage battery supplies more energy per coulomb.Easy
Show Answer

True. Voltage = energy per coulomb (joules per coulomb).

Set B Application — Medium

5 C is moved through a p.d. of 12 V. How much work is done?Medium
Show Answer

W = VQ = 12 × 5 = 60 J

Explain why a voltmeter has very high resistance.Medium
Show Answer

High resistance means negligible current passes through it, so it does not disturb the circuit and reads the true p.d.

A 6 V battery moves 3 C around a circuit. How much energy does it supply?Medium
Show Answer

W = 6 × 3 = 18 J

What does it mean when two circuit points are at the same potential?Medium
Show Answer

The potential difference between them is zero, so no work is done moving charge between them and no current flows due to that potential alone.

Voltage across a resistor is 15 V; 30 C flows per minute. Calculate the work done per minute.Medium
Show Answer

W = 15 × 30 = 450 J per minute

In the water-pipe analogy, what does voltage correspond to?Medium
Show Answer

Voltage corresponds to water pressure — higher pressure drives more water, just as higher voltage drives more current.

Bulb A has 6 V across it, Bulb B has 0 V, both connected to a 6 V battery. What can you conclude about Bulb B?Medium
Show Answer

Bulb B has zero p.d. across it — it is either short-circuited or disconnected so no current flows through it.

Why is 230 V mains more dangerous than a 9 V battery?Medium
Show Answer

Higher voltage drives proportionally higher current through the body (I = V/R). 230 V can push lethal current (>0.1 A) through the body; 9 V cannot.

360 J of energy moves 12 C between two terminals. What is the p.d.?Medium
Show Answer

V = 360/12 = 30 V

A student connects a voltmeter in series. What problem occurs?Medium
Show Answer

The voltmeter's very high resistance almost stops all circuit current — components won't work, and the voltmeter reads nearly the full supply voltage rather than just one component's share.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Hard

[MYP D] Evaluate the "voltage = water pressure" analogy — two strengths and two limitations.Hard
Show Answer

Strengths: Both "push" flow through a medium; both decrease along the circuit/pipe.
Limitations: Water is material and storable; charge is not; water can flow in open channels but current requires a closed circuit; the source of pressure (gravity/pump) differs from EMF.

A motor lifts 2 kg by 3 m on 12 V supply. What minimum charge must flow? (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

W = mgh = 2×10×3 = 60 J; Q = W/V = 60/12 = 5 C

[MYP B] Plan an investigation to verify that battery voltage equals the sum of voltages across series components.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: V_battery = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ (Kirchhoff's Voltage Law).
Method: Connect 3 resistors in series; measure voltage across each and across the battery with a voltmeter; compare sum.
Expected: Sum of component voltages equals battery voltage — confirms energy conservation.

A battery's EMF is 12 V but terminal voltage drops to 10.8 V under load. Explain the energy implication.Hard
Show Answer

The 1.2 V drop is due to internal resistance — energy is dissipated as heat inside the battery. No real battery delivers its full EMF; losses increase with current, so high-current loads waste more energy internally.

Calculate work done moving 0.5 C through 240 V. Express in joules and calories (1 cal = 4.18 J).Hard
Show Answer

W = 240 × 0.5 = 120 J; in calories: 120/4.18 ≈ 28.7 cal

[MYP D] Why do birds sit safely on high-voltage power lines while humans would be electrocuted? Use potential difference.Hard
Show Answer

Both the bird's feet are at the same potential on one wire — p.d. between its feet ≈ 0 V, so no current flows through its body. A human touching the wire while grounded creates a p.d. of thousands of volts between hand and feet, driving lethal current through the body.

A 1.5 V cell and 4.5 V battery are connected (a) in series same polarity, (b) opposing. Find the net voltage each time.Hard
Show Answer

(a) Same polarity: 1.5 + 4.5 = 6 V
(b) Opposing: 4.5 − 1.5 = 3 V

[MYP B] A student claims "voltage is used up as current flows." Critically evaluate this statement.Hard
Show Answer

The statement is imprecise. Voltage is not consumed — it is energy per unit charge. What is actually dissipated is electrical energy, converted to heat/light/mechanical work in components. Better: "electrical energy supplied per coulomb is converted to other forms across the circuit."

What does the area under a V–Q graph represent?Hard
Show Answer

Area = V × Q = work done (energy transferred) in joules.

[MYP D] A defibrillator delivers 200 J through 2 C of charge. Calculate the voltage and relate it to medical precision.Hard
Show Answer

V = W/Q = 200/2 = 100 V. Precision is critical: too little voltage fails to restart the heart; too much causes additional cardiac damage. Patient chest resistance varies, so energy (not just voltage) is carefully calibrated per patient.

Electrical Resistance

EducationalElectrical Resistance Explained

Set A Fundamentals — Easy

What is electrical resistance?Easy
Show Answer

The opposition to the flow of electric current through a conductor.

What is the SI unit of resistance?Easy
Show Answer

The ohm (Ω).

Write the formula for resistance in terms of voltage and current.Easy
Show Answer

R = V / I

A wire carries 2 A when 10 V is applied. What is its resistance?Easy
Show Answer

R = 10/2 = 5 Ω

Does a thicker wire have more or less resistance than a thin wire (same material, same length)?Easy
Show Answer

Less resistance — more cross-sectional area means more pathways for electrons.

Does a shorter wire have more or less resistance than a longer wire (same material, same thickness)?Easy
Show Answer

Less resistance — fewer electron collisions along the shorter path.

Name one good conductor and one good insulator of electricity.Easy
Show Answer

Conductor: copper. Insulator: rubber.

What happens to the resistance of a metal wire as its temperature increases?Easy
Show Answer

Resistance increases — hotter atoms vibrate more, causing more collisions with electrons.

A resistor has 6 V across it and 3 Ω resistance. What current flows?Easy
Show Answer

I = V/R = 6/3 = 2 A

4 A flows when 8 V is applied. What is the resistance?Easy
Show Answer

R = 8/4 = 2 Ω

Set B Application — Medium

List four factors that affect the resistance of a wire.Medium
Show Answer

(1) Length; (2) Cross-sectional area; (3) Material (resistivity); (4) Temperature

Wire X: 1 m long, 1 mm² area. Wire Y: 2 m long, 2 mm² area. Same material. Which has higher resistance?Medium
Show Answer

R ∝ L/A. X: 1/1=1; Y: 2/2=1. They have equal resistance.

Why is nichrome used in heater coils instead of copper?Medium
Show Answer

Nichrome has high resistivity (generates useful heat) and a high melting point so it survives heater temperatures. Copper's resistance is too low to generate heat efficiently.

A 230 V lamp has 460 Ω resistance when lit. What current flows?Medium
Show Answer

I = 230/460 = 0.5 A

A filament bulb's resistance is much higher when lit than when cold. Why?Medium
Show Answer

The tungsten filament reaches ~2500°C when lit. At this temperature atomic vibrations dramatically increase electron collisions, raising resistance far above its cold value.

Why do home wiring cables use thick copper wire?Medium
Show Answer

Thick copper = low resistance. Minimal energy wasted as heat in cables, and negligible voltage drop ensures appliances receive full supply voltage.

A student doubles length and halves cross-sectional area of a wire. By what factor does resistance change?Medium
Show Answer

R ∝ L/A → new R ∝ 2L/(A/2) = 4L/A. Resistance increases by a factor of 4.

A 12 V battery connects to a component drawing 0.25 A. What is the resistance?Medium
Show Answer

R = 12/0.25 = 48 Ω

What voltage is needed to push 3 A through a 15 Ω resistor?Medium
Show Answer

V = IR = 3 × 15 = 45 V

Semiconductors decrease in resistance as temperature rises. How does this differ from metals and why?Medium
Show Answer

In metals, higher temperature increases vibrations → resistance rises. In semiconductors, higher temperature liberates more charge carriers from bonds → resistance falls. This opposite behaviour is used in thermistors and solar cells.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Hard

[MYP B] Design an experiment to find how resistance varies with wire length. Include hypothesis, variables, and method.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: R ∝ L (directly proportional).
IV: length (10–100 cm in 10 cm steps); DV: resistance (R = V/I); CV: material, thickness, temperature.
Method: Connect nichrome wire between crocodile clips on metre rule; apply fixed voltage; read ammeter; calculate R. Allow wire to cool between readings.

Resistivity of copper = 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m. Calculate resistance of a 5 m wire with area 2 × 10⁻⁶ m².Hard
Show Answer

R = ρL/A = (1.7×10⁻⁸ × 5)/(2×10⁻⁶) = 0.0425 Ω (42.5 mΩ). Very low — copper is an excellent conductor.

[MYP D] "Resistance is always bad." Evaluate this with two examples where high resistance is desirable.Hard
Show Answer

The claim is incorrect. High resistance is useful in: (1) Heater/toaster coils — high resistance converts electrical energy to heat; (2) Fuses — thin fuse wire melts at overload to protect the circuit; (3) Resistors in electronics — limit current to protect components.

A thermistor: 2000 Ω at 20°C, 500 Ω at 60°C. In a voltage divider with a 500 Ω fixed resistor and 5 V supply, find voltage across fixed resistor at each temperature.Hard
Show Answer

At 20°C: Total = 2500 Ω; I = 0.002 A; V_fixed = 0.002×500 = 1 V.
At 60°C: Total = 1000 Ω; I = 0.005 A; V_fixed = 0.005×500 = 2.5 V. Used in temperature-sensing circuits.

[MYP D] Explain superconductivity and why it matters for energy transmission.Hard
Show Answer

Below a critical temperature, some materials have exactly zero resistance. With R = 0, no energy is lost as heat (P = I²R = 0). Superconducting cables would eliminate the ~7–10% transmission losses in current copper grids. Currently, superconductivity only occurs near absolute zero, making large-scale use costly.

Wire A: copper, 1 m, 1 mm². Wire B: nichrome, 1 m, 1 mm². Nichrome resistivity is 65× copper. If wire A carries 10 A, how much current does wire B carry at the same voltage?Hard
Show Answer

R_B = 65 R_A. I_B = I_A/65 = 10/65 ≈ 0.15 A. Nichrome severely limits current — ideal for heating elements where little current should flow.

[MYP B] Suggest two improvements to increase reliability when investigating resistance vs temperature using a water bath.Hard
Show Answer

(1) Repeat each temperature 3 times and take mean to reduce random error; (2) Place calibrated thermometer next to the wire (not just the bath) to ensure the wire has equilibrated; (3) Use 4-probe (Kelvin) measurement to eliminate contact resistance.

A 10 Ω, 0.5 W resistor is connected to 3 V. (a) Find current. (b) Find power. (c) Is it safe?Hard
Show Answer

(a) I = 3/10 = 0.3 A
(b) P = I²R = 0.09×10 = 0.9 W
(c) Not safe — 0.9 W exceeds the 0.5 W rating; the resistor may overheat and fail.

[MYP D] Compare R–T graph shapes for (a) a metal wire, (b) an NTC thermistor, (c) a filament bulb. Explain each shape.Hard
Show Answer

(a) Metal wire: linear upward slope — resistance increases proportionally with temperature.
(b) NTC thermistor: steep downward exponential — more charge carriers liberated at higher temperature.
(c) Filament bulb: steep upward non-linear curve — filament resistance at ~2500°C is ~10× its cold value.

A 230 V iron has resistance 57.5 Ω. Calculate (a) current drawn, (b) charge flowing in 30 minutes.Hard
Show Answer

(a) I = 230/57.5 = 4 A
(b) Q = 4 × 1800 = 7200 C

Ohm's Law

Recommended Videos

KurzgesagtHow Ohm's Law Works

EducationalOhm's Law Experiments

Set A Fundamentals — Easy

State Ohm's Law in words.Easy
Show Answer

At constant temperature, the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it: V = IR.

Write Ohm's Law as a formula.Easy
Show Answer

V = I × R

A resistor of 10 Ω carries 2 A. What is the voltage across it?Easy
Show Answer

V = IR = 2 × 10 = 20 V

What shape is the V–I graph for an ohmic conductor?Easy
Show Answer

A straight line through the origin.

What does the gradient (slope) of a V–I graph represent?Easy
Show Answer

The gradient equals the resistance (R) in ohms.

At what condition does Ohm's Law hold?Easy
Show Answer

At constant temperature (and constant physical conditions).

12 V is applied across a 4 Ω resistor. What current flows?Easy
Show Answer

I = V/R = 12/4 = 3 A

Is a filament bulb an ohmic or non-ohmic conductor? Why?Easy
Show Answer

Non-ohmic — its resistance changes significantly with temperature (V–I graph is not a straight line).

If resistance doubles, and voltage stays constant, what happens to current?Easy
Show Answer

Current halves (I = V/R — inversely proportional to R).

A 5 A current flows through a 6 Ω resistor. What is the voltage across it?Easy
Show Answer

V = 5 × 6 = 30 V

Set B Application — Medium

A student collects V–I data for a resistor: (2V, 0.4A), (4V, 0.8A), (6V, 1.2A). Does it obey Ohm's Law? Calculate R.Medium
Show Answer

V/I = 5 Ω in all three cases. The graph is linear through origin → yes, it obeys Ohm's Law; R = 5 Ω.

Distinguish between ohmic and non-ohmic conductors. Give one example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Ohmic: constant resistance at constant temperature; V–I graph is a straight line. Example: metal wire at constant temperature.
Non-ohmic: resistance changes; V–I graph is curved. Example: filament bulb, diode.

Two resistors: R₁ = 6 Ω at 3 A, R₂ = 10 Ω at 3 A. What is the total voltage across both (connected in series)?Medium
Show Answer

V₁ = 3×6 = 18 V; V₂ = 3×10 = 30 V; Total V = 48 V

A graph of V (y-axis) vs I (x-axis) gives a slope of 25. What is the resistance of the component?Medium
Show Answer

Slope of V–I graph = R = 25 Ω

Why does Ohm's Law break down for a filament bulb at high currents?Medium
Show Answer

High current heats the filament to very high temperatures, increasing resistance. Since R is no longer constant, V is no longer proportional to I — Ohm's Law (which requires constant R) no longer applies.

An electric kettle element has resistance 28.75 Ω on India's 230 V mains. Calculate the current it draws.Medium
Show Answer

I = 230/28.75 = 8 A

A wire obeys Ohm's Law. When V = 5 V, I = 0.1 A. What is I when V = 15 V?Medium
Show Answer

R = 5/0.1 = 50 Ω; I = 15/50 = 0.3 A

Explain why Ohm's Law is described as an "empirical law" rather than a fundamental law.Medium
Show Answer

Ohm's Law is based on experimental observation, not derived from more fundamental principles. It holds for many materials under specific conditions (constant temperature) but breaks down for semiconductors, electrolytes, and non-linear components — it is not universally true.

A current of 0.5 A flows through a component. When V is doubled, I becomes 0.75 A. Does the component obey Ohm's Law?Medium
Show Answer

If it obeyed Ohm's Law, doubling V should double I to 1.0 A. Since I only rose to 0.75 A, it does not obey Ohm's Law — resistance increased as voltage/current increased.

A 9 V battery drives current through a resistor. If R = 180 Ω, calculate (a) the current and (b) the charge flowing in 1 minute.Medium
Show Answer

(a) I = 9/180 = 0.05 A
(b) Q = 0.05 × 60 = 3 C

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Hard

[MYP B] Design a full investigation to verify Ohm's Law for a metal wire. Include circuit diagram description, variables, data table structure, and how to calculate R from your graph.Hard
Show Answer

Circuit: Battery → variable resistor (rheostat) → ammeter → wire under test → back to battery; voltmeter in parallel across wire.
IV: voltage (adjusted via rheostat); DV: current; CV: same wire, temperature (allow cooling).
Data table: Columns: V (volts), I (amperes), V/I (Ω).
Graph: Plot V on y-axis, I on x-axis. If straight line through origin → Ohm's Law confirmed. R = gradient = ΔV/ΔI.

V–I data for a diode: (0.2V, 0 mA), (0.4V, 0 mA), (0.6V, 1 mA), (0.8V, 50 mA). Does the diode obey Ohm's Law? Explain what the data shows.Hard
Show Answer

No — the V–I graph is not a straight line. The diode only conducts significantly above ~0.6 V (the "threshold" or "knee" voltage). R = V/I changes dramatically: at 0.6 V, R is very high; at 0.8 V, R = 0.8/0.05 = 16 Ω. This non-ohmic behaviour allows diodes to act as one-way valves for current.

[MYP D] A student plots a V–I graph and gets a straight line that does NOT pass through the origin. What does this suggest about the circuit?Hard
Show Answer

A non-zero y-intercept suggests a systematic error — most likely a contact resistance (resistance at connections not accounted for), a zero error on the voltmeter, or the presence of another EMF in the circuit (e.g., back-EMF). The component may still be ohmic, but the measurement includes an additional fixed voltage offset.

A circuit contains a 12 V battery, an ammeter (0 Ω ideal), and two resistors 8 Ω and 4 Ω in series. Calculate (a) total resistance, (b) current, (c) voltage across each resistor.Hard
Show Answer

(a) R_total = 8+4 = 12 Ω
(b) I = 12/12 = 1 A
(c) V₁ = 1×8 = 8 V; V₂ = 1×4 = 4 V; total = 12 V ✓

[MYP B] Predict what a V–I graph would look like for a thermistor (NTC type) as voltage is gradually increased. Sketch and explain the shape.Hard
Show Answer

The graph would curve upward then become steeper (super-linear). As voltage increases, more current flows → thermistor heats up → resistance falls → even more current for the same additional voltage. The slope (R = V/I) decreases as I increases, producing a curve bending toward the I-axis — opposite to a filament bulb which curves toward the V-axis.

Using Ohm's Law, calculate the resistance of the human body between wet and dry hands. Dry: 100,000 Ω; a shock of 0.1 A (minimum lethal). What voltage is needed in each case?Hard
Show Answer

Dry hands: V = IR = 0.1 × 100,000 = 10,000 V needed — mains (230 V) is safe for dry contact in most cases.
Wet hands: skin resistance drops to ~1000 Ω; V = 0.1 × 1000 = 100 V — well within mains voltage, hence the danger of wet hands near electrical outlets.

[MYP D] Georg Ohm published his law in 1827 and was initially ridiculed. From a scientific perspective, explain how the scientific community eventually accepted it and what this tells us about science.Hard
Show Answer

Ohm's Law was accepted when independent experimenters reproduced his results reliably, confirming the V–I relationship. This illustrates: (1) science requires reproducibility; (2) initial rejection of valid ideas is common when they challenge existing paradigms; (3) empirical evidence over time overrides authority — the law is now a cornerstone of circuit analysis, validated billions of times. This reflects the self-correcting nature of science.

A student uses a 6 V battery and 30 Ω resistor to light an LED in series. The LED has a forward voltage of 2 V. What is the current through the circuit and the voltage across the resistor?Hard
Show Answer

Voltage across resistor = 6 − 2 = 4 V. I = V/R = 4/30 ≈ 0.133 A (133 mA). The LED "uses" 2 V and the resistor uses 4 V, totalling 6 V.

[MYP B] How would you modify a standard Ohm's Law investigation to also test whether a diode follows Ohm's Law? What additional equipment is needed?Hard
Show Answer

Replace the wire with a diode. Apply voltages from 0 to ~1 V in 0.05 V steps using a variable power supply. Measure current with a milliammeter (diode currents are small). Plot V–I graph. Additional equipment: milliammeter (sensitive), low-voltage variable supply, and a reverse-bias test (connect diode backwards) to show it blocks current — confirming non-ohmic, asymmetric behaviour.

[MYP D] Is Ohm's Law a "law" in the strictest scientific sense? Compare it to Newton's laws of motion in terms of universality and scope.Hard
Show Answer

Ohm's Law is more of an empirical rule than a universal law — it applies only to ohmic conductors at constant temperature and breaks down for semiconductors, gases, and non-linear elements. Newton's laws (of motion), while also having limitations at relativistic speeds, apply to a far broader range of situations. Ohm's Law's scope is deliberately limited, making it a model rather than a fundamental principle — useful but not universally valid.
