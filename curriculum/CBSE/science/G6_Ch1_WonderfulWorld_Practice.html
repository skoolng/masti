What is Science

Set A Recall — What is Science

What is science?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Science is a systematic way of asking questions about the natural world and finding answers through observation and evidence.

Key: Science uses evidence, not opinion. It is organised, repeatable and based on the natural world.
What does MRS GREN stand for?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition.

Key: These 7 life processes are used to define whether something is living. All living organisms carry out all 7 processes.
What is an observation in science?
Easy
Answer

Answer: An observation is information gathered directly using the five senses (or instruments that extend them).

Key: Observations are factual and based on what is actually detected — not what you think it means.
What is an inference?
Easy
Answer

Answer: An inference is a logical explanation or conclusion drawn from an observation.

Key: You observe "smoke" → you infer "there is fire." The inference goes beyond what is directly seen.
Name the three main branches of science.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Biology, Chemistry, and Physics.

Key: Biology = living things; Chemistry = matter and substances; Physics = energy, forces and motion.
List the steps of the scientific process in order.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Observe → Question → Hypothesis → Plan experiment → Collect data → Analyse → Conclude → Communicate.

Key: The process is cyclical — a conclusion often leads to new questions and further investigations.
What is the difference between a fact and an opinion in science?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A fact is a statement supported by evidence; an opinion is a personal belief that may not be backed by data.

Key: Science relies on facts and evidence. Opinions can change; scientific facts only change when new evidence emerges.
Which MRS GREN process describes an organism getting rid of waste products?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Excretion.

Key: Excretion removes waste products of chemical reactions (e.g., CO₂ from respiration). It is different from egestion (removing undigested food).

Set B Apply — What is Science

A student says "The plant died because I forgot to water it." Is this an observation or an inference? Explain.
Medium
Answer

Answer: It is an inference. The student observed the dead plant but inferred the cause (lack of water).

Key: Observations describe what is seen; inferences explain why. Other causes (disease, overwatering) are also possible.
A fire has been put out, but you see smoke rising. State one observation and one inference.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Observation: Smoke is rising from the site. Inference: Something is still burning or smouldering underneath.

Key: Observations are directly detected; inferences use prior knowledge to explain what is observed.
A virus replicates inside a host cell. Does a virus carry out all MRS GREN processes? What does this tell us?
Medium
Answer

Answer: No — viruses lack most MRS GREN processes outside a host. This places them in a grey area between living and non-living.

Key: Viruses reproduce but cannot respire or carry out nutrition independently — they are considered non-living by most definitions.
Classify each as Biology, Chemistry or Physics: (a) rusting of iron, (b) how birds migrate, (c) how a light bulb glows.
Medium
Answer

Answer: (a) Chemistry — a chemical reaction. (b) Biology — animal behaviour and life processes. (c) Physics — electrical energy converted to light and heat.

Key: Many real-world phenomena overlap branches — rusting also involves physics (electron transfer), showing science is interconnected.
Rearrange these into the correct scientific process order: Conclude, Observe, Communicate, Hypothesis, Collect data.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Observe → Hypothesis → Collect data → Conclude → Communicate.

Key: The process always starts with an observation that sparks curiosity, and ends with sharing results so others can verify or build upon them.
Why is it important that scientific results are shared with other scientists?
Medium
Answer

Answer: So other scientists can check, repeat and verify the results (peer review), building reliable scientific knowledge.

Key: A single result is not enough — repeated verification by independent scientists strengthens confidence in a finding.
A student watches a caterpillar move and says "It moved 2 cm in 10 seconds." A second student says "It moved because it was scared." Identify which is observation and which is inference.
Medium
Answer

Answer: The first is an observation (measurable, factual). The second is an inference (explains the reason based on an assumption).

Key: Good scientists clearly separate what they see from what they think it means.
Which MRS GREN process does photosynthesis in plants represent? Justify your answer.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Nutrition — plants make their own food using sunlight, CO₂ and water.

Key: Nutrition = obtaining food/energy. Plants are autotrophs that produce glucose through photosynthesis, meeting their own nutritional needs.

Set C Investigate — What is Science (MYP Criterion B/D)

Design a simple investigation to test whether plants show the MRS GREN process of sensitivity. Describe what you would do and what you would observe.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Place a seedling near a light source and observe over 3–5 days whether the stem grows towards the light (phototropism), demonstrating sensitivity to a stimulus.

Key: Sensitivity = responding to stimuli. Keep all other variables (water, soil, temperature) constant to ensure a fair test. The response (bending) is the measurable observation.
Evaluate this claim: "Science can answer every question humans have." Do you agree? Use examples to support your reasoning.
Hard
Answer

Answer: No. Science can answer empirical questions (e.g., "What causes malaria?") but not value-based or philosophical questions (e.g., "Is it right to clone humans?").

Key: Science is limited to questions that can be tested with evidence. Ethical, aesthetic and philosophical questions fall outside the scope of science.
A scientist observes that a lake turns green every summer. She infers it is caused by algae. Describe TWO additional investigations she could carry out to test this inference.
Hard
Answer

Answer: (1) Collect water samples and examine under a microscope to confirm algal presence. (2) Measure nutrient levels (nitrogen, phosphorus) in summer vs. winter to identify triggers for algal growth.

Key: Inferences need testing. Multiple independent investigations provide stronger evidence than a single test.
Explain why the scientific process is described as "cyclical" rather than "linear." Use the steps of the process in your answer.
Hard
Answer

Answer: After communicating conclusions, scientists often discover new questions or anomalies that return them to the observation and hypothesis stage — creating a cycle of inquiry.

Key: E.g., concluding that plant growth is affected by light may raise a new question: "Does the wavelength of light matter?" — restarting the process.
Compare the roles of curiosity and scepticism in science. Why are both needed?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Curiosity drives scientists to ask new questions and explore the unknown; scepticism ensures claims are questioned, evidence is demanded, and results are verified.

Key: Without curiosity, science stagnates. Without scepticism, errors and false claims go unchallenged. Both together produce reliable, advancing knowledge.
A student claims that crystals "grow," so they must be alive. Use MRS GREN to evaluate and refute this claim.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Crystals increase in size (similar to growth) but do not carry out respiration, nutrition, excretion, reproduction, movement or sensitivity independently — they fail 6 of 7 MRS GREN criteria.

Key: Growth alone is not sufficient. All 7 life processes must be present for an organism to be classified as living.
Design an investigation to show that observation can be unreliable without instruments. What variables would you control and why?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Ask students to estimate the length of the same object first by eye, then with a ruler. Compare results to show how unaided estimation varies between individuals.

Key: Control: same object, same lighting, same time limit. IV = method of measurement; DV = recorded length. This shows why standardised instruments improve accuracy.
A research team finds that a new antibiotic kills bacteria in a lab dish. Evaluate whether this is sufficient evidence to sell it as a medicine. What further steps are needed and why?
Hard
Answer

Answer: No — lab results alone are insufficient. Further steps include animal trials, clinical trials with human volunteers, peer review, and regulatory approval to ensure safety and effectiveness.

Key: Lab conditions differ from a living body. Ethics require that potential harm is assessed before use on humans. This reflects both scientific rigour and responsibility.

Set A Recall — Scientific Questions & Hypotheses

What is a testable question in science?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A testable question is one that can be answered by collecting evidence through observation or experiment.

Key: Testable questions focus on measurable variables. "Does light affect plant growth?" is testable; "Is nature beautiful?" is not.
What is a hypothesis?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A hypothesis is a prediction or proposed explanation that can be tested by an experiment.

Key: A hypothesis is not a guess — it is based on prior knowledge or observation and must be testable and falsifiable.
Write the format used to state a hypothesis.
Easy
Answer

Answer: "If [independent variable] then [dependent variable] because [scientific reason]."

Key: The "because" clause is crucial — it links the prediction to a scientific explanation, not just a guess.
What does it mean for a hypothesis to be "falsifiable"?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A hypothesis is falsifiable if it is possible to design an experiment that could prove it wrong.

Key: Falsifiability is a core feature of scientific hypotheses. A hypothesis that cannot be disproven is not scientific.
Give ONE example of a non-testable question and explain why it cannot be tested.
Easy
Answer

Answer: "Is the sky the most beautiful colour?" — this is a matter of personal opinion and cannot be measured or tested objectively.

Key: Questions involving feelings, beliefs, or values do not have measurable outcomes and therefore cannot be scientifically tested.
What is the difference between a hypothesis and a theory in science?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A hypothesis is an untested prediction; a theory is a well-supported explanation backed by extensive evidence from many experiments.

Key: In everyday speech "theory" means a guess, but in science it means a well-tested, reliable explanation (e.g., the Theory of Evolution).
What is a prediction? How is it different from a hypothesis?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A prediction states what you expect to happen in a specific experiment; a hypothesis is a broader explanation that includes a reason.

Key: Prediction: "The plant in bright light will grow taller." Hypothesis: "If light intensity increases, then growth rate will increase because more light energy drives photosynthesis."
Name TWO characteristics of a good scientific question.
Easy
Answer

Answer: (1) It must be testable (can be investigated with an experiment). (2) It must be focused on a specific variable that can be measured.

Key: Good questions lead to clear experiments. Vague or value-based questions do not produce useful scientific data.

Set B Apply — Scientific Questions & Hypotheses

Write a hypothesis for this question: "Does the amount of fertiliser affect how tall a bean plant grows?"
Medium
Answer

Answer: If the amount of fertiliser increases, then the bean plant will grow taller, because fertiliser provides more nutrients needed for cell growth.

Key: The "because" clause uses a scientific reason (nutrients for growth), not just "because fertiliser helps."
Identify whether each is testable or non-testable: (a) "Does music affect plant growth?" (b) "Is this music nice?" (c) "Does salt affect ice melting?"
Medium
Answer

Answer: (a) Testable — plant growth can be measured. (b) Non-testable — "nice" is subjective. (c) Testable — melting time can be measured.

Key: Testable questions always include a measurable outcome (height, time, temperature, mass).
A student writes: "I think plants grow better in sunlight." Rewrite this as a proper hypothesis in If–Then–Because format.
Medium
Answer

Answer: If plants receive more sunlight, then they will grow taller, because sunlight provides energy for photosynthesis which produces glucose for growth.

Key: A proper hypothesis names the IV (sunlight) and DV (height) and includes a scientific mechanism in the "because" clause.
Why must a hypothesis come before the experiment, not after?
Medium
Answer

Answer: A hypothesis must be written before the experiment so that results can genuinely test it. Writing it after seeing results is biased and unscientific.

Key: If the hypothesis is written after results are known, it is not a prediction — it is just describing what happened, which is not a fair test of an idea.
Transform this vague question into a good scientific question: "Does temperature matter for bacteria?"
Medium
Answer

Answer: "Does increasing water temperature from 20°C to 40°C affect the growth rate of E. coli bacteria?"

Key: A good scientific question specifies the variable being changed (temperature range), the thing being measured (growth rate), and the organism (E. coli).
What is wrong with this hypothesis: "If I water the plant, then it will grow, because plants need water"? Improve it.
Medium
Answer

Answer: It is too vague — "water" and "grow" are not quantified. Improved: "If the amount of water given to a plant increases from 50 ml to 200 ml per day, then its height will increase more rapidly, because water is needed for cell expansion."

Key: Good hypotheses specify the range or amount of change and use measurable terms for the outcome.
A student predicts: "If I exercise for longer, I will get fitter." Is this a scientific hypothesis? Explain.
Medium
Answer

Answer: It is partially scientific but incomplete. "Fitter" is not clearly defined or measurable. It needs to specify what will be measured (e.g., resting heart rate, sprint time).

Key: Every term in a hypothesis must be operationally defined — described in a measurable way.
A hypothesis is tested and the results do not support it. Does this mean the experiment failed? Explain.
Medium
Answer

Answer: No — a result that does not support a hypothesis is still valuable. It rules out one explanation and points scientists towards a new or revised hypothesis.

Key: In science, a "negative result" (hypothesis not supported) is just as informative as a positive one. Disproving an idea is progress.

Set C Investigate — Scientific Questions & Hypotheses (MYP Criterion B/D)

You notice that bread goes mouldy faster in summer than winter. Write a full scientific question and a hypothesis for this observation.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Question: "Does temperature affect the time taken for mould to grow on bread?" Hypothesis: "If temperature increases from 10°C to 30°C, then mould will appear sooner, because warmer conditions speed up fungal reproduction."

Key: The question identifies the IV (temperature) and DV (time for mould growth). The hypothesis links them with a biological mechanism.
Distinguish between a hypothesis and a scientific law. Give an example of each.
Hard
Answer

Answer: A hypothesis is an untested prediction (e.g., "If a heavier ball is dropped, it will fall faster"). A scientific law is a description of a consistent pattern observed in nature (e.g., Newton's Law of Gravity — all objects fall at the same rate in a vacuum).

Key: Laws describe what happens; theories explain why. Neither replaces the other — they serve different roles in science.
Evaluate the following hypothesis for quality: "If plants are given coloured light, they will grow differently." What improvements would you make?
Hard
Answer

Answer: The hypothesis is too vague. Improved: "If bean plants are grown under red light compared to white light for 14 days, then they will grow taller, because red wavelengths (660 nm) are most efficiently absorbed by chlorophyll for photosynthesis."

Key: Improvements: specify the organism, the colours being compared, the time period, the measurable DV (height), and a specific scientific mechanism.
A scientist finds results that partially support her hypothesis. Explain what she should do next and why this reflects good scientific practice.
Hard
Answer

Answer: She should repeat the experiment with more trials, refine the hypothesis to account for unexpected findings, and communicate her results so others can verify them.

Key: Partial support means the relationship is more complex than predicted. Refining the hypothesis and seeking peer verification is the scientific response.
Why is it scientifically important that a hypothesis can be proven wrong? What term describes this property?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Falsifiability. If a hypothesis cannot be proven wrong, no experiment can genuinely test it, making it scientifically meaningless.

Key: Karl Popper proposed falsifiability as the key criterion of science. A claim like "everything happens for a reason" cannot be tested and is therefore unscientific.
Create TWO testable scientific questions from the observation: "Some students in the class seem more alert in the morning than others."
Hard
Answer

Answer: (1) "Does the number of hours of sleep affect reaction time measured at 8 am?" (2) "Does eating breakfast affect a student's score on a 10-question recall quiz taken at 9 am?"

Key: Each question has a clear IV (sleep hours; breakfast), a measurable DV (reaction time; quiz score), and is specific enough to design an experiment around.
Explain why it is important to include a "because" clause in a hypothesis, using an example to illustrate.
Hard
Answer

Answer: The "because" clause shows that the hypothesis is based on scientific understanding, not just a guess. E.g., "…because chlorophyll absorbs red light for photosynthesis" connects the prediction to a biological mechanism that can also be investigated.

Key: A hypothesis with a "because" clause can be tested at two levels: does the predicted outcome occur, and is the proposed mechanism correct?
Describe how confirmation bias could affect the way a scientist interprets results. How can the scientific process reduce this bias?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Confirmation bias is the tendency to favour results that support your hypothesis and ignore those that don't. It can be reduced by blind trials, peer review, and having independent scientists repeat the experiment.

Key: The scientific process is designed to overcome human biases — replication, peer review and pre-registered hypotheses all reduce the influence of confirmation bias.

Set A Recall — Variables & Fair Tests

What is an independent variable (IV)?
Easy
Answer

Answer: The independent variable is the one variable the scientist deliberately changes in an experiment.

Key: There should be only ONE independent variable per experiment. Changing multiple variables makes it impossible to identify the cause of any change.
What is a dependent variable (DV)?
Easy
Answer

Answer: The dependent variable is what is measured or observed — it changes in response to the independent variable.

Key: The DV "depends on" the IV. If you change fertiliser amount (IV), you measure plant height (DV).
What is a controlled variable (CV)?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Controlled variables are all factors kept the same throughout the experiment so they do not affect the results.

Key: Keeping CVs constant ensures that only the IV is responsible for any change in the DV — this is what makes a test "fair."
What is a control experiment?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A control experiment is a setup where the independent variable is absent or at zero — it provides a baseline to compare experimental results against.

Key: E.g., in a fertiliser experiment, the control plant receives no fertiliser. Without a control, you cannot know if the results are caused by the IV.
What is a fair test?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A fair test is an experiment where only the independent variable is changed while all other variables are kept constant.

Key: A fair test allows the scientist to conclude that changes in the DV are caused by the IV, not by any other factor.
In an experiment testing how light intensity affects photosynthesis rate, identify the IV and DV.
Easy
Answer

Answer: IV = light intensity; DV = rate of photosynthesis (measured by, e.g., oxygen bubbles per minute).

Key: The IV is always what you change; the DV is always what you measure in response.
Name TWO controlled variables in an experiment testing the effect of sugar concentration on yeast activity.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Temperature of the water and amount of yeast used.

Key: Both temperature and yeast quantity affect fermentation rate. Keeping them constant ensures only sugar concentration is responsible for any change.
What does the abbreviation IV, DV, CV stand for?
Easy
Answer

Answer: IV = Independent Variable; DV = Dependent Variable; CV = Controlled Variable.

Key: A useful memory aid: "I change the IV, I Measure the DV, I keep the CV Constant."

Set B Apply — Variables & Fair Tests

An experiment tests how the height of a ramp affects the distance a toy car travels. Identify the IV, DV, and two CVs.
Medium
Answer

Answer: IV = height of ramp; DV = distance travelled by the car; CVs = same car, same surface, same release position.

Key: CVs are anything else that could affect how far the car travels. Changing the car model or surface material would make the test unfair.
A student tests whether caffeine affects heart rate by giving one group cola and another group water, then measuring pulse. What is the control group here?
Medium
Answer

Answer: The group given water is the control group — they do not receive caffeine, providing a baseline heart rate for comparison.

Key: The control group receives no treatment (or a neutral one). It shows what happens without the IV, making it possible to measure the IV's effect.
Why is it important to change only ONE variable at a time in an experiment?
Medium
Answer

Answer: If more than one variable is changed at once, it is impossible to know which variable caused the observed change in the dependent variable.

Key: E.g., if you change both fertiliser type and watering frequency, you cannot tell which factor made the plant grow taller.
A student tests whether brand of parachute material affects drop time, but uses different parachute sizes each time. Identify the mistake and explain its effect on results.
Medium
Answer

Answer: The mistake is changing parachute size (a CV) alongside the material (IV). This makes the test unfair because the size, not just the material, also affects drop time.

Key: The results cannot reliably tell us whether differences in drop time are due to material or size — both variables were changed simultaneously.
Describe the difference between a control experiment and a controlled variable. Students often confuse these terms.
Medium
Answer

Answer: A controlled variable is a factor kept the same throughout; a control experiment is an entire additional setup with no treatment applied, used as a baseline for comparison.

Key: Think: controlled variables = "kept the same in all groups." Control experiment = "an extra group where nothing is changed."
In a clinical drug trial, one group receives the drug and another receives a sugar pill. What is the sugar pill called and why is it used?
Medium
Answer

Answer: The sugar pill is called a placebo. It is used to control for the placebo effect — the tendency of patients to feel better simply because they believe they are being treated.

Key: Without a placebo control, the observed improvement could be due to the patient's belief rather than the drug itself.
An experiment tests how soil type affects the growth of tomato plants. List THREE controlled variables you would need to keep constant.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Amount of water, amount of light, temperature (also: pot size, same tomato variety, same amount of initial fertiliser).

Key: Any factor that could affect plant growth — other than soil type — must be controlled. The list of CVs should be comprehensive and justified.
Why should scientists repeat their experiments multiple times rather than testing each condition only once?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Repeating experiments reduces the effect of random errors and anomalies, making results more reliable and allowing an average to be calculated.

Key: A single result could be a fluke. Repeated trials ensure that the pattern observed is consistent and not due to chance variation.

Set C Investigate — Variables & Fair Tests (MYP Criterion B/D)

Design a fair test to investigate whether the type of liquid (water, juice, energy drink) affects seed germination rate. Include IV, DV, CVs, and a control.
Hard
Answer

Answer: IV = type of liquid; DV = percentage of seeds germinated after 7 days; CVs = same seed type, same number per tray, same volume of liquid, same temperature, same light; Control = seeds watered with plain water.

Key: The control (plain water) acts as the baseline. The CVs ensure the only difference between groups is the liquid type — making the test fair.
Explain how an uncontrolled variable could lead to an incorrect conclusion in science. Use a specific example.
Hard
Answer

Answer: If a student tests two fertilisers but accidentally gives one group more sunlight, any observed difference in plant growth could be due to sunlight, not fertiliser — leading to a false conclusion about fertiliser effectiveness.

Key: Uncontrolled variables introduce confounding factors — alternative explanations for results that make it impossible to attribute the change to the IV.
A student claims: "My experiment was a fair test because I measured carefully." Evaluate this claim. What does a fair test actually require?
Hard
Answer

Answer: The claim is incorrect. Careful measurement improves accuracy but does not make a test fair. A fair test requires that only the IV is changed and all other variables are kept constant.

Key: Accuracy (precision in measurement) and fairness (controlled variables) are separate concepts. Both are needed for reliable results.
Design an experiment to test whether noise level affects memory recall. Clearly state how you would ensure it is a fair test.
Hard
Answer

Answer: IV = noise level (silent / 50 dB / 80 dB); DV = number of words recalled from a 20-word list; CVs = same word list, same time to memorise (2 min), same recall time (5 min), same age group, same time of day; Control = silent condition.

Key: Controlling time of day and age group removes confounding variables. Using a standardised word list removes variation in difficulty.
What is a double-blind trial? Why is it considered more reliable than a single-blind trial?
Hard
Answer

Answer: In a double-blind trial, neither the participants nor the researchers know who received the real treatment. This prevents both participant expectation and researcher bias from influencing the results.

Key: Researcher bias can subtly affect how data is recorded. Double-blind design eliminates this, making drug trials more scientifically valid.
Identify and explain TWO sources of error that could make an experiment unfair, other than changing too many variables.
Hard
Answer

Answer: (1) Measurement error: using an uncalibrated instrument gives inaccurate readings. (2) Human error: reading a scale at an angle (parallax error) gives inconsistent values.

Key: Random errors vary unpredictably; systematic errors consistently skew results in one direction. Both need to be identified and minimised for a reliable investigation.
A pharmaceutical company tests its own drug and reports 95% success. Identify TWO reasons why independent scientists should repeat this experiment before the drug is approved.
Hard
Answer

Answer: (1) Conflict of interest — the company profits from approval, so may (consciously or unconsciously) select favourable data. (2) Independent replication verifies reliability and ensures the results hold under different conditions.

Key: Peer-reviewed, independent replication is the scientific community's safeguard against biased or fraudulent research.
A student measures the growth of 3 plants over 4 weeks and records each plant's height daily. Explain why using 3 plants rather than 1 plant improves the reliability of the investigation.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Using 3 plants allows an average to be calculated, reducing the impact of anomalies (e.g., one plant is accidentally damaged). It makes the pattern observed more representative.

Key: More replicates → more reliable data. Anomalous results from one plant are less likely to skew the overall conclusion when three results are averaged.

Set A Recall — Collecting & Recording Data

What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative data?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Qualitative data describes qualities using words (e.g., colour, texture, smell); quantitative data uses numbers and units (e.g., 45°C, 12 cm).

Key: Quantitative data is preferable in science because it can be measured, compared and analysed mathematically.
Why must units always be included when recording quantitative data?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Without units, a number is meaningless — "12" could be 12 cm, 12 kg or 12 seconds.

Key: Units tell us what is being measured and allow data to be compared and communicated clearly between scientists worldwide.
What is a data table and why is it used?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A data table organises collected data in rows and columns, making it easy to read, compare and use for graphing.

Key: A well-structured table has column headings with units in brackets, the IV in the first column, and repeated trials in subsequent columns.
What are repeated trials and why are they important?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Repeated trials mean performing the same experiment several times. They increase reliability by revealing anomalies and allowing an average to be calculated.

Key: A minimum of 3 trials per condition is recommended. Averages smooth out random variations in the data.
How do you calculate the mean (average) of a set of data?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Add all the values together and divide by the number of values.

Key: Mean = (sum of all values) ÷ (number of values). E.g., (4 + 6 + 8) ÷ 3 = 6. Anomalous values should be excluded before calculating the mean.
What is an anomalous result?
Easy
Answer

Answer: An anomalous result is a data point that does not fit the overall pattern and is likely caused by an error in measurement or procedure.

Key: Anomalous results should be circled on a graph or table and excluded from the calculation of averages. The cause should be investigated.
What is the difference between accuracy and precision in measurement?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Accuracy is how close a measurement is to the true value; precision is how consistent repeated measurements are with each other.

Key: You can be precise but inaccurate (consistently measuring 5 cm when the true value is 7 cm). Ideal measurements are both accurate and precise.
Give ONE example of qualitative data and ONE example of quantitative data from a plant growth experiment.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Qualitative: "The leaves turned yellow." Quantitative: "The plant grew 8.5 cm in 7 days."

Key: Both types of data can be collected in the same experiment. Quantitative data provides more precise and comparable information.

Set B Apply — Collecting & Recording Data

A student records the following heights of bean plants (cm) after 10 days: 6, 7, 5, 7, 14, 6. Identify the anomalous result and calculate the mean without it.
Medium
Answer

Answer: The anomalous result is 14 cm. Mean of remaining values: (6 + 7 + 5 + 7 + 6) ÷ 5 = 31 ÷ 5 = 6.2 cm.

Key: 14 cm is far outside the cluster of 5–7 cm values. Excluding it gives a more reliable average that represents the typical plant height.
Design a data table for an experiment testing how temperature (10, 20, 30, 40°C) affects the time taken for sugar to dissolve. Include 3 trials and an average column.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Columns: Temperature (°C) | Trial 1 (s) | Trial 2 (s) | Trial 3 (s) | Mean (s). Rows for each temperature: 10, 20, 30, 40°C.

Key: IV (temperature) goes in the first column; DV (time) with units in brackets in each data column; a mean column at the end for analysis.
Explain why a scientist would use a digital thermometer rather than a liquid-in-glass thermometer for precise temperature measurements.
Medium
Answer

Answer: A digital thermometer gives more precise readings (e.g., to 0.1°C), eliminates parallax error, and records faster and more consistently than a glass thermometer.

Key: The choice of instrument affects precision and accuracy. More sensitive instruments reduce human error in reading values.
A student records "the water was hot" in their results. Suggest how this could be improved for a scientific report.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Replace with a quantitative measurement: "The water temperature was 78°C" — recorded with appropriate units and a calibrated thermometer.

Key: Qualitative descriptions like "hot" are subjective. Quantitative data provides an objective, reproducible record.
Why should you record raw data immediately rather than calculating averages in your head and recording only those?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Raw data preserves all original readings, allowing anomalous values to be identified. Mental averages are error-prone and cannot be checked or reviewed by others.

Key: Raw data is the foundation of scientific integrity. It allows you — and others — to verify, reanalyse or spot mistakes later.
In an experiment, Trial 1 gives 25 s, Trial 2 gives 26 s, Trial 3 gives 24 s. Calculate the mean and comment on the reliability of these results.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Mean = (25 + 26 + 24) ÷ 3 = 25 s. The results are reliable — the three trials are very close, indicating low random error.

Key: Small variation between trials (25–26 s range) suggests consistent measurement technique and controlled conditions.
Classify each piece of data as qualitative or quantitative: (a) 37.2°C, (b) bluish-green colour, (c) 450 ml, (d) rough texture.
Medium
Answer

Answer: (a) Quantitative, (b) Qualitative, (c) Quantitative, (d) Qualitative.

Key: Quantitative data = numbers with units; qualitative data = descriptions without numbers. Both are valid but quantitative is more useful for analysis.
What is a systematic error? Give one example and explain how it differs from a random error.
Medium
Answer

Answer: A systematic error consistently shifts all measurements in the same direction (e.g., a balance always reading 2 g too heavy due to a calibration error). Random errors vary unpredictably between readings.

Key: Systematic errors can be corrected by calibrating instruments. Random errors are reduced by taking more repeated measurements and averaging.

Set C Investigate — Collecting & Recording Data (MYP Criterion B/D)

A student collects the following spring lengths (cm) at different weights: 0g→5cm, 100g→8cm, 200g→11cm, 300g→14cm, 400g→21cm. Identify the anomalous result. Calculate the correct mean change per 100g (excluding it) and comment on the pattern.
Hard
Answer

Answer: 400g→21cm is anomalous (expected ~17 cm). Change per 100g for 0–300g: (14–5)÷3 = 3 cm per 100g. The data shows a linear (proportional) relationship between weight and spring extension.

Key: This illustrates Hooke's Law. The anomaly at 400g may indicate the spring stretched beyond its elastic limit. Excluding it reveals the true linear pattern.
Explain why a data table alone is not enough to draw conclusions from an experiment. What else is needed?
Hard
Answer

Answer: A data table shows raw numbers but doesn't reveal patterns, trends or relationships clearly. A graph (especially a line graph or bar chart) is also needed to visualise and interpret the data.

Key: Conclusions require: (1) a clear pattern from the graph, (2) comparison to the hypothesis, (3) scientific explanation of the pattern, and (4) evaluation of reliability.
Design a data collection plan for an investigation into whether phone screen time affects sleep duration in Grade 6 students. What data will you collect, how, and how will you ensure it is reliable?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Collect: daily screen time (hours) and sleep duration (hours) from 20 students over 2 weeks using self-report logs and phone screen-time data. Reliability: large sample size, multiple days, standardise the time period measured, cross-check phone records with student logs.

Key: Reliability improves with more data points and participants. Self-reported data has validity concerns — using phone data as a cross-check reduces this bias.
A student records all results as whole numbers even though the scale shows decimal places. Explain how this affects data quality and what the correct approach is.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Rounding to whole numbers reduces precision — fine details are lost. The student should record to the same number of decimal places as the instrument allows (e.g., 12.4 g, not 12 g).

Key: The number of decimal places recorded should match the precision of the measuring instrument. Losing precision can mask important trends in data.
Explain the difference between the mean, median and mode. In what situation would the median be more useful than the mean?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Mean = average of all values; Median = middle value when ordered; Mode = most frequent value. The median is more useful when anomalous values are present — extreme values skew the mean but do not affect the median.

Key: E.g., house prices use median because a few very expensive properties would make the mean unrepresentative of typical prices.
Propose how a scientist studying penguin behaviour in Antarctica could collect both qualitative and quantitative data in the field. Give TWO examples of each.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Qualitative: (1) describing social interactions ("penguins huddled in groups"), (2) noting feather condition. Quantitative: (1) measuring temperature (°C), (2) counting number of chicks per colony.

Key: Field scientists combine both data types. Qualitative data often contextualises quantitative findings — numbers alone may not capture the full picture.
A student uses the same ruler for all measurements but the zero-mark is worn and starts at 0.3 cm. What type of error does this create? How would you detect and correct it?
Hard
Answer

Answer: This creates a systematic error — all measurements will be 0.3 cm less than the true value. Detect by calibrating against a known standard; correct by adding 0.3 cm to all recorded values.

Key: Systematic errors are consistent and directional — they can be corrected mathematically once identified. Random errors cannot be corrected in the same way.
Compare primary data (collected by the scientist themselves) with secondary data (from published sources). Give ONE advantage and ONE disadvantage of each.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Primary: advantage = collected for your specific purpose; disadvantage = time-consuming and limited in scale. Secondary: advantage = large datasets from peer-reviewed sources; disadvantage = may not match your exact conditions and could be outdated.

Key: Both primary and secondary data are used in science. Critically evaluating the source, date and methodology of secondary data is essential.

Set A Recall — Analysing & Concluding

Which variable goes on the x-axis of a graph?
Easy
Answer

Answer: The independent variable (IV) goes on the x-axis (horizontal).

Key: Remember: "I change the IV" and it goes on the x-axis. The DV "depends" on the IV and goes on the y-axis.
Which variable goes on the y-axis of a graph?
Easy
Answer

Answer: The dependent variable (DV) goes on the y-axis (vertical).

Key: A quick check: the DV is what you measure — it goes up the y-axis. Always include the variable name and units in brackets on each axis.
What is a line of best fit?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A line of best fit is a straight line (or smooth curve) drawn through a scatter of data points to show the overall trend, with roughly equal numbers of points above and below the line.

Key: The line of best fit should not connect every point — it averages the trend. It should pass through the origin only if the data supports it.
What does a positive correlation between two variables mean?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A positive correlation means that as the independent variable increases, the dependent variable also increases.

Key: Positive correlation = both variables go up together. The opposite (one up, one down) is a negative correlation.
What is the difference between a bar chart and a line graph? When would you use each?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A bar chart is used when the IV is categorical (e.g., type of fertiliser); a line graph is used when the IV is continuous (e.g., temperature, time).

Key: Continuous data can take any value in a range → line graph. Discrete or categorical data → bar chart. Never draw a line graph for categorical data.
What should a scientific conclusion always refer to?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A conclusion should refer back to the original hypothesis, state whether the data supports or refutes it, and explain the trend using scientific knowledge.

Key: Structure: "My results show… This supports/does not support my hypothesis because… This is because (scientific explanation)…"
What is the difference between correlation and causation?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Correlation means two variables change together; causation means one variable directly causes the change in the other.

Key: Ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer — they are correlated, but ice cream doesn't cause drowning. A third variable (hot weather) causes both.
What feature must every graph have to be complete?
Easy
Answer

Answer: A title, labelled axes with units, an appropriate scale, plotted data points, and a line/curve of best fit (where appropriate).

Key: Checklist: Title ✓ | x-axis label + units ✓ | y-axis label + units ✓ | even scale ✓ | all points plotted ✓ | line of best fit ✓

Set B Apply — Analysing & Concluding

An experiment shows that as light intensity increases, oxygen production by a plant increases. Describe the trend and state what type of correlation this is.
Medium
Answer

Answer: As light intensity increases, oxygen production increases — this is a positive correlation. The line of best fit on a graph would slope upward from left to right.

Key: More light → more photosynthesis → more oxygen. This is both a correlation and likely a causal relationship (light directly drives photosynthesis).
A student draws a bar chart for data showing how temperature (10, 20, 30, 40°C) affects reaction rate. Is this the best choice of graph? Explain.
Medium
Answer

Answer: No — temperature is a continuous variable, so a line graph is more appropriate. A line graph shows the trend between values (e.g., what happens at 25°C) whereas a bar chart cannot.

Key: Continuous IV → line graph. A bar chart loses the trend between data points and cannot show a line of best fit.
Write a conclusion for this result: "Plants kept at 20°C grew an average of 12 cm; plants at 30°C grew 18 cm; plants at 40°C grew 8 cm." The hypothesis was: "Higher temperature increases plant growth."
Medium
Answer

Answer: The data partially supports the hypothesis — growth increased from 20°C to 30°C, but decreased at 40°C. The hypothesis was too simple; there is an optimal temperature (30°C) for growth, beyond which heat may denature enzymes.

Key: Conclusions must address the full pattern — not just whether the hypothesis was confirmed or refuted. Partial support is common and scientifically valid.
Describe the trend shown by this data: Mass of NaOH dissolved (g): 5, 10, 15, 20 — Time to dissolve (s): 40, 32, 24, 16.
Medium
Answer

Answer: As the mass of NaOH increases, the time to dissolve decreases — a negative correlation. The decrease is linear (by 8 s for every 5 g added).

Key: Describe: (1) direction of change, (2) type of correlation, (3) whether the relationship is linear or curved, and (4) size of change if possible.
A student's conclusion states: "My results proved my hypothesis." Explain why a scientist would say "support" rather than "prove."
Medium
Answer

Answer: In science, a single experiment cannot "prove" something — results can only support or fail to support a hypothesis. New evidence from different experiments could later disprove even a well-supported idea.

Key: Science deals in probability and evidence, not absolute proof. The language of science uses "supports," "suggests," or "is consistent with."
Explain how to draw a line of best fit on a scatter graph. What are TWO common mistakes students make?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Use a ruler to draw a single straight line with roughly equal numbers of points above and below it. Mistakes: (1) connecting every point like a dot-to-dot, (2) forcing the line through the origin when the data doesn't support it.

Key: A best-fit line represents the overall trend, not each individual point. It smooths out random variation in the data.
A graph shows ice cream sales and crime rates both increase in summer. Does this prove that eating ice cream causes crime? Explain using the concept of correlation vs causation.
Medium
Answer

Answer: No — this shows correlation, not causation. Both ice cream sales and crime rates are driven by a third variable (hot weather brings people outside), not by each other.

Key: Correlation ≠ causation. A confounding variable (hot weather) explains both trends. Scientists must design controlled experiments to establish causal relationships.
What is an extrapolation and what risk is associated with it?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Extrapolation means extending a line of best fit beyond the range of data to predict values outside the experiment. The risk is that the trend may not continue (e.g., a plant may not grow indefinitely as light increases).

Key: Extrapolated values are unreliable — they assume the pattern continues, which may not be true. Interpolation (reading within the data range) is more reliable.

Set C Investigate — Analysing & Concluding (MYP Criterion B/D)

A student's graph shows these points for enzyme activity vs. temperature: 10°C=5, 20°C=12, 30°C=20, 37°C=25, 50°C=8, 60°C=1 (units/min). Describe the full trend and explain the pattern scientifically.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Activity increases from 10°C to 37°C (optimum), then drops sharply at 50–60°C. Above the optimum, heat causes enzymes to denature — the active site changes shape, so the substrate can no longer bind.

Key: Full trend description: rise to optimum, then rapid fall. Scientific explanation links shape of active site and denaturation to the data pattern.
Evaluate this conclusion: "My results showed the plant grew more with more light, proving that light is the only factor affecting plant growth." Identify TWO flaws.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Flaw 1: "Proving" is too strong — one experiment only supports, not proves. Flaw 2: "Only factor" is incorrect — water, nutrients, CO₂ and temperature also affect growth; this experiment only tested light.

Key: Conclusions must be limited to what the data shows. Overgeneralising beyond the scope of the experiment is a common scientific error.
A scientist collects data on hours of sunlight and crop yield across 50 farms. The graph shows a positive correlation (r = 0.87). Can she conclude that more sunlight causes higher yield? What additional evidence is needed?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Not yet. The high correlation (r = 0.87) is suggestive but not causal. Additional evidence needed: controlled greenhouse experiments where all other variables (water, nutrients, soil) are kept constant while only sunlight varies.

Key: Observational/correlational data cannot establish causation. Only a controlled experiment — where the IV is the only variable changed — can demonstrate a causal relationship.
Design the ideal graph for an experiment where IV = concentration of acid (0.5 to 3 mol/L) and DV = rate of reaction (cm³/s). Describe all features it must include.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Line graph. Title: "Effect of acid concentration on rate of reaction." x-axis: "Concentration of acid (mol/L)" with even scale 0–3.5; y-axis: "Rate of reaction (cm³/s)." Plotted data points (×), a smooth curve of best fit, anomalous points circled if present.

Key: Concentration is continuous → line graph. Scale must include the origin (0,0). If the curve levels off (plateau), it must be drawn accordingly — not forced to be straight.
Explain the difference between interpolation and extrapolation. In which scenario would each be used and which is more reliable?
Hard
Answer

Answer: Interpolation reads values within the range of collected data (reliable). Extrapolation predicts values beyond the data range (less reliable — assumes the trend continues unchanged).

Key: E.g., if data was collected at 20–60°C, reading from the graph at 40°C = interpolation (reliable); predicting at 80°C = extrapolation (unreliable without further evidence).
A student's results support the hypothesis but two data points are outliers. Should she (a) ignore them, (b) delete them, or (c) report and investigate them? Justify your answer.
Hard
Answer

Answer: Option (c) — report and investigate. Outliers should be noted in the results (circled, excluded from mean) and the possible cause investigated. Deleting or ignoring them without explanation is scientifically dishonest.

Key: Outliers sometimes reveal genuine phenomena (a new discovery) or errors in procedure. Both possibilities must be explored rather than concealed.
Compare the information provided by a line graph versus a scatter graph. When would a scatter graph be preferred over a line graph?
Hard
Answer

Answer: A line graph shows a trend over time or continuous change; a scatter graph shows the relationship/correlation between two variables using individual data points. A scatter graph is preferred when you have many data points from different subjects (e.g., comparing the height and arm span of 30 students).

Key: In a scatter graph, the line of best fit (not connecting points) reveals the correlation strength and direction — key for analysing biological variation data.
Write a full MYP-standard conclusion for an experiment that found: as salt concentration in soil increases, plant height decreases, with plants dying at concentrations above 3%. Hypothesis was: "Higher salt concentration will reduce plant growth."
Hard
Answer

Answer: The results support the hypothesis. As salt concentration increased from 0% to 3%, plant height decreased (from 18 cm to 3 cm), and plants died above 3%. This is because high salt concentrations cause osmotic stress — water leaves plant cells by osmosis, causing wilting and cell death. The pattern was consistent across three trials, increasing confidence in the conclusion.

Key: MYP conclusion structure: state if hypothesis is supported → describe the specific trend with data values → give a scientific explanation → comment on reliability.

Set A Recall — Science in Society

Who was Aryabhatta and what is he famous for?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Aryabhatta was a 5th-century Indian mathematician and astronomer famous for calculating the value of pi (π), proposing that the Earth rotates on its axis, and early work on the concept of zero.

Key: Aryabhatta's work preceded many European discoveries by centuries. His astronomical model was heliocentric in approach — the Earth moves, not the Sun.
What did C.V. Raman discover? Why was it significant?
Easy
Answer

Answer: C.V. Raman discovered the Raman Effect (1928) — that light changes wavelength when scattered by molecules. It earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930 and is used today in medical diagnostics and material analysis.

Key: Raman was the first Asian to win the Nobel Prize in Physics. The Raman Effect is now a fundamental tool in spectroscopy.
What is APJ Abdul Kalam known for in science and technology?
Easy
Answer

Answer: APJ Abdul Kalam was an aerospace scientist known as the "Missile Man of India" for his role in developing India's ballistic missiles (Agni, Prithvi) and satellite launch vehicles (SLV-3).

Key: Kalam also served as the 11th President of India (2002–2007). He is celebrated as an inspiration for students in science and technology.
What is scientific ethics?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Scientific ethics refers to the moral principles that guide how scientists conduct research, report data, treat subjects, and use knowledge responsibly.

Key: Key ethical principles include: honesty in reporting data, informed consent from participants, avoiding harm, and sharing results for the public good.
What is citizen science?
Easy
Answer

Answer: Citizen science is scientific research carried out by the general public, often in collaboration with professional scientists — collecting observations, data or classifications on a large scale.

Key: Examples: bird counting (eBird), classifying galaxies (Galaxy Zoo), monitoring air quality. Citizen science generates vast datasets impossible for small research teams alone.
Give ONE example of how technology has improved scientific investigation.
Easy
Answer

Answer: The electron microscope allows scientists to see structures far smaller than visible light permits, enabling discoveries in cell biology, virology and materials science.

Key: Technology extends human senses. Other examples: MRI scanners (medicine), spectrometers (chemistry), satellite imaging (earth science).
What is pseudoscience? Give ONE example.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Pseudoscience is a belief or practice that claims to be scientific but does not follow scientific methods — it lacks testable hypotheses and peer-reviewed evidence. Example: astrology.

Key: Pseudoscience often uses scientific-sounding language but cannot be falsified or independently verified. Critical thinking is essential to distinguish it from real science.
Name ONE way science is used to protect the environment.
Easy
Answer

Answer: Scientists monitor greenhouse gas concentrations and model climate patterns to predict climate change and advise governments on reducing emissions.

Key: Environmental science combines biology, chemistry and physics to understand and protect ecosystems. Data-driven policy (e.g., the Paris Agreement) depends on scientific research.

Set B Apply — Science in Society

How did C.V. Raman's discovery of the Raman Effect contribute to modern technology? Give a specific modern application.
Medium
Answer

Answer: The Raman Effect is used in Raman spectroscopy — a technique to identify substances non-destructively. Modern applications include detecting cancer cells in tissue samples and identifying counterfeit drugs in pharmacies.

Key: Basic scientific discoveries often have unforeseen practical uses decades later. Raman's discovery is a classic example of how fundamental research shapes technology.
Explain ONE ethical issue a scientist might face when conducting research on animals.
Medium
Answer

Answer: A key ethical issue is minimising harm — the experiment must not cause unnecessary suffering, and the potential benefit to humans must justify the pain caused to the animal.

Key: The 3Rs framework governs animal research: Replace (use alternatives), Reduce (use fewer animals), Refine (minimise suffering). Ethical approval is required before any such research begins.
Explain how Aryabhatta's ideas about Earth's rotation were ahead of his time. What challenges might he have faced in getting others to accept his ideas?
Medium
Answer

Answer: Aryabhatta proposed in the 5th century CE that the Earth rotates on its axis, explaining the apparent motion of stars — a concept that challenged prevailing geocentric beliefs. He would have faced religious and cultural resistance to ideas that contradicted traditional cosmologies.

Key: Science often challenges established beliefs. Aryabhatta's work shows that evidence-based thinking existed in India 1,000 years before Copernicus in Europe.
A pharmaceutical company wants to test a new cancer drug. List THREE ethical guidelines the clinical trial must follow.
Medium
Answer

Answer: (1) Informed consent: participants must be fully informed and volunteer freely. (2) No harm: the trial must be stopped if serious side effects occur. (3) Randomised and peer-reviewed: results must be independently reviewed before the drug is approved.

Key: The 1964 Declaration of Helsinki sets international standards for ethical medical research. Patient welfare must always outweigh scientific curiosity.
How does citizen science help professional scientists? Give ONE benefit and ONE limitation.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Benefit: citizen scientists collect data at a scale (millions of observations) that professional teams cannot achieve alone. Limitation: data quality can vary because volunteers are untrained and may make errors.

Key: Platforms like iNaturalist and eBird address quality concerns through community validation — members verify each other's observations. Technology (AI image recognition) is also improving data accuracy.
Give ONE example of how a scientific discovery has had both a positive and a negative impact on society.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Nuclear physics: positive = nuclear energy provides low-carbon electricity; negative = nuclear weapons cause mass destruction. The same discovery can serve both humanitarian and destructive purposes depending on how it is used.

Key: Science is a tool — ethics and policy determine whether it is used beneficially. This is why science education includes discussion of responsibility alongside discovery.
Explain how APJ Abdul Kalam's career illustrates the connection between science and national development.
Medium
Answer

Answer: Kalam led India's missile and space programmes (SLV-3, Agni missile), giving India strategic and technological independence. His work shows how science and engineering drive national security, economic growth and technological self-reliance.

Key: Kalam believed that science education — especially for rural students — was the foundation of a developed India. His autobiography "Wings of Fire" continues to inspire young scientists.
Distinguish between a scientific claim and a non-scientific claim. Give an example of each from everyday life.
Medium
Answer

Answer: A scientific claim is testable, falsifiable, and based on evidence (e.g., "Vitamin C reduces cold duration"). A non-scientific claim is based on belief, tradition or authority without testable evidence (e.g., "Eating turmeric cures all diseases").

Key: Even traditional remedies can become scientific once properly tested. The difference lies in the method of establishing evidence — not whether the claim sounds reasonable.

Explain how the principle of peer review protects the scientific community from false claims. Include a real or hypothetical example in your answer.

Compare the scientific contributions of Aryabhatta and C.V. Raman. What do their careers tell us about the nature of scientific progress?

Answer: Aryabhatta worked deductively — using mathematical reasoning to explain astronomical observations without modern instruments. Key: Science is a global, cumulative enterprise. Progress builds on prior work regardless of nationality — Raman knew of Einstein's photon theory; Aryabhatta built on Indian and Greek astronomy before him.
A scientist discovers that a commonly used food preservative may cause cancer in rats at very high doses. Discuss the ethical responsibilities of the scientist, the government, and the public in responding to this finding.
