Set A Literal — What does the passage say? (Easy)

Passage: "Researchers at Westfield University tested whether daily walking reduces blood pressure. After an eight-week programme, participants who walked 30 minutes each day showed an average blood pressure reduction of 6 mmHg. The researchers concluded that regular walking is an effective intervention for managing mild hypertension."
What is the central claim of this passage? Easy
Show Answer

Regular walking is an effective intervention for managing mild hypertension. This is explicitly stated as the researchers' conclusion in the final sentence.

Passage: "Scientists monitoring a coral reef found that water temperature rose by 2°C over ten years. The study's main argument is that rising sea temperatures are the primary driver of coral bleaching events in the region."
According to the passage, what does the study argue is the PRIMARY driver of coral bleaching? Easy
Show Answer

Rising sea temperatures. The passage explicitly states this is the primary driver identified by the study.

Passage: "A nutritional study followed 400 adults for six months. Half ate a diet rich in leafy greens; half did not change their diet. The leafy-greens group showed improved iron levels. The authors argue that leafy-green consumption can address iron deficiency in adults. The authors do not claim this applies to children."
What population does the study's main claim apply to, according to the passage? Easy
Show Answer

Adults. The passage explicitly states the authors do not claim the finding applies to children.

Passage: "The report examines air pollution levels in five cities. Its central purpose is to document seasonal variations in particulate matter. The report does not attempt to explain why those variations occur."
What is the stated purpose of the report? Easy
Show Answer

To document seasonal variations in particulate matter. The passage directly states this as the central purpose.

Passage: "Ecologists studying a forest found that tree density declined 18% over 20 years. They claim deforestation, not disease, is the main cause of this decline. Evidence cited includes logging records showing increased activity during the same period."
What do the ecologists claim is the main cause of tree decline? Easy
Show Answer

Deforestation (not disease). The passage explicitly states this as the ecologists' claim.

Passage: "A sleep study monitored 200 teenagers. The researchers argue that teenagers who sleep fewer than 7 hours per night perform worse on cognitive tests. The study focused only on cognitive performance and did not measure physical health outcomes."
Which outcome did the study NOT measure? Easy
Show Answer

Physical health outcomes. The passage states the study focused only on cognitive performance and did not measure physical health.

Passage: "An agricultural study tested three irrigation methods. The central argument is that drip irrigation conserves the most water among the methods tested. The study was conducted over one growing season in a semi-arid region."
What is the main claim of the agricultural study? Easy
Show Answer

Drip irrigation conserves the most water among the methods tested. This is directly stated as the central argument.

Passage: "Physicists propose that a newly discovered particle may help explain dark matter. The article's main argument is that the particle's properties are consistent with theoretical dark matter models. The physicists do not claim they have proven dark matter exists."
What do the physicists explicitly NOT claim? Easy
Show Answer

They do not claim they have proven dark matter exists. The passage directly states this limitation.

Passage: "Marine biologists tracked 50 sea turtles using GPS. The study's central finding is that sea turtles return to the same nesting beaches year after year. The researchers conclude that beach preservation is critical for sea turtle survival."
What conclusion do the researchers draw from their finding? Easy
Show Answer

That beach preservation is critical for sea turtle survival, since turtles return to the same beaches each year.

Passage: "Chemists developed a new polymer that degrades in sunlight within 90 days. The paper argues this polymer could replace conventional plastics in packaging. The scope of the claim is limited to outdoor packaging applications."
To what scope does the paper limit its claim? Easy
Show Answer

Outdoor packaging applications. The passage explicitly states the claim is limited to this scope.

Set B Interpretation — What does it mean? (Medium)

Passage: "Botanists found that Plant A grew 40% taller than Plant B under identical light conditions. They conclude that Plant A is 'more efficient at converting light energy.' A student claims the study proves Plant A is healthier overall."
Which statement best describes what the authors actually claim versus what the student claims? Medium
Show Answer

The authors claim only that Plant A converts light energy more efficiently under the tested conditions. The student's claim about overall health is broader and not supported by the passage — the study only measured height under specific light conditions.

Passage: "A climate study states: 'Average global temperatures have risen 1.1°C since pre-industrial times. This warming trend is primarily attributable to human greenhouse gas emissions.' A newspaper headline reads: 'Scientists Say All Temperature Change Is Man-Made.'"
How does the newspaper headline misrepresent the passage's claim? Medium
Show Answer

The passage claims warming is "primarily attributable" to human emissions — not entirely. The headline overstates the claim by saying "all" temperature change is man-made, which goes beyond what the passage states.

Passage: "Researchers tested a new vaccine in 1,000 volunteers. The study's main claim is that the vaccine reduced infection rates by 72% compared to a placebo group. The researchers note the study was conducted during a single flu season."
Why might the phrase "during a single flu season" be important for understanding the scope of the main claim? Medium
Show Answer

It limits the claim's scope — the 72% reduction was measured in one season only. The vaccine's effectiveness in other seasons or against different virus strains is not established by this study alone.

Passage: "A psychology paper argues that students who take handwritten notes retain information better than those using laptops. The study involved 80 university students over one semester. The paper does not address primary school children."
A teacher wants to apply this finding to her class of 10-year-olds. What limitation of the main claim should she consider? Medium
Show Answer

The study was conducted with university students, not primary school children. The paper explicitly does not address younger children, so applying the claim to 10-year-olds goes beyond the study's stated scope.

Passage: "An environmental report presents three main arguments: (1) river pollution has increased, (2) fish populations have declined, and (3) industrial run-off is responsible for both trends. A reader summarises the report as 'arguing that fish are disappearing.'"
What is missing from the reader's summary of the main claim? Medium
Show Answer

The reader's summary omits the causal claim — that industrial run-off is responsible — and the river pollution trend. The report's central argument is about a cause-and-effect relationship, not just the observation that fish are declining.

Passage: "A medical study tracked 500 patients taking a new drug. The authors argue the drug 'significantly reduces symptoms' in patients with moderate disease. The study excluded patients with severe disease and those under 18."
If a doctor wants to prescribe this drug to a patient with severe disease, what does the study's main claim tell us about this situation? Medium
Show Answer

The main claim does not apply — the study explicitly excluded patients with severe disease. The claim about symptom reduction was only established for patients with moderate disease.

Passage: "Researchers studying wildfire frequency argue that drought conditions are the strongest predictor of wildfire outbreaks in the study region. They also note that wind speed and human activity contribute to fire spread, but these were not their primary focus."
Which factors does the main claim address, and which does it acknowledge but not primarily focus on? Medium
Show Answer

The main claim focuses on drought as the strongest predictor. Wind speed and human activity are acknowledged as contributing factors to fire spread, but they are not the primary subject of the study's central argument.

Passage: "An astronomy paper claims that the newly observed exoplanet lies in the 'habitable zone' of its star, meaning liquid water could theoretically exist on its surface. The authors are careful to state they have not detected water or any signs of life."
What is the difference between what the authors claim and what the term 'habitable zone' might suggest to a general reader? Medium
Show Answer

The authors claim only that conditions allow for the theoretical possibility of liquid water. They have not detected water or life. A general reader might interpret 'habitable zone' as meaning life exists there — which goes beyond the actual claim.

Passage: "A study of 200 urban gardens found that gardens with diverse plant species had 30% more pollinator visits than single-species gardens. The central claim is that plant diversity increases pollinator activity in urban settings."
If a researcher finds the same pattern in rural gardens, does this strengthen, weaken, or neither affect the passage's main claim? Medium
Show Answer

It neither directly strengthens nor weakens the passage's specific claim, which is limited to urban settings. The rural finding is consistent with the claim but extends beyond its stated scope. The main claim's validity in urban settings remains the same.

Passage: "Geologists studying rock layers in a canyon argue that the layers were deposited over approximately 10,000 years. This estimate is based on known rates of sediment deposition. The researchers acknowledge that deposition rates may have varied."
How does the researchers' acknowledgement affect the strength of their main claim? Medium
Show Answer

It introduces uncertainty. If deposition rates varied, the 10,000-year estimate could be off. The acknowledgement shows intellectual honesty but also indicates the main claim is an estimate rather than a definitive fact.

Set C Evaluation — Is the claim valid? (Hard)

Passage: "A study of 30 athletes found that those who listened to music while training improved their sprint times by an average of 4%. The authors claim: 'Music is an effective performance enhancer for all athletes.'"
Evaluate the validity of the authors' claim. What is the most significant problem with generalising from this study? Hard
Show Answer

The sample is too small (30 athletes) and not representative enough to support "all athletes." The claim over-generalises — it ignores sport type, athlete level, musical preference, and context. The word "all" makes the claim far broader than the evidence supports.

Passage: "Researchers gave caffeine supplements to sleep-deprived participants and found improved reaction times. The paper's central claim is: 'Caffeine improves cognitive function.' A critic notes that all participants were sleep-deprived."
Why does the critic's observation challenge the validity of the main claim? Hard
Show Answer

The study only tested caffeine in sleep-deprived individuals. The main claim is broader — it implies caffeine improves cognition generally. The results may only show that caffeine counteracts sleep deprivation effects, not that it enhances cognition in well-rested individuals.

Passage: "A five-year study of a lake found that water clarity improved after introducing species X. The study's main claim: 'Species X improves water clarity in lakes.' A reviewer notes no control lake was studied."
Why is the absence of a control lake a critical flaw in evaluating this claim? Hard
Show Answer

Without a control lake (a similar lake without species X), we cannot know whether water clarity would have improved naturally over the same five years regardless of species X. The improvement might be due to other environmental factors, not the introduced species.

Passage: "A pharmaceutical company funds a study showing their new drug reduces headaches in 80% of patients. The study's main claim is that the drug is 'highly effective.' The study was not peer-reviewed and lasted only two weeks."
Identify TWO factors that weaken the validity of the main claim. Hard
Show Answer

(1) Funding bias — the company funding the study has a financial interest in positive results, creating potential bias. (2) Short duration — two weeks may not reveal long-term effectiveness or side effects, making "highly effective" an overstatement. Lack of peer review also means the methodology was not independently validated.

Passage: "A study of children in one city found that those who attended pre-school scored higher on literacy tests at age 7. The main claim: 'Pre-school attendance causes improved literacy outcomes.' The study did not account for parental education levels."
Why is the unaccounted variable a threat to the causal claim? Hard
Show Answer

Children with more educated parents may be more likely to attend pre-school AND to have better literacy outcomes due to enriched home environments. If parental education is a confounding variable, it — not pre-school attendance — could explain the higher literacy scores. The claim of causation is premature.

Passage: "Scientists observe that every summer, ice cream sales and drowning rates both increase. A tabloid reports: 'Ice cream consumption causes drowning — scientists find.' The scientists' actual claim was only that both variables increase simultaneously in summer."
What logical error does the tabloid commit in forming its claim? Hard
Show Answer

The tabloid commits a correlation-causation error. The scientists only noted that both variables rise in summer — likely because hot weather increases both swimming and ice cream sales. Correlation does not imply causation; ice cream sales do not cause drowning.

Passage: "A botanist grows ten plants under red light and ten under blue light. Plants under red light grew taller. The botanist's main claim: 'Red light is superior to blue light for plant growth.' A colleague points out that 'growth' was measured only as height."
How does the colleague's observation limit the validity of the claim? Hard
Show Answer

Growth encompasses more than height — it includes root development, leaf area, biomass, and fruit production. Using height as the sole metric is a narrow operational definition. The claim that red light is "superior" is too broad given only one growth measure was assessed.

Passage: "Anthropologists studying two remote villages found that Village A, which had more social gatherings, had longer average lifespans. Their main claim: 'Social interaction extends human lifespan.' The villages differed in diet, altitude, and healthcare access."
Why do the differences between villages undermine the main claim? Hard
Show Answer

Multiple confounding variables (diet, altitude, healthcare) could explain the lifespan difference. Since these variables were not controlled, we cannot isolate social interaction as the cause. The claim attributes the lifespan difference to one variable when many uncontrolled variables differ between the groups.

Passage: "An experiment exposed bacteria to antibiotic X. After 10 generations, resistant bacteria appeared. The researcher's main claim: 'Antibiotic X creates resistant bacteria.' A reviewer argues the antibiotic does not create resistance but rather selects for it."
What important conceptual distinction does the reviewer raise about the main claim? Hard
Show Answer

The reviewer distinguishes between creating resistance and selecting for it. The antibiotic does not generate new resistance genes; rather, it kills non-resistant bacteria, allowing pre-existing resistant variants to survive and reproduce. The researcher's claim implies the antibiotic is the origin of resistance, which is mechanistically inaccurate.

Passage: "A 2-week survey found that students in schools with green spaces reported lower stress. The study's main claim: 'Green spaces in schools reduce student stress long-term.' The study used self-reported stress levels and lasted only two weeks."
Identify two methodological weaknesses that challenge this specific claim. Hard
Show Answer

(1) The claim says "long-term" but the study lasted only two weeks — far too short to establish long-term effects. (2) Self-reported stress is subjective and susceptible to response bias — students may report what they think is expected. Neither issue alone invalidates the general finding, but both prevent the "long-term" aspect of the claim from being supported.

Set A Literal — What does the word mean here? (Easy)

"The drug significantly reduced pain scores, dropping them from an average of 7.2 to 4.1 on a 10-point scale."
In this context, "significantly" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

Meaningfully or notably. In scientific writing, "significantly" often means the result is large enough to matter. Here, a drop of 3.1 points on a 10-point scale is a substantial, noticeable change — not just a small fluctuation.

"Compound Y inhibits the growth of bacteria by blocking a key enzyme in cell wall construction."
In this sentence, "inhibits" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

Slows down or prevents. The context describes a blocking action — the compound stops or reduces bacterial growth by interfering with enzyme function.

"Researchers found a correlation between hours of screen time and sleep duration: students with more screen time tended to sleep fewer hours."
In this context, "correlation" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

A relationship or pattern between two variables. The sentence describes how two things (screen time and sleep) tend to change together, which is the meaning of correlation.

"The mechanism by which caffeine keeps people awake involves blocking adenosine receptors in the brain."
In this sentence, "mechanism" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

The process or way in which something works. The sentence explains how (the process by which) caffeine produces its effect — through a specific biological action.

"The team's hypothesis was that increased CO₂ levels would accelerate plant growth, but results were inconclusive."
In this sentence, "hypothesis" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

A testable prediction or proposed explanation. The team proposed an expected outcome before conducting their experiment — this is the definition of a hypothesis in scientific context.

"The variable that researchers deliberately changed was the temperature; all other conditions remained constant."
In this sentence, "variable" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

A factor or condition that can change. In experimental design, variables are the elements that can be altered or measured — here, temperature is the one being deliberately changed.

"The results were deemed inconclusive because the data did not clearly support or refute the hypothesis."
In this context, "inconclusive" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

Not providing a clear answer or definite result. The context explains that the data neither confirmed nor denied the hypothesis — so no firm conclusion could be drawn.

"Species diversity in the soil sample was robust, with over 200 distinct microbial types identified."
In this sentence, "robust" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

Strong, rich, or high. The context describes finding over 200 types, which supports the idea that diversity is substantial and well-represented.

"The enzyme catalyses the breakdown of sugar molecules, speeding up a reaction that would otherwise take hours."
In this sentence, "catalyses" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

Speeds up or facilitates. The sentence explains that the enzyme makes a reaction happen much faster than it would naturally — catalysing means enabling or accelerating a process.

"The experiment yielded anomalous results: three of the data points fell far outside the expected range."
In this sentence, "anomalous" most nearly means: Easy
Show Answer

Unusual or not fitting the expected pattern. The context describes data points that fall outside the expected range — anomalous data is data that deviates from what was anticipated.

Set B Interpretation — What does the usage imply? (Medium)

"The study found a statistically significant improvement in recovery time (p = 0.03). However, the average improvement was only 0.4 days."
What is the implication of describing a 0.4-day improvement as "statistically significant"? Medium
Show Answer

Statistical significance means the result is unlikely to be due to chance — but it does not mean the result is practically meaningful. A 0.4-day difference, while real, may not be clinically or practically important, showing that "significant" in science means something different from everyday usage.

"Pesticide Z was found to inhibit reproduction in frogs at concentrations of 50 parts per billion (ppb). Typical river concentrations are 2–5 ppb."
What does the use of "inhibit" combined with the concentration comparison tell you about the real-world relevance of this finding? Medium
Show Answer

The word "inhibit" tells us reproduction is reduced at 50 ppb. However, since typical rivers only have 2–5 ppb, the concentrations shown to inhibit reproduction are 10–25 times higher than normal exposure. This suggests the effect may not be significant in typical environmental conditions.

"The team proposed a novel mechanism for heat transfer in deep-sea vents, suggesting that mineral-laden water acts as a thermal conductor along the ocean floor."
Why is the word "proposed" important in interpreting the strength of this claim about the mechanism? Medium
Show Answer

"Proposed" indicates the mechanism is a suggestion or hypothesis, not a confirmed fact. The team is offering a possible explanation — it has not yet been tested or verified. This hedging language signals the claim is preliminary.

"Although a correlation was observed between urban tree cover and reduced hospital admissions, the researchers caution that confounding variables were not fully controlled."
What does the researchers' caution about confounding variables suggest about the word "correlation" in this context? Medium
Show Answer

It emphasises that correlation is not causation. Other factors (income, access to healthcare, pollution) that correlate with both tree cover and health outcomes may be responsible for the pattern. The researchers are careful not to imply trees cause lower admissions.

"Our working hypothesis is that increased iron availability drives algal blooms; however, we have not yet conducted controlled field trials."
What does the phrase "working hypothesis" imply about the current status of this idea? Medium
Show Answer

A "working hypothesis" is a preliminary, operational assumption being used to guide research — not a confirmed conclusion. The phrase signals the idea is tentative and subject to revision, especially since the researchers note they haven't yet done field trials.

"The drug appeared to mitigate symptoms in the short term; long-term effects remain uncharacterised."
What does "uncharacterised" imply about what the researchers know regarding long-term effects? Medium
Show Answer

"Uncharacterised" means not yet studied or described — the long-term effects are unknown, not necessarily harmful or harmless. The word does not say effects are absent; it says they haven't been investigated, which is an important distinction for safety evaluation.

"Subjects in the control group received a placebo — an inert substance designed to look identical to the treatment pill."
Why is an "inert" substance important in understanding what the control group tells us? Medium
Show Answer

An inert substance has no active chemical effect. Using it means any difference between the groups cannot be attributed to the substance itself, only to the actual drug. This isolates the drug's effect and controls for the placebo effect (psychological response to taking a pill).

"Viral replication was attenuated in cells treated with the compound, suggesting potential antiviral properties."
What does "attenuated" tell you about the extent of the compound's effect on viral replication? Medium
Show Answer

"Attenuated" means reduced or weakened — not eliminated. Viral replication was slowed or diminished, not stopped entirely. This nuance matters: the compound may reduce virus activity without completely preventing infection.

"The species is considered endemic to the island, meaning it is found nowhere else in the world."
Using only the context provided, what does "endemic" mean, and why is this ecologically important? Medium
Show Answer

The sentence itself defines endemic as "found nowhere else in the world." Ecologically, this means if the island's habitat is destroyed, the species has no other population to fall back on — making it particularly vulnerable to extinction.

"The experiment was replicated three times by independent teams, each yielding concordant results."
What does "concordant" tell us about the reliability of the experimental results? Medium
Show Answer

Concordant means in agreement — all three independent replications produced the same or very similar results. This increases confidence that the original results are reliable and not due to experimental error or chance in a single trial.

Set C Evaluation — Precision of language in claims (Hard)

"The study found a significant correlation between sugar consumption and hyperactivity in children (r = 0.12, p = 0.04)."
The correlation coefficient r = 0.12 is very close to 0. What does this tell you about the use of "significant" in this statement, and is the claim misleading? Hard
Show Answer

r = 0.12 indicates a very weak correlation — nearly no relationship. While p = 0.04 meets the threshold for statistical significance (meaning the result is unlikely to be chance), the effect size is tiny and practically meaningless. Calling it "significant" without noting the weakness of r is potentially misleading to a general reader.

"Scientists have established the mechanism by which Vitamin D regulates calcium absorption. However, the precise molecular steps within the intestinal cell membrane are still debated."
How does the word "precise" change the meaning of what is and is not known about the mechanism? Hard
Show Answer

The broad mechanism is established, but the fine detail (precise molecular steps) remains uncertain. "Precise" limits the scope of what is debated — the general process is accepted, only the exact molecular-level sequence is contested. This is a nuanced distinction between partial and complete mechanistic understanding.

"This study suggests that air pollution may contribute to cognitive decline in elderly populations. We cannot rule out reverse causation."
What does "reverse causation" mean in this context, and how does it affect interpretation of the finding? Hard
Show Answer

Reverse causation would mean cognitive decline leads to living in more polluted areas (perhaps due to reduced economic mobility), rather than pollution causing decline. The researchers acknowledge they cannot determine the direction of causality — the association could run either way, undermining the implied causal interpretation.

"The new material exhibits superconductive properties at temperatures below −150°C. It is being heralded as a 'room-temperature superconductor.'"
Evaluate the accuracy of the term "room-temperature superconductor" based on the information given. Hard
Show Answer

The term is inaccurate — −150°C is far below room temperature (approximately 20–25°C). The material requires extreme cold to superconduct. The label "room-temperature superconductor" is either an error, an exaggeration, or (in context) may mean it superconducts at a warmer temperature than previous materials — but that is not what the literal phrase means.

"The research team observed that the treatment inhibited tumour growth in vitro (in laboratory cell cultures). The lead researcher stated in a press release: 'We have found a cure for cancer.'"
What is the gap between the research finding and the researcher's press release claim? Hard
Show Answer

In vitro results in cell cultures are very early-stage — what works in isolated cells often fails in living organisms. The jump from "inhibited tumour growth in a lab dish" to "cured cancer" is enormous: no animal trials, no human trials, no evidence of safety or efficacy in vivo. The press release catastrophically over-states the finding.

"Our model predicts a 3°C rise in global temperature by 2100. This projection assumes current emission trends continue unchanged."
A politician quotes this study saying: "Scientists guarantee a 3-degree rise." Identify the misuse of scientific vocabulary in the politician's statement. Hard
Show Answer

"Predict" and "guarantee" are very different. A model prediction is conditional (based on stated assumptions) and carries uncertainty ranges. The study explicitly states the projection assumes unchanged trends — if emissions change, the prediction changes. "Guarantee" implies certainty, which is never appropriate for model projections.

"Participants who exercised showed elevated levels of Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF), a protein associated with neuroplasticity. The study hypothesises that exercise directly enhances memory."
Evaluate the logic connecting BDNF levels to the memory hypothesis. Hard
Show Answer

There are multiple inferential leaps: (1) BDNF rose with exercise; (2) BDNF is associated with neuroplasticity; (3) neuroplasticity is associated with memory. Each "associated with" link is correlational, not causal. The direct exercise-to-memory claim requires evidence that elevated BDNF actually causes improved memory — not just that these things correlate. The word "hypothesises" correctly signals this is not yet proven.

"The species population declined by 40% over the study period. This decline was deemed 'negligible' by the environmental impact assessment."
What problem does this sentence raise about the use of the word "negligible"? Hard
Show Answer

A 40% population decline is not ordinarily described as negligible — negligible means too small to be worth considering. The use of the word here is highly questionable; it appears to minimise a substantial decline. This may reflect a motivated use of vocabulary (perhaps to avoid regulatory action), and readers should scrutinise whether the classification is scientifically justified.

"The treatment was found to be non-inferior to the standard therapy in clinical trials." A patient reads this and concludes: "The new treatment is just as good as the standard one."
Is the patient's interpretation of "non-inferior" accurate? Explain. Hard
Show Answer

"Non-inferior" is a specific statistical term meaning the new treatment did not perform worse than the standard by more than a pre-specified margin — it does not mean they are equally good. The new treatment could still be slightly worse, just within an acceptable limit. The patient's interpretation is close but technically imprecise; "non-inferior" is not the same as "equivalent."

"The genome of Species A was sequenced with 98.7% accuracy." A journalist writes: "Scientists have perfectly decoded the DNA of Species A."
How does 98.7% accuracy differ from "perfectly decoded," and why does the difference matter? Hard
Show Answer

98.7% accuracy means 1.3% of the genome was misread or missed. For a genome of 3 billion base pairs, that represents roughly 39 million errors or unknown bases. "Perfectly decoded" implies zero errors. This distinction matters enormously for research applications where even small sequencing errors can lead to incorrect biological conclusions.

Set A Literal — Does this evidence relate to the claim? (Easy)

Claim: "Regular exercise reduces the risk of type 2 diabetes." Evidence presented: "Study participants who exercised 150 minutes per week had a 35% lower incidence of type 2 diabetes over 10 years."
Does this evidence directly support the claim? Why or why not? Easy
Show Answer

Yes, it directly supports the claim. The evidence measures exercise frequency and diabetes incidence in the same group, showing a quantified reduction in diabetes risk associated with regular exercise — matching the claim precisely.

Claim: "Bees are essential for pollinating apple trees." Evidence presented: "Apple orchards located near bee colonies produced 60% more fruit per tree than orchards without nearby bee colonies."
Is this evidence relevant to the claim? What does it show? Easy
Show Answer

Yes, it is directly relevant. It shows a measurable difference in fruit production related to bee presence, supporting the idea that bees play an important role in apple tree pollination.

Claim: "Ocean acidification harms coral reef growth." Evidence presented: "Coral reefs are beautiful ecosystems with hundreds of species."
Does this evidence support the claim? Explain. Easy
Show Answer

No. The evidence describes coral reefs generally but says nothing about acidification or growth rates. It is irrelevant to the specific claim about ocean acidification harming reef growth.

Claim: "Solar panels are becoming more affordable." Evidence presented: "The average cost per watt of solar panels dropped from $5.00 in 2010 to $0.80 in 2023."
Does this evidence support the claim? Easy
Show Answer

Yes, strongly. The data shows a dramatic decrease in cost over 13 years — from $5.00 to $0.80 per watt — directly demonstrating that solar panels have become more affordable.

Claim: "Lack of sleep impairs memory consolidation." Evidence presented: "Sleep-deprived rats performed worse on maze-navigation tasks they had learned the previous day, compared to well-rested rats."
Does this animal study provide relevant evidence for the human claim? Easy
Show Answer

It is relevant but limited. The study supports the general idea that sleep deprivation impairs memory-related performance. However, it is an animal study, so direct applicability to human memory consolidation requires additional evidence from human studies.

Claim: "Deforestation increases local temperatures." Evidence presented: "Countries with high deforestation rates rank highly on global inequality indices."
Is this evidence relevant to the temperature claim? Easy
Show Answer

No. Inequality indices measure economic and social factors, not temperatures. The evidence is completely unrelated to the claim about deforestation and local temperature change.

Claim: "Hand washing reduces the spread of respiratory illness in schools." Evidence presented: "Schools that introduced mandatory hand-washing programmes saw a 28% reduction in student absences due to illness over one academic year."
Evaluate whether this evidence supports the claim. Easy
Show Answer

Yes, it supports the claim. The evidence directly connects hand-washing programmes to a measurable reduction in illness-related absences in the school setting — matching both the intervention and the population in the claim.

Claim: "Nitrogen fertiliser increases crop yield." Evidence presented: "Farmers who used nitrogen fertiliser reported feeling more confident about their harvest season."
Does this evidence support the claim? Why or why not? Easy
Show Answer

No. Farmer confidence is a subjective feeling, not a measurement of crop yield. The claim requires data on actual plant growth or harvest amounts — feelings do not constitute scientific evidence of increased yield.

Claim: "Wetlands act as natural water filters." Evidence presented: "Water samples taken downstream of intact wetlands showed 45% lower levels of agricultural runoff pollutants compared to water upstream of the wetlands."
Does this evidence support the claim? Easy
Show Answer

Yes, directly. The upstream/downstream comparison shows water pollutant levels are substantially lower after passing through wetlands, supporting the claim that wetlands filter water.

Claim: "Wolves reintroduced to Yellowstone changed river behaviour." Evidence presented: "After wolves were reintroduced, elk herds avoided riverbanks, allowing vegetation to regrow, which reduced erosion and changed how rivers meandered."
Does this evidence support the claim? Easy
Show Answer

Yes. The evidence provides a causal chain: wolves → elk behaviour change → vegetation regrowth → reduced erosion → altered river behaviour. This directly supports and explains the claim.

Set B Interpretation — What additional evidence would help? (Medium)

Claim: "Listening to classical music improves mathematical performance in students." Current evidence: "Students who attended a classical music concert scored higher on a maths test the next day than students who did not attend."
What additional evidence would most strengthen this claim? Medium
Show Answer

A controlled experiment where students are randomly assigned to listen to classical music (or not) immediately before a maths test, measuring scores under identical conditions — and ideally repeated across multiple sessions and student groups to rule out the concert experience itself (excitement, social activity) as the cause.

Claim: "Low-income households are disproportionately affected by flooding." Evidence: "A survey of flood-affected residents in City A found that 68% had annual incomes below the city median."
What additional evidence would strengthen the claim that this is a widespread pattern, not specific to City A? Medium
Show Answer

Data from multiple cities or regions showing the same income-flooding relationship would strengthen the claim. A comparison of flood zone locations overlaid with income maps across diverse geographic areas would demonstrate the pattern is systematic, not a local coincidence.

Claim: "Reducing screen time improves sleep quality in teenagers." Evidence: "Teenagers who reported using screens less than 1 hour before bed slept an average of 45 minutes longer per night."
This evidence uses self-reported data. What type of evidence would most strengthen the claim? Medium
Show Answer

Objective measurement of sleep using polysomnography or actigraphy (wrist-worn sleep trackers) combined with experimentally controlled screen time — where researchers randomly assign screen time limits rather than relying on self-reporting — would provide much stronger evidence.

Claim: "Antibiotics in livestock feed contribute to antibiotic-resistant infections in humans." Evidence: "Farms using antibiotics prophylactically have higher rates of resistant bacteria in animal samples."
What evidence is still missing to complete the causal chain to humans? Medium
Show Answer

Evidence that the resistant bacteria from animals transfer to humans — for example, identical resistance genes found in both farm animals and nearby human patients, or tracking of specific resistant strains from food products into human gut bacteria. The current evidence only shows resistance in animals, not transmission to humans.

Claim: "Urban green spaces reduce stress." Evidence: "People who spent 20 minutes in a park reported lower stress levels than before their visit."
A sceptic argues that people who choose to visit parks may already be less stressed. What study design would address this concern? Medium
Show Answer

A randomised controlled trial where participants are randomly assigned to spend time in a park OR an urban non-green space (e.g., a shopping area), measuring stress levels before and after with objective measures (cortisol levels), would control for self-selection bias and allow comparison of the two environments directly.

Claim: "A new drug X cures Disease Y in 90% of patients." Evidence: "In a trial of 20 patients, 18 recovered after taking Drug X."
What is the most important limitation of this evidence, and what would address it? Medium
Show Answer

The sample size of 20 is very small — 18 out of 20 could be coincidental. A larger trial (hundreds or thousands of patients) with a placebo control group would establish whether recovery rates with Drug X exceed those without it, and whether 90% is a reliable figure rather than a small-sample artefact.

Claim: "Cycling to school improves children's academic performance." Evidence: "Schools with high rates of cycling to school had higher average test scores than schools with low cycling rates."
What confounding variable is likely present, and what additional evidence would control for it? Medium
Show Answer

School location and socioeconomic status are likely confounders — schools where cycling is common may be in wealthier, safer neighbourhoods with better resources. Controlling for school funding level, neighbourhood income, and parental education when comparing test scores, or tracking individual students' scores before and after they begin cycling, would help isolate the cycling effect.

Claim: "Plastic pollution reduces fish populations." Evidence: "Beaches with high plastic pollution had fewer fish in nearby waters."
What does this evidence not tell us that would be important to establish? Medium
Show Answer

The evidence shows correlation — plastic-polluted beaches coincide with fewer fish. It does not establish: (1) whether plastic caused the decline or other factors (overfishing, water temperature) are responsible, (2) the mechanism by which plastic affects fish, or (3) whether fish populations declined after plastic levels rose (temporal evidence). Longitudinal studies or controlled exposure experiments would be needed.

Claim: "Morning sunlight exposure regulates circadian rhythms." Evidence: "Lab mice exposed to bright light at 7 a.m. showed more regular sleep-wake cycles than mice kept in constant artificial light."
What additional evidence would best extend this finding to human populations? Medium
Show Answer

Human studies tracking circadian biomarkers (melatonin levels, core body temperature rhythms) in participants randomly assigned to morning sunlight exposure versus indoor conditions would directly test whether the mouse findings translate to humans. Ecological studies of shift workers or those in polar regions (with unusual light patterns) could also provide relevant data.

Claim: "Reforestation programmes restore biodiversity." Evidence: "A replanted forest in Brazil showed 40 bird species after 15 years, compared to zero in an adjacent cleared field."
What would strengthen the claim that the reforestation programme — rather than natural recovery — caused the biodiversity increase? Medium
Show Answer

Comparing the replanted forest against a naturally regenerating area of similar age would isolate the specific effect of the programme. If the programme forest showed greater diversity than natural regrowth, we could credit the intervention. Without this comparison, some recovery may have occurred naturally regardless of the replanting effort.

Set C Evaluation — What weakens the claim? (Hard)

Claim: "A high-protein diet causes faster muscle growth." Evidence: "Athletes who consumed 2g protein/kg body weight daily gained 3kg of muscle over 12 weeks, compared to 1.2kg for those on a standard diet." New finding: "The high-protein group also trained 20% more hours per week."
How does the new finding affect the evidence for this claim? Hard
Show Answer

The new finding introduces a critical confounding variable — the high-protein group trained significantly more. The muscle gain could be due to extra training rather than the high-protein diet. The two variables (diet and training volume) were not controlled, so we cannot attribute the difference to diet alone. The evidence no longer clearly supports the causal claim.

Claim: "Compound X prevents tumour growth." Supporting evidence: "In cell cultures, Compound X reduced tumour cell division by 70%." Weakening evidence: "In three mouse trials, Compound X showed no significant effect on tumour size."
How should the weakening evidence change our assessment of the supporting evidence? Hard
Show Answer

The in vitro (cell culture) success does not translate to in vivo (living organism) results. This is a common pattern in drug research — cell culture results frequently fail in animal models. The mouse trials provide a higher level of biological complexity, and their null results significantly weaken confidence in the original claim. The in vitro evidence alone is insufficient support.

Claim: "Social media use causes depression in teenagers." Evidence A: "Teenagers with high social media use report higher depression rates." Evidence B: "Teenagers already experiencing depression report spending more time on social media."
How does Evidence B challenge Evidence A's support for the causal claim? Hard
Show Answer

Evidence B raises the reverse causation possibility — depression may cause increased social media use, not the other way around. Both pieces of evidence show the same correlation but suggest opposite causal directions. The causal claim (social media causes depression) is undermined because the direction of causation cannot be determined from either piece of evidence alone.

Claim: "Noise pollution from traffic harms bird communication." Evidence: "Bird species in noisy urban areas sing at higher frequencies than the same species in quiet rural areas."
A researcher notes that urban birds may also face different predator pressures. How does this weaken the evidence? Hard
Show Answer

Different predator environments could also drive changes in song frequency independent of noise — for example, urban predators may differ in the frequencies they detect, selecting for different song pitches. Without controlling for predator pressure, we cannot be certain that noise (rather than predation selection pressure) is responsible for the frequency shift.

Claim: "Vitamin C supplements reduce cold duration." Evidence A (supports): "A meta-analysis of 20 studies found supplements reduced cold duration by 8% on average." Evidence B (weakens): "When only double-blind placebo-controlled studies are included, the effect drops to 1.5% and is not statistically significant."
Why is Evidence B more compelling than Evidence A in evaluating the claim? Hard
Show Answer

Evidence B filters for higher-quality studies (double-blind, placebo-controlled), which eliminate placebo effects and researcher bias. The 8% average in Evidence A may be inflated by weaker study designs. When only the most rigorous studies are considered, the effect nearly disappears and is statistically insignificant — far less supportive of the claim.

Claim: "The new fertiliser increases wheat yield by 25%." Evidence: "Trials at three farms in the same valley showed a 25% yield increase." Weakening observation: "All three farms are in the same microclimate and drew from the same irrigation source during an unusually wet year."
How do the weakening details undermine the generalisability of the 25% figure? Hard
Show Answer

The three farms are not independent — they share the same microclimate, irrigation source, and experienced unusual weather. The 25% increase may be due to the wet conditions or irrigation quality rather than the fertiliser. The evidence cannot be generalised to other farms in different climates until trials in diverse, independent conditions confirm the result.

Claim: "Mindfulness meditation reduces workplace stress." Evidence supports it with a study of 50 volunteers. Weakening factor: All 50 participants volunteered for the meditation programme; none were randomly assigned.
Why is the self-selection of participants a significant methodological weakness? Hard
Show Answer

People who voluntarily sign up for meditation programmes may already be more motivated to reduce stress, more open to its benefits, or less severely stressed. This self-selection bias means the group is not representative of all employees. The improvement could reflect participant characteristics rather than meditation's effectiveness.

Claim: "LED street lights deter crime." Evidence: "Crime rates fell 15% in neighbourhoods where LED lights replaced older sodium lights." Weakening evidence: "During the same period, the city increased police patrols by 20% in those same neighbourhoods."
How does the weakening evidence affect the validity of the claim? Hard
Show Answer

Both LED lighting and increased police patrols were implemented simultaneously — making it impossible to attribute the crime reduction to lights alone. Increased policing is a well-established crime deterrent. Without a comparison neighbourhood where only lights changed (not patrols), the evidence cannot isolate the lighting effect. The claim is not supported as stated.

Claim: "Diet A is more effective than Diet B for weight loss." Evidence: "Participants on Diet A lost an average of 5.2kg; participants on Diet B lost 3.1kg over 3 months." Weakening factor: "Drop-out rates were 5% for Diet A and 42% for Diet B."
How do the different drop-out rates undermine the comparison? Hard
Show Answer

Participants who drop out often do so because the diet isn't working or is unsustainable. The 42% who dropped out of Diet B likely include those losing little weight. The remaining 58% who completed Diet B may be its best performers — yet Diet A still outperformed them. This means Diet B's reported average is artificially inflated by survivor bias. Diet A's lower drop-out rate also suggests it may be more sustainable, which complicates the comparison further.

Claim: "Species diversity prevents ecosystem collapse." Evidence: "Ecosystems with more species showed greater stability over 20 years." New contradictory finding: "A highly diverse but fragile coral reef collapsed within 2 years following a single bleaching event."
How should this contradictory case be weighed against the broader evidence? Hard
Show Answer

One contradictory case (an outlier or exception) does not invalidate a pattern observed across many ecosystems. However, it does reveal that diversity is not a sufficient protection against all disturbance types — extreme acute events may override diversity benefits. The claim should be qualified: diversity may improve resilience against gradual or moderate stressors but may not protect against sudden, severe disruptions.
