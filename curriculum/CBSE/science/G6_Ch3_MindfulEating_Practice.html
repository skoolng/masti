Set A Recall — Why Our Body Needs Food

What are the three main functions of food in the human body?Easy
Show Answer

Food provides energy for all activities, building materials for growth and repair of body tissues, and protection from diseases by supporting the immune system.

What is a nutrient?Easy
Show Answer

A nutrient is a useful chemical component found in food that the body can use for energy, growth, repair, or regulation of body processes.

Name the six classes of nutrients.Easy
Show Answer

Carbohydrates, Proteins, Fats, Vitamins, Minerals, and Water.

How is the energy content of food measured?Easy
Show Answer

Energy in food is measured in kilocalories (kcal) or kilojoules (kJ). 1 kcal = 4.18 kJ.

Which nutrients are called energy-giving nutrients?Easy
Show Answer

Carbohydrates and Fats are the primary energy-giving nutrients. Proteins can also provide energy when needed.

Which nutrients are called body-building nutrients?Easy
Show Answer

Proteins are the main body-building nutrients. Minerals like calcium also contribute to building bones and teeth.

Which nutrients are called protective nutrients?Easy
Show Answer

Vitamins and Minerals are protective nutrients; they help the immune system and regulate body processes to protect against disease.

Does water provide energy? Give a reason.Easy
Show Answer

No, water does not provide energy (0 kcal). However, it is essential for all chemical reactions in the body, including those that release energy from food.

What happens to the body if it does not receive enough food over a long period?Easy
Show Answer

The body may suffer from malnutrition, resulting in stunted growth, weakness, impaired immune function, and in severe cases, organ failure.

State one difference between macronutrients and micronutrients.Easy
Show Answer

Macronutrients (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) are needed in large amounts and provide energy. Micronutrients (vitamins, minerals) are needed in tiny amounts and do not provide energy but regulate body functions.

Set B Apply — Why Our Body Needs Food

A packet of biscuits shows 250 kcal per serving. Convert this to kilojoules.Medium
Show Answer

250 kcal × 4.18 = 1045 kJ.

Explain why a growing 12-year-old needs more protein in their diet than an elderly person who is not very active.Medium
Show Answer

A growing child needs protein for building new tissues (muscle, organs, bone matrix) as the body is actively developing. An elderly, less-active person mainly needs protein for repair and maintenance, requiring a lower amount.

Why is it incorrect to say "eating fat is always bad for you"?Medium
Show Answer

Fat is an essential nutrient — it provides long-term energy storage, insulates the body, and is needed for absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). The type and amount of fat matters; unsaturated fats are beneficial.

A student skips breakfast and feels tired and unable to concentrate by mid-morning. Link this observation to the role of food in the body.Medium
Show Answer

The brain relies on glucose (from carbohydrates) as its primary fuel. Without breakfast, blood glucose falls, reducing energy supply to the brain — causing fatigue and poor concentration.

How does food contribute to maintaining body temperature?Medium
Show Answer

Food provides energy through cellular respiration. Some of this energy is released as heat, which maintains body temperature at around 37 °C — essential for enzyme function.

Distinguish between the role of nutrients and the role of water in the body.Medium
Show Answer

Nutrients provide energy, building materials, and regulatory functions. Water acts as a solvent and transport medium — dissolving nutrients and carrying them through the blood — but does not itself provide energy or building materials.

A doctor says a patient's diet is "energy-sufficient but nutritionally poor." What does this mean?Medium
Show Answer

The patient consumes enough calories (from carbs/fats) to meet energy needs, but lacks adequate vitamins, minerals, and/or protein — so body-building and protective functions are compromised even without being underweight.

Why do athletes require more calories than non-athletes of the same age and size?Medium
Show Answer

Athletes perform greater physical activity, so their muscles use more ATP (energy). More food is needed to fuel muscle contractions, repair micro-tears, and maintain overall metabolic processes at a higher rate.

Describe one way in which nutrients help protect the body from disease.Medium
Show Answer

Vitamin C, for example, supports the production of white blood cells and antibodies, strengthening the immune response to pathogens. Protein provides the building blocks for antibodies.

Explain why fibre, though not absorbed by the body, is still considered an important part of a healthy diet.Medium
Show Answer

Fibre adds bulk to food, stimulates peristalsis in the intestines, prevents constipation, and feeds beneficial gut bacteria — promoting a healthy digestive system even though the body cannot digest it for energy.

Set C Investigate & Evaluate (MYP Criterion B/D) — Why Our Body Needs Food

Design an experiment to compare the energy content of two foods using the flame calorimetry method. Identify the independent variable, dependent variable, and two controlled variables.Hard
Show Answer

IV: Type of food tested (e.g., peanut vs. bread). DV: Temperature rise of a known volume of water (°C). Controlled variables: Mass of food burned (e.g., 1 g each); volume of water in the calorimeter (e.g., 20 mL). Energy released ∝ temperature rise, calculated as Q = mcΔT.

Evaluate the statement: "All calories are equal — it doesn't matter where they come from." Use evidence from nutritional science.Hard
Show Answer

This is partly false. While 1 kcal = 1 kcal energetically, nutrients carry different metabolic signals. Protein calories promote satiety and muscle synthesis; sugar calories spike insulin and promote fat storage. Fibre in whole foods slows glucose release. Nutrient quality matters beyond just calorie count.

A study finds that populations eating traditional whole-food diets have lower rates of obesity than those eating ultra-processed diets, even when calorie intake is similar. Suggest two scientific reasons why.Hard
Show Answer

1. Whole foods have higher fibre content, slowing digestion and reducing overall calorie absorption. 2. Ultra-processed foods contain additives, emulsifiers, and refined sugars that disrupt gut microbiome and hormonal satiety signals (leptin/ghrelin), promoting overconsumption.

Predict what would happen at the cellular level if the body had no carbohydrate intake for two weeks. Use your knowledge of metabolism.Hard
Show Answer

Without carbohydrates, the body enters gluconeogenesis — making glucose from amino acids and glycerol. Fat is broken down into fatty acids and ketone bodies (ketosis) to fuel the brain. Long-term, muscle protein may be broken down for glucose, leading to muscle wasting.

Analyse why two children eating the same calorie intake might have different growth outcomes. Consider at least three factors.Hard
Show Answer

Factors include: (1) Protein quality — complete vs. incomplete amino acid profiles. (2) Micronutrient status — e.g., zinc and calcium availability. (3) Gut health — absorption efficiency varies. (4) Hormonal differences — growth hormone levels. (5) Physical activity — stimulates muscle protein synthesis.

A government health study shows children in a low-income neighbourhood eat fewer nutrients despite adequate calories. Suggest two systemic causes and one intervention.Hard
Show Answer

Causes: (1) Limited access to fresh vegetables and fruits ("food deserts"). (2) Cheaper calorie-dense but nutrient-poor ultra-processed foods dominate local stores. Intervention: Subsidise fresh produce in these areas or launch school lunch programs providing balanced meals with fruit and vegetables daily.

Evaluate: "Taking vitamin supplements is just as good as eating whole foods for meeting nutrient needs." Discuss using evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Not equivalent. Whole foods contain phytonutrients, fibre, and synergistic nutrient combinations not captured in supplements. Bioavailability of nutrients from food is often higher. Supplements may fill specific deficiencies but cannot replicate the full health matrix of real food. Research shows high-dose supplements sometimes cause harm (e.g., excess Vitamin A = toxic).

Design a 24-hour dietary record study to investigate whether Grade 6 students at your school meet their recommended daily allowance (RDA) of key nutrients. Describe your method and one ethical consideration.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Participants record all food and drink consumed in 24 hours (type + estimated quantity). Use a nutrient database (e.g., IFCT) to calculate intake of protein, iron, calcium, and Vitamins C and D. Compare averages to RDA values for 11–12-year-olds. Ethical consideration: Obtain parental consent; ensure data is anonymous to prevent stigma.

Formulate a hypothesis for the following investigation: "Does soaking vegetables in water before cooking reduce their vitamin content?" State the hypothesis, and explain how you would test it.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Soaking vegetables in water for 30 minutes before cooking reduces their Vitamin C content compared to unsoaked vegetables cooked the same way. Test: Use a titration test (DCPIP dye) on the cooking water and vegetable extract from soaked vs. unsoaked samples to measure Vitamin C quantity.

Critically evaluate the role of food advertising in shaping children's food choices. Should schools restrict food advertising on school premises? Justify using nutritional science and ethical reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

Food advertising targets emotional responses, not nutritional information, often promoting ultra-processed foods high in sugar and saturated fats. Studies show exposure to food ads increases children's preference for unhealthy options. Nutritionally, this leads to excess calorie intake and micronutrient gaps. Ethically, children cannot fully evaluate marketing — schools have a duty of care. Restricting ads on premises is justified as schools should model healthy environments, though enforcement and industry pushback are challenges.

Set A Recall — Carbohydrates and Energy

What is the main function of carbohydrates in the body?Easy
Show Answer

Carbohydrates are the body's main and quickest source of energy for all cellular activities.

Give two examples of simple carbohydrates and two food sources.Easy
Show Answer

Simple carbohydrates include glucose (found in fruits) and fructose (found in honey and fruits). Also sucrose (table sugar) and lactose (milk).

Give two examples of complex carbohydrates and two food sources.Easy
Show Answer

Complex carbohydrates include starch (found in rice, wheat, potatoes) and glycogen (stored in liver and muscle).

How many kilocalories does 1 gram of carbohydrate provide?Easy
Show Answer

1 gram of carbohydrate provides 4 kilocalories (kcal).

What chemical test is used to detect starch? Describe the positive result.Easy
Show Answer

The iodine test is used. A positive result: iodine solution (orange-brown) turns blue-black in the presence of starch.

What is glucose? Why is it important to the body?Easy
Show Answer

Glucose is a simple sugar (monosaccharide) — the primary fuel used by cells in cellular respiration to produce ATP energy. The brain depends almost entirely on glucose.

Name two foods that are rich in complex carbohydrates.Easy
Show Answer

Rice and wheat (roti/bread) are both rich in starch, a complex carbohydrate.

What happens to excess glucose in the body?Easy
Show Answer

Excess glucose is first stored as glycogen in the liver and muscles. If glycogen stores are full, glucose is converted to fat and stored in adipose tissue.

Is fibre a carbohydrate? Can humans digest it?Easy
Show Answer

Yes, fibre (cellulose) is a complex carbohydrate. Humans cannot digest it because we lack the enzyme cellulase, but it is essential for healthy bowel function.

Name the colour change seen when iodine is added to a potato slice.Easy
Show Answer

The iodine turns blue-black because potatoes are rich in starch.

Set B Apply — Carbohydrates and Energy

A runner eats a bowl of pasta (complex carbohydrate) before a race. Explain why this is a better choice than eating a chocolate bar (simple sugar).Medium
Show Answer

Complex carbohydrates in pasta are broken down slowly, providing a steady, sustained release of glucose throughout the race. A chocolate bar releases glucose rapidly — causing a quick energy spike then a crash — which is less beneficial for endurance performance.

Calculate the energy in kcal from carbohydrates if a meal contains 60 g of carbohydrates.Medium
Show Answer

60 g × 4 kcal/g = 240 kcal from carbohydrates.

You test two food samples with iodine solution. Sample A turns blue-black; Sample B stays orange-brown. What can you conclude?Medium
Show Answer

Sample A contains starch (positive iodine test). Sample B does not contain starch (negative result). Sample A could be bread or a potato; Sample B might be meat or oil.

Explain why white rice and brown rice, though both high in starch, have different health effects.Medium
Show Answer

Brown rice retains its bran layer, which contains fibre, vitamins (B vitamins) and minerals. White rice has had the bran removed — digests faster, causing a higher glycaemic response. Brown rice provides sustained energy and more micronutrients.

A student adds iodine to a food sample and gets no colour change. Does this prove the food has no carbohydrates? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

No. The iodine test only detects starch. The food may still contain simple sugars (glucose, fructose) or fibre which do not react with iodine. A Benedict's test would be needed to detect reducing sugars.

Describe the role of glycogen as an energy store in the human body.Medium
Show Answer

Glycogen is a branched-chain polysaccharide stored in the liver and muscles. When blood glucose falls (e.g., between meals), the liver breaks down glycogen and releases glucose back into the blood to maintain stable energy levels.

Why are sugary soft drinks considered a poor source of energy despite being high in calories?Medium
Show Answer

They provide "empty calories" — lots of simple sugar (energy) but no vitamins, minerals, protein, or fibre. The rapid glucose spike causes an insulin surge, promoting fat storage and leaving the person hungry again soon after.

How does the body use carbohydrates in cellular respiration? Write the word equation.Medium
Show Answer

Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy (ATP)
Glucose is broken down in cells to release energy stored in its chemical bonds.

Compare the digestion speed of simple vs. complex carbohydrates and explain the consequence for blood sugar levels.Medium
Show Answer

Simple carbohydrates digest quickly → rapid blood glucose rise → insulin spike → glucose stored as fat → energy crash. Complex carbohydrates digest slowly → gradual blood glucose rise → sustained energy → more stable insulin response.

A food label shows: Total Carbohydrates 45 g, of which Sugars 30 g, Fibre 3 g. Comment on the nutritional quality of this food's carbohydrate content.Medium
Show Answer

30 g of sugars out of 45 g total = 67% simple sugars — indicating this is a high-sugar, low-fibre food. Only 3 g fibre is low (recommended ≥ 25 g/day). This food would cause rapid glucose spikes and provides limited digestive health benefit.

Analyse the impact of a high-sugar diet on long-term health using evidence from nutritional science. Include at least two health conditions.Hard
Show Answer

High sugar intake → chronic elevated blood glucose → Type 2 Diabetes (insulin resistance over time). Excess glucose stored as fat → obesity and fatty liver disease. Sugar also feeds oral bacteria → dental decay. High fructose from added sugars → elevated triglycerides → cardiovascular disease risk.

A scientist claims: "Removing all carbohydrates from the diet is the healthiest approach." Evaluate this claim using your knowledge of carbohydrate types and body function.Hard
Show Answer

Partially flawed. Removing refined sugars has proven benefits. However, eliminating all carbohydrates also removes: (1) fibre — essential for gut health; (2) whole grains — source of B vitamins; (3) glucose — only fuel for red blood cells and primary fuel for the brain. Long-term zero-carb diets may cause nutrient deficiencies and kidney strain from excess protein metabolism.

Predict how a diet high in refined carbohydrates (white rice, sugar) but low in fibre would affect gut health over time. Justify using digestive physiology.Hard
Show Answer

Low fibre → slow peristalsis → constipation → increased risk of colorectal cancer (prolonged exposure of gut wall to waste products). Lack of fibre impairs the gut microbiome (beneficial bacteria feed on fibre), reducing immune function and increasing inflammation markers.

The glycaemic index (GI) ranks foods by how quickly they raise blood sugar. White bread has GI 75; lentils GI 32. Explain what this means and which is healthier for a person with diabetes.Hard
Show Answer

White bread (GI 75) causes a rapid, large rise in blood glucose — dangerous for diabetics who struggle to produce or respond to insulin. Lentils (GI 32) cause a slow, modest rise, easier to manage. Lentils are healthier for diabetics; they also provide fibre and protein which further moderate glucose absorption.

Evaluate whether schools should ban sugary snacks from canteens. Consider student health, personal choice, and school responsibility.Hard
Show Answer

For ban: Schools have a duty of care; high-sugar snacks contribute to childhood obesity and tooth decay; children's prefrontal cortex (decision-making) is not fully developed — they need a supportive environment. Against ban: Personal choice is important; banning may not change habits outside school; education may be more effective. Balanced view: Restricting availability while offering healthy alternatives and nutrition education is the most evidence-based approach.

A nutrition researcher finds that two villages in India have similar calorie intakes but different rates of diabetes. Village A eats mainly polished white rice; Village B eats red hand-pounded rice. Formulate a hypothesis and suggest a variable to test.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Village A has higher diabetes rates because polished white rice has a higher glycaemic index and lower fibre content than red hand-pounded rice, causing greater insulin stress. Variable to test: Fasting blood glucose and HbA1c levels in participants from both villages, controlling for age, physical activity, and total calorie intake.

Critically evaluate food labelling laws that require manufacturers to list "total carbohydrates" but not always distinguish between added sugars and natural sugars. Why does this distinction matter?Hard
Show Answer

Added sugars (e.g., HFCS, sucrose in processing) have no nutritional benefit beyond energy, whereas natural sugars in fruit come packaged with fibre and vitamins, moderating absorption. Listing only "total carbohydrates" misleads consumers — a fruit yogurt and a candy bar may show similar sugar values but have very different health impacts. Transparent labelling (as in the US FDA's 2020 rule) helps consumers make informed decisions and is essential for public health literacy.

A student's experiment shows that chewing bread for longer before swallowing causes it to taste sweeter. Explain the biochemical reason for this observation and link it to digestion.Hard
Show Answer

Saliva contains salivary amylase, an enzyme that begins breaking down starch (complex carbohydrate) into maltose and glucose (simpler sugars). Longer chewing = more contact time with amylase = more starch converted to sweet-tasting sugars. This demonstrates the start of chemical digestion in the mouth.

Should the Indian government tax sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) to reduce consumption? Evaluate using nutritional, economic, and social justice arguments.Hard
Show Answer

Nutritional case for tax: SSBs are linked to obesity, diabetes, and dental decay with no nutritional benefit — taxing reduces consumption (evidence from Mexico: 10% tax = 12% consumption drop). Economic case: Revenue can fund health education and subsidise nutritious foods. Social justice concern: Sugar taxes are regressive — they disproportionately affect low-income families who spend a higher proportion of income on food. Balanced conclusion: A sugar tax is justified if revenue is earmarked to support healthier food access for low-income groups.

Set A Recall — Proteins and Body Building

What are the building blocks of proteins?Easy
Show Answer

The building blocks of proteins are amino acids.

Name three functions of proteins in the human body.Easy
Show Answer

Proteins are needed for: (1) Growth and repair of tissues; (2) Making enzymes (biological catalysts); (3) Making antibodies for the immune system. Also hormones (e.g., insulin) and structural proteins (keratin, collagen).

Name four food sources rich in protein.Easy
Show Answer

Dal (lentils), eggs, meat (chicken/fish), soya, milk/paneer, nuts and seeds.

What chemical test is used to detect protein? Describe the positive result.Easy
Show Answer

The Biuret test is used. Biuret solution (sodium hydroxide + copper sulphate) turns purple/violet in the presence of protein.

How many kilocalories does 1 gram of protein provide?Easy
Show Answer

1 gram of protein provides 4 kilocalories (kcal).

What is a complete protein? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

A complete protein contains all 9 essential amino acids that the body cannot make. Example: eggs, meat, fish, soya.

What is an essential amino acid?Easy
Show Answer

An essential amino acid is one the body cannot synthesise on its own and must therefore be obtained through food. There are 9 essential amino acids.

What is the Biuret reagent made of?Easy
Show Answer

Biuret reagent is made of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and copper sulphate (CuSO₄) solution. It is pale blue in colour before the test.

What happens to proteins that are eaten but not immediately used by the body?Easy
Show Answer

Excess protein cannot be stored. It is broken down in the liver; the nitrogen-containing part is excreted as urea in urine, and the carbon skeleton is converted to glucose or fat for energy.

Give one example of a structural protein found in the body.Easy
Show Answer

Collagen (connective tissue, skin, tendons) or keratin (hair, nails) are structural proteins.

Set B Apply — Proteins and Body Building

A child who eats mainly white rice and no pulses or dairy shows poor growth. Explain using protein science why this diet is insufficient.Medium
Show Answer

White rice has very low protein content and what it has is incomplete (lacks lysine). Without pulses, dairy, or meat, the child lacks sufficient essential amino acids for building new tissues, muscle, and growth hormones — resulting in stunted growth and muscle wasting.

Calculate the energy from protein in a meal that contains 25 g of protein.Medium
Show Answer

25 g × 4 kcal/g = 100 kcal from protein.

Explain why enzymes are proteins and why this makes them vital for digestion.Medium
Show Answer

Enzymes are biological catalysts made of protein. Their specific 3D shape (active site) allows them to bind to specific substrates and speed up chemical reactions — such as breaking down starch (amylase), protein (pepsin), and fat (lipase) during digestion. Without them, digestion would be too slow to sustain life.

Why is dal + roti considered a nutritionally complete protein meal, even though neither is a complete protein on its own?Medium
Show Answer

Dal (lentils) is rich in lysine but low in methionine. Wheat (roti) is rich in methionine but low in lysine. Combined, they provide all essential amino acids — a concept called protein complementation. Traditional diets intuitively solved this problem.

Describe what happens to protein during the Biuret test at the molecular level.Medium
Show Answer

The copper ions (Cu²⁺) in the Biuret reagent form a coordinate bond with the peptide bonds in the protein chain. This complex absorbs light differently, producing the characteristic purple colour.

A vegetarian athlete claims they cannot build muscle without eating meat. Evaluate this claim.Medium
Show Answer

False. Muscle is built from amino acids, not specifically from meat. A well-planned vegetarian diet using soya, dairy, eggs, legumes + grains, and nuts can provide all essential amino acids. Soya protein is a complete protein. Many elite athletes follow plant-based diets successfully.

Explain why antibodies are proteins and why this is important during an infection.Medium
Show Answer

Antibodies are Y-shaped glycoproteins produced by B-lymphocytes. Their specific amino acid sequence creates a unique antigen-binding site that matches a specific pathogen. They neutralise pathogens or flag them for destruction. Without adequate dietary protein, the body cannot produce enough antibodies, compromising immunity.

Why does a person recovering from surgery need extra protein in their diet?Medium
Show Answer

Surgery creates tissue damage. Repair requires building new cells and collagen fibres, both made from protein. Extra protein provides the amino acid supply for faster wound healing and regeneration of muscle and connective tissue.

Two food samples are tested with Biuret solution. Sample X turns purple; Sample Y stays blue. Identify what each sample likely contains.Medium
Show Answer

Sample X contains protein (e.g., egg white, milk, dal extract). Sample Y does not contain protein — it could be a sugar solution, starch, or oil.

Explain what denaturation means and give one example relevant to cooking food.Medium
Show Answer

Denaturation is the irreversible unfolding of a protein's 3D structure due to heat, acid, or physical force — destroying its function. Example: when an egg is cooked, the egg-white protein (albumin) denatures and solidifies — changing from clear to white and from liquid to solid.

Set C Investigate & Evaluate (MYP Criterion B/D) — Proteins and Body Building

Design an experiment to compare protein content in three foods (egg white, dal water, bread extract) using the Biuret test. Specify controls, variables, and how you would rank results.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Add equal volumes (2 mL) of each food extract to separate test tubes. Add 1 mL NaOH, then 3–4 drops CuSO₄. Observe colour after 2 minutes. IV: Type of food. DV: Intensity of purple colour. Control: Distilled water (negative). Ranking: Deeper purple = higher protein. Expected: egg white > dal water > bread extract.

Evaluate the following claim: "A high-protein diet is always beneficial for everyone." Use nutritional evidence to agree or disagree.Hard
Show Answer

Not universally true. While adequate protein is essential, excess protein (especially from animal sources) is metabolised to urea, stressing the kidneys — harmful for people with kidney disease. High animal-protein diets are linked to increased saturated fat intake → cardiovascular risk. Also environmentally costly. Recommended protein = 0.8–1.2 g/kg body weight; excess provides no additional benefit.

Analyse the impact of protein deficiency in children living in poverty. Include the names of specific deficiency conditions and their symptoms.Hard
Show Answer

Kwashiorkor: severe protein deficiency despite adequate calories. Symptoms: swollen abdomen (oedema from low blood albumin), skin lesions, hair discolouration, lethargy. Marasmus: both protein and calorie deficiency → extreme wasting, no oedema. Both cause stunted growth, impaired brain development, weak immunity, and high child mortality in resource-poor settings.

A scientist tests whether cooking dal reduces its protein content using the Biuret test. She finds no difference. Is the Biuret test the best method for this investigation? Justify your answer and suggest a more precise alternative.Hard
Show Answer

The Biuret test is qualitative/semi-quantitative — it detects presence but cannot precisely measure protein quantity. She should use a colorimetric quantitative method such as the Bradford assay (Coomassie dye) or Kjeldahl nitrogen analysis, which measure the actual concentration of protein more accurately and can detect small changes due to cooking.

Critically evaluate plant-based protein sources compared to animal-based protein sources on three criteria: nutritional quality, environmental impact, and affordability.Hard
Show Answer

Nutritional quality: Animal proteins are generally complete; plant proteins often incomplete but complementary combinations work. Environmental impact: Plant proteins (legumes) have far lower carbon footprint, land, and water use — beef emits ~27× more GHGs per gram of protein than lentils. Affordability: Dal, soya, and legumes are significantly cheaper than meat and eggs in most of India, making them accessible for low-income populations. Overall, plant-based proteins are preferable on environmental and affordability grounds with careful dietary planning.

Predict what would happen to muscle mass and immune function if a teenage athlete consumed adequate calories but severely restricted protein. Explain using protein metabolism.Hard
Show Answer

Without dietary protein, the body enters negative nitrogen balance — breaking down muscle protein to obtain amino acids for vital enzymes and immune proteins. The athlete would experience: (1) muscle wasting (catabolism exceeds anabolism); (2) reduced antibody production → weakened immunity; (3) slower recovery from training injuries. Performance would decline significantly despite adequate energy intake.

Should protein powder supplements be marketed to children and teenagers? Evaluate the scientific, ethical, and commercial dimensions.Hard
Show Answer

Scientific: Most children in developed countries already meet protein needs through diet; supplements are unnecessary and may cause excess protein intake, straining kidneys. Long-term safety data for children is limited. Ethical: Marketing exploits body image anxieties common in adolescents. Many products contain artificial sweeteners and additives. Commercial dimension: Industry profits from creating demand where it does not exist. Conclusion: Marketing protein supplements to children is unjustified; dietary education and whole-food protein sources should be promoted instead.

Formulate a testable hypothesis: Does fermenting dal (e.g., idli/dosa batter) increase its protein bioavailability? Describe the experiment and the expected outcome.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: Fermented dal batter has higher protein bioavailability than unfermented dal because fermentation partially breaks down anti-nutritional factors (phytates, tannins) that inhibit protein absorption. Experiment: Feed two groups of rats identical protein quantities — one group fermented dal, one unfermented. Measure nitrogen retention and weight gain over 4 weeks. Expected: fermented group shows greater weight gain and nitrogen retention, indicating higher bioavailability.

A hospital nutrition team is designing a post-surgery meal for a patient. Evaluate three different protein sources (egg, dal, tofu) in terms of completeness, digestibility, and suitability for a vegetarian patient.Hard
Show Answer

Egg: Complete protein (all 9 EAAs), DIAAS score ~1.0 (highest digestibility). Not suitable for vegan; acceptable for lacto-ovo vegetarian. Dal: Incomplete (low methionine); moderate digestibility. Needs to be combined with grains. Suitable for vegetarians/vegans. Tofu (soya): Complete protein, good digestibility, suitable for vegetarians/vegans, anti-inflammatory properties. Recommendation: Tofu or egg (if vegetarian) for highest amino acid completeness; dal + rice as a complementary vegetarian option.

The Gaudium School canteen sells paneer, dal, and eggs as protein options. A student argues "paneer is the healthiest protein choice." Critically evaluate this using nutritional evidence, considering fat content, micronutrients, and completeness.Hard
Show Answer

Paneer is a complete protein and provides calcium — advantages. However, full-fat paneer has high saturated fat content (~20 g/100 g), potentially raising LDL cholesterol. Eggs are complete proteins with better fat profiles (unsaturated fats) and provide B12, selenium, and choline. Dal is high in fibre and micronutrients but incomplete. Conclusion: No single food is universally "healthiest" — it depends on the whole diet. A combination of these sources provides the best nutritional outcome; declaring paneer categorically healthiest oversimplifies nutrition science.

What is the overall function of the digestive system?Easy
Show Answer

To break down food into molecules small enough to be absorbed into the blood and transported to body cells for energy, growth, and repair.

What is the difference between mechanical digestion and chemical digestion?Easy
Show Answer

Mechanical digestion: physically breaking food into smaller pieces (chewing, churning in stomach). Chemical digestion: using enzymes and acids to break food molecules into their building blocks (e.g., starch → glucose).

Name the organ where most nutrient absorption occurs.Easy

The small intestine — it has a large surface area (due to villi and microvilli) through which digested nutrients are absorbed into the blood.

Name two accessory organs of digestion (not part of the digestive tube itself) and their roles.Easy

Liver: produces bile (emulsifies fat). Pancreas: secretes digestive enzymes (amylase, lipase, protease) into the small intestine. Also: salivary glands, gallbladder.

What is absorption in the context of digestion?Easy
Show Answer

Absorption is the process by which digested nutrient molecules pass through the wall of the small intestine into the blood (or lymph for fats) to be transported around the body.

What is egestion?Easy
Show Answer

Egestion is the removal of undigested and indigestible material (faeces) from the body through the anus. It is not the same as excretion.

Name the canal that runs from the mouth to the anus.Easy
Show Answer

The alimentary canal (also called the gastrointestinal (GI) tract or digestive tract).

What type of digestion happens in the mouth?Easy
Show Answer

Both mechanical digestion (teeth chewing food into smaller pieces) and chemical digestion (salivary amylase beginning to break down starch) occur in the mouth.

Which part of the digestive system absorbs water from undigested food material?Easy

The large intestine (colon) absorbs water (and some minerals) from the remaining material, forming semi-solid faeces.

Set B Apply — The Digestive System Overview

Explain why the digestive system must break down large food molecules before they can be used by cells.Medium
Show Answer

Large molecules (starch, protein, fat) are too big to cross the cell membrane or the intestinal wall. They must be broken down into small, soluble molecules (glucose, amino acids, fatty acids) that can be absorbed by diffusion or active transport through intestinal cells into the blood.

Why would a person who has had their gallbladder removed need to eat a low-fat diet?Medium
Show Answer

The gallbladder stores bile produced by the liver and releases it in a concentrated burst when fat is eaten. Without a gallbladder, bile drips continuously in small amounts — insufficient for emulsifying a large fat load. A high-fat meal cannot be properly digested and can cause discomfort and diarrhoea.

Describe the path food takes from the moment it is swallowed until nutrients enter the blood.Medium
Show Answer

Swallowed food travels down the oesophagus (via peristalsis) → stomach (mechanical churning + acid + pepsin) → small intestine (bile emulsifies fat; pancreatic and intestinal enzymes complete digestion; villi absorb nutrients into blood) → nutrients are transported in the portal vein to the liver.

Explain why the small intestine is well suited for absorption. Name two structural adaptations.Medium
Show Answer

(1) Villi: finger-like projections increasing surface area. (2) Microvilli (brush border): further increase surface area on each villus cell. Also: long length (~6–7 m), rich blood supply, and thin epithelium — all increase absorption efficiency.

Why does diarrhoea (rapid transit through the large intestine) cause dehydration?Medium
Show Answer

The large intestine normally absorbs water from intestinal contents. In diarrhoea, material passes through too quickly for sufficient water to be absorbed → large amounts of water (and electrolytes) are lost in liquid faeces → dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.

Distinguish between digestion and absorption using an example for each process.Medium
Show Answer

Digestion: Amylase in the small intestine breaks starch into glucose molecules (chemical breakdown). Absorption: Glucose molecules pass through villus cells into the blood capillary (physical uptake into the blood). Digestion must precede absorption — you cannot absorb starch whole.

A patient has their entire large intestine removed after disease. What challenges would they face in digestion and health?Medium
Show Answer

Without the large intestine: (1) Water and electrolyte reabsorption fails → chronic diarrhoea and dehydration risk. (2) Loss of the gut microbiome (trillions of bacteria in colon) → reduced immune function and vitamin K / B12 production. (3) Fibre fermentation and production of butyrate is lost → reduced colon protection.

Explain why enzymes are essential for digestion and what would happen if they were absent.Medium
Show Answer

Digestive enzymes act as biological catalysts that dramatically speed up the breakdown of food molecules. Without them, chemical reactions that normally take seconds would take years at body temperature. Undigested food molecules could not be absorbed — the person would starve despite eating, and intestinal bacteria would ferment undigested food, causing bloating and infection.

Explain the role of peristalsis in moving food through the digestive system.Medium
Show Answer

Peristalsis is a wave of muscular contractions — the circular muscles behind a food bolus contract while muscles in front relax, pushing food forward. This occurs along the entire alimentary canal — oesophagus, stomach, and intestines — enabling movement even against gravity (you can swallow food upside down).

Explain the difference between ingestion, digestion, absorption, assimilation, and egestion.Medium
Show Answer

Ingestion: taking food into the mouth. Digestion: mechanical and chemical breakdown of food into small molecules. Absorption: passage of digested molecules into the blood through the gut wall. Assimilation: use of absorbed nutrients by body cells for energy, growth, repair. Egestion: removal of undigested waste (faeces) via the anus.

Villi are tiny finger-like projections on the inner wall of the small intestine. They massively increase the surface area for absorption of digested nutrients into the blood.

What is peristalsis and where does it occur?Easy

Carbohydrates → glucose (and other monosaccharides). Proteins → amino acids. Fats → fatty acids and glycerol.

What happens in the large intestine?Easy

The large intestine absorbs water and mineral salts from the remaining indigestible material, forming semi-solid faeces. Gut bacteria ferment fibre here, producing gases and some vitamins (K, B12).

Trace the digestion of a roti (wheat starch) from the mouth to absorption in the small intestine, naming enzymes and products at each stage.Medium
Show Answer

Mouth: Salivary amylase begins breaking starch → maltose. Stomach: Acidic pH inactivates amylase — no further starch digestion. Small intestine: Pancreatic amylase continues starch → maltose; maltase (intestinal enzyme) breaks maltose → glucose. Absorption: Glucose absorbed by villi into blood capillaries → portal vein to liver.

Explain why the stomach does not digest itself despite producing HCl and pepsin.Medium
Show Answer

The stomach wall is protected by a thick layer of mucus secreted by goblet cells. This mucus forms a barrier preventing HCl and pepsin from contacting the epithelium. Pepsinogen is also secreted in inactive form and only activated by HCl in the stomach lumen — not in the wall itself.

Trace the digestion of a piece of chicken (protein) from the mouth to amino acid absorption. Name enzymes and products.Medium
Show Answer

Mouth: Teeth mechanically chew; no protein-digesting enzyme in saliva. Stomach: HCl activates pepsinogen → pepsin; pepsin breaks protein → polypeptides. Small intestine: Pancreatic trypsin and chymotrypsin continue → shorter peptides; intestinal peptidases → individual amino acids. Absorption: Amino acids absorbed by villi into blood capillaries.

Explain the role of bile in fat digestion. Why is it not an enzyme?Medium
Show Answer

Bile emulsifies fat — its bile salts reduce the surface tension of fat globules, breaking them into tiny fat droplets (micelles), greatly increasing the surface area available for lipase to act on. Bile is not an enzyme because it does not chemically break bonds — it is a physical emulsifier. The actual chemical digestion is done by lipase (pancreatic enzyme).

What happens to digested fats after absorption — how do they enter the circulatory system differently from glucose and amino acids?Medium
Show Answer

Glucose and amino acids are absorbed into blood capillaries in the villi and travel via the portal vein to the liver. Fatty acids and glycerol are reassembled into triglycerides in villus cells and packaged into chylomicrons, which enter the lymphatic lacteals (not blood capillaries) and travel through the lymph system before entering the bloodstream at the thoracic duct.

Show Answer

Chronically neutralising stomach acid would: (1) Reduce activation of pepsinogen → insufficient protein digestion. (2) Allow more bacteria to survive in the stomach (acid normally kills pathogens) → risk of infections. (3) Raise stomach pH → may impair absorption of iron, calcium, magnesium, and Vitamin B12 (which require acidic environment for release from food). Long-term antacid use is associated with these nutrient deficiencies.

Without bile, fat cannot be emulsified → lipase has limited surface area to work on → fat is poorly digested → steatorrhoea (fatty stools). Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require fat micelles for absorption → their absorption drops severely → deficiencies develop (night blindness, bone weakness, bleeding disorders).

Chewing increases food's surface area, allowing more contact between food and salivary amylase → begins chemical digestion of starch in the mouth. Mixing with saliva forms a slippery bolus. Signals to the stomach and pancreas to begin producing digestive juices (cephalic phase of digestion). Also activates taste receptors linked to satiety signalling.
