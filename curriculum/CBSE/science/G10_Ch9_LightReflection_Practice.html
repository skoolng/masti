Set A Easy — Laws of Reflection

State the first law of reflection.Easy
Show Answer

The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r), both measured from the normal at the point of incidence.
State the second law of reflection.Easy
Show Answer

The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
Define the normal to a mirror surface.Easy
Show Answer

The normal is an imaginary line perpendicular (at 90°) to the mirror surface at the point where the light ray strikes it.
What is regular (specular) reflection?Easy
Show Answer

When a parallel beam of light reflects off a smooth, polished surface and the reflected rays remain parallel — forming a clear image (e.g., a mirror).
What is diffuse reflection?Easy
Show Answer

When light reflects off an irregular or rough surface, rays scatter in different directions. No clear image forms (e.g., a wall, paper).
If the angle of incidence is 35°, what is the angle of reflection?Easy
Show Answer

35°. By the first law of reflection, ∠i = ∠r always.
Give two examples of surfaces that produce regular reflection.Easy
Show Answer

A plane mirror and a calm water surface both produce regular (specular) reflection.
Why can we see objects that do not emit light?Easy
Show Answer

Non-luminous objects reflect light falling on them from sources like the Sun or a bulb; the reflected light enters our eyes, making the objects visible.

Set B Medium — Laws of Reflection

A ray of light strikes a plane mirror at 90° to the surface. Describe the path of the reflected ray.Medium
Show Answer

A ray hitting the surface at 90° travels along the normal (∠i = 0°), so ∠r = 0° and the ray reflects straight back along its original path.
Distinguish between a luminous and a non-luminous object with one example each.Medium
Show Answer

Luminous objects emit their own light (e.g., the Sun, a candle). Non-luminous objects reflect borrowed light (e.g., the Moon, a book).
Why does a rough road appear dull while a wet road appears shiny?Medium
Show Answer

A rough dry road causes diffuse reflection (scattered light → no image). Water fills the irregularities creating a smooth surface, giving regular reflection and a shiny appearance.
If a plane mirror is rotated by 10°, by how much does the reflected ray rotate?Medium
Show Answer

The reflected ray rotates by 20°. When the mirror rotates by θ, the normal rotates by θ, so both the angle of incidence and angle of reflection change by θ, giving a total rotation of 2θ in the reflected ray.
What is the angle between the incident ray and reflected ray when light strikes a mirror at 40° to the surface?Medium
Show Answer

Angle to surface = 40°, so ∠i = 90° − 40° = 50°. Then ∠r = 50°. Angle between incident and reflected ray = ∠i + ∠r = 100°.
Explain why a cinema screen is painted white and rough instead of smooth and shiny.Medium
Show Answer

A rough white screen causes diffuse reflection, scattering light in all directions so the audience sitting at different angles can all see the image clearly. A smooth shiny screen would only reflect toward one angle.
How many images are formed when two plane mirrors are placed at 90° to each other?Medium
Show Answer

Number of images = (360°/θ) − 1 = (360°/90°) − 1 = 4 − 1 = 3 images.
A ray makes an angle of incidence of 30° with the mirror surface (not the normal). What is ∠r?Medium
Show Answer

Angle with mirror surface = 30°, so ∠i = 90° − 30° = 60°. By law of reflection, ∠r = 60°.

Set C Hard — Laws of Reflection

Prove geometrically that when a plane mirror is rotated by angle θ, the reflected ray rotates by 2θ.Hard
Show Answer

Let original normal be N. After rotating mirror by θ, new normal N' makes angle θ with N. Original ∠i becomes (∠i + θ) with N', so ∠r' = ∠i + θ. The reflected ray moves by (∠r' − ∠r) + θ = θ + θ = 2θ from its original direction.
Why do laws of reflection hold equally for curved mirrors even though the surface is not flat?Hard
Show Answer

For a curved mirror, the normal at each point is drawn to the centre of curvature. Each tiny portion of the curved surface acts as a flat mirror locally. The laws apply at each point independently using the local normal.
Two mirrors are inclined at 60°. An object is placed between them. Find the number of images and explain why one image may differ from the others.Hard
Show Answer

Images = 360°/60° − 1 = 5. When 360°/θ is even, all 5 images are clear. The final image formed by both mirrors simultaneously may be slightly dimmer because it undergoes two reflections, losing more light energy at each reflection.
Explain how a periscope uses laws of reflection. Draw a ray diagram description for a periscope with two mirrors at 45°.Hard
Show Answer

Two plane mirrors placed at 45° to the horizontal. Light enters the top aperture, hits upper mirror (∠i = 45°, ∠r = 45°), reflects downward, hits lower mirror (∠i = 45°, ∠r = 45°), and exits horizontally to the observer. The image shifts vertically but is not inverted.
Why does diffuse reflection not violate the laws of reflection?Hard
Show Answer

Each microscopic point on the rough surface obeys the laws of reflection — the local normal direction varies from point to point. So different parts of the surface reflect light at different angles. The laws hold at every point; it is the varying normal direction that causes scattering, not a breakdown of the laws.
A student claims: "In a darkroom, a white wall and a mirror both look dark." Is this scientifically accurate? Explain.Hard
Show Answer

Yes. Both the wall and mirror are non-luminous — they need incident light to reflect. With no light source in the room, neither reflects any light to the eye. Both appear equally dark, demonstrating that visibility requires a light source.
How do laws of reflection explain the formation of a virtual image in a plane mirror? Why can the image not be caught on a screen?Hard
Show Answer

Reflected rays from a plane mirror diverge. When extended behind the mirror they appear to meet at a point — this is the virtual image. Since actual light rays never converge there, no real light energy is present behind the mirror; a screen placed there would not intercept any rays, so the image cannot be projected.
Compare the image characteristics formed by (i) a plane mirror (ii) a rough white paper, for the same incident beam.Hard
Show Answer

(i) Plane mirror: regular reflection → virtual, erect, laterally inverted image, same size, appears as far behind the mirror as the object is in front. (ii) Rough paper: diffuse reflection → no image formed; light scatters uniformly in all directions. Both obey reflection laws at each surface point.

Set A Easy — Concave Mirrors

What is a concave mirror?Easy
Show Answer

A concave mirror is a spherical mirror whose reflecting surface curves inward (like the inside of a bowl). It is also called a converging mirror.
What is the relationship between focal length (f) and radius of curvature (R) for a spherical mirror?Easy
Show Answer

f = R/2. The focal length is half the radius of curvature.
Where does a concave mirror form the image when the object is beyond C?Easy
Show Answer

Between F and C. The image is real, inverted, and smaller than the object.
What happens to parallel rays after reflecting off a concave mirror?Easy
Show Answer

All parallel rays converge at the principal focus (F) after reflection. This is why the concave mirror is called a converging mirror.
State one use of a concave mirror in daily life.Easy
Show Answer

Concave mirrors are used as shaving/makeup mirrors — they form a magnified, erect, virtual image when the face is within the focal length.
Where is the object placed so that a concave mirror forms a real, inverted image of the same size?Easy
Show Answer

At the centre of curvature (C). The image also forms at C — real, inverted, same size.
What type of image does a concave mirror form when the object is between F and P?Easy
Show Answer

Virtual, erect, and magnified. The image is behind the mirror and cannot be projected on a screen.
Name the point on a concave mirror where all parallel rays meet after reflection.Easy
Show Answer

The principal focus (F), which lies on the principal axis at a distance f (focal length) in front of the mirror.

Set B Medium — Concave Mirrors

A concave mirror has R = 30 cm. Find its focal length and state where the image forms when object is at 40 cm.Medium
Show Answer

f = R/2 = 15 cm. Object is at 40 cm (beyond C=30 cm). Image forms between F and C — real, inverted, smaller.
Why is a concave mirror used in solar furnaces?Medium
Show Answer

A large concave mirror converges parallel solar rays at its focus, concentrating a large amount of solar energy to a small area, generating very high temperatures sufficient to melt metals.

Draw ray rules for concave mirrors — list the three standard rays used in ray diagrams.Medium
Show Answer

(1) Ray parallel to principal axis → reflects through F. (2) Ray passing through F → reflects parallel to principal axis. (3) Ray passing through C → reflects back along the same path.
Explain why a concave mirror is used in the headlights of vehicles.Medium
Show Answer

The bulb is placed at the focus of a concave mirror. Diverging rays from the bulb reflect off the mirror and emerge as a strong, parallel beam of light, illuminating the road ahead effectively.
Describe the change in image as an object moves from infinity toward a concave mirror.Medium
Show Answer

∞→beyond C: image at F, real tiny point. Beyond C: between F and C, real inverted smaller. At C: at C, same size. Between C and F: beyond C, real inverted larger. At F: image at ∞. Inside F: behind mirror, virtual erect magnified.
Why does a concave mirror produce a highly magnified image for a dentist examining teeth?Medium
Show Answer

The dentist holds the concave mirror close to the teeth (object inside F). This gives a virtual, erect, magnified image, making small details of the teeth easily visible.
If R = 20 cm and the object is at 15 cm from a concave mirror, find the image position using the mirror formula.Medium
Show Answer

f = −10 cm, u = −15 cm (sign convention). 1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/(−10) − 1/(−15) = −3/30 + 2/30 = −1/30. ∴ v = −30 cm. Image is 30 cm in front of the mirror (real, inverted, magnified).
Distinguish between real and virtual images formed by a concave mirror.Medium
Show Answer

Real image: formed when reflected rays actually meet; in front of mirror; inverted; can be projected on screen. Virtual image: formed when reflected rays appear to diverge from a point behind mirror; erect; cannot be projected.

Set C Hard — Concave Mirrors

An object 4 cm tall is placed 30 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 15 cm. Find image distance, magnification, and image height.Hard
Show Answer

u = −30 cm, f = −15 cm. 1/v = 1/f − 1/u = −1/15 + 1/30 = −2/30 + 1/30 = −1/30. v = −30 cm. m = −v/u = −(−30)/(−30) = −1. Image height = m × h = −1 × 4 = −4 cm (real, inverted, same size, 30 cm in front).
Explain the concept of "conjugate foci" in a concave mirror and give a numerical example.Hard
Show Answer

Conjugate foci: if an object at point A forms an image at point B, then an object at B forms an image at A. For f = 15 cm: object at u = −30 cm gives v = −30 cm. Placing object at −30 cm gives image at −30 cm — they are conjugate foci. Confirmed: 1/(−30) + 1/(−30) = −2/30 = 1/(−15) ✓.
Derive the relationship f = R/2 for a concave mirror using geometry.Hard
Show Answer

Consider a ray parallel to the principal axis hitting mirror at M. Normal passes through C. ∠i = ∠r (law of reflection). F is the midpoint of CM (since triangle CMF has CF=FM for small apertures — paraxial approximation). Therefore f = CF = CM/2 = R/2.
A concave mirror forms a real image three times the size of the object. If f = 20 cm, find object distance.Hard
Show Answer

m = −3 (real inverted magnified). m = −v/u → v = 3u (in magnitude, v = −3u since both negative for real). 1/f = 1/v + 1/u → 1/(−20) = 1/(−3u) + 1/(−u) = −4/(3u). ∴ 3u/4 = 20, u = 80/3 ≈ 26.7 cm in front of mirror.
Why is a parabolic mirror preferred over a spherical concave mirror for reflecting telescopes?Hard
Show Answer

Spherical mirrors suffer from spherical aberration — rays from the periphery focus at a slightly different point than paraxial rays, blurring the image. A parabolic mirror focuses all parallel rays exactly at one point regardless of where they hit the surface, producing a sharper image for astronomical observation.
An object is moved from C toward F along the principal axis of a concave mirror. How does magnification change? Explain with formula.Hard
Show Answer

m = −v/u. At C: u = −2f, v = −2f, m = −1. As object moves from C to F, u decreases from 2f to f. Using mirror formula, v increases (moves farther from mirror). So |m| = |v/u| increases from 1 toward ∞ as u → f. Magnification increases continuously as object approaches F.
Compare the image formation by a concave mirror when (i) u = ∞, (ii) u = 2f, (iii) u = f, (iv) u < f with position, nature, size.Hard
Show Answer

(i) u=∞: image at F, real, inverted, point-sized. (ii) u=2f: image at 2f, real, inverted, same size. (iii) u=f: image at ∞ (no image forms). (iv) u<f: image behind mirror, virtual, erect, magnified. Summary: real images for u>f; virtual image for u<f.
A concave mirror of f = 10 cm forms a virtual image 30 cm behind the mirror. Find object distance and magnification.Hard
Show Answer

Virtual image behind mirror: v = +30 cm, f = −10 cm. 1/u = 1/f − 1/v = −1/10 − 1/30 = −3/30 − 1/30 = −4/30. u = −7.5 cm. m = −v/u = −30/(−7.5) = +4. Image is virtual, erect, 4× magnified, object is 7.5 cm in front.

Set A Easy — Convex Mirrors

What is a convex mirror?Easy
Show Answer

A convex mirror is a spherical mirror whose reflecting surface curves outward (like the outside of a sphere). It is a diverging mirror.
What is the nature of image always formed by a convex mirror?Easy
Show Answer

Always virtual, erect (upright), and diminished (smaller than object) — regardless of object position.
Give two uses of convex mirrors.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Rear-view (wing) mirrors in vehicles — wide field of view. (2) Security mirrors in shops — monitor large areas.
Where is the focus of a convex mirror located?Easy
Show Answer

The focus of a convex mirror is virtual — it lies behind the mirror on the principal axis at a distance f from the pole.
Why is a convex mirror called a diverging mirror?Easy
Show Answer

Parallel rays hitting a convex mirror reflect and spread apart (diverge). When extended backward, the diverging reflected rays appear to come from the virtual focus behind the mirror.
State the sign of focal length for a convex mirror according to the New Cartesian Sign Convention.Easy
Show Answer

Positive (+). The focus is behind the mirror, in the direction opposite to the incident light, which is positive by convention.
Compare the field of view of a convex mirror vs. a plane mirror of the same size.Easy
Show Answer

A convex mirror has a much wider field of view than a plane mirror. Its curved surface captures light from a wider angle, making it ideal for surveillance and vehicle mirrors.
Can a convex mirror form a real image? Why?Easy
Show Answer

No. A convex mirror always diverges reflected rays. They never actually meet in front of the mirror. Only their backward extensions meet behind the mirror, forming a virtual image.

Set B Medium — Convex Mirrors

An object is 20 cm in front of a convex mirror of f = 15 cm. Find image distance using mirror formula.Medium
Show Answer

u = −20 cm, f = +15 cm. 1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/15 − 1/(−20) = 1/15 + 1/20 = 4/60 + 3/60 = 7/60. v = 60/7 ≈ +8.6 cm behind the mirror. Image is virtual, erect, diminished.
Why are convex mirrors used as rear-view mirrors in cars despite giving smaller images?Medium
Show Answer

Convex mirrors give a wider field of view (cover more area behind the car), produce erect images for easy interpretation, and always keep the image within the mirror regardless of distance. The trade-off of smaller image size is acceptable for safety monitoring.
Derive the magnification formula for mirrors and calculate it for a convex mirror with u = −25 cm, f = +10 cm.Medium
Show Answer

m = −v/u. First find v: 1/v = 1/10 + 1/25 = 5/50 + 2/50 = 7/50, v = 50/7 cm. m = −(50/7)/(−25) = 50/(7×25) = 2/7 ≈ +0.29. Positive — image is erect and diminished.
Explain why the image in a convex mirror always appears farther away than it actually is, and what danger this poses for drivers.Medium
Show Answer

Convex mirrors form smaller images, which our brain interprets as being farther away (distance perception relies on image size). Drivers may misjudge the distance of vehicles behind them, which is why rear-view mirrors carry the warning "objects in mirror are closer than they appear."
How does the image change as an object moves from infinity toward a convex mirror?Medium
Show Answer

At infinity: image appears at F (virtual focus behind mirror), very tiny. As object approaches: image remains virtual, erect, and diminished but moves from F toward P (pole). At pole (u→0): image also at P, very close to actual size. Size increases but stays smaller than object throughout.
A convex mirror is used at a road bend. What property makes it suitable for this purpose?Medium
Show Answer

Its wide field of view — a convex mirror reflects light from a wide angle, allowing drivers approaching a blind bend to see oncoming vehicles from the other direction. The image, though small, covers a large area.
A convex mirror has R = 40 cm. Where does the image form when the object is at infinity?Medium
Show Answer

f = R/2 = +20 cm. When object is at infinity, image forms at F = 20 cm behind the mirror. It is a virtual, erect, highly diminished (point-sized) image.
Why is the magnification produced by a convex mirror always positive and less than 1?Medium
Show Answer

Positive: v is always positive (virtual image behind mirror) and u is negative; m = −v/u gives positive value → image is erect. Less than 1: v is always less than |u| for a convex mirror (image is between F and P, object is in front), so |v/u| < 1 → diminished image.

Set C Hard — Convex Mirrors

Prove algebraically that a convex mirror always produces a diminished image for any real object.Hard
Show Answer

Let object distance u = −d (d > 0), focal length f = +F (F > 0). 1/v = 1/F + 1/d = (d+F)/Fd, so v = Fd/(d+F) > 0. m = −v/u = −[Fd/(d+F)]/(−d) = F/(d+F). Since F > 0 and d > 0, m = F/(d+F) < 1 always. Hence image is always diminished. ∎
Two convex mirrors A (f = 10 cm) and B (f = 20 cm) face the same object at u = 30 cm. Compare their image distances and magnifications.Hard
Show Answer

Mirror A: 1/v = 1/10 + 1/30 = 4/30; v = 7.5 cm; m = 7.5/30 = 0.25. Mirror B: 1/v = 1/20 + 1/30 = 5/60; v = 12 cm; m = 12/30 = 0.40. B forms a larger (less diminished) image farther behind the mirror. A larger focal length convex mirror gives less diminution.
Explain how to experimentally determine the focal length of a convex mirror using a plane mirror and a convex lens.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Place the convex mirror facing a convex lens. Use a distant object. Adjust a plane mirror behind the convex mirror so reflected rays from the convex mirror retrace their path (image coincides with object). The distance from the convex mirror to the plane mirror equals R (radius of curvature). Then f = R/2. This works because when rays from the convex lens hit the convex mirror and retrace back, the centre of curvature lies where the object would be.
A convex mirror forms an image 1/3 the size of the object. If the object is 45 cm from the mirror, find f and R.Hard
Show Answer

m = +1/3 (erect). m = −v/u → 1/3 = −v/(−45) → v = +15 cm. 1/f = 1/v + 1/u = 1/15 + 1/(−45) = 3/45 − 1/45 = 2/45. f = +22.5 cm. R = 2f = 45 cm.
Compare concave and convex mirrors systematically across: nature of focus, image nature, uses, and sign of f.Hard
Show Answer

Concave: real focus (in front), can form real OR virtual image, uses: headlights/solar furnace/shaving mirror, f is negative. Convex: virtual focus (behind), always forms virtual erect diminished image, uses: rear-view/security mirrors, f is positive. Concave converges; convex diverges.
Why does increasing the radius of curvature of a convex mirror improve its usefulness as a security camera?Hard
Show Answer

Larger R → larger f → mirror is flatter (less curved). A flatter convex mirror covers an even wider field of view, capturing more of the surroundings in one image. Also, with larger f, magnification m = F/(d+F) approaches 1 less steeply — images are less distorted for nearby objects. Overall spatial coverage improves.
Show that for a convex mirror, the image distance is always less than the focal length (v < f).Hard
Show Answer

v = Fd/(d+F). We need to show v < F. Fd/(d+F) < F → d/(d+F) < 1 → d < d+F → 0 < F, which is always true since F > 0. Hence v < f for all real objects. ∎ The image always lies between the pole and the focus.
A vehicle driver can see objects in a convex rear-view mirror (f = 15 cm) up to 300 cm behind. What is the image distance and what fraction of actual size is the image?Hard
Show Answer

u = −300 cm, f = +15 cm. 1/v = 1/15 + 1/300 = 20/300 + 1/300 = 21/300. v = 300/21 ≈ 14.3 cm. m = v/|u| = 14.3/300 ≈ 0.048. The image is only ~4.8% of the actual object size — very diminished but still visible and upright.

Set A Easy — Mirror Formula

Write the mirror formula.Easy
Show Answer

1/v + 1/u = 1/f, where v = image distance, u = object distance, f = focal length (all measured from the pole).
State the New Cartesian Sign Convention for mirrors.Easy
Show Answer

All distances are measured from the pole. Distances in the direction of incident light (in front of mirror) are positive; distances opposite to incident light (behind mirror) are negative. Heights above principal axis are positive; below are negative.
Write the formula for linear magnification (m) in terms of v and u.Easy
Show Answer

m = −v/u = h'/h, where h' is image height and h is object height.
What does a negative magnification indicate?Easy
Show Answer

Negative magnification means the image is real and inverted (upside down) relative to the object.
What does m = +2 tell us about an image?Easy
Show Answer

m = +2 means the image is virtual, erect, and magnified to twice the size of the object.
An object is placed at u = −20 cm and f = −10 cm. Find v.Easy
Show Answer

1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/(−10) − 1/(−20) = −1/10 + 1/20 = −1/20. v = −20 cm. Image forms 20 cm in front of mirror.
What is the sign of u (object distance) for a real object in front of a mirror?Easy
Show Answer

Negative (−). The object is in front of the mirror, which is in the direction opposite to incident light (which travels toward the mirror from the object side).
If m = −1, what does this mean about the image?Easy
Show Answer

The image is real, inverted, and exactly the same size as the object. The object is at the centre of curvature (C).

Set B Medium — Mirror Formula

An object 5 cm tall is placed 25 cm in front of a concave mirror of f = 15 cm. Find image position, magnification, and image size.Medium
Show Answer

u = −25, f = −15. 1/v = −1/15 + 1/25 = −5/75 + 3/75 = −2/75 → v = −37.5 cm. m = −(−37.5)/(−25) = −1.5. Image height = −1.5 × 5 = −7.5 cm. Real, inverted, magnified, 37.5 cm in front of mirror.
Use sign convention to explain why a concave mirror has negative f and a convex mirror has positive f.Medium
Show Answer

Concave mirror: focus is in front of mirror (where incident light travels from object). This direction is taken as negative in Cartesian convention, so f is negative. Convex mirror: focus is behind the mirror (opposite to incident light direction), so f is positive.
A concave mirror produces an image at v = −60 cm when u = −20 cm. Find f and R.Medium
Show Answer

1/f = 1/v + 1/u = 1/(−60) + 1/(−20) = −1/60 − 3/60 = −4/60. f = −15 cm. R = 2f = −30 cm (magnitude 30 cm).
What does m = 0.5 tell you about the mirror type and image characteristics?Medium
Show Answer

m = +0.5: positive → virtual, erect image. |m| < 1 → diminished. This could be a convex mirror or a concave mirror with u > f (but the positive sign rules out real image from concave). Most likely a convex mirror or concave mirror beyond C giving such a result isn't possible without virtual image — so it's a convex mirror scenario.
Using the mirror formula, show that as u → ∞, v → f for a concave mirror.Medium
Show Answer

1/v = 1/f − 1/u. As u → ∞, 1/u → 0. Therefore 1/v → 1/f, so v → f. This means parallel rays from a very distant object converge at the focus F. ✓
An image formed by a mirror is twice the size of the object and erect. The object is 10 cm from the mirror. Find mirror type and f.Medium
Show Answer

m = +2 (erect, magnified) → virtual image. Only a concave mirror (object inside F) or convex mirror can give m > 1 virtual. Since m = +2 > 1, it must be concave. m = −v/u → 2 = −v/(−10) → v = −20 cm? No: for virtual m=+2: v = +20 cm (behind mirror). 1/f = 1/20 + 1/(−10) = 1/20 − 2/20 = −1/20. f = −20 cm → concave, f = 20 cm.
A convex mirror of f = +25 cm has an object at u = −50 cm. Calculate image position and magnification.Medium
Show Answer

1/v = 1/25 + 1/50 = 2/50 + 1/50 = 3/50 → v = 50/3 ≈ +16.7 cm. m = −v/u = −(50/3)/(−50) = 1/3 ≈ +0.33. Virtual, erect, diminished to 1/3 size.
Why is it important to apply sign convention consistently in mirror problems?Medium
Show Answer

Inconsistent signs give wrong image positions and nature. The sign convention encodes real vs virtual, front vs behind, upright vs inverted. Without it, the mirror formula cannot distinguish between concave/convex mirrors or real/virtual images — the mathematics would be ambiguous.

Set C Hard — Mirror Formula

Derive the mirror formula 1/v + 1/u = 1/f using a ray diagram for a concave mirror with object beyond C.Hard
Show Answer

Draw concave mirror. Object AB at u, image A'B' at v. Two rays: (1) through F → parallel, (2) parallel → through F. Triangles formed are similar. From △ABP and △A'B'P: A'B'/AB = v/u … (i). From △ABF and △PQF (where PQ is the ray through F): A'B'/PQ = (v−f)/f. Since PQ = AB: A'B'/AB = (v−f)/f … (ii). Equating (i) and (ii): v/u = (v−f)/f → vf = u(v−f) → vf = uv − uf → divide by uvf: 1/u = 1/f − 1/v → 1/v + 1/u = 1/f ✓
A 3 cm object is placed 18 cm from a concave mirror. The image is 4.5 cm tall and inverted. Find f, v, and m.Hard
Show Answer

m = h'/h = −4.5/3 = −1.5 (negative = inverted). m = −v/u → −1.5 = −v/(−18) → v = −27 cm. 1/f = 1/(−27) + 1/(−18) = −2/54 − 3/54 = −5/54. f = −54/5 = −10.8 cm.
Show that for a real object in front of a concave mirror, the image is real when u > f.Hard
Show Answer

Let u = −d (d > 0), f = −F (F > 0, concave). 1/v = 1/(−F) − 1/(−d) = −1/F + 1/d = (F−d)/(dF). For real image v must be negative: (F−d)/(dF) < 0. Since dF > 0, we need F−d < 0 → d > F. So image is real only when |u| > f. ∎
Two concave mirrors A (f = 10 cm) and B (f = 20 cm) both have object at u = 30 cm. Compare image positions, sizes, and nature.Hard
Show Answer

Mirror A: 1/v = −1/10 + 1/30 = −3/30+1/30 = −2/30; v = −15 cm; m = −15/30 = −0.5. Real, inverted, 0.5× (diminished). Mirror B: 1/v = −1/20 + 1/30 = −3/60+2/60 = −1/60; v = −60 cm; m = −60/30 = −2. Real, inverted, 2× (magnified). Mirror B (larger f) produces a more magnified, more distant image.
An object is moved from u = −30 cm to u = −12 cm in front of a concave mirror (f = 15 cm). Calculate how far and in which direction the image moves.Hard
Show Answer

At u = −30: 1/v = −1/15 + 1/30 = −1/30; v = −30 cm (in front). At u = −12: 1/v = −1/15 + 1/12 = −4/60 + 5/60 = 1/60; v = +60 cm (behind mirror — virtual). Image moves from −30 cm (real, front) to +60 cm (virtual, behind). Total shift = 90 cm; direction reverses as object passes through F.
Prove that m = f/(f−u) for a mirror, and use it to find m when u = −3f for a concave mirror.Hard
Show Answer

From 1/v = 1/f − 1/u → v = uf/(u−f). m = −v/u = −f/(u−f) = f/(f−u). At u = −3f (concave, so f = −F): m = (−F)/(−F − (−3F)) = −F/(2F) = −1/2. Real, inverted, half the object size. ✓
A concave mirror of f = 20 cm is to produce an image on a screen 100 cm away. Where should the object be placed, and what is the magnification?Hard
Show Answer

Screen in front → v = −100 cm, f = −20 cm. 1/u = 1/f − 1/v = −1/20 − (−1/100) = −5/100 + 1/100 = −4/100. u = −25 cm. m = −v/u = −(−100)/(−25) = −4. Image is real, inverted, 4× magnified, object must be 25 cm from mirror.
Using m = h'/h = −v/u, explain why a negative h' does not mean the image has negative height in reality.Hard
Show Answer

h' is a signed quantity in Cartesian convention. Negative h' means the image height is measured below the principal axis — physically meaning the image is inverted. The actual height |h'| is positive. The sign only indicates orientation (above/below axis), not that the image shrinks to zero or disappears. So m = −2 means image is twice as tall as the object but upside down.

Set A Easy — Refraction Laws

Define refraction of light.Easy
Show Answer

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another, due to a change in its speed at the boundary.
State Snell's Law of refraction.Easy
Show Answer

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂, where n₁ and n₂ are refractive indices and θ₁ and θ₂ are angles of incidence and refraction measured from the normal.
When does light bend toward the normal on refraction?Easy
Show Answer

When light travels from a less dense (rarer) medium to a more dense (denser) medium — e.g., air to glass. Speed decreases so the ray bends toward the normal.
Define refractive index of a medium.Easy
Show Answer

Refractive index (n) of a medium is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum (c) to the speed of light in that medium (v): n = c/v. It has no unit.
Why does a pencil appear bent when placed in a glass of water?Easy
Show Answer

Light from the submerged part of the pencil refracts (bends away from normal) as it passes from water (denser) to air (rarer). Our brain assumes light travels in straight lines, so the pencil appears shifted/bent at the water surface.
State the two laws of refraction.Easy
Show Answer

(1) The incident ray, refracted ray, and normal at the point of incidence are all in the same plane. (2) The ratio sin θ₁ / sin θ₂ = n₂/n₁ (Snell's Law) — a constant for given media and wavelength.
Light travels from glass (n=1.5) to air (n=1). In which direction does it bend?Easy
Show Answer

Away from the normal. Going from denser (glass) to rarer (air), the speed increases, so the refracted ray bends away from the normal (θ₂ > θ₁).
What is the refractive index of glass if the speed of light in glass is 2×10⁸ m/s? (c = 3×10⁸ m/s)Easy
Show Answer

n = c/v = (3×10⁸)/(2×10⁸) = 1.5

Set B Medium — Refraction Laws

A ray of light passes from air into water (n_w = 1.33) at 30° angle of incidence. Find the angle of refraction.Medium
Show Answer

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ → 1 × sin 30° = 1.33 × sin θ₂ → sin θ₂ = 0.5/1.33 ≈ 0.376 → θ₂ ≈ 22.1°. Ray bends toward normal as expected (air→water).
Why does a fish in a pond appear shallower than it actually is?Medium
Show Answer

Light from the fish refracts away from the normal as it moves from water to air. The refracted rays appear to come from a point above the actual fish. The apparent depth = real depth / n. For n=1.33, fish appears at ~75% of its real depth.
Explain lateral displacement produced when light passes through a glass slab.Medium
Show Answer

Light refracts toward normal entering glass, and away from normal exiting. The exit ray is parallel to the incident ray but shifted sideways — this shift is lateral displacement. It increases with slab thickness, angle of incidence, and higher refractive index.
Why does a swimming pool always look shallower than it is?Medium
Show Answer

Apparent depth = real depth / n. Water has n ≈ 1.33, so apparent depth ≈ real depth × 0.75. A 2-metre-deep pool appears only ~1.5 m deep. This is due to refraction of light as it exits water into air — rays appear to come from a shallower point.
A ray hits a glass surface (n=1.6) at 45°. Find the angle of refraction.Medium
Show Answer

1 × sin 45° = 1.6 × sin θ₂. sin θ₂ = 0.707/1.6 = 0.442. θ₂ = sin⁻¹(0.442) ≈ 26.2°. Ray bends toward normal inside glass.
Why does light change direction but NOT change frequency during refraction?Medium
Show Answer

Refraction is caused by a change in speed (v = fλ). The frequency f is set by the source and does not change (energy of photons depends on frequency). Only the wavelength λ and speed change. The colour (and thus frequency) remains the same in both media.
Define optical density and explain its relationship to refractive index.Medium
Show Answer

Optical density is the ability of a medium to refract light — higher optical density means more bending toward normal. It is directly proportional to refractive index n = c/v. A denser optical medium has higher n and slower light speed. Note: optical density ≠ physical/mass density (e.g., kerosene is optically denser than water but physically lighter).
If light travels from medium A (n=1.2) to medium B (n=1.8) at 40° incidence, is the refracted angle larger or smaller? Calculate it.Medium
Show Answer

Going from less dense (n=1.2) to more dense (n=1.8), light bends toward normal → θ₂ < θ₁. 1.2 sin 40° = 1.8 sin θ₂. sin θ₂ = 1.2 × 0.643 / 1.8 = 0.429. θ₂ ≈ 25.4° (smaller as expected).

Set C Hard — Refraction Laws

Derive the expression for apparent depth using Snell's Law for small angles.Hard
Show Answer

For nearly normal incidence, sin θ ≈ tan θ. Object at real depth d in medium n₂, observer in n₁. tan θ₂ = x/d (x = horizontal distance); tan θ₁ = x/d_app. Snell's Law: n₂ sin θ₂ = n₁ sin θ₁ → n₂ × (x/d) = n₁ × (x/d_app). Therefore d_app = (n₁/n₂) × d. For water→air: d_app = d/n. ∎
A glass slab of thickness 6 cm and n = 1.5 is placed in air. A ray hits at 60°. Find lateral displacement.Hard
Show Answer

sin θ₂ = sin 60°/1.5 = 0.866/1.5 = 0.577 → θ₂ = 35.3°. Lateral displacement d = t(sin(θ₁ − θ₂))/cos θ₂ = 6 × sin(60°−35.3°)/cos 35.3° = 6 × sin 24.7°/0.816 = 6 × 0.418/0.816 ≈ 3.07 cm.
Why does a star appear to twinkle but planets do not?Hard
Show Answer

Stars are point sources (very distant). Atmospheric refraction from layers of varying density causes their single light ray to bend differently over time, causing apparent position and brightness to fluctuate — twinkling. Planets are extended sources (discs); their many rays average out the fluctuations, so planets appear steady.
Light travels through three media with n₁=1, n₂=1.5, n₃=1.2 in slabs. Angle of incidence in medium 1 is 30°. Find angle in medium 3.Hard
Show Answer

At boundary 1-2: 1×sin30° = 1.5×sinθ₂. At boundary 2-3: 1.5×sinθ₂ = 1.2×sinθ₃. Combining: 1×sin30° = 1.2×sinθ₃ → sinθ₃ = 0.5/1.2 = 0.417 → θ₃ ≈ 24.6°. The intermediate medium doesn't matter — only the first and last n determine the final angle.
Explain why a coin at the bottom of a bowl becomes visible when water is added, using refraction principles.Hard
Show Answer

Without water, the rim blocks line-of-sight. When water is added, light from the coin refracts at the water-air boundary (away from normal). The refracted ray now reaches the observer's eye over the rim. The apparent depth also raises the apparent position of the coin, making it visible even though the direct line of sight is still blocked.
Calculate the speed of light in diamond (n = 2.42) and glass (n = 1.5). Which medium bends light more?Hard
Show Answer

v_diamond = c/n = 3×10⁸/2.42 ≈ 1.24×10⁸ m/s. v_glass = 3×10⁸/1.5 = 2×10⁸ m/s. Diamond slows light more. By Snell's Law, diamond bends light more (higher n → more bending toward normal). This is why diamonds sparkle brilliantly — they bend and trap light effectively.
Prove that when a light ray passes through a parallel-sided glass slab, the emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray.Hard
Show Answer

At surface 1 (air→glass): n_air sin θ₁ = n_glass sin θ₂ → sin θ₂ = sin θ₁/n. At surface 2 (glass→air), angle of incidence inside glass is θ₂: n_glass sin θ₂ = n_air sin θ₃ → n sin θ₁/n = sin θ₃ → sin θ₃ = sin θ₁ → θ₃ = θ₁. The emergent ray makes the same angle with the normal as the incident ray, so emergent ∥ incident. ∎
Using Fermat's Principle, explain qualitatively why light bends at a refractive boundary.Hard
Show Answer

Fermat's Principle: light takes the path of least time (not shortest distance). In a denser medium, speed is lower. To minimise total travel time between two points in different media, light "trades off" a longer path in the fast (rare) medium for a shorter path in the slow (dense) medium. This optimal trade-off causes the bending at the boundary — mathematically yielding Snell's Law.

Set A Easy — Total Internal Reflection

Define total internal reflection (TIR).Easy
Show Answer

TIR occurs when light travels from a denser to a rarer medium and the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle — all light is reflected back into the denser medium, none passes through.
State the two conditions for TIR to occur.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Light must travel from a denser medium to a rarer medium. (2) The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (θ > θ_c).
Define critical angle.Easy
Show Answer

The critical angle is the angle of incidence in the denser medium at which the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is exactly 90°. Beyond this angle, TIR occurs.
Name two applications of total internal reflection.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Optical fibre cables for internet data transmission. (2) Endoscopes used in medical diagnostics.
What is a mirage and which optical phenomenon causes it?Easy
Show Answer

A mirage is an optical illusion where a pool of water appears on hot roads. It is caused by total internal reflection — light from the sky hits hot air layers (lower density, lower n) near the surface at an angle exceeding the critical angle and reflects upward to the observer's eye.
How does an optical fibre transmit light?Easy
Show Answer

Light enters one end of the glass fibre and hits the glass-air boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle. TIR keeps the light bouncing along the fibre with minimal loss, transmitting data (as light pulses) over long distances.
Calculate the critical angle for glass (n = 1.5) to air.Easy
Show Answer

sin θ_c = n_rarer/n_denser = 1/1.5 = 2/3. θ_c = sin⁻¹(0.667) ≈ 41.8°. Any ray inside glass hitting the surface at more than ~41.8° undergoes TIR.
Why do diamonds sparkle so brilliantly?Easy
Show Answer

Diamond has n = 2.42 and a very small critical angle (~24°). Most light entering a diamond undergoes multiple total internal reflections, trapped inside, then emerges from specific facets cut at precise angles — creating the brilliant sparkle.

Set B Medium — Total Internal Reflection

Derive the relationship between critical angle θ_c and refractive index n.Medium
Show Answer

At critical angle, θ_r = 90°. Snell's Law: n sin θ_c = 1 × sin 90° = 1. Therefore sin θ_c = 1/n, or n = 1/sin θ_c.
Water has n = 1.33. Find the critical angle for the water-air boundary.Medium
Show Answer

sin θ_c = 1/1.33 = 0.752. θ_c = sin⁻¹(0.752) ≈ 48.8°. Light in water hitting the surface at more than 48.8° will undergo TIR.
Explain how a mirage forms on a hot desert road using TIR.Medium
Show Answer

Hot road heats air layers near the ground, making them less dense (lower n). Higher, cooler air layers have higher n. Light from the sky travels downward through layers of gradually decreasing n, bending away from normal. Eventually the angle exceeds the critical angle, TIR occurs, and the ray curves back upward to the observer — appearing as a sky reflection (pool of water) on the road.
What are the two components of an optical fibre and why must the core have a higher n than the cladding?Medium
Show Answer

Core: dense glass (higher n, e.g. 1.5). Cladding: less dense glass or plastic (lower n, e.g. 1.46). Light travels in the core. For TIR to occur, light must go from dense→rare medium — hence core n > cladding n. This ensures light stays within the core without leaking out.
Compare TIR with regular reflection from a mirror in terms of efficiency and application.Medium
Show Answer

TIR reflects ~100% of light (no absorption/transmission loss) — far more efficient than a mirror, which loses 5-15% per reflection due to absorption. TIR is used in fibre optics and periscope prisms where high-efficiency reflection over many bounces is essential. Mirrors are used for single-reflection applications where some loss is acceptable.
Why is TIR used in endoscopes for medical imaging?Medium
Show Answer

Optical fibres in endoscopes carry light into the body and return images with no signal loss (TIR = ~100% reflection). Flexible fibres can bend around corners inside the body. The image is transmitted as light pulses through thousands of fibres with negligible distortion, allowing doctors to visualise internal organs without surgery.
A medium has critical angle of 30°. Find its refractive index and the speed of light in it.Medium
Show Answer

n = 1/sin 30° = 1/0.5 = 2. Speed v = c/n = 3×10⁸/2 = 1.5×10⁸ m/s. This n = 2 is close to dense glass or crystal.
Why does TIR not occur when light travels from rarer to denser medium?Medium
Show Answer

Going from rarer to denser, light bends toward the normal — the refracted angle is smaller than the incident angle. No matter how large the incident angle, there is always a valid refracted ray. The refracted angle never reaches 90°, so a critical angle doesn't exist for this direction — TIR is impossible.

Set C Hard — Total Internal Reflection

Explain why the critical angle decreases as the refractive index of the denser medium increases. Prove with mathematics.Hard
Show Answer

sin θ_c = 1/n. As n increases, 1/n decreases, so sin θ_c decreases, meaning θ_c decreases. Example: glass (n=1.5): θ_c ≈ 41.8°; diamond (n=2.42): θ_c ≈ 24.4°. Higher n means the medium slows light more; light hits the boundary "more obliquely" at smaller angles before TIR occurs. Diamond's small θ_c (24°) means TIR happens very easily, trapping almost all incident light inside.
A glass prism (n=1.5) has a right-angle. Explain how a 45°-45°-90° prism acts as a perfect reflector using TIR.Hard
Show Answer

θ_c for glass = 41.8°. Light entering the prism perpendicularly hits the 45° face at 45° angle of incidence. Since 45° > 41.8°, TIR occurs. Light reflects at 90°. This gives a perfect (100% efficient) reflection at each 45° face. Used in periscopes and binoculars instead of mirrors (mirrors lose ~10% per reflection; prism TIR loses ~0%).
Calculate the critical angle at the interface between two glass types: dense flint glass (n=1.7) and crown glass (n=1.5).Hard
Show Answer

Light travels from flint (denser, n=1.7) to crown (rarer, n=1.5). sin θ_c = n_rarer/n_denser = 1.5/1.7 = 0.882. θ_c = sin⁻¹(0.882) ≈ 61.9°. Note: the formula is n₁ sin θ_c = n₂ sin 90° → sin θ_c = n₂/n₁.
How does numerical aperture (NA) of an optical fibre relate to TIR and what does it determine?Hard
Show Answer

NA = √(n_core² − n_cladding²). It defines the maximum acceptance angle (θ_max) for light entering the fibre: sin θ_max = NA. A higher NA fibre accepts a wider cone of light. NA depends on the TIR condition inside the fibre — a larger difference in n values between core and cladding gives a higher NA and thus greater light-gathering capacity for data transmission.
Why do optical fibres carry data faster than copper wires? Relate your answer to the physics of TIR.Hard
Show Answer

Light pulses travel through fibres near the speed of light in glass (~2×10⁸ m/s). TIR confines all light within the core with ~100% efficiency, allowing pulses to travel thousands of km with amplifiers only every 80-100 km. Light's high frequency allows enormous bandwidth (many channels). Copper carries electrical signals limited by resistance, capacitance, and skin effect — far lower bandwidth and speed over long distances.
Explain "inferior mirage" and "superior mirage" using TIR and atmospheric refraction.Hard
Show Answer

Inferior mirage (hot road): hot air near ground has lower n. Light from sky curves upward via TIR → appears as water pool below the object. Observer sees a sky image beneath the real object. Superior mirage (cold sea): cold air near surface, warm air above → n decreases upward. Light bends downward via TIR in upper layers → observer sees object apparently above its true position or inverted above it. Both rely on gradual n gradients acting like a curved TIR boundary.
A diver is at the bottom of a pool (n_water = 1.33). What is the half-angle of the cone through which the diver can see the entire outside world?Hard
Show Answer

The diver can only see outside through Snell's window — a cone of half-angle = critical angle. θ_c = sin⁻¹(1/1.33) ≈ 48.8°. The entire outside world (180° hemisphere) is compressed into a circular window of half-angle ~48.8°. Beyond this angle, the surface acts like a mirror (TIR from below).
Compare the sparkle of diamond (n=2.42) with glass (n=1.5) cut into identical shapes, explaining why diamond sparkles more.Hard
Show Answer

Diamond θ_c ≈ 24.4°; glass θ_c ≈ 41.8°. In diamond-cut facets, almost any ray hitting a face undergoes TIR (since θ_c is so small). Light bounces multiple times inside, undergoing dispersion at each exit point, breaking into spectral colours. Glass has a larger θ_c — more light escapes at each surface without TIR, resulting in fewer internal bounces, less dispersion, and much less sparkle. Diamond's uniquely high n is the physical basis of its brilliance.
