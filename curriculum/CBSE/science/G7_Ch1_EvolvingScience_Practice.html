SET B Apply — Use your understanding

A student says: "My grandmother told me garlic cures colds, so it must be true." Explain why this is not a scientific claim.Medium
Show Answer

It is based on personal testimony, not controlled evidence. Science requires repeatable, testable evidence rather than anecdotal reports.

How does the self-correcting nature of science make it more reliable than other ways of knowing?Medium
Show Answer

When new evidence emerges, scientists revise theories. This built-in error-correction means scientific knowledge improves over time rather than staying fixed.

Distinguish between a fact and a scientific theory.Medium
Show Answer

A fact is an observation verified many times; a scientific theory is a well-tested, broad explanation that accounts for many facts.

A newspaper headline reads: "Scientists discover exercise helps memory." What should a careful reader ask before accepting this?Medium
Show Answer

Who conducted the study? How many subjects? Was it peer-reviewed? Could other factors explain the result? Has it been replicated?

Why is skepticism considered a scientific virtue?Medium
Show Answer

Skepticism means questioning claims until sufficient evidence is provided, preventing acceptance of false or unsupported ideas.

Give an example where common sense contradicts scientific evidence, and explain why science wins.Medium
Show Answer

Common sense says heavy objects fall faster; evidence (Galileo's experiments) shows all objects fall at the same rate in a vacuum. Evidence overrides intuition.

How does science differ from technology? Give one example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Science seeks to understand nature (e.g., how viruses replicate); technology applies that knowledge to solve problems (e.g., vaccines). Science often precedes technology.

Explain what "evidence-based" means in science with a classroom example.Medium
Show Answer

Evidence-based means conclusions come from measurable data. Example: testing whether plants grow taller in sunlight vs shade and measuring heights instead of guessing.

A student repeats an experiment three times and gets different results each time. What does this suggest about the experiment?Medium
Show Answer

The experiment may lack control of variables; the method may be unreliable or the student may be making measurement errors. More trials and stricter controls are needed.

Why is science called a process rather than a product?Medium
Show Answer

Because scientific knowledge is always being tested, questioned, and refined — it is a continuous cycle of inquiry, not a finished set of permanent facts.

Design a simple investigation to test whether music affects plant growth. Identify the independent variable, dependent variable, and two controlled variables.Hard
Show Answer

IV: presence/absence of music. DV: plant height after 2 weeks. Controlled: same species, same soil, same water, same light. Grow two groups of identical plants — one with music, one without — measure daily.

Evaluate this statement: "Science cannot prove anything — it can only disprove things." Do you agree or disagree? Justify with evidence from how science works.Hard
Show Answer

Largely agree — Karl Popper's falsifiability principle says science tests hypotheses by trying to disprove them. A single contradictory result can overturn a theory; no amount of supporting data gives absolute proof. However, repeated confirmation builds very strong practical certainty.

A new study claims that a particular food cures cancer. Using your knowledge of how science works, outline four questions a scientist should ask before accepting this claim.Hard
Show Answer

1) Was the study peer-reviewed? 2) Was it a controlled, double-blind trial? 3) How large was the sample? 4) Has it been independently replicated? Additional: Who funded it? What are the effect sizes?

Explain the difference between a correlation and a causation, giving an original example. Why does confusing the two lead to bad science?Hard
Show Answer

Correlation: two variables change together. Causation: one directly causes the other. Example: ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer (correlated) but ice cream does not cause drowning — heat causes both. Confusing them leads to wrong conclusions and potentially harmful decisions.

MYP Criterion D: Reflect on the impact of science on society. Give one example where scientific knowledge changed how people lived, for better or worse.Hard
Show Answer

Better: Germ theory led to handwashing and vaccines, preventing millions of deaths. Worse: Nuclear physics led to atomic weapons causing mass destruction. Science itself is neutral; the application depends on human choices and ethics.

Compare and contrast deductive and inductive reasoning in science. Which does science primarily use, and why?Hard
Show Answer

Deductive: starts with a general rule, applies to a specific case (if all metals conduct electricity, and copper is a metal, then copper conducts). Inductive: observes specific cases to form a general rule. Science uses both but relies heavily on induction for discovery and deduction for testing hypotheses.

Science is sometimes described as "organised scepticism." Explain this phrase in the context of a scientist receiving a surprising result from an experiment.Hard
Show Answer

Organised scepticism means scientists apply systematic doubt: they check methods, repeat trials, share findings for peer critique, and only accept surprising results after multiple independent confirmations. It prevents both premature rejection and uncritical acceptance of new findings.

A classmate argues that astrology is a science because it makes predictions. Write a structured response explaining why astrology does not qualify as a science.Hard
Show Answer

Science requires falsifiable hypotheses, controlled testing, and reproducible results. Astrological predictions are vague enough to fit any outcome (not falsifiable), there is no known mechanism, and controlled studies show astrological forecasts perform no better than chance. Therefore it is a pseudoscience.

Discuss how confirmation bias can damage the scientific process. Give a historical or contemporary example.Hard
Show Answer

Confirmation bias is the tendency to accept evidence that supports existing beliefs and ignore contradicting evidence. Historical example: early physicians ignored evidence against bloodletting because it matched their humour theory. Modern example: a researcher might dismiss data that contradicts their favoured drug's effectiveness. Peer review and pre-registration of experiments help combat this.

Create a concept map (describe in words) linking these terms: observation, hypothesis, experiment, data, conclusion, revision. Explain how the process is cyclical.Hard
Show Answer

Observation → leads to Question → forms Hypothesis → tested by Experiment → produces Data → interpreted in Analysis → reaches Conclusion → if conclusion contradicts hypothesis, Revision occurs → new hypothesis formed → cycle repeats. It is cyclical because conclusions always generate new questions.

SET B Apply — Use your understanding

Explain why the shift from geocentric to heliocentric was more than just a scientific change — it was a cultural revolution.Medium
Show Answer

It displaced Earth (and humanity) from the centre of the universe, challenging religious and philosophical worldviews that placed humans as central to creation. It changed how people understood their place in the cosmos.

How did Newton's Laws of Motion change everyday life and technology?Medium
Show Answer

They provided a mathematical framework for predicting movement, enabling design of machines, bridges, vehicles, and later rockets and satellites.

Darwin's theory of evolution was revolutionary but faced strong opposition. Give two reasons why.Medium
Show Answer

1) It contradicted religious creation narratives. 2) It suggested humans descended from common ancestors shared with apes, which many found deeply unsettling to human dignity/identity.

How does Einstein's relativity differ from Newton's view of gravity?Medium
Show Answer

Newton saw gravity as a force acting between masses across empty space. Einstein described gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass — objects follow curved paths through bent spacetime, not due to a pulling force.

Explain how each revolution (Copernican, Newtonian, Darwinian, Einsteinian) built on or overturned previous knowledge.Medium
Show Answer

Copernicus overturned geocentrism; Newton built mathematical laws for Copernican motion; Darwin overturned fixed species concept; Einstein refined/extended Newton for extreme speeds/gravity. Each revolution replaced or upgraded the existing framework.

Why is Darwin's natural selection called a mechanism and not just an observation?Medium
Show Answer

A mechanism explains how something happens, not just that it happens. Natural selection explains the process: variation exists → environment selects → fittest survive and reproduce → traits change over generations.

Newton's equations still work perfectly for designing a car. Why then is Einstein's theory considered "better" science?Medium
Show Answer

Einstein's theory has a wider domain — it works correctly at all speeds including near light speed and in strong gravitational fields, where Newton's equations fail. A better theory accounts for more phenomena.

What role did technology play in enabling scientific revolutions? Give one specific example.Medium
Show Answer

The telescope (technology) enabled Galileo to observe Jupiter's moons and Venus's phases, providing key evidence for the Copernican revolution. Without the instrument, the evidence could not be gathered.

How does the concept of a paradigm shift challenge the idea that science is purely gradual and cumulative?Medium
Show Answer

A paradigm shift involves a sudden, revolutionary replacement of one worldview with another (Kuhn's idea), not just adding facts. Old assumptions are discarded wholesale, suggesting science progresses through both gradual accumulation and sudden revolutionary breaks.

Why was it brave for Copernicus to publish his heliocentric theory in 1543?Medium
Show Answer

It contradicted the powerful Catholic Church's geocentric doctrine. Scientists who challenged this faced persecution (as Galileo later experienced). Publishing required intellectual courage against religious and social authority.

MYP Criterion D: Reflect on the ethical implications of the Einsteinian revolution. How did E=mc² change what was possible in warfare and energy production?Hard

E=mc² showed mass converts to energy, enabling nuclear fission and fusion. This led to atomic bombs (Hiroshima/Nagasaki, 1945) and nuclear power plants. Scientists like Einstein and Oppenheimer wrestled with moral responsibility. Design a timeline comparing when each revolution happened versus when it was widely accepted. What conclusions can you draw about the pace of scientific change?Hard
Show Answer

Copernicus (1543) → accepted ~100 years later; Newton (1687) → accepted within decades; Darwin (1859) → still debated socially 150+ years later; Einstein (1905/1915) → experimentally confirmed 1919. Conclusion: acceptance pace depends on cultural resistance, available technology to test, and whether it threatens non-scientific (religious/social) beliefs.

Darwin's theory initially lacked a mechanism for inheritance. How did the later discovery of genetics and DNA strengthen evolutionary theory? What does this tell us about how scientific knowledge develops?Hard
Show Answer

Darwin observed natural selection but didn't know how traits were passed. Mendel's genetics (rediscovered 1900) and later Watson-Crick DNA structure (1953) provided the molecular mechanism — the Modern Synthesis united both. This shows science is cumulative: gaps in one theory are filled by advances in other fields, making the overall framework stronger.

If you were a scientist in 1600, what evidence would you use to argue for the heliocentric model against a geocentrist? Structure your argument with at least three pieces of evidence.Hard
Show Answer

1) Retrograde motion of planets is simpler to explain with heliocentrism. 2) Galileo's telescope shows moons orbiting Jupiter — not everything revolves around Earth. 3) Phases of Venus match predictions of Venus orbiting the Sun. 4) Simplicity (Occam's razor) — fewer epicycles needed. Acknowledge the weakness: no stellar parallax yet detected (too far).

Newton said he stood "on the shoulders of giants." Who were those giants, and how did their work prepare for Newton's Laws?Hard
Show Answer

Copernicus (heliocentric framework), Tycho Brahe (precise planetary data), Kepler (Laws of Planetary Motion — elliptical orbits), Galileo (laws of falling bodies and inertia). Newton synthesised these into his unified laws, showing how orbital motion and earthly motion obey the same mathematics.

Compare how scientific revolutions happen in biology versus physics. Are the patterns the same?Hard
Show Answer

Both follow Kuhn's pattern: normal science → anomalies accumulate → crisis → new paradigm. In physics, mathematical precision enables sharper paradigm breaks (Newton vs Einstein). In biology, revolutions are broader and more gradual — Darwin's theory merged with genetics over decades. Biology's complexity means revolutions often integrate rather than wholly replace prior frameworks.

The letter should identify current anomalies (e.g., we can't explain 95% of the universe — dark matter and dark energy) and speculate on what new framework might replace current models. SET B Apply — Use your understanding

Explain how the invention of zero changed mathematics. What would arithmetic be like without it?Medium
Show Answer

Zero is a placeholder in positional notation (distinguishing 1, 10, 100) and a number itself. Without it, calculations require systems like Roman numerals — no place value, no algebra, no calculus, no computers. Almost all modern science and technology depends on zero.

How did Brahmagupta's rules for negative numbers advance algebra?Medium
Show Answer

He defined rules like: negative × negative = positive, negative + positive depends on magnitude. This allowed equations to have solutions in contexts below zero (debt, temperature below 0, coordinates) — essential for algebra and later physics.

Compare Sushruta's surgical knowledge to what was available in Europe at the same time period.Medium
Show Answer

While Sushruta (c. 600 BCE) was performing complex surgeries including rhinoplasty and cataract removal, European medicine was largely based on humour theory with minimal surgical practice. Systematic surgical classification and technique were far more advanced in ancient India at that time.

Why might India's scientific contributions be less well known in Western textbooks? Is this a problem?Medium
Show Answer

Eurocentric history of science focused on Greek and European contributions. Many Indian texts were not translated until recently, and colonial-era education marginalised non-Western knowledge. Yes, it is a problem — incomplete history of science gives students a skewed view of human intellectual achievement and national scientific identity.

Give two examples of how ancient Indian science influenced modern mathematics or medicine.Medium
Show Answer

1) The decimal system and zero, transmitted through Arab scholars (al-Khwarizmi) to Europe, underpins all modern mathematics and computing. 2) Ayurvedic surgery concepts (particularly rhinoplasty) influenced reconstructive surgery in Europe when British surgeons observed Indian techniques in the 18th century.

How was Aryabhatta's astronomy similar to Copernicus's ideas, even though they lived ~1,000 years apart?Medium
Show Answer

Both proposed Earth moves (Aryabhatta: rotates on its axis; Copernicus: also orbits the Sun). Aryabhatta used mathematics to predict planetary positions, similar to Copernicus's approach. Both challenged the prevailing assumption that Earth is stationary.

What can modern scientists learn from the Charaka Samhita's approach to medicine?Medium
Show Answer

Charaka emphasised systematic observation of symptoms, classification of diseases, diet and lifestyle factors, and ethical conduct for physicians — all principles still central in modern medicine. It also discusses clinical observation and reasoning, analogous to modern evidence-based medicine.

Why is it historically significant that Sushruta described over 120 surgical instruments?Medium
Show Answer

It shows the high level of systematic, specialised knowledge in ancient Indian medicine — each instrument designed for specific procedures. This level of surgical specialisation was not matched in Europe for over a millennium.

How did the concept of negative numbers change our ability to model the real world?Medium
Show Answer

Negative numbers allow us to represent directions (north vs south), temperatures below zero, financial debts, electrical charge, and displacements. Without them, physics equations could not represent forces in opposite directions or charges, making most of modern physics impossible.

What is Ayurveda and how does it relate to modern medicine?Medium
Show Answer

Ayurveda is the ancient Indian system of medicine focused on balancing body, mind, and spirit using herbs, diet, and lifestyle. Some of its plant-based remedies have been validated by modern pharmacology. Modern medicine shares its diagnostic logic but uses clinical trials to validate treatments rather than tradition.

Compare the transmission of Indian mathematical knowledge to the Arab world and then to Europe. What does this tell us about how scientific knowledge spreads across cultures?Hard
Show Answer

Indian numerals and zero were transmitted to Arab scholars (Brahmagupta's work translated ~773 CE), systematised by al-Khwarizmi (algebra), then brought to Europe via Arab-controlled Spain and the Crusades (12th–13th century). Fibonacci's Liber Abaci (1202) popularised them in Europe. This shows scientific knowledge spreads through trade, translation, and political contact — it is a global, multicultural process, not the property of any single civilisation.

Elements of scientific method present: systematic observation (Charaka describes patient examination protocols), classification (Sushruta categorises surgical procedures), testing (plant remedies tested on patients), documentation (Samhitas are detailed records). Missing: controlled experiments, statistical analysis, peer review in the modern sense. Conclusion: Proto-scientific — systematic and empirical but not fully formalised.

How would you argue against a student who says "ancient Indian science was just philosophy or religion, not real science"? Use at least three specific examples.Hard
Show Answer

1) Aryabhatta used mathematical calculation to accurately predict solar and lunar eclipses — demonstrably correct, not philosophical. 2) Sushruta's 120+ surgical instruments are material evidence of systematic technical knowledge. 3) Brahmagupta's rules for zero and negatives are formal mathematical theorems — abstract but rigorously logical. 4) Charaka's disease classifications and treatment protocols were documented and transmitted systematically — proto-scientific methodology. Compare Aryabhatta's estimate of Earth's circumference with the modern value. What does the closeness of his estimate tell us about his methods?Hard
Show Answer

Aryabhatta estimated Earth's circumference at approximately 39,968 km; modern value is 40,075 km — an error of less than 0.3%. This suggests he used careful astronomical observation (shadow angles, star positions), mathematical modelling (trigonometry/sine tables), and systematic calculation rather than guesswork. His methods, though not identical to modern science, were genuinely empirical and quantitative.

Investigate how Brahmagupta's treatment of zero as a number (not just a placeholder) was philosophically revolutionary. Why did some mathematicians in other cultures resist this idea?Hard
Show Answer

Treating zero as a number means it can be added, subtracted, multiplied. But division by zero is undefined — which Brahmagupta initially got wrong (he wrote 0/0=0). Greek mathematicians resisted zero because they thought of numbers as representing quantities of real objects — "nothing" cannot be a number in that philosophy. Medieval European Church also resisted because zero and infinity were theologically troubling. Brahmagupta's breakthrough was treating zero abstractly, not concretely.

Scientific justification: Indian contributions (zero, decimal system, surgery, astronomy) are foundational to modern science — omitting them gives an incomplete history. Intellectual honesty requires crediting original sources. Societal justification: 1.4 billion Indians deserve accurate representation; global students benefit from understanding science as a multicultural endeavour; correcting Eurocentrism promotes equity; diverse scientific role models inspire broader participation in STEM. Counter-argument addressed: "tradition not rigour" — refuted by evidence of quantitative, empirical methods shown above.

Design a rubric to evaluate the quality of a scientific model. Include five criteria a scientist could use to judge whether a model is good.Hard

Sharing data allows other scientists to verify results, detect errors, replicate studies, and build on work — advancing collective knowledge. Withholding data prevents the scientific community from checking and correcting findings.

Give one historical example of a serious violation of research ethics involving human subjects.Easy
Show Answer

The Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932–1972): US researchers deliberately withheld treatment from Black American men with syphilis to study disease progression — without informed consent. This led to major reforms in research ethics and the Belmont Report.

What is a conflict of interest in science?Easy
Show Answer

When a scientist has a personal, financial, or professional interest that could bias their research — for example, a researcher funded by a tobacco company studying whether smoking causes cancer.

A pharmaceutical company funds a study on its own drug. What ethical concerns arise and how can they be addressed?Medium
Show Answer

Concern: conflict of interest — the company benefits financially from a positive result, creating incentive to bias study design, data analysis, or reporting. Address by: requiring independent researchers, mandatory disclosure of funding, pre-registration of study design, independent data audit, requiring the company to fund independent replication before approval. Financial conflicts must be declared in all publications.

Explain the purpose of ethics review boards (Institutional Review Boards) in research involving humans.Medium
Show Answer

IRBs are independent committees that review proposed research on human participants to ensure: informed consent is properly obtained; risks are minimised and justified by benefits; vulnerable populations are protected; privacy is respected; participants can withdraw at any time. They prevent the kind of abuses seen in Tuskegee and Nazi medical experiments.

How do the 3Rs of animal research reflect a balance between scientific progress and animal welfare?Medium
Show Answer

The 3Rs acknowledge that animal research currently generates important medical knowledge (justifying some use) but also that animals experience suffering (requiring its minimisation). Replace: use cell cultures, computer models, or human volunteers instead. Reduce: statistical power analysis to determine minimum effective sample size. Refine: better anaesthesia, less invasive methods, enriched environments. This is a compromise — not eliminating all animal research but making it as ethical as possible within current limitations.

A scientist discovers that their published results were based on a calculation error. What should they do ethically?Medium
Show Answer

Immediately contact the journal to publish a correction or retraction. Notify key researchers who cited the paper. Acknowledge the error publicly. If the conclusions remain valid with corrected calculations, a correction notice suffices; if conclusions change substantially, full retraction is needed. Career consequences must be accepted — scientific integrity trumps personal reputation.

Why is plagiarism particularly harmful in science, beyond just being "unfair"?Medium
Show Answer

In science, plagiarism: obscures the true source of ideas (making it hard to trace and verify knowledge); may involve also stealing methods or data, undermining reproducibility; damages the plagiarised scientist's career and funding; corrupts the scientific record; if it involves fabrication of credit, it distorts who is building on whose work — breaking the cumulative nature of scientific knowledge.

What is the difference between scientific misconduct and honest error? Give an example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Scientific misconduct: deliberate fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism (e.g., Hwang Woo-suk fabricated stem cell data). Honest error: accidental miscalculation, instrument malfunction, or sampling mistake that is corrected when discovered. Distinction: intent. Both require correction, but only misconduct results in sanctions. Science's openness to correction is what distinguishes it from fraud — errors corrected honestly are part of normal science.

What ethical considerations arise when conducting research on children?Medium
Show Answer

Children cannot give full informed consent (they lack adult decision-making capacity) — parents/guardians must consent on their behalf (proxy consent). But the child's assent (agreement appropriate to their understanding) should also be sought. Risk must be minimal relative to direct benefit. Children cannot be used in research that could be done on adults. Special protections ensure vulnerable participants are not exploited.

How might a scientist's cultural background or personal beliefs inadvertently bias their research?Medium
Show Answer

Cultural background shapes what questions are asked, which populations are studied, what is considered a "normal" baseline, and how data is interpreted. Example: medical research conducted almost entirely on white male subjects for decades led to dosage guidelines unsuitable for women and non-white populations. Scientists can counter bias by: diverse research teams, pre-registration, blind analysis, peer review by diverse reviewers, and conscious reflection on assumptions.

Should all scientific research be published, even if the results could be misused? Explain your position.Medium
Show Answer

Generally yes — suppressing scientific knowledge creates its own risks (secret research, no peer review). But "dual-use" research (e.g., enhanced pathogen research) may require pre-publication review by biosecurity experts. Extreme cases: detailed synthesis routes for dangerous pathogens or weapons may need redaction. The default should be open publication with oversight for high-risk categories — balancing the scientific right to know with the public's right to safety.

Why is transparency about research funding considered an ethical obligation?Medium
Show Answer

Funding sources can create conflicts of interest that bias research outcomes — readers have a right to know who paid for a study to assess potential bias. Transparency allows the scientific community to appropriately weight evidence and detect systematic bias. Concealing funding is itself a form of deception — it prevents informed evaluation of the research.

He Jiankui (2018) used CRISPR to edit the germline of human embryos, creating the world's first gene-edited babies. Evaluate this case using the principles of scientific ethics.Hard
Show Answer

Violations: 1) Lack of genuine informed consent (parents not fully told about risks). 2) No clinical necessity — the children were not at imminent risk that justified this. 3) No independent ethics review or oversight. 4) Germline editing creates heritable changes in future generations who cannot consent. 5) Safety of CRISPR off-target effects in humans was unknown. 6) He concealed the work from scientific community until after birth. Consequence: He was convicted of illegal medical practice in China; global scientific community called for moratorium on germline editing. Illustrates why oversight frameworks for transformative technologies must exist before, not after, they are used.

Compare the ethics of animal experimentation for medical research versus for cosmetic testing. Are both equally justifiable?Hard
Show Answer

Medical research: may be ethically justified when: alternatives don't exist, potential benefits are large (life-saving medicines), the 3Rs are applied, ethical review approves. The human benefit can outweigh animal suffering in serious diseases. Cosmetic testing: harder to justify — cosmetics are not essential for life; many alternatives (cell cultures, computer modelling) exist; already-approved ingredients can be used. Most developed countries (EU, UK) have banned animal testing for cosmetics. The key ethical principle: the scale of human benefit must be proportionate to animal suffering imposed. Cosmetics fail this test; critical medicines may pass it.

MYP D: Reflect on the responsibilities scientists in developing countries have when international research standards conflict with local cultural practices. How should this tension be resolved?Hard
Show Answer

Tension examples: informed consent processes may conflict with community-level decision-making cultures; Western-designed protocols may not translate meaningfully in different languages/contexts; international standards were often developed without input from the communities now expected to follow them. Resolution principles: universal minimum standards (no fabrication, no coercion, genuine consent) must apply everywhere. How standards are implemented should involve local communities — consent forms in local languages and formats, community leaders in protocol design, capacity-building not just extraction. International ethics should not become a form of cultural imperialism, but core human rights protections cannot be culturally relative.

Investigate the history of the Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932–1972). What ethical principles were violated, and what reforms followed?Hard
Show Answer

Violations: 1) No informed consent — men told they were being treated for "bad blood," not enrolled in a syphilis study. 2) Treatment deliberately withheld even after penicillin was available (1940s). 3) Racial exploitation — only Black men studied in conditions not applied to white participants. 4) Deception maintained for 40 years. Reforms: Belmont Report (1979) — established three principles: Respect for persons (informed consent), Beneficence (do good, minimise harm), Justice (fair selection of participants). These became the foundation of modern research ethics. The study remains a key reason why Black Americans have had lower trust in medical institutions and COVID vaccines — showing how ethical violations have long social consequences.

Evaluate whether science itself is objective, or whether it is inevitably shaped by the values and biases of the scientists who practice it. Use three examples.Hard
Show Answer

Science aims for objectivity through methods (controlled experiments, peer review, replication) but is practiced by humans with biases. Examples of bias: 1) Medical research on predominantly white male samples led to sex and race-inappropriate treatment guidelines. 2) Research on socially stigmatised groups (LGBTQ+, drug users) has historically been underfunded or framed pathologically. 3) Cold War funding shaped which physics research occurred. Conclusion: the methods of science create a self-correcting system that tends toward objectivity over time, but the process is embedded in social contexts that affect priorities, interpretation, and who does science. Scientific objectivity is a goal to strive for, not a given — requiring conscious effort and structural commitment to diversity and transparency.

Design a code of ethics for a fictional student science fair. Include five principles and explain why each is important.Hard
Show Answer

1) Honesty: report all data including results that don't support your hypothesis — science advances by truth, not by winning. 2) Safety: design experiments that pose no undue risk to yourself, others, or animals — human and animal welfare is non-negotiable. 3) Attribution: cite all sources; give credit to teachers, partners, and referenced scientists — intellectual honesty builds trust. 4) Informed consent: if your project involves human subjects (even fellow students answering surveys), inform them fully and get permission — respects autonomy. 5) Environmental responsibility: minimise waste, use environmentally safe materials, dispose of chemicals properly — scientists model environmental stewardship. Each principle mirrors professional scientific ethics at an appropriate scale for student research.

Discuss the tension between open data sharing and patient privacy in medical research. How is this tension currently managed, and is it sufficient?Hard
Show Answer

Tension: open data accelerates research (sharing genomic data globally speeds cancer research); patient privacy is a fundamental right and breach causes real harm (discrimination, stigma, insurance consequences). Currently managed through: data anonymisation (removing identifiable fields); data access tiers (full open vs restricted access for qualified researchers); ethics committee approval for data use; GDPR (Europe) and HIPAA (USA) legal frameworks; consent specifically for data sharing. Sufficient? Mostly, but: re-identification of "anonymised" genomic data is technically possible with sufficient reference data; consent forms are often not understood; global data sharing crosses jurisdictions with different protections. This remains an active area where technical, legal, and ethical standards must evolve together.

A scientist discovers that a widely-used food additive may be harmful but their funding comes from the food company that makes it. Trace all the ethical obligations involved.Hard
Show Answer

Obligations: 1) The scientist must report findings honestly — they cannot suppress data to protect the funder. 2) They must disclose the conflict of interest in any publication. 3) They should seek independent verification of findings before going public. 4) They have an obligation to notify regulatory authorities if risk appears significant. 5) If the company pressures them to suppress data, they have a whistleblower obligation to report this to their institution or the public. The fundamental principle: scientists' primary obligation is to public welfare and scientific truth, not to their funders. Scientific independence is not just an ideal — it is an ethical requirement.

Is there such a thing as unethical knowledge — knowledge that should never be sought? Build an argument for and against, using the example of research into making more dangerous pathogens (gain-of-function research).Hard
Show Answer

For restricting knowledge: gain-of-function research (making pathogens more transmissible) risks accidental release; a lab leak of an enhanced pathogen could cause a pandemic worse than COVID-19. The knowledge itself is the weapon — unlike nuclear physics, the "dangerous application" is inseparable from the knowledge. Against restriction: such research helps us understand and prepare for natural pandemic threats; restricting knowledge is a slippery slope; determined bad actors will do the research anyway. Resolution: not all knowledge should be restricted but some research requires biosafety level 4 containment, international oversight, and publication scrutiny — a pragmatic middle ground between "all knowledge is safe" and "ban all dangerous research." The COVID pandemic has intensified this debate significantly.
