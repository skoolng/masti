SET A Recall – SI Units and Measurement

What is the SI unit of length?Easy
Show Answer

The SI unit of length is the metre (m). It is the base unit in the International System of Units used by scientists worldwide for consistency.

How many centimetres are in 1 metre?Easy
Show Answer

There are 100 centimetres in 1 metre. The prefix 'centi-' means one-hundredth, so 1 cm = 0.01 m.

Convert 3.5 km into metres.Easy
Show Answer

3.5 km = 3.5 × 1000 = 3500 m. The prefix 'kilo-' means one thousand, so multiply by 1000.

Name two instruments used to measure length.Easy
Show Answer

A ruler and a measuring tape are used to measure length. A vernier calliper is used for more precise small-object measurements.

What does the prefix 'milli-' mean in millimetre?Easy
Show Answer

'Milli-' means one-thousandth. So 1 mm = 0.001 m, and there are 1000 mm in 1 metre.

Why do scientists use the SI system of units?Easy
Show Answer

The SI system provides a universal, standardised set of units allowing scientists worldwide to communicate measurements accurately without confusion from different local systems.

Convert 250 cm into metres.Easy
Show Answer

250 cm ÷ 100 = 2.5 m. Since 1 m = 100 cm, divide by 100.

Which instrument measures the diameter of a coin most precisely?Easy
Show Answer

A vernier calliper — it reads to 0.1 mm precision, far better than a ruler's 1 mm precision.

SET B Apply – SI Units and Measurement

A student measures a table as 120 cm. Express this in metres and millimetres.Medium
Show Answer

120 cm = 1.20 m and 1200 mm. Conversions: ÷100 for metres, ×10 for millimetres.

Explain why a tape measure is more suitable than a ruler for measuring a curved path.Medium
Show Answer

A tape measure is flexible and follows the curve, giving the actual curved length. A rigid ruler can only measure straight-line distances and cannot conform to curves without repeated repositioning.

A student needs to measure the thickness of one page of a book using only a ruler. Describe the method.Medium
Show Answer

Measure total thickness of 100 pages (e.g. 10 mm), then divide by 100 = 0.1 mm per page. This averaging bypasses the ruler's 1 mm precision limit.

Why might a scientist use millimetres rather than centimetres for small specimens?Medium
Show Answer

Millimetres avoid decimal points (e.g. 3 mm vs 0.3 cm), reducing recording errors. Using the smallest practical unit improves precision and readability.

A marathon is 42.195 km. Convert to metres and to centimetres.Medium
Show Answer

42.195 × 1000 = 42,195 m. Then × 100 = 4,219,500 cm. Convert km→m (×1000), m→cm (×100).

A ruler is placed from 2 cm to measure a pencil which ends at 14 cm. What is the pencil's length?Medium
Show Answer

Length = 14 − 2 = 12 cm. Always subtract the starting mark from the end mark when a ruler is not placed from zero.

Explain why the choice of measuring instrument depends on both the size of the object and precision required.Medium
Show Answer

A ruler (1 mm) suits large objects; a vernier calliper (0.1 mm) suits small ones. Matching precision to context avoids wasted effort and ensures data quality.

A pencil measures 14.5 cm with a ruler and 14.52 cm with a vernier calliper. Which is more precise and why?Medium
Show Answer

The vernier calliper (14.52 cm) is more precise — it reads to 0.01 cm vs the ruler's 0.1 cm. Precision is how small a difference the instrument can detect.

SET C Investigate – SI Units and Measurement (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an experiment to find the average thickness of one sheet of paper using only a ruler. Identify sources of uncertainty and how to minimise them.Hard
Show Answer

Count 100 pages, measure total thickness (e.g. 10 mm), divide by 100 = 0.1 mm per page. Uncertainties: cover thickness included (remove covers), uneven compression (apply consistent pressure), parallax error (read at eye level). Minimise: repeat 5 times and average.

Critically analyse why the international community adopted the metre over local units (feet, cubits). What scientific and social benefits resulted?Hard
Show Answer

Local units varied by region and were based on human body parts, making international trade and science inconsistent. The metre is reproducible and universal. Scientific benefit: consistent global data sharing. Social benefit: standardised engineering parts, fair trade, aviation safety.

A student argues digital devices are always more accurate than analogue ones. Evaluate this claim with examples.Hard
Show Answer

Not always true — accuracy depends on calibration, not display type. A miscalibrated digital ruler is wrong despite showing decimals. A well-calibrated vernier calliper can outperform a cheap digital device. Digital devices reduce parallax error and improve repeatability, but do not guarantee accuracy without calibration.

The metre definition has changed three times (1791: Earth meridian; 1889: platinum bar; 1983: speed of light). Analyse why scientists keep redefining fundamental units.Hard
Show Answer

Each redefinition improves reproducibility. The meridian was imprecise. The platinum bar could change dimensionally. Defining the metre via the speed of light (a universal constant) means any laboratory can reproduce the standard without a physical artefact — reflecting science's drive toward the most stable, universal foundation.

Design an investigation to compare accuracy and precision of three students each measuring the same 30 cm object five times. Explain how you would use mean and range.Hard
Show Answer

Each student measures 5 times. Mean closest to 30 cm = most accurate. Smallest range = most precise. A student can be precise but inaccurate (consistent but wrong) or vice versa. Compare all three students' means and ranges in a summary table.

Evaluate limitations of using a metre ruler to measure the circumference of a cylinder. Propose an improved method.Hard
Show Answer

A rigid ruler cannot wrap around a cylinder; measuring diameter then using d×π introduces compound error. Improved method: wrap a strip of paper around the circumference, mark the overlap, then measure the paper strip — direct measurement of circumference with only one source of error.

A spacecraft travels at 7,800 m/s. Express in km/h and mm/s. Which unit is most appropriate for the public and why?Hard
Show Answer

7,800 × 3.6 = 28,080 km/h. In mm/s: 7,800,000 mm/s. For public communication, km/h is best — familiar from road speeds, making 28,080 km/h intuitively comprehensible. mm/s produces an unwieldy large number with no added clarity.

Propose a method to measure the length of a curved coastline on a map using a ruler and string. Discuss sources of error and how scale affects the result.Hard
Show Answer

Lay string along the coastline following all curves; measure string length with ruler; multiply by map scale factor. Sources of error: string may skip fine details, scale distorts at map edges, string may stretch. Scale impact: a larger-scale map shows more detail, giving a longer measured coastline — the coastline paradox.

SET A Recall – Errors in Measurement

What is parallax error?Easy
Show Answer

Parallax error occurs when the observer's eye is not directly in line with the measurement scale and object. The reading appears different depending on the viewing angle.

How can parallax error be avoided when using a ruler?Easy
Show Answer

Position your eye directly above (perpendicular to) the ruler's scale at the point of measurement. Reading at an angle shifts the reading left or right.

What is zero error in a measuring instrument?Easy
Show Answer

Zero error occurs when an instrument does not read zero when it should. All readings will be offset by this constant amount and must be corrected.

What is the difference between a systematic error and a random error?Easy
Show Answer

Systematic error consistently shifts all readings in the same direction by the same amount (e.g. zero error). Random error causes readings to vary unpredictably and can be reduced by averaging multiple measurements.

Why do scientists take repeated measurements?Easy
Show Answer

Repeated measurements reduce the effect of random errors. Calculating the mean of several readings gives a result closer to the true value than a single measurement.

What is an anomalous result?Easy
Show Answer

An anomalous result (outlier) is a measurement significantly different from the others. It usually results from a procedural error and is excluded when calculating a mean.

What does 'precision' mean in measurement?Easy
Show Answer

Precision is how consistently close repeated measurements are to each other, regardless of whether they match the true value. High precision means readings cluster tightly together.

What does 'accuracy' mean in measurement?Easy
Show Answer

Accuracy is how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value. An accurate result is near the real value even if individual readings vary.

SET B Apply – Errors in Measurement

A student measures a 10 cm rod five times: 10.2, 10.2, 10.3, 10.2, 10.2 cm. Are the results precise? Accurate?Medium
Show Answer

Precise (range = 0.1 cm, tightly clustered) but not fully accurate (mean ≈ 10.22 cm, above true 10 cm). This suggests a systematic error such as zero error in the ruler.

Explain how a zero error in a ruler affects every measurement made with it.Medium
Show Answer

A zero error adds a constant offset to every measurement. If the ruler starts at 0.3 cm, all readings are 0.3 cm too high. This systematic error cannot be removed by repetition — every result must be corrected by subtracting the zero error value.

A student reads a thermometer from below the mercury level. What type of error is this and what effect does it have?Medium
Show Answer

Parallax error. Looking from below makes the mercury appear higher, giving a reading larger than the true temperature. The student should read at eye level, perpendicular to the scale.

Pendulum readings: 1.8, 1.9, 1.8, 2.5, 1.8 s. Identify the anomaly, explain what to do, and calculate the best estimate of period.Medium
Show Answer

2.5 s is the anomaly — far from others, likely a timing error. Exclude it. Mean = (1.8+1.9+1.8+1.8) ÷ 4 = 7.3 ÷ 4 = 1.825 ≈ 1.83 s.

Why does taking 20 measurements and averaging reduce random error more than taking just 3?Medium
Show Answer

Random errors vary above and below the true value. With more measurements, positive and negative deviations cancel more effectively. Standard error decreases as 1/√n, so 20 readings give a mean approximately 2.6× more reliable than 3.

A measuring tape has stretched over time. Classify the type of error and explain its effect on a room's measured length.Medium
Show Answer

A systematic error — every measurement reads smaller than the true length because scale markings are too far apart. The room appears shorter than it actually is. Replacing or recalibrating the tape is required; repeating measurements will not fix it.

Why is it important to know whether a systematic error is positive or negative when correcting data?Medium
Show Answer

A positive systematic error (all readings too high) requires subtracting a correction; a negative one requires adding. Applying the wrong sign would worsen the data — understanding the error's direction is essential for accurate correction.

In 1999 NASA's Mars Climate Orbiter was lost due to teams using metric vs imperial units. What type of error was this and how could it be prevented?Medium
Show Answer

A systematic error from a consistent unit conversion mistake that skewed all trajectory calculations. Prevention: standardise all teams to SI units; implement software checks flagging unit inconsistencies; conduct independent peer review of navigational calculations.

SET C Investigate – Errors in Measurement (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an experiment to investigate parallax error: students read the same scale at 0°, 30°, and 60° from vertical. Predict results and describe how to quantify error at each angle.Hard
Show Answer

Fix a ruler vertically with a pointer at 15 cm. Students read from 0° (perpendicular), 30°, and 60°. Error = |reading − 15.0 cm|. Predicted result: error increases with angle, largest at 60°. Plot angle vs. error to show the non-linear increase and demonstrate why perpendicular reading is essential.

A student claims random errors can never be fully eliminated, only reduced. Evaluate using statistical reasoning and suggest the practical limit.Hard
Show Answer

Correct. Standard error = σ/√n, approaching zero as n→∞ but never reaching it for finite n. Practically, beyond ~20–30 measurements the improvement per additional reading diminishes sharply. Physical sources of random error (thermal noise, human reaction time) also have irreducible floors.

Critically evaluate why scientists report measurements with uncertainty (e.g. 14.5 ± 0.1 cm) rather than a single number.Hard
Show Answer

A single number implies false certainty. The ± value conveys the range within which the true value almost certainly lies. Two results of 14.5 ± 0.1 and 14.6 ± 0.1 overlap — their difference may not be significant. Without uncertainty, it is impossible to fairly compare results or determine if data supports a hypothesis.

Analyse how random and systematic errors could affect an experiment measuring the speed of sound using echoes. Propose strategies to minimise each.Hard
Show Answer

Random: human reaction time causes variable stopwatch readings — minimise by repeating 10+ times and averaging, or using electronic timing. Systematic: stretched measuring tape or consistent start/stop delay — minimise by calibrating tape and using a two-clap electronic method. Report speed with uncertainty from standard deviation.

Propose a quality control investigation for a factory making 10 cm bolts. How would you use sampling, mean, and error range to accept or reject a batch?Hard
Show Answer

Sample 30 bolts randomly. Measure each with a vernier calliper. Calculate mean and standard deviation. Acceptance criterion: mean = 10.0 ± 0.05 cm. If mean is outside range → systematic production error, reject and recalibrate. If mean is within range but std dev is large (>0.05 cm) → excessive random variation, reject and inspect process.

Distinguish between instrument precision and measurement result precision. Can a result be more precise than the instrument? Justify with an example.Hard
Show Answer

Instrument precision: smallest readable division (ruler = 1 mm). Result precision: repeatability characterised by standard deviation. Yes — averaging 100 page thicknesses with a ruler gives 0.1 mm per page, 10× more precise than the 1 mm instrument limit. Random errors partly cancel when averaged, but this cannot beat the instrument's resolution for a single reading.

Evaluate the impact of human error vs instrument error in a typical school experiment. Which is usually larger and what does this imply for experimental design?Hard
Show Answer

Human error (reaction time ~0.2–0.3 s, parallax, inconsistent technique) typically exceeds instrument error (stopwatch ±0.01 s, ruler ±0.5 mm). Design implication: standardise procedures, use electronic triggers, and repeat measurements. Light gates eliminating reaction time yield the biggest accuracy improvement for least cost.

A student gets 9.5 ± 0.3 m/s for the speed of sound; the accepted value is 343 m/s. Analyse the quality of this experiment and identify likely error types.Hard
Show Answer

The accepted value (343) is far outside the measured range (9.2–9.8), off by a factor of ~36. Small uncertainty (±0.3) means high precision but catastrophically poor accuracy — pointing to a large systematic error, not random. Likely cause: distance measured in cm instead of m (100× scale error). Repeating the flawed procedure will not help; the systematic source must be identified and fixed.

SET A Recall – Types of Motion

Name the four types of motion studied in this chapter.Easy
Show Answer

The four types are: (1) linear motion — straight line, (2) circular motion — circular path, (3) oscillatory motion — back and forth about a fixed point, and (4) random motion — irregular unpredictable paths.

Give one example of linear motion.Easy
Show Answer

A train moving along a straight track. Other examples: a ball rolling straight, a car on a straight road.

What type of motion does a fan blade exhibit?Easy
Show Answer

Circular motion. Each point on the blade moves along a circular path at constant distance from the central axis.

Describe oscillatory motion and give one example.Easy
Show Answer

Oscillatory motion is repetitive back-and-forth movement about a fixed central position. Examples: a pendulum swinging, a guitar string vibrating, a child on a swing.

What is Brownian motion? Name one example.Easy
Show Answer

Random unpredictable movement of small particles in a fluid, caused by collisions from surrounding molecules. Examples: smoke particles in air, pollen grains in water.

Is the motion of the Earth around the Sun linear, circular, or oscillatory?Easy
Show Answer

Approximately circular (actually elliptical). Earth moves along a nearly circular orbit at roughly constant distance from the Sun.

What is the difference between oscillatory and random motion?Easy
Show Answer

Oscillatory motion is regular and repeating about a fixed point (predictable). Random motion is completely irregular with no pattern — unpredictable direction changes, as seen in gas molecules.

Is a spinning top an example of circular or oscillatory motion?Easy
Show Answer

Primarily circular (rotational) motion — each point rotates around the vertical axis. If it wobbles as it slows (precession), it also shows oscillatory features, but the primary motion is circular.

SET B Apply – Types of Motion

A car travels along a winding road. Is this purely linear motion? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

No. Linear motion requires a straight path. A car on a winding road continuously changes direction — it is not purely linear but a combination of curves.

Explain how a washing machine drum during spin demonstrates circular motion and not linear or oscillatory.Medium
Show Answer

Each drum point moves continuously around a fixed central axis in a circular path at constant radius — defining circular motion. It does not travel in a straight line and does not reverse direction, so it is neither linear nor oscillatory.

What causes Brownian motion in pollen grains observed in water?Medium
Show Answer

Pollen grains are bombarded from all sides by invisible, fast-moving water molecules. At any instant, collision forces are unequal, pushing the grain in a random direction. This constantly changing unequal bombardment produces the observed random zig-zag motion.

One student says a pendulum shows oscillatory motion; another says linear motion in an arc. Who is correct and why?Medium
Show Answer

The first student is correct. The defining feature of oscillatory motion is the repetitive reversal of direction about a central rest position — not the straightness of the path. The arc is the path, but the back-and-forth reversal makes it oscillatory.

Give one example of an object exhibiting two different types of motion simultaneously.Medium
Show Answer

A rolling ball on a straight path: its centre of mass undergoes linear motion while each point on its surface undergoes circular (rotational) motion about the centre.

How does Brownian motion provide evidence for the particle model of matter?Medium
Show Answer

Brownian motion shows an invisible force randomly pushing visible particles. The particle model explains this: matter consists of tiny, constantly moving molecules. Unequal molecular bombardment from different sides causes the observed random motion — direct observational evidence of the particle nature of fluids.

How is the motion of a satellite orbiting Earth different from that of a ball thrown horizontally?Medium
Show Answer

A satellite maintains circular orbit because it moves so fast that as it falls, Earth's surface curves away beneath it — gravity provides exactly the centripetal force needed. A thrown ball follows a parabolic path and hits the ground because it is too slow for Earth's curvature to compensate for its fall.

A child on a swing completes 20 full oscillations in 40 seconds. What is the period?Medium
Show Answer

Period = 40 s ÷ 20 = 2 s per oscillation. The period is the time for one complete back-and-forth cycle.

SET C Investigate – Types of Motion (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an experiment to investigate whether pendulum length affects its period. Identify all variables, method, and sketch a results table.Hard
Show Answer

Independent variable: pendulum length (10, 20, 30, 40, 50 cm). Dependent variable: period. Controlled: same bob mass, small amplitude (<15°), same location. Method: time 10 oscillations, divide by 10. Repeat 3 times, average. Results table: Length (cm) | Trial 1 | Trial 2 | Trial 3 | Mean period (s). Expected: longer pendulum → longer period (T ∝ √L).

Evaluate the use of a simple pendulum to keep time in a clock compared to a digital quartz clock.Hard
Show Answer

Pendulum advantages: simple, no batteries, period nearly amplitude-independent for small angles. Limitations: affected by temperature (rod expands changing length and period), gravity variation at different latitudes, air resistance, cannot be miniaturised. Quartz advantages: tiny, temperature-resistant, accurate to seconds per year, portable. Verdict: pendulums are historically significant but impractical for modern precision timekeeping.

A student finds adding mass to a pendulum bob does not change the period. Analyse using the physics of gravitational acceleration.Hard
Show Answer

Period T = 2π√(L/g) contains no mass term — period depends only on length and g. Gravitational force and inertia are both proportional to mass, so they cancel exactly. Just as all objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass (Galileo), the pendulum period is mass-independent.

Propose an investigation to verify that Brownian motion increases when a liquid is heated. Describe observations, measurements, and links to the particle model.Hard
Show Answer

Prepare dilute milk at 10°C, 20°C, 30°C, 40°C. Observe under microscope for 1 minute each; count globules making significant movements per 30 s (or track paths on video). Expected: more vigorous motion at higher temperatures. Particle model link: higher temperature → greater kinetic energy of water molecules → harder, more frequent collisions with fat globules → increased random motion. Macroscopic observation directly supports particle-level explanation.

Critically evaluate whether 'random motion' is truly random or could be perfectly predicted with complete knowledge of all particle positions and velocities.Hard
Show Answer

Classical (Newtonian) physics is deterministic — perfect knowledge of all molecular positions and velocities would allow prediction of all future Brownian paths. 'Randomness' reflects ignorance rather than true indeterminacy. However, quantum mechanics (Heisenberg's uncertainty principle) introduces genuine irreducible uncertainty at the sub-atomic level. For macroscopic Brownian motion, quantum effects are negligible, so it is technically deterministic but practically unpredictable due to ~10²³ interacting molecules.

Compare the energy changes in a pendulum throughout one complete cycle. Link to conservation of energy.Hard
Show Answer

At maximum displacement: all energy is gravitational potential energy (GPE), KE = 0. At the lowest point: all GPE converted to KE (maximum). Going back up: KE converts back to GPE. In an ideal pendulum, total mechanical energy (KE + GPE) is constant throughout (conservation of energy). In reality, air resistance and pivot friction convert some energy to heat, causing amplitude to gradually decrease.

Design a model to demonstrate all four motion types to a Grade 6 class using everyday materials. Justify why each represents its type clearly.Hard
Show Answer

Linear: roll a ball along a straight ruler guide. Circular: spin a coin on a flat surface. Oscillatory: suspend a weight from a rubber band — bobs up and down about equilibrium. Random: half-fill a clear bottle with water and glitter, shake it. Justification: each is designed to eliminate ambiguity — the ruler prevents curving; the coin's axis stays fixed; the oscillation has a clear equilibrium; glitter cannot follow a predicted path.

Analyse how engineers use knowledge of oscillatory motion to design earthquake-resistant buildings.Hard
Show Answer

Earthquakes cause ground oscillation at 0.1–10 Hz. If this matches a building's natural resonant frequency, oscillations amplify and the building can collapse. Engineers use: (1) tuned mass dampers — large pendulums oscillating in opposite phase to cancel building vibration; (2) base isolators — rubber pads absorbing oscillations; (3) flexible frames that deform without breaking. Key properties: resonant frequency, amplitude, damping coefficient, and material flexibility.

SET A Recall – Speed Formula

State the formula for speed.Easy
Show Answer

Speed = Distance ÷ Time, written as v = d/t. It tells us how much distance is covered per unit of time.

What is the SI unit of speed?Easy
Show Answer

Metres per second (m/s). Derived from the SI unit of distance (metres) divided by the SI unit of time (seconds).

Convert 10 m/s to km/h.Easy
Show Answer

10 × 3.6 = 36 km/h. Multiply by 3.6 since 1 m/s = 3600 m/h = 3.6 km/h.

A car travels 150 m in 10 seconds. What is its speed?Easy
Show Answer

Speed = 150 ÷ 10 = 15 m/s. The car covers 15 metres every second.

What is the difference between speed and velocity?Easy
Show Answer

Speed is a scalar — it tells how fast, with no direction. Velocity is a vector — it gives both speed and direction (e.g. 10 m/s northward).

How do you convert km/h to m/s?Easy
Show Answer

Divide by 3.6. Example: 72 km/h ÷ 3.6 = 20 m/s. This is because 1 km/h = 1000 m ÷ 3600 s = 1/3.6 m/s.

A cyclist travels at 5 m/s for 20 seconds. How far does she travel?Easy
Show Answer

Distance = speed × time = 5 × 20 = 100 m. Rearranging v = d/t gives d = v × t.

A runner covers 400 m in 50 seconds. Calculate speed in m/s and km/h.Easy
Show Answer

Speed = 400 ÷ 50 = 8 m/s. In km/h: 8 × 3.6 = 28.8 km/h.

SET B Apply – Speed Formula

A train travels at 90 km/h. How long does it take to travel 270 km? Give answer in hours and minutes.Medium
Show Answer

Time = 270 ÷ 90 = 3 hours = 180 minutes. Rearranging v = d/t gives t = d/v.

A sound wave travels at 343 m/s. How far does it travel in 2.5 seconds?Medium
Show Answer

Distance = 343 × 2.5 = 857.5 m ≈ 0.86 km. Sound travels nearly 1 km in 2.5 seconds.

A student jogs 3 km in 20 minutes. Calculate speed in m/s.Medium
Show Answer

Convert: 3 km = 3000 m; 20 min = 1200 s. Speed = 3000 ÷ 1200 = 2.5 m/s. Always convert to SI units before applying the formula.

Two cars start simultaneously. Car A at 20 m/s, Car B at 15 m/s. How far apart after 30 seconds?Medium
Show Answer

Car A: 20×30 = 600 m. Car B: 15×30 = 450 m. Gap = 600 − 450 = 150 m.

A cheetah runs at 30 m/s. Convert to km/h and compare to a car at 100 km/h.Medium
Show Answer

30 × 3.6 = 108 km/h. The cheetah (108 km/h) is faster than the car (100 km/h) — it exceeds typical motorway speeds over short sprints.

A student walked 2 km in 25 min and ran 1 km home in 5 min. Calculate average speed for the whole journey.Medium
Show Answer

Total distance = 3 km = 3000 m. Total time = 30 min = 1800 s. Average speed = 3000 ÷ 1800 = 1.67 m/s. Average speed uses total distance and total time, not the average of individual speeds.

A bullet travels at 900 m/s. How long to travel 1 km? Express in milliseconds.Medium
Show Answer

Time = 1000 ÷ 900 = 1.11 s = 1110 ms. The bullet crosses 1 km in just over one second.

Explain why 'average speed' might not reflect how fast you were going at any particular moment during a journey.Medium
Show Answer

Average speed = total distance ÷ total time, smoothing out all variation. During a journey you may stop at traffic lights or accelerate on a highway. The average gives one number for the whole trip, while instantaneous speed is constantly changing and differs from the average at most points.

SET C Investigate – Speed Formula (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an experiment to measure average speed of a toy car on a ramp. Identify variables, equipment, and how to improve accuracy.Hard
Show Answer

Equipment: ramp, toy car, ruler, stopwatch or light gates. Method: set ramp at fixed angle; measure distance d; release car, time journey t; calculate v = d/t. Repeat 5 times, average t. Improve accuracy: use light gates (eliminates reaction time); use same release point and surface each time. Variables: independent = ramp angle; dependent = speed; controlled = car, surface, distance.

A student times a 100 m sprint at 12.3 s using a stopwatch (±0.5 s). Calculate speed and estimate uncertainty. What does this tell us about reliability?Hard
Show Answer

Speed = 100 ÷ 12.3 = 8.13 m/s. Max: 100 ÷ 11.8 = 8.47; Min: 100 ÷ 12.8 = 7.81. Speed = 8.13 ± ~0.33 m/s (±4%). Reliable enough for performance classification but not world record verification (which needs electronic timing to ±0.001 s). The stopwatch uncertainty dominates the result.

Critically evaluate why average speed is less useful than a speed–time graph for analysing an athlete's 400 m performance.Hard
Show Answer

Average speed gives one value for the entire race, hiding acceleration, top speed, deceleration in the final stretch, and corner vs. straight effects. A speed–time graph shows speed at every instant; its gradient gives acceleration; its area gives total distance. For coaching, a speed–time graph is far more informative. Average speed is only useful for a quick comparison.

Light travels at 3×10⁸ m/s. How long to travel from Sun to Earth (1.5×10¹¹ m)? Express in minutes and seconds and analyse the implication.Hard
Show Answer

Time = (1.5×10¹¹) ÷ (3×10⁸) = 500 s = 8 min 20 s. We always see the Sun as it was 8.3 minutes ago. If it disappeared, we would not know for 8 min 20 s. All astronomical observations are observations of the past — the further the object, the further back in time we see.

A speed camera takes two photos 0.5 s apart; the car appears 15 m further ahead in the second photo. Speed limit is 30 m/s. Was the driver speeding? Show all working.Hard
Show Answer

Speed = 15 ÷ 0.5 = 30 m/s exactly — at the limit, not over it. If the distance were 15.1 m, speed = 30.2 m/s, which would be speeding. This demonstrates the critical importance of precise measurement in legal applications of physics.

Propose a full investigation to find whether ramp angle affects average speed of a rolling ball. Write method, variable list, results table, and graph prediction.Hard
Show Answer

Independent variable: ramp angle (10°–50°). Dependent: average speed (m/s). Controlled: same ball, surface, distance (1 m). Method: time with light gates at base; v = 1/t; repeat 3×, average. Results table: Angle | Time 1 | Time 2 | Time 3 | Mean time | Speed. Predicted graph: non-linear increase — steeper ramp → greater gravitational component → greater acceleration → higher speed.

A criminal's car travels at 40 m/s. A police car starts from rest 500 m behind, accelerating at 5 m/s². When does the police car catch up?Hard
Show Answer

Criminal position: 500 + 40t. Police position: ½×5×t² = 2.5t². Set equal: 2.5t² − 40t − 500 = 0. Using the quadratic formula: t = [40 + √(1600 + 5000)] ÷ 5 = [40 + √6600] ÷ 5 ≈ [40 + 81.2] ÷ 5 ≈ 24.2 s. The police car catches up in approximately 24 seconds.

Evaluate ethical and social implications of average speed cameras vs. instantaneous speed cameras. Which is fairer and safer?Hard
Show Answer

Average speed cameras enforce speed over a distance — drivers cannot slow at the camera and speed up between. More representative of sustained driving behaviour and have stronger evidence of reducing accidents. Instantaneous cameras detect momentary speeding but can penalise careful drivers for brief lapses. Evaluation: average speed systems are generally fairer and reduce the dangerous 'brake-then-accelerate' pattern around point cameras.

SET A Recall – Distance–Time Graphs

What does the gradient (slope) of a distance–time graph represent?Easy
Show Answer

The gradient represents the speed of the object. Steeper gradient = greater speed; shallower gradient = lower speed.

What does a horizontal (flat) line on a distance–time graph mean?Easy
Show Answer

The object is stationary — distance is not changing with time, so speed = 0.

What does a straight steep gradient indicate on a distance–time graph?Easy
Show Answer

The object is moving at a high, constant speed — it covers a large distance in a short time.

What does a curve on a distance–time graph represent?Easy
Show Answer

Changing speed. If the curve gets steeper, the object is accelerating. If it flattens, the object is decelerating.

On which axis is distance plotted in a distance–time graph?Easy
Show Answer

Distance is plotted on the y-axis (vertical) and time on the x-axis (horizontal).

How do you calculate speed from a distance–time graph?Easy
Show Answer

Speed = gradient = Δd ÷ Δt. Choose two clear points on the straight line, read their coordinates, and divide the change in distance by the change in time.

Which line is faster: gradient 5 m/s or gradient 2 m/s?Easy
Show Answer

Gradient 5 m/s is faster — higher gradient means more distance covered per second.

Can the distance–time graph ever have a negative (downward) gradient?Easy
Show Answer

No. Distance (total path length) can only stay the same or increase — it never decreases. A displacement–time graph CAN have a negative gradient, but not a distance–time graph.

SET B Apply – Distance–Time Graphs

A graph shows: (0,0) → (10 s, 50 m), then horizontal to (20 s, 50 m). Describe the motion and calculate speed in the first section.Medium
Show Answer

First section (0–10 s): constant speed = 50 ÷ 10 = 5 m/s. Second section (10–20 s): stationary (distance unchanged). The object moved at 5 m/s then stopped for 10 seconds.

Two cars are on the same distance–time graph. Car A has a steeper line than Car B. What can you conclude?Medium
Show Answer

Car A is faster — steeper gradient = greater speed. If the lines cross, both cars are at the same location at that moment in time.

A graph starts steeply then gradually flattens. What motion does this describe?Medium
Show Answer

The object is decelerating — slowing down. Decreasing gradient means speed is falling over time. The object still moves forward (distance still increases) but covers less distance per second.

From a graph: (0,0m), (4s,20m), (8s,20m), (12s,50m). Calculate speed in each section.Medium
Show Answer

Section 1 (0–4s): 20÷4 = 5 m/s. Section 2 (4–8s): 0÷4 = 0 m/s (stationary). Section 3 (8–12s): 30÷4 = 7.5 m/s.

A student cycles to a park (2 km in 10 min), rests 5 min, cycles home (2 km in 8 min). Describe the graph and calculate average speed to the park.Medium
Show Answer

Graph: rising line (0–10 min), horizontal (10–15 min), rising line (15–23 min, reaching 4 km total distance). Average speed to park: 2000 m ÷ 600 s = 3.33 m/s.

Explain why the distance–time graph cannot have a negative gradient if plotting distance (not displacement).Medium
Show Answer

Distance is total path length — it can only stay the same or grow. A negative gradient would imply distance decreasing, which is impossible for a scalar distance quantity.

A distance–time curve gets steeper over time. What does this tell us about the object's acceleration?Medium
Show Answer

The object is accelerating — increasing gradient means increasing speed. More distance is being covered per second as time progresses.

Student A's line is a straight diagonal; Student B's line curves (starts flat, ends steep) over the same 100 m. What can you conclude about each student's motion?Medium
Show Answer

Student A walked at constant speed throughout. Student B started slowly and accelerated, walking much faster toward the end. If they finish at the same time, Student B's speed increased significantly during the journey.

Design an experiment to collect data for a distance–time graph of a toy car at different ramp angles. Describe data collection and predict graph patterns.Hard
Show Answer

Method: set ramp at fixed angle; record distance at t = 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5 s using motion sensor or marks on ramp. Plot d (y) vs. t (x). Repeat for multiple angles. Predicted pattern: all produce upward curves (acceleration). Steeper ramp → sharper curve (faster acceleration). Very gentle ramp → nearly straight line.

Critically evaluate the limitations of a distance–time graph compared to a speed–time graph for analysing a sprinter's 100 m race.Hard
Show Answer

A d–t graph shows position at each moment; gradient gives speed. To find acceleration requires gradient of gradient — difficult visually. A speed–time graph directly shows speed at every instant, gradient gives acceleration, and area under graph gives total distance. For coaching analysis, the speed–time graph is far more informative. The d–t graph is simpler but less analytically powerful.

Compare using a motion sensor vs. manual stopwatch-and-ruler for collecting distance–time data. Advantages and disadvantages of each.Hard
Show Answer

Sensor: samples at precise intervals, eliminates reaction time, produces smooth continuous graphs, enables digital analysis. Disadvantages: requires calibration; students learn less if fully automated. Manual: develops measurement skills, cheap and simple. Disadvantages: reaction time error, limited data points, cannot capture fast motion. For scientific precision, sensors are superior; for learning, manual is more educational.

A bus journey graph: (0,0), (5 min, 2 km), (8 min, 2 km), (13 min, 5.5 km), (15 min, 5.5 km). Calculate speed in each moving section, total distance, and overall average speed. Identify each stop.Hard
Show Answer

Section 1 (0–5 min): 2000 m ÷ 300 s = 6.67 m/s. Stop 1 (5–8 min): stationary. Section 2 (8–13 min): 3500 m ÷ 300 s = 11.67 m/s. Stop 2 (13–15 min): stationary. Total distance = 5.5 km = 5500 m. Total time = 900 s. Average speed = 5500 ÷ 900 = 6.11 m/s.

Analyse how GPS technology uses distance–time graph principles to calculate a vehicle's real-time speed.Hard
Show Answer

GPS satellites transmit precise time signals; the receiver calculates its exact position by triangulation. By recording position at frequent intervals (e.g. every 0.1 s), speed = Δposition/Δtime — the instantaneous gradient of a real-time d–t graph. The GPS speed display is a rolling average of many such calculations per second.

Compare a distance–time graph and a displacement–time graph for a car that drives 5 km north, then 3 km south, then stops.Hard
Show Answer

Distance–time: rises 0→5 km, continues rising to 8 km total (southward 3 km adds to total distance), then horizontal at 8 km. Displacement–time: rises to +5 km, decreases to +2 km (net 3 km south), then horizontal at +2 km. Key difference: distance is always non-decreasing; displacement can decrease. Displacement graph gradient gives velocity (with direction); distance graph gradient gives speed (magnitude only).

Propose an investigation to compare the motion of two students walking at normal vs. fast pace using a distance–time graph. Describe a fair comparison and explain how the graphs would differ.Hard
Show Answer

Mark a 20 m course with cones every 2 m. Record time when each student reaches each mark. Plot d vs. t for both on the same axes. Fair comparison: same course, same start signal, same timer, same measurement points. Fast walker's graph has a steeper gradient throughout. Both should be approximately straight lines if speeds are constant — gradient difference directly quantifies the speed difference.

A seismograph's d–t graph shows P-waves arriving 200 s before S-waves at a distant station. What does this tell scientists about the waves and Earth's interior?Hard
Show Answer

P-waves (compressional) travel faster than S-waves (shear). The 200-second gap, combined with known wave speeds, allows calculation of the earthquake epicentre distance using the S–P time gap method. Since S-waves cannot pass through liquid, if a station on the far side receives P-waves but no S-waves, this proves the outer core is liquid. Seismic wave d–t relationships thus map Earth's interior structure non-invasively.

SET A Recall – Relative Motion

What is relative motion?Easy
Show Answer

Relative motion is the motion of an object as observed from a particular reference point. The same object can appear to move differently depending on the observer's own state of motion.

A passenger sits in a moving train. What does the passenger see when they look at the platform?Easy
Show Answer

The passenger sees the platform moving in the opposite direction to the train. From the passenger's reference frame, the platform appears to be moving backward — this is relative motion.

What is a reference point (frame of reference)?Easy
Show Answer

A reference point is a fixed position from which motion is measured. The same object's motion looks different depending on which reference point is chosen.

Two cars move in the same direction at 60 km/h and 40 km/h. What is their relative speed?Easy
Show Answer

Relative speed = 60 − 40 = 20 km/h. When two objects move in the same direction, relative speed is the difference of their speeds.

Two trains approach each other at 50 km/h each. What is their relative speed?Easy
Show Answer

Relative speed = 50 + 50 = 100 km/h. When objects move toward each other (opposite directions), relative speed is the sum of their individual speeds.

Is the Earth moving? Justify with reference to a chosen reference point.Easy
Show Answer

Depends on the reference point. Relative to the Sun, Earth orbits at ~30 km/s. Relative to a person standing on Earth, the ground appears stationary. Motion is always relative to a chosen frame.

You are in a car at 80 km/h. A lorry passes at 95 km/h in the same direction. How fast does the lorry appear to move relative to you?Easy
Show Answer

Relative speed = 95 − 80 = 15 km/h. The lorry slowly drifts past at 15 km/h from your perspective.

Why does a passenger in one stationary train sometimes feel they are moving backward when the adjacent train moves forward?Easy
Show Answer

This is a relative motion illusion. The brain uses the adjacent train as reference. When that train moves forward, the passenger perceives themselves as moving backward relative to it — even though their own train is stationary.

SET B Apply – Relative Motion

A boat moves east at 8 m/s relative to water. The river current flows east at 3 m/s. What is the boat's speed relative to the riverbank?Medium
Show Answer

Both move east: speed relative to bank = 8 + 3 = 11 m/s east. The current adds to the boat's speed from a fixed observer's perspective.

A swimmer swims at 1.5 m/s in still water. She swims against a 0.5 m/s current. What is her speed relative to the riverbank?Medium
Show Answer

Speed relative to bank = 1.5 − 0.5 = 1.0 m/s. The opposing current reduces her effective speed seen from the bank.

A person walks toward the front of a train at 2 m/s. The train moves at 20 m/s. What is the person's speed relative to the ground?Medium
Show Answer

Both in the same direction: 20 + 2 = 22 m/s relative to ground.

Explain why astronauts in the ISS feel weightless even though gravity still acts on them.Medium
Show Answer

The ISS and astronauts are all in free fall together. Since everything (including the floor) falls at the same rate, there is no normal force between astronaut and floor — weightlessness results. Gravity keeps them in orbit, but in their reference frame it appears to be zero.

Two cars approach each other: Car A at 25 m/s, Car B at 15 m/s. They are 400 m apart. When do they meet?Medium
Show Answer

Relative speed = 25 + 15 = 40 m/s. Time to meet = 400 ÷ 40 = 10 s. After 10 s, the combined distance covered equals the 400 m gap.

A pilot wants to fly due north at 200 km/h (airspeed). A crosswind blows east at 50 km/h. What must the pilot do to compensate?Medium
Show Answer

Without correction the wind pushes the plane northeast. The pilot must angle slightly west of north so the westward component of airspeed exactly cancels the eastward wind, resulting in a net northward ground track. This technique is called wind correction (crabbing).

A child on a merry-go-round throws a ball straight ahead. Describe the ball's path as seen by a ground observer.Medium
Show Answer

From the stationary ground frame, the ball travels in a straight line after release (no horizontal force — Newton's first law). However, from the child's rotating frame, the ball appears to curve away (Coriolis effect) because the child rotates underneath the ball's straight path.

How does a sailor use relative motion to understand apparent wind versus true wind?Medium
Show Answer

True wind is measured relative to the ground. Apparent wind is what the sailor feels — the vector combination of true wind and the wind created by the boat's own forward motion. A moving boat makes apparent wind feel stronger and from a different angle; sailors must set sails for apparent wind to maximise propulsion.

SET C Investigate – Relative Motion (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design an experiment using toy cars to demonstrate relative motion. Describe what observers at different positions (on a moving car vs. stationary) would measure and how results differ.Hard
Show Answer

Two toy cars on a straight track. Measure each car's speed with light gates (stationary observer). Mount a camera on Car A recording Car B. Stationary observer records vA and vB independently. Camera records Car B's relative speed = vB − vA (same direction) or vB + vA (opposite). Comparing both demonstrates that speed is frame-dependent and Galilean velocity addition applies at everyday speeds.

Analyse why Einstein's Special Relativity challenged classical Galilean relative motion. What experiment forced scientists to reconsider?Hard
Show Answer

Galilean relativity says velocities add: if you move at v and throw a ball at u, the ball moves at u+v. Einstein showed this fails near the speed of light. The Michelson–Morley experiment (1887) found that light speed is the same for all observers regardless of their motion — directly contradicting Galilean addition. Einstein's solution: c is a universal constant; space and time stretch/compress so light always measures c, leading to experimentally confirmed time dilation and length contraction.

A river is 100 m wide. A boat travels at 5 m/s; current flows at 3 m/s south. The boat heads directly east. Calculate the actual path taken and time to cross.Hard
Show Answer

Time to cross = 100 ÷ 5 = 20 s. Current drift south = 3 × 20 = 60 m. Actual displacement = √(100² + 60²) = √13600 ≈ 116.6 m at an angle south of east. The boat lands 60 m south of its intended landing point. To land directly east, the pilot would need to aim northwest to cancel the southward current component.

Critically evaluate: 'There is no such thing as absolute rest — everything is moving relative to something.' Use at least three reference frames.Hard
Show Answer

Frame 1 (Earth surface): a building appears stationary. Frame 2 (Sun): Earth orbits at ~30 km/s — building moves at 30 km/s. Frame 3 (Milky Way centre): Sun orbits at ~220 km/s — building moves at ~220 km/s. Frame 4 (cosmic microwave background): Earth moves at ~370 km/s. Conclusion: no universally privileged 'stationary' frame exists. Einstein's Principle of Relativity confirms that physics laws are identical in all inertial frames — absolute rest does not exist.

How do spacecraft navigation teams account for relative motion between Earth, spacecraft, and target planets? What physics principles are applied?Hard
Show Answer

At launch, the spacecraft inherits Earth's orbital velocity (~30 km/s). Teams aim where the target planet will be when the craft arrives months later. Hohmann transfer orbits (minimum energy paths) are calculated in a heliocentric frame using Newton's laws and vector addition of relative velocities. The Deep Space Network measures Doppler shift of radio signals to determine real-time spacecraft velocity relative to Earth.

GPS satellites must correct for both special and general relativistic effects. Explain why both are needed and what would happen without them.Hard
Show Answer

Special relativity: satellites move at ~14,000 km/h — clocks tick slower, losing ~7 μs/day. General relativity: satellites in weaker gravity — clocks tick faster, gaining ~45 μs/day. Net: +38 μs/day without correction. At the speed of light, 38 μs = ~11 km position error per day. Without both relativistic corrections GPS would give positions wrong by over 10 km daily — useless for navigation. This is a real-world confirmation of Einstein's theories.

Propose an investigation to demonstrate the Coriolis effect using a rotating turntable and marbles. Describe observations from stationary vs. rotating frames.Hard
Show Answer

Spin a turntable at constant speed. Roll a marble straight across it (stationary observer: marble travels straight). Mount a camera on the turntable to record from the rotating frame. Camera observation: marble appears to curve (Coriolis effect). Physics: in a rotating reference frame, an apparent force acts perpendicular to velocity — no actual force causes the curve, it is a consequence of the rotating frame. The same effect causes weather systems to rotate anticlockwise in the Northern Hemisphere.

Two spacecraft are in parallel orbits 1000 m apart, both at 7800 m/s relative to Earth. What is their relative velocity? Discuss implications for docking.Hard
Show Answer

Relative velocity = 7800 − 7800 = 0 m/s (near zero). From Spacecraft A, Spacecraft B appears nearly stationary — this is why astronauts can 'float' beside the ISS despite travelling at 7.8 km/s relative to Earth. For docking, small thruster burns change relative velocity by only a few m/s, making docking manageable. Without this near-zero relative speed, closing the gap would require enormous fuel. Precise orbital matching before docking is therefore essential.
