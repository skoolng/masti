Set A Foundation – Identifying Expressions

Which of the following is a numerical expression? (a) 3 + x (b) 7 − 2 + 4 (c) y × 5 (d) a + b
Easy
Show Answer

Answer: (b) 7 − 2 + 4

Method: Definition Check
Step 1: A numerical expression uses only numbers and operation signs (no variables).
Step 2: Options (a), (c), (d) contain letters (variables).
Step 3: Only 7 − 2 + 4 uses purely numbers.
Key Rule: Numerical expression = numbers + operations, no variables.

Method: Count Terms
Step 1: Terms are numbers separated by + or −.
Step 2: 5 is the first term, 8 is the second, 3 is the third.
Key Rule: Each number in an expression is called a term.

Method: Definition
Step 1: An expression can be a single number, a single variable, or a combination with operations.
Step 2: 15 alone is a valid (though trivial) expression — its value is 15.
Key Rule: Every number by itself is a constant expression.

Step 1: The symbol × means multiplication.

Explain the difference between a "term" and an "operation" using the expression 7 + 5 − 2.
Hard
Show Answer

Answer: Terms are 7, 5, 2 (the numbers). Operations are + and − (the symbols connecting them).

Method: Vocabulary distinction
Step 1: Terms → the numbers: 7, 5, 2.
Step 2: Operations → the action symbols: + (addition), − (subtraction).
Step 3: Operations act on terms to produce the value of the expression.
Key Rule: Terms are "what" you operate on; operations are "how" you combine them.
Can two different expressions have the same value? Give an example to justify your answer.
Hard
Show Answer

Answer: Yes — e.g. 2 + 4 = 1 + 5 (both equal 6 but are written differently).

Method: Counter-example construction
Step 1: 2 + 4 = 6 and 1 + 5 = 6.
Step 2: "2 + 4" and "1 + 5" are different expressions (different numbers used).
Step 3: Both evaluate to 6, so different expressions CAN share the same value.
Key Rule: Value (result) and form (written expression) are separate ideas.

How many different expressions can you write using only the numbers 2 and 3 and one operation? List them.
Medium
Show Answer

Answer: 8 expressions — 2+3, 3+2, 2−3, 3−2, 2×3, 3×2, 2÷3, 3÷2

Step 1: 4 operations × 2 orderings = 8.
Step 2: + : 2+3=5, 3+2=5. − : 2−3=−1, 3−2=1. × : 2×3=6, 3×2=6. ÷ : 2÷3≈0.67, 3÷2=1.5.

Explain why "45 ÷ 0" cannot be an expression we evaluate.
Hard
Show Answer

Answer: Division by zero is undefined — no number multiplied by 0 gives 45.

Method: Definition of division
Step 1: a ÷ b = c means b × c = a.
Step 2: If b = 0: 0 × c = 45 has no solution (0 × anything = 0, never 45).
Step 3: Therefore 45 ÷ 0 is undefined, not a valid expression to evaluate.
Key Rule: Division by zero is always undefined.

Step 2: (ii) "4 +" — an operation with no right operand. Incomplete.
Step 3: (iii) "6 + 2" — both operands present, operation symbol present. Valid.
Key Rule: Every binary operation needs exactly two operands.

Make an expression using four 4s that equals 4. (Classic puzzle!)
Hard

Is the expression 100 − 50 + 25 the same as 25 + 100 − 50? Explain.
Hard
Show Answer

Answer: Yes — both equal 75, because addition is commutative and subtraction can be rearranged carefully.

Method: Evaluate both
Step 1: 100 − 50 + 25 = 50 + 25 = 75.
Step 2: 25 + 100 − 50 = 125 − 50 = 75.
Step 3: Same value — but be careful: 50 − 100 + 25 = −25 (different!). Order of the minus sign matters.
Key Rule: Addition terms can be rearranged freely; subtractions must keep their sign.
Using only the number 1 and addition, write an expression equal to 9. What is the minimum number of terms needed?
Hard
Show Answer

Answer: 1+1+1+1+1+1+1+1+1 (nine terms, nine 1s)

Method: Reasoning
Step 1: Each 1 contributes exactly 1 to the sum.
Step 2: To reach 9, we need exactly nine 1s.
Step 3: Minimum terms = 9. Any fewer 1s gives a sum less than 9.
Key Rule: Adding 1 repeatedly is repeated addition — the basis of multiplication (9 = 9 × 1).
