Set A Recall & Understanding — Easy

What does temperature measure at the particle level? Easy
Answer

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. Higher temperature = particles moving faster on average.

What is heat? Easy
Answer

Heat is energy transferred from a hotter object to a cooler object. It is measured in joules (J).

What unit is temperature measured in at school? Easy
Answer

Temperature is measured in degrees Celsius (°C).

What unit is heat (thermal energy) measured in? Easy
Answer

Heat is measured in joules (J).

In which direction does heat naturally flow? Easy
Answer

Heat naturally flows from a hotter object to a cooler object.

Can two objects have the same temperature but different amounts of heat? Give an example. Easy
Answer

Yes. A small cup and a large pot of boiling water are both at 100 °C, but the pot contains far more heat energy because it has many more particles.

What happens to particles when an object is heated? Easy
Answer

Particles move faster — their average kinetic energy increases and temperature rises.

Is temperature a property of a single particle or of many particles? Easy
Answer

Temperature is a property of many particles — it is the statistical average kinetic energy across all particles in the substance.

What instrument measures temperature? Easy
Answer

A thermometer.

True or False: A very large ice cube can contain more heat than a tiny flame. Easy
Answer

True. The ice has billions of particles; even with low individual kinetic energy, the total thermal energy can exceed that of a tiny flame with very few particles.

Set B Application & Analysis — Medium

Explain why a large swimming pool at 25 °C contains more heat than a cup of boiling water at 100 °C. Medium
Answer

The pool has an enormously greater number of particles. Total heat = sum of all particle energies, so despite each molecule having lower kinetic energy, the pool's total thermal energy vastly exceeds the cup's.

A student says "the iron rod has more heat because it is hotter." Identify the error. Medium
Answer

The student confused temperature with heat. Heat depends on both temperature and mass. Without knowing the mass we cannot determine which rod contains more heat.

Why does a metal door handle feel colder than a wooden door at the same room temperature? Medium
Answer

Both are at the same temperature, but metal conducts heat away from your hand much faster than wood, so more heat leaves your hand quickly, making metal feel colder.

A blacksmith heats a small nail and a large steel bar to the same temperature. Which received more heat from the furnace? Medium
Answer

The large steel bar — it has greater mass (more particles), each needing to gain kinetic energy to reach the same temperature. More total energy was transferred.

Describe what happens at the particle level when you add ice to a warm drink. Medium
Answer

Fast-moving drink particles collide with slower ice particles, transferring energy. Ice particles speed up (melt) and drink particles slow down (cool) until both reach thermal equilibrium.

Beaker X: 200 mL at 80 °C. Beaker Y: 400 mL at 40 °C. Heat flows from X to Y when mixed. Explain using particle theory. Medium
Answer

Particles in X have higher average kinetic energy (80 °C). When mixed, fast X-particles collide with slower Y-particles, transferring energy until all reach a common equilibrium temperature.

Why can steam at 100 °C cause a more severe burn than water at 100 °C? Medium
Answer

Steam carries extra latent heat of vaporisation (~2260 J/g). When it condenses on skin it releases this additional energy on top of the heat at 100 °C, causing a worse burn.

Explain the difference between heat and temperature using the term "internal energy." Medium
Answer

Internal energy is the total energy of all particles. Heat is the transfer of internal energy due to a temperature difference. Temperature measures average kinetic energy per particle and determines the direction heat flows.

What is true about the temperatures of two objects that have reached thermal equilibrium? Medium
Answer

Both objects have reached the same temperature. No net heat transfer occurs because there is no temperature difference.

Object A has more joules of thermal energy than Object B, yet Object B has a higher temperature. How is this possible? Medium
Answer

Object A must have a much larger mass. Total thermal energy = mass × specific heat × temperature. A large mass at lower temperature can store more total energy than a small mass at higher temperature.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Hard

Design an experiment to show that temperature and heat are different quantities. State variables, method, and expected results. Hard
Answer

Variables: IV – volume of water (100 mL vs 500 mL); DV – time to reach 60 °C; CV – same heater power, same starting temperature.
Method: Heat both with identical heaters; record temperature every minute.
Expected: Both reach 60 °C but the 500 mL beaker takes ~5× longer — it required 5× more heat energy (joules) to achieve the same temperature rise.

Evaluate: "On a cold day, cold enters a building through the walls." Using scientific reasoning, explain what is actually happening. Hard
Answer

"Cold" is not a substance and cannot flow. What actually occurs is that heat (thermal energy) flows out of the warm building to colder surroundings. This contradicts nothing — energy always flows from higher to lower temperature (Second Law of Thermodynamics).

100 g iron and 100 g water are both heated from 20 °C to 70 °C. A student claims both absorbed the same heat. Is this correct? Use specific heat capacity values. Hard
Answer

Incorrect. Q = mcΔT. Water: 0.1 × 4200 × 50 = 21,000 J. Iron: 0.1 × 450 × 50 = 2,250 J. Same mass and ΔT but different specific heat capacities mean very different heat absorbed.

Explain using particle theory and thermal equilibrium why a thermometer gives a reading representing the object's temperature, not its own temperature. Hard
Answer

When the thermometer touches an object, heat flows between them until thermal equilibrium is reached — both at the same temperature. The thermometer's small mass means it negligibly affects the object's temperature. The liquid expands to reflect this shared equilibrium temperature, so the reading represents the object's temperature.

MYP D: Give one medical and one engineering example where confusing heat and temperature could be dangerous. Hard
Answer

Medical: A nurse using a heat pad that feels "only slightly warm" but is in contact for hours — transferring a dangerously large total energy (joules), causing burns despite low temperature.
Engineering: A designer who focuses only on temperature (not total stored energy) when sizing pressure-relief valves for a large boiler could fatally underestimate the explosion risk.

Predict the final temperature when 100 mL of water at 80 °C is mixed with 100 mL at 20 °C. Explain using particle theory. Hard
Answer

Equal masses, same substance: final T = (80 + 20) / 2 = 50 °C. Fast-moving 80 °C particles collide with slow 20 °C particles, transferring kinetic energy until all particles share equal average kinetic energy at 50 °C.

The Sun is ~150 million km away at ~5500 °C yet Earth receives enormous energy. Use the distinction between temperature and heat to explain this. Hard
Answer

The Sun emits heat as electromagnetic radiation needing no medium. Its total radiated power (~3.8 × 10²⁶ W) is determined by both temperature and enormous surface area (Stefan-Boltzmann law). Earth intercepts a tiny fraction but that fraction is still enormous in joules — temperature alone does not determine total energy delivered.

Compare temperature and heat across: (a) definition, (b) SI unit, (c) state property vs process quantity, (d) direction of measurement. Hard
Answer

(a) Temperature = average KE per particle; Heat = energy transferred due to temperature difference.
(b) Temperature: kelvin (K) or °C; Heat: joule (J).
(c) Temperature is a state property; Heat is a process quantity (only exists during transfer).
(d) Temperature is measured at any instant; heat is measured over a transfer event.

Explain why temperature is meaningless for a single particle but heat transfer can still occur between two particles. Hard
Answer

Temperature is a statistical average — meaningless for one particle whose KE changes every collision. However, two particles can exchange energy via elastic collision: the faster slows down (loses KE) and the slower speeds up (gains KE). This collision-based exchange is the microscopic mechanism of heat transfer.

A 1 kg copper block at 200 °C and a 10 kg copper block at 30 °C are placed in thermal contact. (a) Which direction does heat flow? (b) Will final temperature be closer to 200 °C or 30 °C? Justify. Hard
Answer

(a) Heat flows from the 1 kg hot block to the 10 kg cool block.
(b) Closer to 30 °C — the 10 kg block has 10× the thermal mass. It takes far more energy to raise it, so the 1 kg block cools dramatically while the large block warms only slightly.

At what temperature does pure water freeze on the Celsius scale? Easy
Answer

0 °C

At what temperature does pure water boil at standard pressure? Easy
Answer

100 °C

What is normal human body temperature in °C? Easy
Answer

37 °C

What is typical room temperature in °C? Easy
Answer

Approximately 25 °C (range 20–25 °C accepted).

Who invented the Celsius scale and when? Easy
Answer

Swedish astronomer Anders Celsius in 1742.

Write the formula to convert °C to °F. Easy
Answer

°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

Convert 0 °C to Fahrenheit. Easy
Answer

°F = (0 × 9/5) + 32 = 32 °F

Convert 100 °C to Fahrenheit. Easy
Answer

°F = (100 × 9/5) + 32 = 212 °F

Is –10 °C above or below the freezing point of water? By how many degrees? Easy
Answer

10 degrees below the freezing point of water (0 °C).

What two fixed points define the Celsius scale? Easy
Answer

The freezing point of pure water (0 °C) and the boiling point of pure water at 1 atm (100 °C), divided into 100 equal intervals.

Set B Application & Analysis — Medium

Convert 37 °C (body temperature) to Fahrenheit. Medium
Answer

°F = (37 × 9/5) + 32 = 66.6 + 32 = 98.6 °F

Convert –40 °C to Fahrenheit. What is special about this temperature? Medium
Answer

°F = (–40 × 9/5) + 32 = –72 + 32 = –40 °F. At –40°, both scales give the same numerical value.

A patient has a fever of 104 °F. Convert to °C and state how far above normal body temperature this is. Medium
Answer

°C = (104 – 32) × 5/9 = 72 × 5/9 = 40 °C. This is 40 – 37 = 3 °C above normal — a high fever.

Explain why using two fixed reference points (0 °C and 100 °C) makes the Celsius scale reliable worldwide. Medium
Answer

Pure water always freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C at the same pressure, regardless of location. These are universal, reproducible physical events — any scientist anywhere can calibrate their thermometer to the same standard.

The weather forecast says 25 °C. A tourist from the USA asks if they need a jacket. Convert to °F and advise. Medium
Answer

°F = (25 × 9/5) + 32 = 45 + 32 = 77 °F. This is pleasantly warm — no jacket needed.

Why does the boiling point of water change at high altitude, and how does this affect the Celsius upper fixed point? Medium
Answer

At high altitude, atmospheric pressure is lower, so water boils at a lower temperature (e.g. ~70 °C on Everest). The 100 °C fixed point is only valid at standard atmospheric pressure (1 atm); scientists must specify this condition.

A thermometer reads –5 °C outside and 22 °C inside. Calculate the difference and convert both to °F. Medium
Answer

Difference = 22 – (–5) = 27 °C. Outside: (–5 × 9/5) + 32 = 23 °F. Inside: (22 × 9/5) + 32 = 71.6 °F.

Why is the Kelvin scale more useful than Celsius for scientific calculations? Medium
Answer

Kelvin starts at absolute zero (0 K = –273.15 °C) where particles have minimum energy. All temperatures are positive, so proportional relationships in gas laws and radiation laws hold correctly — impossible with the Celsius scale's negative values.

Convert 25 °C to Kelvin. Medium
Answer

K = 25 + 273.15 = 298.15 K (≈ 298 K).

A recipe requires an oven at 180 °C. The oven dial shows only Fahrenheit. What temperature should be set? Medium
Answer

°F = (180 × 9/5) + 32 = 324 + 32 = 356 °F.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Hard

Design an experiment to calibrate a liquid-in-glass thermometer using the two Celsius fixed points. Include apparatus, procedure, and scale division method. Hard
Answer

Apparatus: Unmarked glass tube with liquid, ice-water bath (0 °C), boiling water (100 °C at 1 atm), ruler, marker.
Procedure: (1) Immerse in ice-water; mark liquid level as 0. (2) Immerse in boiling water; mark level as 100. (3) Measure distance between marks; divide into 100 equal intervals. (4) Mark every 10 divisions.
Precaution: Read at eye level to avoid parallax error.

Explain the limitations of using water's phase changes as reference points. What alternative do scientists use? Hard
Answer

Limitations: water's phase changes depend on pressure and purity — dissolved salts or altitude shifts both fixed points. Modern scientists use the triple point of water (273.16 K / 0.01 °C) — where ice, water, and steam coexist — as a single-point standard independent of pressure.

A student plots °C on the x-axis and °F on the y-axis. Predict the graph shape, identify slope and y-intercept, and explain what they represent physically. Hard
Answer

The graph is a straight line. From °F = (9/5)°C + 32:
• Slope = 1.8 — for every 1 °C rise, °F rises 1.8 (the degree sizes differ).
• y-intercept = 32 — at 0 °C (water's freezing point), °F = 32.

Why did scientists adopt the Kelvin scale for scientific work rather than extending Celsius to cover negative values? Hard
Answer

Many physical laws (Boyle's, Charles's, Stefan-Boltzmann) require proportional temperature relationships. Negative Celsius values break these ratios. The Kelvin scale starts at true zero (0 K = absolute zero), ensuring all temperatures are positive and proportional relationships hold in calculations.

MYP D: Evaluate whether Celsius is the best choice for everyday global temperature communication. Compare advantages and disadvantages versus Fahrenheit and Kelvin. Hard
Answer

Celsius advantages: Based on water's phase changes (universally meaningful); metric/SI aligned; used by ~95% of countries; intuitive (0 = freeze, 100 = boil).
Fahrenheit advantages: Finer divisions for human comfort range; historically entrenched in USA.
Kelvin advantages: Scientifically rigorous; no negatives; proportional.
Conclusion: Celsius is best for everyday use — internationally standardised, SI-compatible, physically meaningful. Kelvin remains essential for science.

–196 °C is the temperature of liquid nitrogen. Convert to Kelvin and Fahrenheit, then explain why it is dangerous to handle carelessly. Hard
Answer

Kelvin: –196 + 273.15 = 77.15 K. Fahrenheit: (–196 × 9/5) + 32 = –320.8 °F.
Dangers: instant cryogenic burns on skin contact; rapid vaporisation expands ~700× risking pressure explosions; oxygen displacement in enclosed spaces causes asphyxiation.

Anders Celsius originally proposed 0 °C = boiling, 100 °C = freezing. Why was this reversed? What does this reveal about how scientific scales are chosen? Hard
Answer

The reversal (attributed to Linnaeus/Stromer) made the scale more intuitive: higher numbers = hotter, matching human instinct. Scientific scales are chosen partly for practicality and human intuition, not just physical logic. International standardisation (via CGPM/SI) ensures global consistency.

Water is heated from 20 °C to 80 °C. Express this change in (a) Celsius degrees, (b) kelvin, (c) Fahrenheit degrees. Identify the pattern. Hard
Answer

(a) ΔT = 60 °C. (b) ΔT = 60 K (1 °C change = 1 K change). (c) ΔT = 60 × 9/5 = 108 °F.
Pattern: A Celsius degree change equals a Kelvin change (same interval size). A Fahrenheit degree is smaller, so the same physical change is a larger number in °F.

Explain the significance of –273.15 °C. What happens to particles there and why is it theoretically impossible to reach? Hard
Answer

At –273.15 °C (0 K = absolute zero), particles would have minimum possible kinetic energy. Impossible to reach because: (1) The Third Law of Thermodynamics — removing the last energy requires infinite effort. (2) Quantum zero-point energy — particles always retain a fundamental minimum vibration that cannot be removed. Scientists have reached within billionths of a kelvin but never exactly 0 K.

A thermometer is miscalibrated: its 0 °C mark is at true –2 °C, and its 100 °C mark is at true 102 °C. If it reads 37 °C, what is the true temperature? Show working. Hard
Answer

True span = 102 – (–2) = 104 °C over 100 displayed divisions. Scale factor = 104/100 = 1.04.
True T = (displayed reading × 1.04) + (–2) = (37 × 1.04) – 2 = 38.48 – 2 = 36.48 °C ≈ 36.5 °C.
This illustrates why regular calibration of medical instruments is critical.

Set A Recall & Understanding — Easy

What is the temperature range of a clinical thermometer? Easy
Answer

35 °C to 42 °C — designed to measure human body temperature only.

What is the temperature range of a laboratory thermometer? Easy
Answer

–10 °C to 110 °C — suitable for most school science experiments.

Why must you read a thermometer at eye level? Easy
Answer

To prevent parallax error — reading at an angle makes the liquid level appear to be at a different graduation than it actually is.

Name two types of thermometers used in everyday life. Easy
Answer

Any two of: clinical thermometer, digital thermometer, infrared (non-contact) thermometer, ear thermometer.

What liquid is used inside modern mercury-free laboratory thermometers? Easy
Answer

Coloured alcohol (ethanol) or sometimes coloured kerosene.

What is an infrared thermometer and what is its key advantage? Easy
Answer

It measures temperature by detecting infrared radiation emitted by an object. Key advantage: non-contact measurement — no need to touch the object.

Why does a clinical thermometer have a kink (constriction) in the tube? Easy
Answer

The kink prevents the liquid from falling back when the thermometer is removed from the patient, allowing the reading to be taken after removal.

Why is a clinical thermometer NOT suitable for measuring boiling water? Easy
Answer

Boiling water is 100 °C, far above the clinical thermometer's maximum of 42 °C. The liquid would overflow or the thermometer would break.

State one advantage of a digital thermometer over a liquid-in-glass thermometer. Easy
Answer

Digital thermometers give an exact numerical display with no parallax error, are faster to read, and have no toxic liquid risk.

Which thermometer would you use to measure the temperature of a 300 °C oven? Easy
Answer

An infrared (non-contact) thermometer or a specialist thermocouple/oven thermometer with a range exceeding 300 °C.

Set B Application & Analysis — Medium

A student reads a lab thermometer from above at an angle and reads 46 °C; the actual temperature is 45 °C. Name the error and explain how to correct it. Medium
Answer

This is a parallax error. Correction: position eyes horizontally level with the liquid meniscus when taking the reading.

Explain why clinical thermometers must be sterilised between patients. What infection risk does this address? Medium
Answer

Without sterilisation, bacteria and viruses from one patient can transfer to the next (cross-infection). Infrared non-contact thermometers eliminate this risk entirely.

Compare the range and sensitivity of a clinical vs a laboratory thermometer. Which is more sensitive and why? Medium
Answer

Clinical range: 7 °C span. Lab range: 120 °C span. The clinical thermometer is more sensitive — its small range means the tube length is divided into finer intervals (0.1 °C markings), detecting tiny temperature changes needed for fever diagnosis.

Why are infrared thermometers especially useful in food safety inspections? Medium
Answer

They instantly check surface temperatures without contact, preventing contamination. Inspectors rapidly verify fridges are below 5 °C and cooked food above 75 °C across many items.

A lab thermometer bulb is placed in a beaker but the water level is below the bulb. Why is this reading unreliable? How do you correct it? Medium
Answer

The bulb is measuring air temperature above the water, not the water itself. Correction: ensure the thermometer bulb is fully submerged in the liquid, not touching the beaker walls or base.

Why do alcohol-in-glass thermometers show a larger liquid column rise per degree than mercury thermometers of the same bore? Medium
Answer

Alcohol has a greater coefficient of thermal expansion than mercury — it expands more per degree, making the column rise further. However, alcohol has a lower boiling point (~78 °C), limiting its upper range.

During COVID-19, infrared forehead thermometers were used at building entrances. Give two advantages and one limitation. Medium
Answer

Advantages: (1) No physical contact — reduces infection spread. (2) Very fast — enables mass screening.
Limitation: Forehead surface temperature can be affected by sweating, cold wind, or sun exposure, causing false readings. Core body temperature differs from forehead surface temperature.

A mercury thermometer is broken in a classroom. Explain the hazard and the correct response. Medium
Answer

Hazard: Mercury vapour is neurotoxic; even small amounts evaporate at room temperature causing neurological damage.
Response: Evacuate and ventilate; do NOT vacuum (spreads vapour); use a mercury spill kit; seal in a bag; report to hazardous waste disposal.

Describe how a thermocouple thermometer works. Give one application where it outperforms a liquid-in-glass thermometer. Medium
Answer

Two different metal wires joined at one end (hot junction) generate a voltage proportional to temperature (Seebeck effect). Best used in: measuring very high temperatures (furnaces up to 2000+ °C) or rapidly-changing temperatures — beyond any liquid-in-glass thermometer's range.

Why should a lab thermometer be held vertically rather than placed at an angle against a beaker wall? Medium
Answer

An angled thermometer may touch the hotter/cooler beaker walls or base, giving a false reading. It also increases parallax error. Vertical placement ensures the bulb measures only the liquid temperature.

Set C MYP Criteria B & D — Hard

Design an investigation comparing accuracy and response time of: (a) liquid-in-glass lab thermometer, (b) digital thermometer, (c) infrared thermometer. State method, variables, and evaluation criteria. Hard
Answer

Method: Prepare five water baths at 0, 25, 50, 75, 100 °C (verified by calibrated reference). Measure each with all three thermometers. Record: reading, time to stable reading, ease of reading.
Variables: IV – thermometer type; DV – accuracy vs reference, response time; CV – same baths, depth/distance, observer.
Evaluation: Accuracy (±0.5 °C), response time (seconds), range suitability, cost, hygiene.

Evaluate the risks and ethical considerations of using mercury thermometers in schools. Justify an alternative recommendation. Hard
Answer

Risks: Breakage releases toxic mercury vapour; improper disposal contaminates soil and water.
Ethics: Schools have a duty of care; the UN Minamata Convention recommends phasing out mercury instruments.
Recommendation: Alcohol-in-glass (safe, similar range) or digital thermometers (accurate, fast). The modest cost increase is outweighed by safety and the educational value of modelling responsible chemical management.

A clinical thermometer overreads by 0.3 °C due to calibration error. The doctor diagnoses fever above 38 °C. What does the thermometer need to display before the patient truly has a 38 °C fever? What is the implication? Hard
Answer

The thermometer needs to display 38.3 °C before the patient's true temperature reaches 38.0 °C.
Implication: The thermometer would diagnose fever (≥38 °C displayed) when the patient is only at 37.7 °C — causing over-diagnosis and unnecessary treatment. This illustrates why regular calibration of medical instruments is critical.

Explain the physics behind infrared thermometers. What fundamental property of matter does the technology rely on? Hard
Answer

All objects above 0 K emit infrared radiation with intensity and wavelength depending on temperature (Stefan-Boltzmann and Wien's laws). The thermometer's thermopile detector absorbs IR radiation and converts it to a voltage. Software applies Stefan-Boltzmann (P ∝ T⁴) to calculate surface temperature. It relies on the principle that every object emits thermal radiation proportional to its absolute temperature.

MYP D: Reflect on how thermometer technology has evolved from mercury to digital to infrared to quantum thermometry. What are the trade-offs of each generation? Hard
Answer

Mercury (1714–present): Accurate, cheap — but toxic, slow (3–5 min), fragile.
Digital (1980s+): Fast (30 sec), safe, numerical display — needs batteries, can malfunction.
Infrared (2000s+): Instant, hygienic, mass screening — but surface measurements less accurate; environment-sensitive.
Quantum/fibre-optic (emerging): Extreme precision in milli-kelvin, works in MRI fields — very expensive, specialised only.
Pattern: Each generation gains speed/safety but increases cost/complexity. Appropriate selection depends on accuracy need, setting, and risk level.

A student claims: "Infrared thermometers are always more accurate than liquid-in-glass thermometers." Critically evaluate this claim. Hard
Answer

The claim is overstated and context-dependent. Infrared thermometers measure surface radiation temperature; shiny/metallic surfaces have low emissivity and give artificially low readings. A calibrated glass thermometer with 0.1 °C divisions can be more accurate for immersed liquid temperature than a consumer IR gun (±1–2 °C). Accuracy depends entirely on the application — IR excels at remote/non-contact scenarios; glass thermometers excel at stable liquid measurements.

A 30 cm lab thermometer has its bulb 2 cm from the bottom. The liquid is at 2 cm from the bottom at 0 °C and at 22 cm at 110 °C. Calculate where the 37 °C mark should be placed from the bottom. Hard
Answer

Liquid rises 22 – 2 = 20 cm over 110 °C span.
Rise per degree = 20 ÷ 110 = 0.182 cm/°C.
Position of 37 °C mark = 2 + (37 × 0.182) = 2 + 6.73 = 8.73 cm from the bottom.

Why can't a thermometer measure the temperature of a star? What methods do astronomers use instead? Hard
Answer

Stars are millions of km away at millions of degrees — no physical thermometer can be placed near one. Astronomers measure stellar temperature by:
(1) Wien's law: Peak wavelength of emitted light (hotter stars emit bluer).
(2) Spectroscopy: Absorption/emission lines reveal ionisation temperatures.
(3) Stefan-Boltzmann law: Luminosity-radius-temperature relationship. All are remote sensing methods — no physical contact needed.

Explain why thermometer construction must account for the expansion of both the liquid and the glass tube. What would happen if both expanded at the same rate? Hard
Answer

A thermometer works because the liquid expands more than the glass — this net difference drives the liquid up the tube. If both expanded at the same rate, the liquid would not rise relative to the tube — the thermometer would show no reading change regardless of temperature. Thermometer liquids are chosen because their thermal expansivity greatly exceeds that of glass (~30× for mercury vs glass).

MYP B: Three digital thermometers are tested. A reads consistently 0.5 °C high; B gives random readings ±1.5 °C; C reads correctly. Distinguish systematic and random error. Which thermometer shows which? How is each corrected? Hard
Answer

Systematic error: Consistent, predictable offset in one direction. Thermometer A — corrected by subtracting 0.5 °C from all readings or recalibrating.
Random error: Unpredictable fluctuations with no consistent direction. Thermometer B — reduced by taking multiple readings and averaging; device may need repair.
Thermometer C shows neither error — it is the most reliable instrument.

Set A Recall & Understanding — Easy

What happens to most solids when they are heated? Easy
Answer

Most solids expand — they increase in size — when heated, because their particles vibrate more vigorously and push further apart.

Why are gaps left between railway tracks? Easy
Answer

The gaps allow the metal rails to expand in hot weather without buckling or bending dangerously. Without gaps, the track would bow outward.

What is a bimetallic strip made of? Easy
Answer

A bimetallic strip is made of two different metals bonded together (e.g., brass and steel/invar). They expand at different rates when heated, causing the strip to bend.

Why does the liquid in a glass thermometer rise when it is heated? Easy
Answer

The liquid expands more than the glass tube when heated, so the net expansion forces the liquid up the narrow tube, raising the column.

What happens to most materials when they are cooled? Easy
Answer

Most materials contract (decrease in size) when cooled, as particles lose kinetic energy and move closer together.

Name one everyday application of thermal expansion in engineering. Easy
Answer

Any one of: expansion joints in bridges, gaps in railway tracks, gaps in concrete roads, overhead power cable slack.

Do liquids or gases expand more for the same temperature rise? Easy
Answer

Gases expand far more than liquids (and liquids more than solids) for the same temperature rise, because gas particles are already widely spaced and move much more freely.

What is unusual about water's expansion behaviour between 0 °C and 4 °C? Easy
Answer

Water is anomalous: between 0 °C and 4 °C it actually contracts as temperature rises (maximum density at 4 °C), then expands above 4 °C like most substances.

Why do bridges have expansion joints (gaps) at regular intervals? Easy
Answer

Bridges expand in summer heat and contract in winter cold. Expansion joints provide space for this thermal expansion and contraction without creating dangerous stress or cracking the structure.

How does a bimetallic strip work as a thermostat? Easy
Answer

When heated, one metal expands more than the other, causing the strip to bend toward the metal that expands less. This bending movement can open or close an electrical circuit, switching heating/cooling on or off automatically.

Set B Application & Analysis — Medium

A railway track is 25 m long at 10 °C. At 40 °C it expands by 0.9 mm. Explain at the particle level why the length increases. Medium
Answer

At higher temperatures, iron atoms vibrate more vigorously. Each atom's average distance from its neighbours increases slightly as the vibration amplitude grows. Summed over billions of atoms in a 25 m track, this tiny per-atom increase becomes a measurable 0.9 mm expansion.

In summer, a steel bridge is 500 m long. In winter the temperature drops by 30 °C. Explain why the bridge designer must include expansion joints and predict what would happen without them. Medium
Answer

Steel contracts when cooled. Without expansion joints, the contracted steel would develop enormous tensile stress (pulling forces) as it tries to shorten but is fixed at the ends. This stress could crack welds, shear bolts, or even collapse the bridge. Expansion joints allow the metal to freely contract and expand.

A glass jar lid is stuck. Explain scientifically why running hot water over the lid helps open it. Medium
Answer

Set A Recall & Understanding — Easy

What is conduction? Easy
Answer

Conduction is the transfer of heat through a material by vibrating particles passing energy to neighbouring particles, without the particles themselves moving from place to place.

In which states of matter does conduction mainly occur? Easy
Answer

Conduction occurs most effectively in solids, because particles are closely packed and vibrations are easily passed on. It also occurs in liquids and gases, but less efficiently.

Are metals good or poor conductors of heat? Easy
Answer

Metals are good conductors of heat. They have free electrons that carry energy quickly through the material.

Name two materials that are poor conductors (insulators) of heat. Easy
Answer

Any two of: wood, plastic, rubber, glass, air, wool, foam.

Why are cooking pot handles made of plastic or wood rather than metal? Easy
Answer

Plastic and wood are poor conductors (insulators) — they do not transfer heat well from the hot pot to your hand, preventing burns.

What type of energy transfer is involved in conduction — do particles move from place to place? Easy
Answer

In conduction, particles do NOT move from place to place. They vibrate in fixed positions and pass kinetic energy to neighbouring particles through collisions.

Why does a metal spoon left in hot soup become hot at the handle? Easy
Answer

Heat conducts along the metal spoon from the hot soup end to the cooler handle end through vibrating metal particles passing energy along the spoon.

Can conduction transfer heat through a vacuum? Easy
Answer

No. Conduction requires particles to be in contact. A vacuum has no particles, so conduction cannot occur.

Why is air considered a poor conductor of heat? Easy
Answer

Air particles are far apart and collide infrequently, so they transfer energy by conduction very slowly. Trapped air is used as an insulator in double-glazed windows and woolly jumpers.

What is the role of free electrons in heat conduction through metals? Easy
Answer

Free electrons in metals can move rapidly through the lattice, carrying kinetic energy from hot regions to cooler regions far faster than vibration alone. This makes metals excellent thermal conductors.

Set B Application & Analysis — Medium

Explain at the particle level how heat travels from the hot end to the cold end of a metal rod. Medium
Answer

At the hot end, particles gain energy and vibrate more vigorously. These particles collide with adjacent particles, transferring kinetic energy to them. Those particles then collide with their neighbours, passing the energy along. This chain of vibration-transfers progresses steadily from the hot end to the cold end.

Explain at the particle and electron level why metals conduct heat far better than non-metals. Include the role of free electrons and lattice vibrations. Hard
Answer

Metals have a sea of delocalised (free) electrons that can move freely through the lattice. When one end is heated: (1) Lattice vibrations (phonons) carry energy through atomic collisions — same as non-metals, but metals' tightly packed lattice does this efficiently. (2) Free electrons absorb kinetic energy at the hot end, travel rapidly to the cool end, and transfer energy to cooler atoms there. This dual mechanism (phonons + electron conduction) makes metals conduct ~100–1000× better than non-metals, which rely on phonon conduction alone.

A thick layer of blubber (fat) under the skin has very low thermal conductivity (~0.2 W/m·K), reducing conductive heat loss from the warm body core to the cold skin surface. All three layers — air-trapping fur, skin, blubber — act as insulating layers in series, providing total resistance high enough to maintain ~37 °C core temperature at –40 °C ambient.

Why is it incorrect to say that insulators "block heat"? What is the accurate scientific description of what insulators do? Hard
Answer

Heat will always flow from hot to cold — this cannot be "blocked" (Second Law of Thermodynamics). What insulators actually do is reduce the rate of heat transfer (slow it down). They increase thermal resistance (R), so less energy is transferred per second for a given temperature difference. Eventually, enough time, heat will pass through any insulator. The correct statement: "Insulators greatly reduce the rate of heat conduction" — not "block heat."

Why does wrapping a cold drink in newspaper keep it cool but wrapping a hot drink also keeps it hot? Explain this dual function using conduction theory. Hard
Answer

Thermal resistance works in both directions. Newspaper (with trapped air) has high resistance to heat flow. For a cold drink: it slows heat flowing IN from the warm room, keeping the drink cold longer. For a hot drink: it slows heat flowing OUT to the cool room, keeping the drink hot longer. The insulator doesn't "know" which direction — it simply reduces the rate of heat transfer across the temperature difference in whichever direction that difference exists.

A student claims that covering a hot baked potato in aluminium foil keeps it hot by insulating it. Is this correct? What does the foil actually do? Hard
Answer

The claim is partially correct but the primary mechanism is different. Aluminium foil is an excellent conductor — it does NOT insulate conductively. However, its shiny surface has very low emissivity, dramatically reducing heat loss by infrared radiation. The foil also traps a thin layer of air against the potato skin (some conduction insulation). The dominant benefit is radiation reduction (see Topic 9), not conduction insulation — the aluminium actually conducts heat quickly through itself, but prevents radiative loss.

Explain why birds fluff their feathers in cold weather. Connect to conductivity, trapped air, and thermoregulation. Hard
Answer

Fluffed feathers create a thicker layer that traps more still air close to the bird's body. Still air has very low thermal conductivity (k ≈ 0.024 W/m·K). By increasing the thickness of the trapped air layer, the bird increases its total thermal resistance, reducing the rate of conductive heat loss from its warm body (40–42 °C) to cold surroundings. This is thermoregulation through behavioural control of insulation thickness. The same principle explains why humans wear puffy down jackets in cold weather.

Set A Recall & Understanding — Easy

What is convection? Easy
Answer

Convection is the transfer of heat through a fluid (liquid or gas) by the actual movement of particles from hotter regions to cooler regions, carrying thermal energy with them.

Does warm water rise or sink? Why? Easy
Answer

Warm water rises because it is less dense than cold water — when heated, water expands and its particles are further apart, making it lighter per unit volume.

Does cold water rise or sink? Why? Easy
Answer

Cold water sinks because it is denser than warm water — cooler particles are closer together, making cold water heavier per unit volume.

What is a convection current? Easy
Answer

A convection current is the circular movement of fluid: warm fluid rises, reaches the top (cools), sinks back down, is reheated at the bottom, and rises again — forming a continuous loop of fluid movement.

Why is a hot water heater (immersion heater) placed at the bottom of a water tank? Easy
Answer

Placing the heater at the bottom ensures that heated (less dense) water rises to the top and cooler water sinks to replace it, setting up a convection current that heats the entire tank efficiently.

Can convection occur in solids? Explain why or why not. Easy
Answer

No. Convection requires particles to move from place to place, carrying heat with them. In solids, particles are fixed in position and can only vibrate — they cannot flow.

Why does soup at the bottom of a pot get hotter first when placed on a stove? Easy
Answer

Heat from the stove warms the soup at the bottom first by conduction through the pot. This hot soup rises (convection), and cool soup from the top sinks to be heated in turn — eventually the convection current heats the entire pot.

What property change of water causes convection currents to form? Easy
Answer

A change in density — warm water becomes less dense (expands) and cold water becomes denser (contracts) — creates the density difference that drives convection currents.

What happens to water molecules' spacing when water is heated? Easy
Answer

Water molecules move faster and further apart when heated, increasing the average spacing, which decreases the density of the water.

Name one everyday example where convection in a liquid is useful. Easy
Answer

Any one of: hot water central heating system, boiling a pot of soup/water, hot water cylinder (immersion heater), cooling system of a car engine.

Set B Application & Analysis — Medium

Explain step by step how a convection current forms in a beaker of water heated from below. Medium
Answer

1. Heat from the flame conducts through the beaker base to the bottom water layer.
2. Bottom water heats up, expands, becomes less dense and rises.
3. Cooler, denser water from the top sinks down to replace it.
4. The sinking cool water is heated, rises again.
5. This circular motion (convection current) continues, gradually heating all the water.

Why would heating water from the top (instead of the bottom) be very inefficient? Medium
Answer

Hot water rises — if heated from the top, the hot water stays at the top and no convection current forms. The heat would only travel down by conduction, which is very slow in liquids. The lower portion would remain cold for a long time.

A student adds a drop of red ink to the bottom of a beaker of cold water and then gently heats the bottom. Predict and explain what the student would observe. Medium
Answer

The red ink would be carried upward by the rising warm water (convection current). The student would see red streaks moving up from the bottom to the top, then spreading horizontally, then sinking at the cooler edges — visually tracing the convection current pattern.

Explain how a car engine cooling system uses convection in liquid. Why is a pump also included? Medium
Answer

Coolant fluid circulates around the hot engine block, absorbs heat, and carries it to the radiator where it loses heat to the air. Convection alone would be too slow for a high-power engine, so a water pump forces faster circulation — this is forced convection. The pump dramatically increases heat transfer rate to keep the engine below dangerous temperatures.

Why do deep ocean layers remain much colder than surface water, even in tropical regions? Medium
Answer

Sunlight heats only the surface water. Warm surface water is less dense, so it stays at the surface and does not convect downward. Cold deep water is denser and stays at the bottom. This creates a stable temperature stratification (thermocline) that prevents warm surface water from mixing with the deep cold layers.

A fish tank heater is placed at the bottom-right corner. Predict the water circulation pattern in the tank. Medium
Answer

Warm water near the heater rises on the right side. It spreads along the top surface toward the left, cooling as it goes. The cooled (denser) water sinks on the left side. It flows along the bottom back toward the heater on the right. This creates a clockwise convection loop (when viewed from the right). The whole tank gradually reaches the heater's set temperature.

Explain why convection is more effective at heating water than conduction alone. Medium
Answer

Conduction in water is slow — water molecules transfer energy only through close-range collisions and water's conductivity is low (k ≈ 0.6 W/m·K). Convection moves entire masses of hot fluid carrying large amounts of thermal energy across long distances quickly. The bulk fluid movement transfers heat orders of magnitude faster than particle-to-particle conduction.

Why is the cold water pipe connected to the bottom of a domestic hot water cylinder and the hot water outlet at the top? Medium
Answer

Cold incoming water enters at the bottom (it is denser, so it naturally stays at the bottom). The heater at the bottom creates a convection current heating all the water. The hot water outlet is at the top because hot water rises and collects there — always delivering the hottest available water first when a tap is turned on.

A lava lamp contains blobs of coloured wax in liquid. Explain the convection-like motion of the wax blobs when the lamp is heated from below. Medium
Answer

The wax is chosen so that at room temperature it is slightly denser than the liquid and sinks. When heated from below, the wax softens and expands, becoming slightly less dense than the liquid, so it rises. At the top (away from the heat), it cools, becomes denser again, and sinks — repeating in a convection-like cycle driven by density changes. This mimics convection visually.

Why is it important for a swimming pool to have a circulation/filtration pump? Link to convection and even temperature distribution. Medium
Answer

Without a pump, solar heating warms only the surface water (warm water stays on top — stable stratification). Cold water stays at the bottom, causing uncomfortable temperature gradients. The circulation pump creates forced convection, mixing warm and cool water for even temperature throughout. It also ensures chemicals (chlorine) are evenly distributed for effective sanitation.

Explain why the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC, also called the "ocean conveyor belt") is driven by convection. What would happen if global warming disrupted this system? Hard
Answer

AMOC is driven by thermohaline convection: warm, salty surface water from the tropics flows northward (e.g., Gulf Stream). In the North Atlantic, it cools and becomes saltier (sea ice forms, leaving salt behind) → denser → sinks to the deep ocean → flows southward as deep cold water. This global loop transfers enormous heat poleward, moderating climates.
Disruption by global warming: Melting ice caps add fresh water, reducing salinity → less dense → water no longer sinks → AMOC weakens → Northern Europe (including UK) could experience paradoxically colder winters despite global warming. Western Africa could receive less rainfall. This is a major tipping-point risk in climate science.

Explain how convection currents in Earth's mantle drive tectonic plate movement. How is this similar to convection in a pot of soup? Hard
Answer

Earth's mantle is semi-molten rock (viscous). Heat from the core heats the lower mantle; hot rock becomes less dense and rises. At the surface it spreads sideways (mid-ocean ridges), cools, becomes denser, and sinks back into the mantle (subduction zones) — a giant convection loop over millions of years. Tectonic plates "ride" on these convection cells, being dragged or pushed as the mantle flows. This is identical in principle to soup convection — same density-driven mechanism, but operating over geological timescales and far higher temperatures and viscosities.

Calculate the density of water at 20 °C (ρ = 998 kg/m³) vs 80 °C (ρ = 972 kg/m³). Explain how this density difference drives a convection current and estimate the buoyancy force on 1 litre of 80 °C water surrounded by 20 °C water. Hard
Answer

Density difference = 998 – 972 = 26 kg/m³.
1 litre = 0.001 m³.
Mass of displaced 20 °C water = 998 × 0.001 = 0.998 kg.
Mass of 80 °C water = 972 × 0.001 = 0.972 kg.
Buoyancy force = (0.998 – 0.972) × 9.8 = 0.026 × 9.8 = 0.255 N upward.
This net upward force drives the hot water upward, initiating the convection current. Though small per litre, over an entire heated liquid volume it creates significant bulk flow.

MYP D: A solar water heater uses a collector panel on the roof and a storage tank higher up on the wall. Explain how thermosiphon convection heats the water without any pump. Reflect on the sustainability advantages of this design. Hard
Answer

Thermosiphon principle: Cold water from the bottom of the storage tank flows down to the collector panel (denser, so it sinks). Solar radiation heats the water in the panel → it becomes less dense → rises naturally up to the tank's top. This creates a natural convection loop (thermosiphon) with no pump required.
Sustainability advantages: No electricity needed; zero operational energy cost; silent; no mechanical parts to fail/replace; lasts 20+ years; reduces fossil fuel heating demand; works in sunny climates. The design harnesses gravity and density differences — free, perpetual energy-saving mechanism with minimal environmental impact.

A student heats a beaker of water but notices that the water only circulates in one direction. Predict three factors that could affect the speed and direction of convection currents in a liquid. Hard
Answer

Factors affecting convection speed and direction:
1. Temperature difference: Greater temperature difference between hot and cold regions → greater density difference → faster convection.
2. Viscosity: More viscous fluids (thicker, e.g., oil vs water) resist flow → slower convection current despite same temperature difference.
3. Container shape and heating position: A round beaker with central heating creates a symmetrical rising-and-sinking pattern; offset heating (as in this case) drives unidirectional circulation. Heating position determines flow direction.
4. (Bonus) Fluid volume: More fluid takes longer to establish a steady current.

Compare natural convection and forced convection in liquids. Give one engineering application of each and explain which is more efficient and why. Hard
Answer

Natural convection: Driven only by density differences from temperature gradients; no external energy input. Example: solar thermosiphon water heaters, natural circulation in large industrial boilers.
Forced convection: An external device (pump, fan) drives fluid movement. Example: car radiator water pump, hospital HVAC systems, industrial heat exchangers.
Forced convection is more efficient — the pump drives faster fluid velocity, dramatically increasing heat transfer rate (heat transfer ∝ fluid velocity^0.5 to 0.8). Natural convection is limited by small density differences and gravity. Forced convection can transfer heat 10–100× faster but requires energy input for the pump.

Explain why a pot of boiling water with a lid traps more heat than one without a lid. Use convection, condensation, and latent heat in your answer. Hard
Answer

Without a lid: hot steam (convection) carries heat away; water evaporates, absorbing latent heat (~2260 J/g), cooling the remaining water; heat is lost continuously to the air above.
With a lid: steam hits the cold lid, condenses back into water, releasing latent heat — this heat stays within the pot system. Convective heat loss to the room is blocked. The lid also prevents evaporative cooling. Result: water reaches boiling point faster and requires less energy to maintain boiling — saves fuel and time.

Explain how convection in the Sun's outer layer (convective zone) transports energy outward. How is this similar to and different from convection in a beaker of water? Hard
Answer

Similar: In the Sun's convective zone (outer ~30% by radius), hot plasma (essentially a fluid) rises because it is less dense, carries energy upward, cools at the photosphere, becomes denser, sinks, and is reheated — identical convection loop mechanics to water in a beaker.
Different: (1) Temperature scale: ~2 million °C at the base to ~5500 °C at the surface. (2) The "fluid" is plasma (ionised gas), not liquid water. (3) Magnetic fields interact with moving plasma, creating sunspots and solar activity — no equivalent in a water beaker. (4) Timescales: solar convection cells (granules) last ~10 minutes and are ~1000 km across.

Set A Recall & Understanding — Easy

Why does hot air rise? Easy
Answer

Hot air is less dense than cold air because its particles move faster and are further apart. The less dense hot air is pushed upward by the denser cooler air sinking around it — buoyancy.

What causes a sea breeze during the day? Easy
Answer

During the day, land heats up faster than the sea. Hot air above the land rises; cooler air from over the sea blows in to replace it — creating a sea breeze (wind blowing from sea to land).

What causes a land breeze at night? Easy
Answer

At night, land cools faster than the sea. The sea surface stays warmer, so air above the sea rises; cooler air from the land blows out to sea — creating a land breeze (wind blowing from land to sea).

How does a hot air balloon rise? Easy
Answer

A burner heats the air inside the balloon. The hot air inside becomes less dense than the surrounding cooler air, creating a buoyancy force that lifts the balloon upward.

Why is a radiator in a room placed near the floor rather than near the ceiling? Easy
Answer

A floor-level radiator heats the air near the floor; this warm air rises, and cool air from the upper part of the room circulates down — setting up a convection current that heats the entire room. A ceiling radiator would heat air at the top where it stays, never circulating to the floor.

What is wind, in terms of convection? Easy
Answer

Wind is air moving due to convection — hot air rises in one region (creating a low pressure area), and cooler denser air from another region flows in to replace it, creating horizontal air movement we call wind.

Why does smoke from a chimney rise upward? Easy
Answer

Smoke is hot and less dense than the surrounding cooler air. Convection carries the hot smoke upward. The chimney shape concentrates this rising flow, directing smoke away from the building.

Why do birds and gliders circle over hot land surfaces rather than over lakes? Easy
Answer

Sun-heated land creates columns of rising warm air called thermals. Birds and gliders circle within these thermals to gain altitude without flapping/using engine power. Water heats and cools more slowly, producing weaker thermals.

Why do air conditioning units cool a room effectively when placed near the ceiling? Easy
Answer

Cold air from the ceiling unit is denser and sinks to the floor. Warm floor-level air rises to the ceiling where it is cooled again — creating a convection current that circulates cool air throughout the room.

What is a thermal column (thermal)? Where do they typically form? Easy
Answer

A thermal is a column of rising warm air formed when the Sun heats a dark or dry surface (tarmac road, bare rock, desert sand) more than the surrounding area. The hot air above this surface rises as a concentrated column of convecting air.

Set B Application & Analysis — Medium

Explain step by step how a sea breeze develops on a sunny day at a coastal location. Medium
Answer

1. Sun heats the land faster than the sea (land has lower heat capacity).
2. Air above land heats up, becomes less dense, and rises (creating low pressure over land).
3. Air above the sea remains cooler and denser (higher pressure).
4. Cooler sea air flows toward the land to fill the low pressure area — this is the sea breeze.
5. At altitude, warm risen air spreads seaward and sinks over the sea, completing the convection loop.

Why do coastal towns typically experience milder temperatures than inland towns at the same latitude? Link to sea breeze/land breeze convection cycles. Medium
Answer

Sea breezes during the day carry cooler air from the sea onto the coast, moderating summer heat. Land breezes at night carry air that has been warmed by the relatively warm sea — moderating nighttime cooling. This daily convection cycle modifies coastal temperatures, reducing extremes compared to inland locations which only experience continental air masses.

A house has a fireplace. Explain why opening a window on the opposite wall helps the fire burn better. Medium
Answer

The fire consumes oxygen and creates hot rising gases in the chimney. An open window allows fresh cool air to enter at low level, feeding the fire with oxygen and establishing a convection current: fresh air in through window → into fire → hot gases up chimney. Without the window, the room quickly becomes oxygen-depleted and the fire dies down.

Explain why mountain climbers on very high peaks sometimes experience extremely cold temperatures even when the valley below is warm. Medium
Answer

Warm air from the valley rises as convection. As it rises, it expands and cools (adiabatic cooling — expanding gas does work against the atmosphere, losing temperature). At high altitude, the air is also thinner. Temperature drops approximately 6.5 °C for every 1000 m of altitude gain (environmental lapse rate). High peaks are therefore far colder than valley floors even on sunny days.

Answer

Cooler, denser air is drawn DOWN the other tube to replace it — carrying the smoke DOWN through the second tube and across to the candle, then UP the first tube. Explain how thunderstorms develop using convection of air over warm land. Medium
Answer

Intense solar heating of land creates strong convection: warm, moist air rises rapidly. As it rises, it cools, and water vapour condenses (releasing latent heat, fuelling further rise). A towering cumulonimbus cloud develops. The rising air column becomes intense enough to create thunderstorm conditions — heavy rain, lightning from static charge build-up, and strong downdrafts of cooled air.

Why does a hot air balloon descend when the burner is turned off? What must the pilot do to maintain altitude? Medium
Answer

When the burner is off, the air inside the balloon gradually loses heat to the surroundings (conduction through the fabric). The inside air cools, becomes denser, reducing buoyancy. The balloon descends. To maintain altitude, the pilot must periodically fire the burner to reheat the air, restoring the density difference that provides lift.

Why are ventilation openings (air vents) placed near the floor in some buildings and near the ceiling in others? Explain the convection logic for each position. Medium
Answer

Floor-level vents: Used for incoming fresh/cool air. Dense cool air enters at floor level, is heated by the room, rises naturally and exits via ceiling vents — convection-driven ventilation.

Why are forest fires so difficult to control once established? Explain using convection in air. Medium
Answer

The fire heats air dramatically, creating a powerful rising convection column (fire whirl/updraft). This rising air draws in fresh oxygen-rich air from all sides at ground level — a self-reinforcing cycle: more oxygen → hotter fire → stronger convection → more oxygen. The updraft can also carry burning embers far ahead of the fire front, starting new fires. The convection-generated wind is often stronger than ambient wind, making the fire self-perpetuating.

Answer

The stack effect uses natural convection. Warm indoor air (less dense) rises and escapes through high openings near the top. Cooler outdoor air is drawn in through low openings at the base to replace it. Taller buildings create stronger convection currents (greater height = greater pressure difference). This provides energy-free ventilation — no fans or electricity needed — reducing carbon footprint of the building.

Explain how the Hadley, Ferrel, and Polar cells of global atmospheric circulation are all driven by convection. Why does the Earth have three convection cells per hemisphere rather than one giant cell? Hard

Radiation management: Multi-layer insulation (MLI) blankets of aluminised Mylar film (dozens of layers) reflect solar radiation and minimise emission of internal heat to cold space. External surfaces are selectively coated — sunward surfaces reflect; heat radiators on the shadowed side emit excess heat generated by electronics and crew.
Conduction management: MLI layers are separated by very thin spacers minimising conductive contact between layers. Structural connections to cold space structures use low-conductivity titanium fasteners.
Convection management: External vacuum — no convection possible. Inside the pressurised modules, fans circulate air to prevent dangerous CO₂ build-up and maintain even temperature, enabling convective heat distribution.
Active thermal control: A liquid ammonia cooling loop collects waste heat from electronics and crew, pumps it to external radiators where it radiates to space. Without this, electronics would overheat rapidly.

Explain how modern surgical cooling techniques (targeted temperature management, TTM) use heat transfer principles to protect the brain after cardiac arrest. Hard
Answer

Context: After cardiac arrest, the brain suffers ischemia (lack of oxygen). Restarting the heart can trigger reperfusion injury — a burst of damaging chemical reactions as oxygen returns. Cooling slows metabolic rate and reduces this damage.
Target: Cool body to 32–36 °C for 12–24 hours.
Heat transfer methods used:
1. Conduction: Cooling blankets/pads circulating cold water placed against the skin. Cold IV saline infusion directly cools blood by conduction.
2. Convection: Cold water circulation in blankets maintains continuous convective cooling. Fans enhance surface evaporation.
3. Evaporation: Cooling sprays on skin combined with fans increase evaporative heat loss.
4. Intravascular devices: Catheter with cold saline balloon directly cools blood — fastest, most precise method.
Outcome: Each 1 °C reduction in brain temperature reduces metabolic demand by ~5–7%, significantly improving survival and neurological outcome after cardiac arrest.

MYP B + D: A city council wants to reduce the "heat island effect" in their urban area. Design a multi-strategy plan using knowledge of all three heat transfer methods, and evaluate the feasibility and impact of each strategy. Hard
Answer

Strategy 1 — Cool roofs (radiation): Replace dark rooftops with white/reflective coatings. Reduces solar radiation absorption. Feasibility: medium (cost for painting/replacement); Impact: can reduce urban surface temperature by 2–4 °C; reduces building cooling energy by 15–30%.
Strategy 2 — Green roofs and walls (conduction + evaporation): Vegetation provides evaporative cooling and insulates rooftops. Feasibility: higher cost, structural requirements; Impact: local cooling of 1–3 °C; improves air quality and biodiversity.
Strategy 3 — Urban tree canopy (radiation + convection): Trees shade dark surfaces (blocking radiation absorption) and transpire water (evaporative cooling). Feasibility: good — low cost per tree; Impact: shaded streets can be 10–15 °C cooler than unshaded ones; positive long-term investment.
Strategy 4 — Permeable pavements (evaporation): Allow rainwater retention; evaporation provides cooling. Feasibility: requires road redesign; Impact: moderate urban cooling effect.
Evaluation: A combination of cool roofs + urban trees provides the greatest temperature reduction per dollar invested. Tree planting is the most equitable (improves air quality, mental health, flood control) and should be prioritised alongside cool roof mandates for new construction.

MYP B: A student measures the temperature inside a thermos flask every hour for 8 hours after filling it with boiling water. The temperature drops from 95 °C to 78 °C. Identify the main remaining heat loss pathway, explain why the vacuum and shiny walls did not eliminate it completely, and suggest one design improvement. Hard
Answer

Main remaining pathway: Heat loss through the stopper/lid — typically the weakest point. The stopper is made of cork or plastic, both poor conductors but not vacuum-insulated. Heat conducts through the stopper material and convects via small air gaps around it.
Why not eliminated: The vacuum and shiny walls address conduction/convection/radiation through the walls, but the stopper is a solid connection — no vacuum possible there. A perfect thermos would need a vacuum stopper, which is impractical to manufacture cheaply.
Improvement: Use an aerogel-filled stopper (k ≈ 0.015 W/m·K — lowest solid conductivity) combined with a tight magnetic seal to eliminate air gaps. This could reduce stopper losses by ~5×, extending the useful thermal retention time by several additional hours.
