Sexual Reproduction

Set A Easy – Knowledge & Recall

Define sexual reproduction.Easy
Show Answer

Sexual reproduction involves two parents that produce specialised sex cells (gametes) which fuse (fertilisation) to form a new organism with genetic material from both parents.

What is a gamete?Easy
Show Answer

A gamete is a specialised reproductive cell (sperm in males, egg/ovum in females) that is haploid (n) — contains half the normal number of chromosomes.

What is fertilisation?Easy
Show Answer

Fertilisation is the fusion of a male gamete (sperm) with a female gamete (ovum/egg) to form a zygote.

What is a zygote?Easy
Show Answer

A zygote is the fertilised egg — the first cell formed when a sperm fuses with an ovum. It is diploid (2n) and develops into an embryo.

What process produces gametes and how does the chromosome number change?Easy
Show Answer

Meiosis produces gametes. The chromosome number is halved from diploid (2n) to haploid (n) so that when two gametes fuse, the normal diploid number is restored in the offspring.

Why do offspring from sexual reproduction differ from both parents?Easy
Show Answer

Because the offspring inherits half its chromosomes from each parent, resulting in a unique combination of genes. Crossing over during meiosis also shuffles genes, creating additional genetic variation.

Name the male and female reproductive organs in humans.Easy
Show Answer

Male: testes (produce sperm). Female: ovaries (produce eggs).

What is the main evolutionary advantage of sexual reproduction?Easy
Show Answer

Sexual reproduction generates genetic variation in offspring, which provides raw material for natural selection, helping species adapt to changing environments and resist diseases.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why it is essential that gametes are haploid.Medium
Show Answer

If gametes were diploid (2n), the zygote formed by fusion would be tetraploid (4n). Over successive generations, chromosome number would double each time. By being haploid (n), gametes ensure that fertilisation restores the correct diploid (2n) chromosome number in the offspring.

Describe the journey of a sperm from production to fertilisation in a human female.Medium
Show Answer

Sperm produced in testes → passes through epididymis (maturation) → vas deferens → urethra → deposited in vagina → swim through uterus → enter fallopian tube → fertilise ovum in fallopian tube (if ovulation has occurred).

Compare the structure of a sperm cell and an egg cell and relate each structure to its function.Medium
Show Answer

Sperm: small, flagellated tail (motility), head contains DNA and acrosome (enzymes to penetrate egg), many mitochondria (energy for swimming). Egg: large, contains yolk (nutrients), zona pellucida (protective layer, prevents polyspermy), cytoplasm with organelles to support early embryo development.

How does meiosis create genetic variation through crossing over?Medium
Show Answer

During meiosis I, homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis). Segments of non-sister chromatids exchange with each other (crossing over) at points called chiasmata, creating new combinations of alleles on each chromosome. This produces gametes with unique genetic combinations.

Why might genetic variation in offspring be a disadvantage in a stable environment?Medium
Show Answer

In a highly stable environment, the current genotype is already optimally adapted. Introducing variation risks producing offspring less well-adapted than the parents. This may explain why some organisms (bacteria, dandelions) use asexual reproduction preferentially in stable environments — offspring are identical clones of the well-adapted parent.

What is an embryo? Describe the stages from zygote to embryo in humans.Medium
Show Answer

An embryo is the early stage of a developing organism before it acquires the distinctive features of its species. Zygote → divides by mitosis (cleavage) → 2, 4, 8 cells → morula (solid ball) → blastocyst (hollow ball) → implants in uterine wall → gastrulation (3 germ layers form) → embryo with organ rudiments by week 8.

Explain why sexually reproducing species tend to be more resilient to new diseases than asexually reproducing species.Medium
Show Answer

Sexual reproduction continuously shuffles alleles, creating genetically diverse populations. When a new pathogen arrives, some individuals may carry resistance alleles by chance. In asexual populations, all individuals are genetically identical — if one is susceptible, all are, potentially causing total population collapse from a single disease (e.g., crop monocultures).

Name and briefly describe two types of fertilisation that occur in animals.Medium
Show Answer

1. Internal fertilisation: sperm and egg meet inside the female's body. Seen in mammals, birds, reptiles. 2. External fertilisation: sperm and egg meet outside the female's body, usually in water. Seen in frogs and most fish.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Critically analyse why natural selection has preserved sexual reproduction despite its high energy costs compared to asexual reproduction.Hard
Show Answer

Sexual reproduction requires finding a mate, elaborate courtship, and producing only 50% of the offspring directly (males don't gestate). Asexual reproduction is twice as efficient. Yet sexual reproduction persists because: (1) genetic variation helps populations resist parasites that evolve rapidly (Red Queen Hypothesis — constantly changing your genetic locks forces parasites to constantly change their keys); (2) recombination removes harmful mutations from the genome (DNA repair hypothesis). These long-term evolutionary benefits outweigh the short-term cost disadvantage.

Design a controlled experiment to investigate whether genetic diversity in a fish population improves survival when exposed to a pathogen.Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: higher genetic diversity → greater survival from pathogen challenge. Setup: three tanks — Tank A: 20 sexually-bred guppies (high diversity); Tank B: 20 guppies from 2 clonal lines (low diversity); Tank C: control (no pathogen). Independent variable: genetic diversity level. Dependent variable: survival rate after 2 weeks of pathogen exposure. Measure genetic diversity using microsatellite markers (heterozygosity index H). Control variables: identical water conditions, food, and pathogen concentration. Limitation: lab conditions may not reflect wild complexity; ethical issues with deliberate exposure to harmful pathogens.

Evaluate the claim: "Meiosis is the most important process for long-term species survival."Hard
Show Answer

For: meiosis creates genetic variation (crossing over, independent assortment of homologs) — the raw material for natural selection and adaptation. Without meiosis, all offspring would be genetically identical, unable to adapt to environmental change, disease, or new predators. Against: mitosis is equally essential for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction; meiosis errors cause chromosomal disorders (Down syndrome = trisomy 21). Evaluation: for sexual species, meiosis is indeed the ultimate source of adaptive genetic variation. For asexual species, it is irrelevant. The claim is strongest when applied to species living in variable environments.

Compare the chromosomal consequences of fertilisation failure (parthenogenesis) vs. normal fertilisation in terms of genetic variation.Hard
Show Answer

Normal fertilisation: combines genomes from two individuals → offspring genetically distinct from both parents → high variation. Parthenogenesis (development without fertilisation): offspring contain only the mother's chromosomes → no genetic input from father → offspring are genetic variants of the mother (not identical clones due to meiosis, unless diploid parthenogenesis occurs). Result: parthenogenetic populations have much lower overall genetic diversity, making them more vulnerable to parasites and environmental change. Some lizards (Komodo dragon, whiptail lizards) use facultative parthenogenesis as a backup when mates are unavailable.

Analyse how the evolution of internal fertilisation was a key adaptation enabling vertebrate life on land.Hard
Show Answer

External fertilisation requires water to transport sperm to eggs. On land, gametes would dry out before union. Internal fertilisation internalises the aquatic medium — the female reproductive tract provides a moist environment for fertilisation. This allowed vertebrates to reproduce away from water bodies. Paired with water-proof eggs (amniotic egg in reptiles/birds) or placental development (mammals), it enabled complete independence from aquatic reproduction, a critical step in terrestrial vertebrate radiation.

Model mathematically why sexual reproduction doubles genetic combinations relative to mitotic (asexual) reproduction in a diploid organism with 4 chromosomes (2 pairs).Hard
Show Answer

With 2 chromosome pairs, meiosis I's independent assortment gives 2² = 4 possible gamete combinations per parent. Two parents each produce 4 types of gametes → 4 × 4 = 16 possible zygote combinations from assortment alone. Adding crossing over (at least one chiasma per chromosome pair) multiplies this further: each crossover point doubles combinations → tens or hundreds of thousands of unique gametes per individual. Asexual reproduction (mitosis): all offspring genetically identical — 1 combination. Conclusion: sexual reproduction exponentially amplifies genetic diversity.

Predict how a species that switches from sexual to obligate asexual reproduction would change over 1,000 generations in a fluctuating environment.Hard
Show Answer

Short-term (1–50 generations): asexual populations may boom (no mate-finding cost; all individuals reproduce). Medium-term (50–500 generations): accumulation of deleterious mutations (Muller's Ratchet — no recombination to remove them); harmful mutations persist in all clone lines. Long-term (500–1000 generations): as environment fluctuates, genetically uniform population lacks variants to survive new conditions (drought, novel pathogen, temperature shift); population crashes. Evolutionary outcome: near-certain extinction or reversion to some form of recombination under strong selection pressure. This matches the empirical rarity of ancient obligate asexual lineages ("evolutionary dead ends").

A virus mutates rapidly and kills 95% of an asexually reproducing insect population but only 40% of a sexually reproducing population of the same species. Explain this difference using genetic principles.Hard
Show Answer

Asexual population: all individuals nearly genetically identical; if the virus is adapted to the shared genotype, virtually all individuals are susceptible — hence 95% mortality. The 5% survivors likely had spontaneous new mutations conferring resistance. Sexual population: pre-existing genetic diversity means different individuals carry different immune gene variants (MHC diversity); some combinations are resistant to this specific virus strain → only 40% die. This is the Red Queen Hypothesis in action — parasite pressure maintains genetic diversity in host populations through sexual reproduction. The sexual population has a pre-existing "library" of defences that asexual populations lack.

Set A Easy – Knowledge & Recall

Define internal fertilisation and give one example.Easy
Show Answer

Internal fertilisation: sperm and egg fuse inside the female's body. Example: humans, dogs, or hens.

Define external fertilisation and give one example.Easy
Show Answer

External fertilisation: eggs are released by the female into the environment (usually water) and fertilised by sperm outside the body. Example: frogs or salmon.

Name three groups of animals that use internal fertilisation.Easy
Show Answer

Mammals, birds, and reptiles.

Name two animals that use external fertilisation.Easy
Show Answer

Frogs (amphibians) and most fish (e.g., salmon, trout).

Why must external fertilisation typically occur in or near water?Easy
Show Answer

Sperm require a moist medium to swim to the egg. Water prevents desiccation (drying out) of both gametes and provides the medium for sperm motility.

What is spawning?Easy
Show Answer

Spawning is the release of large numbers of eggs and sperm into the water by aquatic animals (such as fish and frogs) for external fertilisation.

Why do fish and frogs produce thousands of eggs at a time?Easy
Show Answer

In external fertilisation, many eggs are not fertilised, and of those that are, most offspring die from predation or environmental hazards. Producing large numbers compensates for low individual survival rates.

How many eggs does a human female typically release per month?Easy
Show Answer

Typically one egg (ovum) per month during ovulation.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why internally fertilising species can afford to produce fewer offspring than externally fertilising ones.Medium
Show Answer

Internal fertilisation greatly increases the probability of egg-sperm meeting. Additionally, many species with internal fertilisation provide extensive parental care (nourishing embryo via placenta, protecting eggs in nests), greatly increasing offspring survival. Fewer offspring per birth are needed to maintain population size when each individual has a high survival probability.

Describe the process of frog fertilisation, including how the male and female coordinate.Medium
Show Answer

Male frog clasps the female in a behaviour called amplexus (embrace). When the female lays eggs (spawn) into water, the male simultaneously releases sperm over them. Fertilisation occurs externally in the water. Both parents then leave; there is generally no parental care.

Compare the advantages and disadvantages of internal fertilisation over external fertilisation.Medium
Show Answer

Internal advantages: high fertilisation success; embryo protected within mother; fewer offspring needed; possible parental care; independence from water availability. Internal disadvantages: energy cost of carrying embryo; requires mate interaction and copulation. External advantages: produces large numbers of offspring; simple mechanism. External disadvantages: most eggs unfertilised; high offspring mortality; dependent on water; vulnerable to environmental conditions.

How does temperature affect the rate of external fertilisation in amphibians?Medium
Show Answer

Sperm motility increases with warmer water temperatures (higher kinetic energy). Most amphibians spawn in spring when water warms. Very high temperatures denature sperm proteins; very low temperatures slow sperm movement. The optimal temperature window is species-specific and correlates with their natural breeding season.

Explain the adaptive significance of synchronised spawning in fish (e.g., all salmon in a river spawning simultaneously).Medium
Show Answer

Synchronised spawning maximises fertilisation success — more sperm and eggs in the water simultaneously increases egg-sperm collision probability. It also overwhelms predators (predator satiation) — too many eggs at once for predators to consume all, improving average offspring survival. Environmental synchrony (temperature, day length) triggers simultaneous spawning as a shared cue.

Why do reptiles use internal fertilisation even though their eggs are laid externally?Medium
Show Answer

Reptiles lay eggs with protective shells. The shell is deposited around the fertilised egg inside the female's body before laying. If fertilisation were external, the shell would prevent sperm from reaching the egg. Internal fertilisation ensures the egg is fertilised before the shell forms, enabling the hardened egg to protect the embryo on land where water is unavailable for external fertilisation.

How has internal fertilisation allowed reptiles and birds to colonise dry terrestrial environments?Medium
Show Answer

Internal fertilisation, combined with the amniotic egg (tough shell, protective membranes), freed reproduction completely from water. Eggs could be laid on dry land, in trees, or in sand — wherever the environment is thermally suitable. This opened vast ecological niches unavailable to amphibians, which must return to water to breed.

What is parthenogenesis? Name one animal that uses it.Medium
Show Answer

Parthenogenesis is development of an unfertilised egg into a new individual. Example: Komodo dragons — females can reproduce without males when isolated, producing viable offspring (which are diploid and genetically similar to the mother).

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Analyse how climate change (warming oceans) might disrupt the timing of spawning in fish, with consequences for population survival.Hard
Show Answer

Many fish use water temperature as the cue to spawn. As oceans warm earlier, spawning occurs earlier in the season. However, zooplankton (larval food) peaks depend on different cues (day length) and may not shift. This phenological mismatch means larvae hatch before their food peaks, causing starvation and recruitment failure — fewer juveniles survive. Over generations, declining recruitment reduces population sizes, potentially causing fisheries collapse. Evidence: North Sea cod recruitment has declined with phenological mismatch under warming.

Evaluate the evolutionary pressures that would cause a species to switch from external to internal fertilisation over evolutionary time.Hard
Show Answer

Pressures favouring internal fertilisation: (1) colonisation of drier environments where water for external fertilisation is absent; (2) high predation pressure on exposed eggs selects for internal protection; (3) intense sperm competition (many males competing to fertilise same eggs) selects for internal placement of sperm before competitors; (4) low population density makes synchronised external spawning inefficient. The transition likely occurred gradually via behaviours like amplexus (frogs), then vent-to-vent contact, then insertion organs evolving. Fossil evidence suggests terrestrial vertebrate radiation drove this transition in multiple independent lineages.

Compare sperm competition in externally fertilising fish versus internally fertilising insects at the molecular and behavioural levels.Hard
Show Answer

Fish (external): multiple males release sperm simultaneously near a spawning female; competition occurs in open water; selection favours sperm with higher swimming speed, longevity, and density. Behaviour: males compete for spawning proximity. Insects (internal): sperm from multiple males stored in spermatheca; competition occurs inside female reproductive tract; selection favours sperm that displace rivals (copulatory plugs in some beetles), resist female-mediated sperm selection, or fertilise eggs first. Behaviour: prolonged copulation, post-copulatory guarding. Both demonstrate that fertilisation is competitive, not passive, even after mating is secured.

Critically assess why sharks (with internal fertilisation and live birth) still face higher extinction risk than externally fertilising bony fish that produce millions of eggs.Hard
Show Answer

Despite advanced reproductive strategy, sharks have: extremely slow reproductive rates (few pups per litter, long gestation 12–24 months, late sexual maturity at 10–20 years), K-strategy reproductive investment. This means populations recover very slowly from overexploitation. Bony fish produce millions of eggs — populations can rebound quickly (r-strategy). When overfishing removes sharks, the reproductive rate cannot replenish losses. Internal fertilisation and parental investment are advantageous in stable environments but become a liability when human harvesting rates far exceed natural reproductive capacity. Combined with targeted shark finning for the fin trade, extinction risk for slow-reproducing sharks is disproportionately high.

Design an investigation to compare fertilisation rates in externally fertilising Xenopus frogs under varying pH conditions. Identify your variables and expected results.Hard
Show Answer

Question: How does water pH affect fertilisation rate in Xenopus laevis? Independent variable: pH of water (4, 5, 6, 7, 8 — five levels). Dependent variable: percentage of eggs fertilised after 30 minutes (counted under dissecting microscope — fertilised eggs are opaque). Controlled variables: water temperature (22°C), sperm concentration, number of eggs per trial, same male and female donor frogs. Method: collect eggs and sperm separately, mix in buffered water at each pH for 30 minutes, count fertilised vs unfertilised (n=200 eggs per replicate, 3 replicates per pH). Expected results: highest fertilisation near pH 7 (physiological optimum); reduced at pH <6 (acid damages sperm motility and flagella); reduced at pH >8 (alkalinity alters egg surface receptors). Real-world relevance: acid rain reduces freshwater pH, threatening amphibian reproduction.

Argue whether hormonal synchronisation of spawning in fish farms is ethically different from hormone-induced ovulation in humans undergoing IVF.Hard
Show Answer

Similarities: both use exogenous hormones to control reproductive timing for human-defined purposes; both override natural reproductive cycles; both carry health risks (ovarian hyperstimulation in women, stress in fish). Differences: fish are used as food production resources — human consent is irrelevant; individual fish welfare is not legally protected. In humans: informed consent is mandatory; hormones are used at the patient's own request for a desired outcome; extensive medical oversight. Ethical framework: the key distinction is sentience and consent. If fish can experience stress (growing scientific evidence), the ethics of hormonal manipulation deserve more scrutiny. For humans, as long as full informed consent and safety protocols are met, hormonal IVF is ethically defensible. The question ultimately reflects broader attitudes toward animal vs. human welfare in biotechnology.

Using the concept of r- and K-selection, predict which fertilisation strategy is most common in each and explain why.Hard
Show Answer

r-selected species (high reproduction, low parental care, unstable environments): typically use external fertilisation — producing vast numbers of cheap offspring maximises chances of some surviving in unpredictable conditions. K-selected species (low reproduction, high parental care, stable environments): typically use internal fertilisation — investing heavily in fewer, well-protected offspring maximises individual survival probability. The correlation is not absolute (sharks are K-selected with internal fertilisation; most bony fish are r-selected with external fertilisation), but the general pattern holds: internal fertilisation co-evolved with parental investment and K-strategy life history in vertebrates.

Evaluate the ecological consequences of introducing externally fertilising species (e.g., Nile tilapia) into Indian water bodies where native species use different reproductive strategies.Hard
Show Answer

Nile tilapia: fast external fertilisation, high fecundity, tolerant of degraded water → rapid population growth. Ecological consequences: (1) competition for spawning sites and food with native carp and catfish; (2) hybridisation risk with related native species, introducing foreign genetic material; (3) predation on native fish eggs and fry by tilapia; (4) alteration of nutrient cycling (tilapia are filter feeders — change phytoplankton community structure); (5) displacement of native species reduces ecological diversity. Indian case: tilapia introduced to Kerala backwaters now dominates over native species, threatening traditional fishery livelihoods and aquatic biodiversity. Policy implication: stringent import controls and ecological risk assessment are required before introducing any non-native aquaculture species.

Set A Easy – Knowledge & Recall

What is a viviparous animal? Give two examples.Easy
Show Answer

A viviparous animal gives birth to live young that develop inside the mother's body. Examples: humans and dogs.

What is an oviparous animal? Give two examples.Easy
Show Answer

An oviparous animal lays eggs, and the embryo develops outside the mother's body. Examples: hens and frogs.

What is the function of the placenta?Easy
Show Answer

The placenta is an organ that connects the embryo to the uterine wall in placental mammals, allowing transfer of oxygen and nutrients from mother to embryo and removal of carbon dioxide and wastes from embryo to mother.

What provides nutrition to the embryo inside a bird's egg?Easy
Show Answer

The yolk (egg yolk) provides nutrients to the developing embryo inside a bird's egg.

Name a mammal that is oviparous (egg-laying).Easy
Show Answer

The platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus) and echidna — both are monotremes, the only egg-laying mammals.

What is an amniotic egg?Easy
Show Answer

An amniotic egg contains membranes (amnion, chorion, allantois) that protect the embryo, provide nutrients via yolk, enable gas exchange, and store waste — allowing reproduction on dry land without water.

What is a marsupial? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

A marsupial is a mammal that gives birth to very undeveloped young, which then complete development in a pouch (marsupium). Example: kangaroo.

Which is more advanced in development at birth — a viviparous or oviparous young?Easy
Show Answer

Viviparous young are generally more advanced (fully developed organ systems, capable of independent life soon after birth) because they develop for longer inside the protected uterine environment with continuous maternal nutrition.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain how the placenta functions as a two-way exchange organ without allowing maternal and foetal blood to mix directly.Medium
Show Answer

The placenta contains vast networks of foetal capillaries bathed in pools of maternal blood (intervillous spaces). Oxygen and nutrients diffuse from high concentration in maternal blood across the thin placental membrane into foetal blood. CO₂ and wastes diffuse in the reverse direction. The two blood supplies are separated by the placental barrier — mixing is prevented, protecting the foetus from maternal immune rejection.

Describe three ways in which a hard-shelled bird egg provides protection to the developing embryo.Medium
Show Answer

1. Calcium carbonate shell: physical barrier against predators and mechanical damage. 2. Shell membrane: prevents bacterial entry. 3. Albumen (egg white): cushions embryo from impact and stores water and protein. 4. Chalaza: suspends yolk centrally, preventing embryo from sticking to shell wall.

Compare the energetic investment of a viviparous mammal and an oviparous reptile in reproduction.Medium
Show Answer

Viviparous mammal: continuous energy investment throughout gestation (months to years) — mother must consume extra food to nourish growing foetus; also extends lactation after birth. High per-offspring cost, few offspring. Oviparous reptile: energy packaged into eggs before laying; no further physiological investment after laying (though some guard eggs). Lower per-offspring running cost but eggs vulnerable to predation and temperature fluctuations. Mammalian viviparity is a much larger energetic investment per offspring.

Why do marsupials give birth to such undeveloped young? What is the evolutionary explanation?Medium
Show Answer

Marsupials lack a complex haemochorial placenta. The early embryo is not well tolerated by the maternal immune system without this barrier, limiting gestation length. Very early birth to the pouch transfers the embryo to a less immunologically hostile environment. The pouch compensates with milk of changing composition as the joey develops. This is an ancestral reproductive mode that pre-dates the highly efficient eutherian (placental) system.

How does incubation temperature affect sex determination in some oviparous reptiles?Medium
Show Answer

Many turtles, crocodiles, and some lizards have temperature-dependent sex determination (TSD) — sex is determined not by chromosomes but by egg incubation temperature. In many turtles: cooler temperatures → mostly male; warmer → mostly female. This is significant under climate change — rising nest temperatures could skew sex ratios toward all-female, threatening population viability.

Describe the significance of colostrum (first milk) in viviparous mammals.Medium
Show Answer

Colostrum is the first secretion from mammary glands after birth — rich in antibodies (immunoglobulins), proteins, and growth factors. It provides passive immunity to the newborn, whose immune system is not yet functional, protecting against pathogens in the first weeks of life. This immunological "starter pack" is critical for neonatal survival.

Why might viviparity evolve in some lizard species living in cold climates?Medium
Show Answer

In cold climates, soil temperatures are too low for egg incubation — eggs would not develop or would hatch late with poor survival. A pregnant female lizard can thermoregulate behaviourally (basking) to keep her embryos warm inside her body. Viviparity evolved multiple times independently in cold-climate lizard lineages (e.g., common lizard Zootoca vivipara) as an adaptation to cold, short summers.

What is ovoviviparity? Give one animal example.Medium
Show Answer

Ovoviviparity: eggs are retained inside the mother's body and hatch internally; the mother gives birth to live young, but the embryo receives nutrition only from the egg yolk (no placenta). Example: many sharks (e.g., dogfish shark) and some snakes.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Evaluate whether viviparity represents an evolutionary "advance" over oviparity, or whether this framing is misleading.Hard
Show Answer

The "advance" framing implies teleological direction in evolution (toward complexity = progress) — this is scientifically problematic. Both strategies are successful adaptations to different environments: viviparity suits mobile, terrestrial species in variable environments with low offspring mortality; oviparity suits species where large clutch size offsets high individual mortality. Birds are oviparous but among the most evolutionarily successful vertebrates. The platypus — an egg-laying mammal — is not "primitive" but exquisitely adapted to its niche. Conclusion: viviparity is not superior — it is one of several successful reproductive modes shaped by different selective pressures.

Analyse how rising global temperatures threaten sea turtle populations that rely on temperature-dependent sex determination.Hard
Show Answer

Sea turtle nests in sand: above ~29°C → mostly female; below → mostly male; 29°C gives 50:50 sex ratio. Current warming: studies on Great Barrier Reef loggerhead turtles show 99% female hatchlings at northern beaches. Short-term: population can still reproduce if some males persist in cooler southern beaches. Long-term: if male-producing beaches warm above threshold, male scarcity will limit fertilisation rates. Potential collapse: even abundant females cannot reproduce without males. Management: shading nests with sand covers or sea-grass, relocating eggs to cooler incubators — are being piloted. Climate change thus threatens turtle populations through a reproductive mechanism invisible in standard population counts.

Compare the immunological challenge faced by the placenta to prevent maternal rejection of the foetus to an organ transplant challenge.Hard
Show Answer

Organ transplant: donor tissue expresses foreign MHC antigens → recipient immune system recognises as non-self → rejection via T-cell attack unless immunosuppressive drugs used. Placenta: foetus is 50% genetically foreign (paternal genes) — similarly foreign to the mother's immune system. Placental solution: (1) trophoblast cells express atypical HLA molecules (HLA-G) that actively suppress NK cells; (2) placenta produces immunomodulatory proteins; (3) local regulatory T-cells suppress immune attack. Unlike transplants, this happens naturally. Failure of this tolerance leads to recurrent miscarriage. The placenta is evolution's solution to the transplant rejection problem — and understanding it could improve transplant medicine.

Design a study to test whether foetal experience inside the uterus influences post-birth preferences (prenatal learning). Describe controls and ethical constraints.Hard
Show Answer

Using sheep (ethical surrogate for mammals): experimental group — mothers fed a novel flavour (anise) daily from gestational day 100 to birth. Control group — mothers fed no anise. Post-birth: present lambs with both plain and anise-flavoured food at 1, 7, and 30 days. Measure time spent eating each food (preference test). Hypothesis: prenatal flavour exposure through amniotic fluid (anise compounds cross placenta) primes olfactory receptors → post-birth preference for anise. Controls: litter mates in control group; equal nutritional content of feeds; observer blind to group assignment. Ethical constraints: no aversive stimuli; lambs are not separated from mothers; experiment ends without harm to animals. This models prenatal flavour learning already demonstrated in human and rat studies.

Critically assess whether the evolution of viviparity and placentation was necessary for the dominance of mammals on Earth after the K-Pg extinction event.Hard
Show Answer

For necessity: placental viviparity allowed rapid geographic spread into diverse environments (mountains, deserts, polar regions) without dependence on warm incubation sites; accelerated offspring development and learning under maternal care; enabled longer gestation for complex brain development (defining mammalian cognitive advantage). Against necessity: marsupials are successful in Australia without placenta; monotremes persist 80 million years after placental mammals evolved. K-Pg extinction created ecological vacuums regardless of reproductive mode — any fast-reproducing, warm-blooded survivor would radiate. Placentation may have accelerated post-extinction radiation speed and diversity but was not strictly necessary for mammalian survival.

Propose how studying platypus reproduction could provide insights into the evolution of viviparity in mammals.Hard
Show Answer

Platypus genome (sequenced 2008) retains ancestral gene families absent in placental mammals, including vitellogenin genes (egg yolk proteins — present in reptiles) and a simplified version of the placental hormone cascade. Studying platypus genes reveals which molecular "toolkit" for viviparity was present in the common mammalian ancestor, allowing identification of the minimal genetic changes needed to transition from oviparity to viviparity. Comparing platypus, marsupial, and placental protein expression profiles during early gestation can trace the step-by-step molecular evolution of the placenta. This kind of comparative genomics is a powerful tool for understanding macroevolutionary transitions.

Construct an argument that oviparous reproduction is more ecologically resilient than viviparity in the context of global biodiversity crises.Hard
Show Answer

Arguments: (1) High fecundity — oviparous species (fish, insects, reptiles) produce thousands of eggs, allowing rapid population recovery from mass mortality events; (2) Ecological flexibility — eggs can be left in various environments (soil, water, vegetation) requiring no parental survival post-laying; (3) Metabolic independence — no maternal energy cost after oviposition; mother can survive even if clutch fails; (4) Diversity — most animal species (fish, reptiles, insects, birds) are oviparous and represent the vast majority of animal biodiversity. Counter: viviparity ensures higher per-offspring survival under stable conditions; large-brained mammals (viviparous) have higher behavioural flexibility to adapt to novel conditions. Conclusion: oviparous strategies dominate in terms of number of species and ecological diversity, suggesting it is the more broadly successful reproductive strategy across animal evolution.

MYP D — Should IVF clinics be permitted to select embryos for implantation based on genetic testing results? Consider at least three ethical perspectives.Hard
Show Answer

Medical perspective: Pre-implantation Genetic Diagnosis (PGD) can prevent serious hereditary diseases (cystic fibrosis, Huntington's) — reducing suffering. Disability rights perspective: selecting against embryos with disability traits implies those lives are less valuable; disabled individuals can live fulfilling lives; creates social pressure against "imperfect" births. Define asexual reproduction.Easy

What is binary fission? Name one organism that reproduces by binary fission.Easy

Binary fission is a form of asexual reproduction where a single-celled organism splits into two equal daughter cells. Example: Amoeba and bacteria.

What is spore formation? Name one organism that uses it.Easy

Spore formation is asexual reproduction where specialised cells (spores) are produced that can withstand harsh conditions and germinate into new organisms. Example: Ferns, Rhizopus (bread mould).

Are offspring produced by asexual reproduction genetically identical to the parent?Easy

Name two advantages of asexual reproduction.Easy

What is vegetative propagation? Is it a form of asexual reproduction?Easy

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain the cellular mechanism behind regeneration in planaria (flatworms).Medium
Show Answer

Planaria contain pluripotent stem cells called neoblasts distributed throughout their body. When injured or cut, neoblasts migrate to the wound site, proliferate by mitosis, and differentiate into the specific cell types needed to rebuild lost structures. Signalling gradients (Wnt, beta-catenin) specify head-tail polarity, ensuring regeneration is correctly oriented.

Why is the rate of binary fission in bacteria considered exponential rather than linear?Medium
Show Answer

In binary fission, each bacterium divides into two. Those two each divide into two more (four total) after one generation time. Formula: N = N₀ × 2ⁿ (where n = number of generations). Starting with 1 cell: 1→2→4→8→16… Each generation doubles the total, creating exponential (geometric) growth rather than linear addition.

Describe how Hydra carries out budding as a form of reproduction.Medium
Show Answer

Under favourable conditions, cells at the bud zone of the Hydra body column undergo rapid mitosis. The bud grows as a small projection, developing its own tentacles and mouth. It then constricts at its base and breaks off (detaches) as an independent organism, genetically identical to the parent.

Compare fragmentation in starfish with budding in Hydra. What is fundamentally different?Medium
Show Answer

Budding: a new individual grows outward from the parent as a bud and separates; parent remains intact. Fragmentation: the parent body breaks into pieces; each piece regenerates into a complete individual. In budding, reproduction is additive (parent + new individual); in fragmentation, the parent is replaced by multiple offspring. Both produce clones via mitosis.

Why are spores particularly well-adapted to surviving harsh environmental conditions?Medium
Show Answer

Spores have thick, resistant walls (often composed of sporopollenin or keratin-like proteins) that protect the genetic material from desiccation, UV radiation, extreme temperatures, and chemical damage. Many spores enter dormancy — drastically reducing metabolic activity — until conditions improve, at which point they germinate. Some bacterial endospores can survive for thousands of years.

Explain why asexual reproduction in bacteria is both an advantage and a danger to human health.Medium
Show Answer

Advantage: bacteria can be grown rapidly for useful purposes (yogurt fermentation, antibiotic production by bacteria-fermenters). Danger: disease-causing bacteria can double in 20 minutes — a single E. coli can become 16 million in 8 hours. Antibiotic resistance mutations quickly spread through entire populations by binary fission — within days, a resistant strain can dominate a patient's infection.

How do some coral reefs use fragmentation for natural population recovery after storm damage?Medium
Show Answer

Storms break coral colonies into fragments. Fragments that land on suitable substrate re-attach and grow into new colonies via asexual reproduction (each polyp in a fragment is a clone of the original). This allows local population recovery without sexual reproduction. Coral restoration programmes exploit this by intentionally fragmenting healthy corals to transplant to degraded reefs.

What is the difference between asexual and sexual spores in fungi?Medium
Show Answer

Asexual spores (conidia, sporangiospores): produced by mitosis — genetically identical to parent; used for rapid colonisation of new food sources under favourable conditions. Sexual spores (ascospores, basidiospores): produced by meiosis — genetically varied; typically formed under stress, creating diversity enabling survival of changed conditions.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Critically evaluate whether animals that can regenerate entire body parts (like planaria) could in theory never die of age. What prevents "biological immortality"?Hard
Show Answer

Planaria do not show observable cellular ageing — their neoblasts continuously renew all body tissues. They could theoretically regenerate indefinitely, and some researchers have called planaria "functionally immortal." However: (1) accumulation of somatic mutations in neoblasts over time may eventually compromise regeneration fidelity; (2) environmental hazards (predation, starvation, desiccation) ensure almost no wild planarian survives long enough to test true immortality; (3) telomere shortening, while slower than in differentiated cells, may still limit stem cell divisions. The Hydra is another example tested for negligible senescence in lab conditions. True biological immortality may be physically possible but evolutionarily disfavoured (resources better spent on reproduction than perpetual maintenance).

Analyse how bacterial binary fission contributes to the rapid emergence of antibiotic resistance. Use your understanding of mutation and selection.Hard
Show Answer

Random mutations occur at ~10⁻⁷ per gene per generation. With 10¹² bacteria in an infected patient dividing every 20 minutes, millions of mutations occur per hour by chance. Most are neutral or harmful. Rarely, a mutation alters the antibiotic target (ribosome, cell wall enzyme, efflux pump) conferring resistance. With antibiotic present, all susceptible bacteria die; the rare resistant bacterium survives and reproduces exponentially (no competition). Selection pressure of the antibiotic converts a rare mutation into the dominant genotype within hours to days — classic Darwinian selection operating at extreme speed. Incomplete antibiotic courses leave partially suppressed but not eliminated bacteria, selecting for intermediate resistance — worsening the problem.

Design a model to mathematically project the population of Amoeba starting from 1 cell after 24 hours if the division time is 3 hours. Identify assumptions and limitations.Hard
Show Answer

Calculation: Number of generations in 24 hours = 24/3 = 8. N = 2⁸ = 256 cells. Formula: N = N₀ × 2^(t/T) where t = total time, T = doubling time. Assumptions: (1) unlimited nutrients; (2) no cell death; (3) synchronised division; (4) no temperature fluctuation; (5) no waste accumulation inhibiting division. Limitations: in reality, nutrients become limiting (logistic growth, not exponential); metabolic waste accumulates; cells compete for space; division is not perfectly synchronised. Real Amoeba populations follow a sigmoid (S-shaped) logistic growth curve, not unbounded exponential growth.

Evaluate the use of asexual propagation in conservation of critically endangered plant species. When is it appropriate and when is it insufficient?Hard
Show Answer

Appropriate when: the last surviving individuals of a species exist (prevents immediate extinction); rapid population increase is needed; cloning maintains the exact genotype of remaining individuals; emergency tissue culture banks. Insufficient when: the single remaining individual carries harmful recessive mutations; lack of genetic diversity makes the cloned population susceptible to a single pathogen; pollinators required for natural reproduction are extinct — cloning produces only vegetative individuals, not seeds; the habitat that supported diverse wild populations has been destroyed, so reintroduction of clones provides no long-term solution. Best practice: asexual propagation as emergency measure, combined with long-term habitat restoration and seed bank diversification.

Construct an argument that the discovery of regeneration in planaria might fundamentally change medicine within 50 years.Hard
Show Answer

Planaria research has already identified key molecular pathways (Wnt signalling, neoblast behaviour, polarity signals). Applications in medicine: (1) understanding neoblast-like human adult stem cells could enable organ regeneration — regrowing severed spinal cords, damaged hearts, or degenerated retinas; (2) understanding planarian ageing resistance could reveal anti-senescence therapies; (3) cancer biology — neoblasts that go wrong may explain tumour formation, and studying their regulation could identify cancer suppression mechanisms; (4) wound healing — activating dormant regenerative pathways in human skin and muscle. Limitations: humans' evolutionary history of differentiation has suppressed regenerative capacity for reasons (tumour suppression, metabolic efficiency) not yet fully understood; translating planarian findings to complex mammalian systems will require decades of translational research.

Compare the evolutionary pressures that would favour asexual reproduction in Hydra versus sexual reproduction in the same organism under different environmental conditions.Hard
Show Answer

Hydra uses both: asexual (budding) when conditions are favourable (warm water, abundant food) — exploits resources quickly, doesn't need mates, maintains well-adapted genotype. Sexual reproduction when stressed (cold, starvation) — produces eggs that can survive winter dormancy; genetic recombination creates diversity that may produce offspring better adapted to the new challenging conditions. This conditional strategy (switch to sex under stress) is the optimal evolutionary solution: exploit success with clones, generate diversity for adversity. This "best of both worlds" strategy is seen across many organisms and supports the general theory that sex evolved primarily as a response to environmental unpredictability.

Why might asexual reproduction be considered evolutionarily "short-sighted"? Analyse using the concept of Muller's Ratchet.Hard
Show Answer

Muller's Ratchet: in asexually reproducing populations, each new deleterious mutation is passed to all offspring (no recombination removes it). Over time, each lineage accumulates mutations — there is no way to "reset" by combining with a less-mutated individual. Like a ratchet, the mutation load can only increase, never decrease asexually. Sexual reproduction allows recombination to occasionally combine genes from low-mutation individuals, creating offspring with fewer deleterious mutations than either parent. Over thousands of generations, asexual lineages accumulate lethal or fitness-reducing mutations and eventually go extinct. This makes asexual reproduction "short-sighted" — it maximises immediate reproductive rate at the cost of long-term genetic health. This is why most complex animals have not abandoned sexual reproduction despite its costs.

MYP D — Should human cloning (somatic cell nuclear transfer) for reproductive purposes be permanently banned? Analyse from scientific, ethical, and religious perspectives.Hard
Show Answer

Scientific perspective: reproductive cloning has extremely high failure rates even in non-human mammals (sheep, dogs); surviving clones often have health issues (premature ageing, immune dysfunction, large-offspring syndrome); genetic identity does not ensure identical experience — epigenetics means even a clone would be a different person. Ethical perspective: reproductive cloning instrumentalises human life — creating a person as a replica of another; violates the cloned child's right to an open future (already knowing their genetic "destiny"); consent is impossible; could exacerbate inequality (only wealthy could access). Religious perspective: most traditions hold that human life is divinely created and should not be replicated artificially; disrupts natural order. Counter-argument: reproductive freedom; could allow infertile individuals to pass on genes. Conclusion: the combination of medical dangers, ethical violations of the cloned child's rights, and lack of legitimate purpose not served by other reproductive technologies justifies a comprehensive ban on reproductive human cloning. Therapeutic cloning (for organ regeneration) is a separate issue with stronger justifications.

What does IVF stand for?Easy

Who was Dolly the sheep and why was she significant?Easy
Show Answer

What is somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT)?Easy
Show Answer

SCNT is a cloning technique where the nucleus from a somatic (non-reproductive) body cell is inserted into an enucleated egg cell. The egg is then stimulated to divide and develop into an embryo that is genetically identical to the nucleus donor.

What is artificial insemination?Easy
Show Answer

Artificial insemination is the process of introducing sperm from a selected male directly into a female's reproductive tract using medical instruments, without natural mating. Used in animal breeding and human infertility treatment.

Why might a couple choose IVF over other fertility treatments?Easy
Show Answer

IVF is suitable when: fallopian tubes are blocked (sperm cannot reach egg naturally); severe male infertility; unexplained infertility; older age; risk of genetic disease (can be combined with pre-implantation genetic diagnosis); when other treatments have failed.

What is a test-tube baby? Is the baby actually grown in a test tube?Easy
Show Answer

A "test-tube baby" is an informal term for a baby conceived through IVF. The baby is not grown in a test tube — fertilisation occurs in a laboratory dish but the embryo is transferred to and grows inside the mother's uterus, just like a naturally conceived baby.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why multiple embryos are typically created during IVF but only one or two are implanted.Medium
Show Answer

Multiple eggs are harvested and fertilised to maximise the chance of having viable, good-quality embryos (many fertilised eggs develop abnormally). Only one or two are implanted to reduce the risk of multiple pregnancies (twins/triplets carry higher health risks for mother and babies). Extra viable embryos are frozen (cryopreserved) for future use if the first cycle fails.

Compare the genetic relationship between Dolly and her "mother" (the donor cell nucleus).Medium
Show Answer

Dolly's nuclear DNA was identical to the mammary gland cell donor (the 6-year-old Finn Dorset ewe) — they are genetically identical twins, just born years apart. However, Dolly's mitochondrial DNA came from the enucleated egg donor (a different sheep). So Dolly was not a perfect clone — her mitochondrial genome differed from the nucleus donor.

How has IVF technology been used in the conservation of endangered wild animals?Medium
Show Answer

IVF used in: northern white rhinoceros (only two females alive, no males — sperm from dead males frozen, eggs harvested surgically, embryos created in lab, to be implanted in surrogate southern white rhino); giant panda breeding; cheetah and snow leopard conservation programmes. Allows breeding of species unable to reproduce naturally in captivity and banks genetic material from recently deceased individuals.

Explain the difference between therapeutic cloning and reproductive cloning.Medium
Show Answer

Therapeutic cloning: creates a cloned embryo (via SCNT) for the purpose of harvesting embryonic stem cells for medical treatment — embryo is not implanted. Reproductive cloning: creates a cloned embryo that is implanted in a uterus to develop into a living cloned individual. Therapeutic cloning is for medicine (treating disease); reproductive cloning aims to create a genetically identical animal or (hypothetically) human.

What are embryonic stem cells and why are they scientifically valuable?Medium
Show Answer

Embryonic stem cells (ESCs) are pluripotent cells from the inner cell mass of a blastocyst (early embryo) — they can differentiate into any cell type in the body. They are valuable because: they could potentially regenerate damaged tissues (heart, spinal cord, retina); used in drug testing; help scientists understand developmental biology and disease mechanisms.

Describe one risk to the mother and one risk to the child associated with IVF treatment.Medium
Show Answer

Risk to mother: Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome (OHSS) — ovaries over-respond to hormones, becoming swollen and painful; in severe cases causes fluid accumulation, blood clots, kidney problems. Risk to child: slightly higher rates of birth defects (major heart defects, chromosomal abnormalities) and preterm birth compared to naturally conceived babies, possibly due to the culture medium, embryo selection, or underlying infertility of parents.

How has genetic technology changed IVF in the last decade?Medium
Show Answer

Pre-implantation Genetic Testing (PGT): one cell removed from embryo (blastomere biopsy) at day 3, genome analysed for chromosomal aneuploidy (PGT-A) or specific disease mutations (PGT-M, e.g., cystic fibrosis, BRCA). Only chromosomally normal embryos implanted, improving live birth rates and reducing miscarriage. CRISPR gene editing at embryo stage (experimental) has also been demonstrated, though not clinically applied (except controversially by He Jiankui in 2018, widely condemned).

What was the scientific significance of Dolly's relatively early death and health problems?Medium
Show Answer

Dolly showed signs of premature ageing — she developed arthritis and progressive lung disease earlier than normal sheep and died at 6 years (half the normal lifespan). Her telomeres were shorter than expected for her age, matching those of the 6-year-old nucleus donor. This suggested that clones inherit the "biological age" of the donor cell, not a reset age, raising concerns about the health of cloned animals and the safety of any human cloning application.

Critically analyse how the development of IVF changed our understanding of human reproduction and identity.Hard

Evaluate the scientific evidence for and against continuing research into de-extinction using cloning technology (e.g., woolly mammoth revival).Hard

Analyse how IVF success rates vary with age and explain the biological mechanisms behind this variation.Hard
Show Answer

IVF live birth rates per cycle: under 35 → ~40%; 35–37 → ~30%; 38–39 → ~21%; 40–42 → ~12%; over 43 → ~5%. Biological mechanisms: (1) Oocyte quantity declines — ovarian reserve (total egg count) decreases from ~1 million at birth to ~1,000 at menopause. Fewer eggs retrieved per stimulation cycle. Design a scientific study to measure whether children born via IVF have different long-term health outcomes compared to naturally conceived peers. Identify confounding variables.Hard

Using molecular biology principles, explain why a clone produced by SCNT is not a perfect genetic copy of the original organism.Hard
Show Answer

Imperfections: (1) Mitochondrial DNA: clone receives nuclear DNA from donor but mitochondrial DNA from the enucleated egg host — mitochondria have their own genome encoding essential metabolic proteins. (2) Epigenetic reprogramming failure: SCNT requires erasing and resetting the epigenome of the donor nucleus. This reprogramming is often incomplete — many genes are incorrectly expressed or silenced compared to natural embryo development. This causes developmental defects (large offspring syndrome, abnormal imprinting). (3) Telomere inheritance: clone may inherit short telomeres from an older donor cell — accelerated ageing. (4) Somatic mutations: the donor cell will have accumulated somatic mutations over its lifetime that are now in every cell of the clone. (5) Environment and epigenetics: post-birth gene expression is shaped by environment — even identical nuclear DNA produces different gene expression profiles in different environments.

Explain how HIV progresses to AIDS and why HIV is considered particularly dangerous.Medium

HIV infects and destroys CD4+ T-lymphocytes (helper T-cells) — key coordinators of the immune response. Over years, CD4+ count falls below 200 cells/µL (normal: 500–1500). Explain what antiretroviral therapy (ART) does and why it does not cure HIV.Medium

MYP D — Analyse the ethical tensions in sex-selective abortion in India: reproductive choice versus gender equality.Hard
Show Answer

Reproductive choice argument: if abortion is legal, a woman has the right to terminate for any reason including sex preference; enforcing sex-selective abortion bans restricts reproductive autonomy and requires invasive surveillance of women's motivations.
