Newton's First Law of Motion

NCERT / CBSE Newton's First Law – Class 9

SET A Foundational (Easy)

State Newton's First Law of Motion in your own words.Easy
Show Answer

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion continues moving in a straight line at constant speed, unless acted on by a net external force.

What is inertia? How does mass relate to inertia?Easy
Show Answer

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist any change in its state of motion. Greater mass = greater inertia. A heavier object is harder to start, stop, or change direction.

Why do passengers lurch forward when a bus brakes suddenly?Easy
Show Answer

Due to inertia. The bus decelerates but the passengers' bodies tend to continue forward at the original speed. They lurch forward until a seatbelt, seat, or friction decelerates them too.

What is a net force? What happens to an object when net force is zero?Easy
Show Answer

Net force is the vector sum of all forces on an object. When net force = 0, the object is in equilibrium — it stays at rest or moves at constant velocity.

Give two everyday examples that demonstrate Newton's First Law.Easy
Show Answer

1. A ball rolling on a smooth floor keeps rolling until friction stops it. 2. A book on a table stays still until pushed. Also: the tablecloth trick.

Why is it important to wear a seatbelt in a car?Easy
Show Answer

In a collision, the car stops rapidly but, due to inertia, the passenger's body continues forward. Without a seatbelt, the passenger hits the windscreen. The seatbelt applies a braking force to decelerate the passenger with the car.

If no forces acted on a moving football, what would happen?Easy
Show Answer

It would continue in a straight line at constant speed forever (Newton's First Law). In practice, gravity, air resistance, and friction cause it to slow and curve.

What does it mean for forces to be "balanced"? Give an example.Easy
Show Answer

Balanced forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, giving net force = 0. Example: a book on a table — weight down equals normal force up.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A car travels at 60 km/h with the engine off. Explain why it slows down, referencing Newton's First Law.Medium
Show Answer

Newton's First Law predicts constant speed if no net force acts. However, friction and air resistance are unbalanced external forces opposing motion. These produce a net backward force, decelerating the car.

Explain why astronauts in a spacecraft with no engine and negligible gravity move in a straight line at constant speed.Medium
Show Answer

With no significant external forces, net force = 0. By Newton's First Law, the spacecraft (and astronauts) maintain their current velocity indefinitely.

A heavy truck and a bicycle both move at 30 km/h. Which is harder to stop? Explain using inertia.Medium
Show Answer

The truck is harder to stop. Its far greater mass gives it much greater inertia. A greater force (or longer time) is required to change its state of motion.

Distinguish between static inertia and dynamic inertia with one example each.Medium
Show Answer

Static inertia: tendency of a stationary object to remain at rest (e.g., a heavy rock that needs a large push to move). Dynamic inertia: tendency of a moving object to keep moving (e.g., a satellite that continues orbiting without thrust).

Explain the "tablecloth trick" using Newton's First Law.Medium
Show Answer

When the cloth is pulled very quickly, the contact time is extremely short. The frictional impulse on the dishes is negligible — they remain essentially at rest by inertia while the cloth slides away beneath them.

Why do objects in a rotating space station feel "simulated gravity"? Is this real gravity?Medium
Show Answer

Objects tend to move in a straight line (Newton's First Law). The station wall provides centripetal force inward, which feels like a push from the "floor." This is not real gravity — it is centripetal force from the rotating structure simulating the effect of weight.

A 5 kg object is pushed with 20 N right and 20 N left simultaneously. What is net force and what happens?Medium
Show Answer

Net force = 20 − 20 = 0 N. The object remains at rest (if stationary) or continues at constant velocity (if moving). No acceleration.

Why must pilots fasten harnesses even when flying straight and level? Relate to Newton's First Law.Medium
Show Answer

If sudden turbulence or a direction change occurs, an unbalanced force acts on the aircraft. By inertia, the pilot's body would continue in its previous state of motion while the aircraft changes. The harness ensures the pilot accelerates/decelerates with the aircraft.

SET C Extended (Hard)

Aristotle believed a continuous force was needed to maintain motion. Galileo and Newton disagreed. Who was correct and why? Provide evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Newton and Galileo were correct. Galileo's thought experiment: on a perfectly smooth surface, a ball neither speeds up nor slows down. Real evidence: a puck on a near-frictionless air table travels the full length at constant speed. Spacecraft maintain speed with engines off. Aristotle confused the force needed to overcome friction with a force needed to maintain motion.

Analyse how car crumple zones relate to Newton's First Law and inertia.Hard
Show Answer

In a crash the car stops rapidly. Due to inertia, passengers continue forward. Crumple zones crush progressively, increasing the stopping time. By F = Δp/t, longer time reduces peak force on passengers. The crumple also absorbs kinetic energy as deformation, further protecting the passenger cabin.

A coin sits on a card over a glass. The card is flicked quickly and the coin drops straight down. Explain using Newton's First Law and impulse.Hard
Show Answer

Contact time between card and coin is extremely short, so the horizontal impulse (F×t) on the coin is negligible — the coin receives almost no horizontal velocity. By inertia it stays in its horizontal position. Gravity (vertical, always acting) pulls the coin straight down into the glass.

Why do racing drivers wear HANS devices? Explain using Newton's First Law and inertia of the head.Hard
Show Answer

In a high-speed crash the car stops almost instantly. The harness restrains the body. But the head has inertia and continues forward — the neck must absorb this, risking basilar skull fracture. The HANS device transfers the head's inertial load from the neck to the harness/helmet, protecting the cervical spine.

An object moves at constant velocity. Does this mean no forces act? Explain the distinction between "no forces" and "balanced forces."Hard
Show Answer

No — it means net force is zero (forces are balanced). Multiple forces can act as long as they vector-sum to zero. A car at constant speed has engine thrust balanced by air resistance and friction. The state "no acceleration" is consistent with both "no forces" and "balanced forces."

Why is a head-on collision between two cars at 50 km/h more dangerous than hitting a stationary wall at 50 km/h? Use relative velocity and Newton's First Law.Hard
Show Answer

Hitting a stationary wall: your car decelerates from 50 to 0 km/h (change = 50 km/h). Head-on with another car also at 50 km/h: relative velocity = 100 km/h — each car decelerates as though hitting a wall at 100 km/h. The inertial force on passengers is roughly double that of the wall scenario.

A ball thrown horizontally in a moving train travels straight to someone on the train but curved to an observer outside. Explain using Newton's First Law and reference frames.Hard
Show Answer

Newton's First Law holds in inertial (non-accelerating) reference frames. The train at constant velocity is an inertial frame — inside, the ball goes straight. The outside observer sees the combined horizontal (train + throw) and vertical (gravity) motion — a parabola. Both observations are valid in their respective inertial frames; neither is "more correct."

Design a simple experiment to demonstrate Newton's First Law. Describe what you observe and how results support the law.Hard
Show Answer

Materials: marble, ruler channel, surfaces of decreasing friction (carpet, wood, polished tile, air track). Roll the marble and measure how far it travels before stopping on each surface. As friction decreases, the marble travels further. On the near-frictionless air track it travels the full length at nearly constant speed. Extrapolating to zero friction predicts infinite travel — supporting Newton's First Law: with no net force, the marble continues indefinitely.

Newton's Second Law of Motion

SET A Foundational (Easy)

State Newton's Second Law as a formula and define each symbol.Easy
Show Answer

F = ma. F = net force (N), m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (m/s²). The net force equals mass multiplied by acceleration.

A 10 kg object is pushed with a net force of 50 N. Calculate the acceleration.Easy
Show Answer

a = F/m = 50/10 = 5 m/s².

What is the SI unit of force? What does 1 Newton represent?Easy
Show Answer

The SI unit is the Newton (N). 1 N is the force needed to accelerate 1 kg at 1 m/s².

If mass doubles but force stays the same, what happens to the acceleration?Easy
Show Answer

a = F/m. If m doubles, a = F/(2m) = half the original. Acceleration halves.

Calculate the force needed to accelerate a 25 kg trolley at 3 m/s².Easy
Show Answer

F = ma = 25 × 3 = 75 N.

What is the mass of an object that accelerates at 8 m/s² when 40 N acts on it?Easy
Show Answer

m = F/a = 40/8 = 5 kg.

How does doubling the force on an object (mass unchanged) affect its acceleration?Easy
Show Answer

Acceleration doubles. It is directly proportional to net force.

Write the formula relating weight, mass, and gravitational field strength.Easy
Show Answer

W = mg. W = weight (N), m = mass (kg), g = 9.8 N/kg near Earth's surface. Weight acts vertically downward.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A 1200 kg car accelerates from rest to 20 m/s in 8 s. Calculate (a) acceleration and (b) net force.Medium
Show Answer

(a) a = 20/8 = 2.5 m/s². (b) F = 1200 × 2.5 = 3000 N.

A 70 kg person is in a lift accelerating upward at 2 m/s². Calculate the normal force. (g = 10 m/s²)Medium
Show Answer

N − W = ma. N = W + ma = 70×10 + 70×2 = 700 + 140 = 840 N.

Two forces act on a 5 kg box: 30 N right, 10 N left. Find net force and acceleration.Medium
Show Answer

Net F = 30 − 10 = 20 N right. a = 20/5 = 4 m/s² right.

Explain why a small car accelerates faster than a large truck with the same engine force.Medium
Show Answer

a = F/m. The car has much smaller mass. Same force, smaller m → larger acceleration. Acceleration is inversely proportional to mass.

A 500 kg rocket has 10,000 N thrust. Ignoring gravity and air resistance, calculate its acceleration.Medium
Show Answer

a = F/m = 10000/500 = 20 m/s².

A person weighs 600 N. What is their mass? (g = 10 m/s²)Medium
Show Answer

m = W/g = 600/10 = 60 kg.

A braking force of 4000 N decelerates a 2000 kg car. Find deceleration and time to stop from 30 m/s.Medium
Show Answer

a = 4000/2000 = 2 m/s². t = 30/2 = 15 s.

Explain the difference between mass and weight. Why do they differ on the Moon?Medium
Show Answer

Mass (kg) is the amount of matter — constant everywhere. Weight (N) = mg — depends on g. On the Moon g ≈ 1.6 m/s², so weight is about 1/6 of Earth weight, while mass is unchanged.

SET C Extended (Hard)

A 1500 kg car has driving force 5000 N and friction 1200 N. Find (a) net force, (b) acceleration, (c) speed after 6 s from rest.Hard
Show Answer

(a) F_net = 5000 − 1200 = 3800 N. (b) a = 3800/1500 = 2.53 m/s². (c) v = 0 + 2.53×6 = 15.2 m/s.

A 70 kg person is in a lift decelerating downward at 3 m/s². Find the normal force. (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

Decelerating downward means acceleration is upward at 3 m/s². N − mg = ma → N = m(g+a) = 70×(10+3) = 910 N.

Two blocks 3 kg and 5 kg are connected by a string, pulled by 16 N on a frictionless surface. Find (a) acceleration and (b) tension in the string.Hard
Show Answer

(a) a = 16/8 = 2 m/s². (b) T = 3×2 = 6 N. (Check: 16 − T = 5×2 → T = 6 N ✓)

Explain terminal velocity. What net force acts at that moment?Hard
Show Answer

Terminal velocity is the constant maximum speed when air resistance equals weight. Net force = 0, so acceleration = 0. For a belly-to-earth skydiver this is about 55 m/s; for a head-down position about 90 m/s.

A 2000 kg rocket has thrust 30,000 N and weight 19,600 N. Find the net force and upward acceleration.Hard
Show Answer

Net F = 30000 − 19600 = 10,400 N upward. a = 10400/2000 = 5.2 m/s².

Explain why a heavier parachutist reaches a higher terminal velocity than a lighter one using the same parachute.Hard
Show Answer

Terminal velocity requires drag = weight. The heavier parachutist has greater weight (mg), so more drag is needed. Drag increases with speed, so a higher speed is required before drag equals weight → higher terminal velocity.

Derive the unit of force (Newton) from F = ma using SI base units.Hard
Show Answer

F = ma. Unit = kg × m/s² = kg·m·s². This derived unit is named the Newton. 1 N = 1 kg·m·s².

A car engine gives 6000 N. Friction = 800 N, air resistance = 700 N. Find net force, acceleration, and state whether the car is speeding up or approaching constant speed.Hard
Show Answer

Total resistance = 1500 N. F_net = 6000 − 1500 = 4500 N. a = 4500/1200 = 3.75 m/s². The car is speeding up. It will reach constant speed when resistance increases to 6000 N (net force = 0).

Newton's Third Law of Motion

SET A Foundational (Easy)

State Newton's Third Law of Motion.Easy
Show Answer

For every action (force), there is an equal and opposite reaction. These forces act on different objects, are equal in magnitude, and opposite in direction.

When you push against a wall the wall pushes back with equal force. Why don't these cancel out?Easy
Show Answer

Action-reaction forces act on different objects. Your push acts on the wall; the wall's reaction acts on you. Forces only cancel when they act on the same object. These do not.

Identify the action-reaction pair when a rocket ejects gas downward.Easy
Show Answer

Action: rocket pushes gas downward. Reaction: gas pushes rocket upward.

How does a swimmer push off a wall to move forward? Identify action and reaction.Easy
Show Answer

Action: swimmer pushes wall backward. Reaction: wall pushes swimmer forward.

Give two examples of Newton's Third Law in everyday life (not rockets or swimming).Easy
Show Answer

1. Walking: foot pushes ground backward; ground pushes foot forward. 2. Gun recoil: gun pushes bullet forward; bullet pushes gun backward.

A horse pulls a cart. The cart pulls the horse back with equal force. Why does the system still move forward?Easy
Show Answer

The equal-and-opposite forces act on different objects. The horse's hooves push backward on the ground; ground pushes horse forward (friction). This external friction on the horse exceeds the cart's backward pull, producing a net forward force on the horse-cart system.

In a collision between a truck and a small car, which experiences greater force and which has greater acceleration?Easy
Show Answer

By Newton's Third Law, both experience equal forces. By F = ma, the car (much smaller mass) experiences much greater acceleration and thus greater deceleration/injury risk.

Explain why a gun recoils when fired.Easy
Show Answer

The gun pushes the bullet forward (action). The bullet pushes the gun backward with equal force (reaction). The gun recoils. Because the gun is much more massive, its recoil speed is much smaller than the bullet's speed.

SET B Developing (Medium)

Explain how a jet engine produces thrust using Newton's Third Law.Medium
Show Answer

The jet engine burns fuel and expels hot exhaust gases backward. By Newton's Third Law, the gases exert an equal force on the engine in the forward direction — this is thrust.

A 60 kg person jumps off a 40 kg skateboard at 3 m/s. Using momentum conservation, find the skateboard's velocity.Medium
Show Answer

System initially at rest: total p = 0. p_person = 60×3 = 180 kg·m/s forward. Skateboard: 40×v = 180 → v = 4.5 m/s backward.

An astronaut in space throws a 2 kg tool at 4 m/s. If the astronaut has mass 80 kg, what happens to the astronaut?Medium
Show Answer

Conservation of momentum: 80×v = −2×4 → v = −8/80 = 0.1 m/s backward.

Explain how birds fly using Newton's Third Law. What is the action and what is the reaction?Medium
Show Answer

Action: wings push air downward and backward. Reaction: air pushes bird upward and forward — providing lift and thrust.

A firefighter holds a hose ejecting 20 kg of water per second at 15 m/s. Calculate the reaction force on the firefighter.Medium
Show Answer

Force = mass flow rate × velocity = 20 × 15 = 300 N backward on the firefighter.

Identify all Newton's Third Law force pairs when a book rests on a table.Medium
Show Answer

Pair 1: Earth pulls book down (gravity on book); book pulls Earth up (gravity on Earth). Pair 2: table pushes book up (normal on book); book pushes table down (normal on table).

Why do rockets work in space where there is no air? Explain using Newton's Third Law.Medium
Show Answer

Rockets push against their own exhaust gases, not external air. Gas is ejected backward; gas pushes rocket forward. This works perfectly in vacuum — no external medium is needed.

A 1200 kg car crashes into a wall with 48,000 N force. What is the force on the wall, and why doesn't the wall move?Medium
Show Answer

By Newton's Third Law, the wall experiences 48,000 N from the car. The wall does not move because it is attached to the Earth. The Earth's enormous mass means a = F/m ≈ 0.

SET C Extended (Hard)

Explain why Newton's Third Law pairs never cancel, and resolve the horse-cart paradox by considering each object separately.Hard
Show Answer

Third Law pairs act on different objects — never included together in one net force calculation. Horse: forward friction from ground minus backward cart pull = net forward force. Cart: forward horse pull minus backward friction = net forward force. The external ground friction on the horse is the key driver, not the internal action-reaction pair between horse and cart.

A 5 g bullet is fired from a 3 kg rifle at 600 m/s. Calculate the recoil velocity and explain why it is much smaller.Hard
Show Answer

Conservation of momentum: 0 = 0.005×600 + 3×v → 3v = −3 → v = −1 m/s (1 m/s backward). Recoil is small because the rifle is 600× the mass of the bullet. Same force, much larger mass → much smaller acceleration and velocity.

Explain how a squid uses Newton's Third Law to propel itself. Describe the action, reaction, and forces.Hard
Show Answer

The squid draws water into its mantle, then forcefully ejects it backward through a siphon (action). The water pushes the squid forward (reaction) — jet propulsion. Direction is controlled by angling the siphon. This works in water exactly as a rocket works in vacuum — no external medium needed to "push against."

Two people on a frictionless surface push off each other from rest. Describe what happens using Newton's Second and Third Laws.Hard
Show Answer

Person A pushes B forward (action); B pushes A backward with equal force (reaction). By F = ma: a_A = F/m_A (backward), a_B = F/m_B (forward). The lighter person moves faster. Total momentum remains zero (equal and opposite momenta). If equal masses, they move at equal speeds in opposite directions.

Analyse all forces on a hovering helicopter using Newton's Third Law and Second Law.Hard
Show Answer

Rotor blades push air downward (action). Air pushes helicopter upward (reaction = lift). Hovering: net force = 0 → lift = weight (mg). The rotor must spin fast enough to push sufficient air downward to generate lift exactly equal to the helicopter's weight.

Two ice skaters (60 kg and 80 kg) push off from rest. The lighter moves at 4 m/s. Find the heavier skater's velocity.Hard
Show Answer

Total p = 0: 60×4 + 80×v = 0 → v = −240/80 = −3 m/s (3 m/s in the opposite direction).

Why do helicopters need a tail rotor? Use Newton's Third Law in your explanation.Hard
Show Answer

The main rotor spins and generates a torque reaction by Newton's Third Law that would spin the fuselage in the opposite direction. The tail rotor generates a sideways thrust (anti-torque) that cancels this rotation, keeping the helicopter pointed in the desired direction. Without it, the fuselage would spin uncontrollably.

Compare how propeller aircraft, jet aircraft, and rockets all rely on Newton's Third Law, noting what each pushes against.Hard
Show Answer

Propeller: blades push ambient air backward → air pushes aircraft forward. Needs external air. Jet: compresses and heats ambient air with fuel combustion, ejects exhaust backward → reaction is forward thrust. Needs external air intake. Rocket: carries both fuel and oxidiser; ejects exhaust at very high speed → forward thrust. Self-contained; works in vacuum. All use Newton's Third Law; only rockets are independent of the surrounding medium.

SET A Foundational (Easy)

What is a free body diagram? What does it show?Easy
Show Answer

A free body diagram isolates one object and shows all forces acting on it as labelled arrows showing direction and relative magnitude. The object is simplified to a dot or box.

Name the four common forces on a free body diagram for an object on a surface.Easy
Show Answer

1. Weight (W = mg) — downward. 2. Normal force (N) — perpendicular to surface, upward. 3. Friction (f) — opposing motion. 4. Applied force (F) — in direction of push/pull.

Describe the FBD for a book resting on a table. State the directions and relative sizes of all forces.Easy
Show Answer

Two equal and opposite forces: Weight W straight downward, Normal force N straight upward. At rest: N = W. No friction or applied force.

What is the normal force, and why is it called "normal"?Easy
Show Answer

The normal force is the contact force exerted by a surface on an object, perpendicular (normal in geometry) to the surface. It prevents objects from sinking into surfaces.

What direction does friction always act in a free body diagram?Easy
Show Answer

Friction always acts opposite to the direction of motion (or opposing the tendency of motion for static friction).

A box is pushed to the right on a rough floor at constant velocity. Describe all forces and the net force.Easy
Show Answer

Weight (down) = Normal (up); Applied force (right) = Friction (left). Constant velocity → net force = 0.

What does the length of an arrow represent on a free body diagram?Easy
Show Answer

The length represents the magnitude (size) of the force. Longer arrow = larger force.

Calculate the weight of a 15 kg object. In which direction does weight always act? (g = 10 m/s²)Easy
Show Answer

W = mg = 15 × 10 = 150 N, acting vertically downward.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A 10 kg block on a rough surface is pushed right with 60 N; friction = 25 N. Describe the FBD and calculate net force and acceleration. (g = 10 m/s²)Medium
Show Answer

W = 100 N down; N = 100 N up; F = 60 N right; f = 25 N left. Net F = 60 − 25 = 35 N right. a = 35/10 = 3.5 m/s².

Describe the FBD for a skydiver (a) just after jumping and (b) at terminal velocity. Compare forces.Medium
Show Answer

(a) Weight > drag → net downward force → accelerating. Arrow for weight longer than drag. (b) Weight = drag → net force = 0 → constant velocity. Both arrows equal length.

Describe the FBD for a car at constant speed on flat road. Confirm net force = 0.Medium
Show Answer

W down = N up; engine thrust forward = air resistance + road friction backward. All balanced → net force = 0. Constant velocity confirmed.

A 5 kg block hangs from a rope at rest. Describe its FBD and calculate tension. (g = 10 m/s²)Medium
Show Answer

W = 5 × 10 = 50 N down; Tension T upward. At rest: T = W = 50 N.

Explain the difference between static and kinetic friction in the context of FBDs. Which is larger?Medium
Show Answer

Static friction: opposes tendency to slide (adjustable up to maximum). Kinetic friction: opposes sliding motion (constant value). Static friction is larger — harder to start sliding than to maintain it.

Describe the forces (directions only) in the FBD for a 20 kg box on a rough 30° incline.Medium
Show Answer

Weight: vertically downward. Normal force: perpendicular to the incline, away from the surface. Friction: parallel to the incline, up the slope (opposing tendency to slide down).

Describe the FBD of a person in a lift accelerating upward. Is N greater than, equal to, or less than weight?Medium
Show Answer

N up; W down. Accelerating upward: N − W = ma → N = W + ma > W. The normal force is greater than weight. The person feels heavier.

What happens to the normal force on a person in a lift in free fall (cable snaps)? Calculate N for m = 60 kg. (g = 10 m/s²)Medium
Show Answer

In free fall, a = g. W − N = mg → N = W − mg = mg − mg = 0 N. The person feels weightless (apparent weight = 0). True weight still acts but the floor exerts no contact force.

SET C Extended (Hard)

A 30 kg box is pulled up a frictionless 30° incline by force F (parallel to incline) with acceleration 2 m/s² up. Calculate F. (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

Weight component down slope = mg sin30° = 30×10×0.5 = 150 N. F − 150 = ma = 30×2 = 60. F = 210 N.

Blocks A (4 kg) and B (6 kg) are stacked vertically on a surface; A is on top of B. Draw and describe each block's FBD including all contact forces.Hard
Show Answer

Block A: W_A = 40 N down; N_BA = 40 N up (B pushes A up). Block B: W_B = 60 N down; A pushes B down with 40 N; floor pushes B up with N = W_A + W_B = 100 N. By Newton's 3rd Law: B pushes A up 40 N ↔ A pushes B down 40 N.

A 1200 kg car brakes from 25 m/s to rest in 5 s. Describe its FBD and calculate the braking force. (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

FBD: W = 12,000 N down; N = 12,000 N up; braking friction backward; no driving force. a = 25/5 = 5 m/s². Braking force = ma = 1200×5 = 6,000 N.

Explain "apparent weight." Use FBDs to show when a person feels lighter, heavier, or normal in a lift.Hard
Show Answer

Apparent weight = normal force N (what a scale reads). At rest/constant velocity: N = mg (normal). Accelerating up: N = m(g+a) > mg (heavier). Accelerating down: N = m(g−a) < mg (lighter). Free fall: N = 0 (weightless). True weight mg is constant; only N changes with acceleration.

A 5 kg block hangs from two ropes at different angles. Explain qualitatively which rope carries more tension and why.Hard
Show Answer

The more inclined (angled) rope carries greater tension. Only the vertical component of each tension contributes to supporting the weight. For an angled rope, T_vertical = T cosθ < T. To provide the same vertical force as a vertical rope, a greater magnitude T is required. The more angled the rope, the higher its tension for the same vertical load.

An aircraft at constant altitude and speed has four main forces. Identify them, their directions, and write the two equilibrium conditions.Hard
Show Answer

Forces: (1) Weight W — vertically down. (2) Lift L — vertically up. (3) Thrust T — horizontally forward. (4) Drag D — horizontally backward. Equilibrium: Vertical: L = W. Horizontal: T = D.

A 10 kg box rests on a 40° rough incline (μ = 0.4). Does it slide? Use FBD analysis to find the net force. (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

W_parallel (down slope) = mg sin40° = 64.3 N. N = mg cos40° = 76.6 N. f_max = μN = 0.4×76.6 = 30.6 N. Since 64.3 N > 30.6 N, the box slides. Net force down slope = 64.3 − 30.6 = 33.7 N. a = 33.7/10 = 3.37 m/s².

Explain why free body diagrams are "free" — i.e., why the object is removed from its surroundings.Hard
Show Answer

"Free" means the object is isolated from its environment. This clearly identifies every force the environment exerts on the object, without confusing them with forces the object exerts on others (Newton's Third Law pairs). Only forces acting on the chosen object are drawn, forming the correct input for F_net = ma applied to that single object.

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Define momentum. State its formula and SI unit.Easy
Show Answer

p = mv. SI unit: kg·m/s. It is a vector quantity (has direction).

Calculate the momentum of a 5 kg ball moving at 8 m/s.Easy
Show Answer

p = 5 × 8 = 40 kg·m/s.

A 1500 kg car moves at 20 m/s. What is its momentum?Easy
Show Answer

p = 1500 × 20 = 30,000 kg·m/s.

State the Law of Conservation of Momentum.Easy
Show Answer

In a closed system (no net external forces), the total momentum before a collision equals total momentum after. Total momentum is conserved.

Is momentum a scalar or vector? Why does this matter?Easy
Show Answer

Momentum is a vector. Direction matters — objects moving in opposite directions have momenta that can cancel. This is essential for correct application of conservation of momentum.

A 2 kg object has momentum 14 kg·m/s. What is its velocity?Easy
Show Answer

v = p/m = 14/2 = 7 m/s.

Two identical balls move at the same speed in opposite directions. What is total momentum?Easy
Show Answer

p_total = +mv + (−mv) = 0 kg·m/s.

What is the momentum of a stationary object?Easy
Show Answer

p = m × 0 = 0 kg·m/s, regardless of mass.

SET B Developing (Medium)

A 3 kg ball at 6 m/s east hits a stationary 5 kg ball; they stick together. Find the combined velocity after.Medium
Show Answer

Distinguish between elastic and inelastic collisions in terms of momentum and kinetic energy.Medium

Both conserve momentum. Elastic: KE also conserved (billiard balls, ideal gas molecules). Inelastic: KE not conserved — some lost to heat/sound/deformation (car crash, clay balls). A perfectly inelastic collision is one where objects stick together.

Ball A (2 kg, 5 m/s right) hits Ball B (3 kg, 2 m/s left). After, A moves at 1 m/s left. Find B's final velocity.Medium

Right = positive. p_before = 10 − 6 = 4. p_after = 2(−1) + 3v_B = 4 → 3v_B = 6 → v_B = 2 m/s right.

Explain why a large ship takes a long time to stop after engines are cut.Medium
Show Answer

Enormous mass and speed give the ship very large momentum (p = mv). The only decelerating force is water resistance — small relative to the ship's momentum. By a = F/m, with huge m and small F, deceleration is tiny → very long stopping time and distance.

A 0.05 kg bullet at 800 m/s embeds in a 4.95 kg stationary block. Find the block+bullet velocity after impact.Medium

p = 0.05×800 = 40. 5v = 40 → v = 8 m/s.

Explain why conservation of momentum applies to an explosion.Medium
Show Answer

Before explosion, total momentum = 0. All forces are internal — Newton's Third Law ensures they cancel in the total. Fragments fly out with momenta that vector-sum to zero. Conservation holds as long as no external force acts.

Show Answer

It follows directly from Newton's Third Law (internal forces cancel) and Second Law (ΣF_ext = Δp/Δt → if ΣF_ext = 0, p is constant). It applies from subatomic to galactic scales. Condition: the system must be closed (isolated) — no net external force.

m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v. Solving: v = (m₁u₁ + m₂u₂) / (m₁ + m₂).

Explain why momentum is a better measure of "difficulty to stop" than kinetic energy alone. Use a numerical example.Hard
Show Answer

Truck: 5000 kg, 2 m/s. p = 10,000 kg·m/s; KE = 10,000 J. Car: 500 kg, 20 m/s. p = 10,000 kg·m/s; KE = 100,000 J. Same impulse (F×t = 10,000 N·s) is needed to stop either. But the car has 10× more KE to absorb structurally. Momentum determines required braking impulse; KE determines energy absorption. Both matter: momentum for stopping force×time, KE for structural damage.

Two equal-mass billiard balls collide elastically head-on: A at 3 m/s, B stationary. Prove A stops and B moves at 3 m/s.Hard
Show Answer

Momentum: 3 = v_A + v_B …(1). KE (elastic): 9 = v_A² + v_B² …(2). From (1): v_B = 3−v_A. Substitute in (2): 9 = v_A² + (3−v_A)² = 2v_A²−6v_A+9 → 2v_A(v_A−3) = 0 → v_A = 0 or 3. v_A = 3 means no collision, so v_A = 0 and v_B = 3 m/s. Proven.

SET A Foundational (Easy)

Define impulse. Write the formula in two ways.Easy
Show Answer

Impulse = F × t = Δp = m(v−u). SI unit: N·s or kg·m/s.

State the impulse-momentum theorem.Easy
Show Answer

F × t = m(v − u) = Δp. The impulse equals the change in momentum.

A force of 200 N acts on a ball for 0.05 s. Calculate the impulse.Easy
Show Answer

Impulse = 200 × 0.05 = 10 N·s.

A 0.5 kg ball changes velocity from 4 m/s to 12 m/s. Calculate the change in momentum.Easy
Show Answer

Δp = 0.5 × (12 − 4) = 4 kg·m/s.

Why do crash helmets protect cyclists? Explain using impulse.Easy
Show Answer

The foam crushes on impact, increasing contact time. Since F = Δp/t and Δp is fixed, larger t → smaller F. Peak force on the skull is reduced, preventing injury.

A 70 kg person running at 5 m/s stops in 2 s. Calculate the average stopping force.Easy
Show Answer

F = Δp/t = (70×5)/2 = 175 N opposing motion.

Show that N·s is equivalent to kg·m/s.Easy
Show Answer

N·s = (kg·m/s²) × s = kg·m/s. Both are units of impulse and momentum, confirming the theorem.

Give two examples where increasing collision time reduces injury.Easy
Show Answer

1. Airbags: inflate during crash, extending deceleration time → reduces peak force on face/chest. 2. Crash mats in gymnastics: compress over longer time → smaller force on landing.

Explain how continuous impulse from rocket exhaust produces acceleration using the impulse-momentum theorem.Medium
Show Answer

Each small mass Δm ejected at v_exhaust imparts momentum Δm×v_exhaust to the rocket forward (Newton's Third Law). Continuous ejection gives continuous impulse → continuous momentum change → acceleration. Thrust = Δm/Δt × v_exhaust.

Derive F = Δp/Δt from Newton's Second Law F = ma. Show the steps clearly.Hard

F = ma = m×Δv/Δt = (m×Δv)/Δt = Δ(mv)/Δt = Δp/Δt. Newton's Second Law is equivalently the rate of change of momentum.

Analyse how crumple zones, airbags, and seatbelts each use the impulse principle and how they complement each other.Hard
Show Answer

Crumple zones: deform progressively, increasing the time the car takes to stop → F = Δp/t → lower peak deceleration force on occupants. Seatbelts: keep occupant moving with the car; load limiters let belt stretch slightly → extends stopping time for the torso → spreads impulse over stronger chest/pelvis. Upward = positive. (a) Δp = 0.1×(6−(−8)) = 0.1×14 = 1.4 kg·m/s upward. (b) F_floor = 1.4/0.01 = 140 N upward. (c) Net = 140 − 0.1×10 = 140 − 1 = 139 N upward.

Explain why a karate expert breaks a brick with a quick strike rather than pressing slowly. Use impulse and force concepts.Hard
Show Answer

F = Δp/t. A quick strike has very short contact time t → enormous force (F = Δp/t is very large). This exceeds the brick's fracture strength. A slow press distributes the same impulse over a long time → small force → well below fracture threshold. Maximum force through minimum time is the key, not maximum energy.

A force-time graph shows a rectangle: 500 N constant for 0.06 s, then drops to 0. Find (a) impulse, (b) velocity change for a 2 kg object, (c) final velocity if initial was 3 m/s.Hard
Show Answer

(a) Impulse = 500×0.06 = 30 N·s. (b) Δv = 30/2 = 15 m/s. (c) v_final = 3 + 15 = 18 m/s.

Explain why F = Δp/Δt is more general than F = ma. Give an example where F = ma fails but F = Δp/Δt still holds.Hard
Show Answer

F = ma assumes constant mass. F = Δp/Δt = d(mv)/dt handles variable mass: F = m(dv/dt) + v(dm/dt). Example: a rocket burns fuel and its mass decreases continuously. F = ma (with fixed m) gives the wrong answer. F = Δp/Δt correctly accounts for both the velocity change and the mass change, and is the basis of the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation. Also applies to conveyor belts, water jets, and relativistic motion.
