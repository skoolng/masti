The Sun and a burning candle (also acceptable: torch, stars, glowing filament bulb).

The Moon and a wooden table — they reflect light but do not produce their own light.

What type of wave is light?Easy
Show Answer

Light is an electromagnetic (transverse) wave.

Can light travel through a vacuum? Give evidence.Easy
Show Answer

Yes. Sunlight reaches Earth by travelling through the vacuum of space — it needs no medium.

What is a ray of light?Easy
Show Answer

A ray of light is a straight line with an arrowhead showing the direction of travel of light.

How many kilometres does light travel in one second?Easy
Show Answer

3 × 10⁵ km = 300,000 km per second.

Is the Moon luminous or non-luminous? Explain why we can see it.Easy
Show Answer

Non-luminous. We see the Moon because it reflects sunlight toward Earth.

Name the phenomenon that explains why a pin-hole camera forms an inverted image.Easy

SET B Medium – Understand & Apply

Explain why we cannot see around corners using the concept of rectilinear propagation.Medium
Show Answer

Light travels only in straight lines and cannot bend around a corner. Objects behind a wall lie in the "shadow" region — no direct light from them reaches our eyes.

Calculate how long light takes to travel from the Sun to Earth (distance ≈ 1.5 × 10¹¹ m). Show working.Medium
Show Answer

Time = distance ÷ speed = 1.5 × 10¹¹ ÷ 3 × 10⁸ = 500 s ≈ 8 minutes 20 seconds.

Distinguish between a luminous source and an illuminated object with one example each.Medium
Show Answer

A luminous source produces its own light (e.g., the Sun). An illuminated object only reflects incident light (e.g., a book in sunlight).

How does a pin-hole camera demonstrate that light travels in straight lines?Medium
Show Answer

Rays from the top of an object pass straight through the pinhole to the bottom of the screen; rays from the bottom reach the top. The resulting inverted image can only be explained if light travels in straight lines without bending.

Why does light travel much faster than sound?Medium
Show Answer

Light is an electromagnetic wave requiring no medium — it travels at 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum. Sound is a mechanical wave needing a medium and travels at only ~340 m/s in air.

What evidence shows that light is a form of energy?Medium
Show Answer

Light drives photosynthesis, powers solar cells (converting light to electricity), causes photographic film to react, and heats surfaces — all showing it carries energy.

A student claims a glowing firefly is non-luminous because it is an animal. Is this correct? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

Incorrect. A luminous object is any object that produces its own light. A firefly produces light through bioluminescence, so it is luminous regardless of it being an animal.

Identify each as luminous or non-luminous: (a) candle flame, (b) mirror, (c) the Sun, (d) planet Venus.Medium
Show Answer

(a) Luminous — combustion produces light. (b) Non-luminous — reflects light. (c) Luminous — nuclear fusion. (d) Non-luminous — reflects sunlight.

Light from a star takes 4 years to reach Earth. What does this tell us about that star?Medium
Show Answer

The star is approximately 4 light-years away. We are seeing the star as it was 4 years ago — it may not even exist anymore in its current form.

Explain why during a thunderstorm you see lightning before you hear thunder, even though both occur simultaneously.Medium
Show Answer

Light (3 × 10⁸ m/s) reaches us nearly instantly from 3 km away (≈0.00001 s). Sound (~340 m/s) takes ~8.8 s to travel the same distance. The enormous speed difference causes the gap we experience.

Valid for everyday distances (nanoseconds to microseconds — negligible). Invalid in astronomy: sunlight takes 8 min; nearest star's light 4 years; galaxy light millions of years. We observe the past, not the present state of distant objects. The approximation fails at cosmic scales.

Design an experiment using a pin-hole camera to confirm rectilinear propagation. State apparatus, method, observation and conclusion.Hard
Show Answer

Apparatus: Cardboard box, pin, tracing paper screen, lit candle, dark room. Method: Make a tiny hole in one face; fix tracing paper opposite; observe screen with candle placed in front. Observation: Inverted image of candle forms on screen. Conclusion: Rays from top of candle travel straight through hole to bottom of screen and vice versa — proving straight-line travel. No other path geometry could produce this inversion.

Compare light and sound using three criteria: nature of wave, medium required, and speed in air.Hard
Show Answer

1. Nature: Light = transverse electromagnetic wave; Sound = longitudinal mechanical wave. 2. Medium: Light needs no medium; Sound needs a material medium. 3. Speed in air: Light ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s; Sound ≈ 340 m/s — light is ~10⁶ times faster.

A spacecraft is 6 × 10⁹ km from Earth. How long (in minutes) does a light-speed signal take to arrive? Show working.Hard
Show Answer

Distance = 6 × 10¹² m. Time = 6 × 10¹² ÷ 3 × 10⁸ = 2 × 10⁴ s = 20,000 ÷ 60 ≈ 333 minutes (~5.6 hours).

Using electromagnetic wave properties, explain why light passes through glass but not through a wooden door.Hard
Show Answer

Glass is transparent — its atomic structure allows visible-light frequencies to pass with minimal absorption. Wood is opaque — its molecular structure absorbs and scatters visible wavelengths, preventing transmission. The interaction depends on the material's atomic properties and the frequency of the EM wave.

Explain why astronauts in space see a black sky even during the day, while on Earth we see a blue sky.Hard
Show Answer

On Earth, the atmosphere scatters short-wavelength blue light in all directions (Rayleigh scattering), making the sky appear blue. In space there is no atmosphere to scatter light, so only direct rays are visible — the surrounding sky looks black.

Critically evaluate: "Non-luminous objects are useless because they produce no light." Do you agree? Justify with three examples.Hard
Show Answer

Disagree strongly. (1) The Moon (non-luminous) reflects sunlight enabling tidal cycles and night navigation. (2) Mirrors (non-luminous) redirect light for telescopes and periscopes. (3) Planets are non-luminous but visible by reflected sunlight, enabling us to study the solar system. Their ability to reflect, refract, or redirect light makes them scientifically and practically essential.

Explain how a pin-hole camera image changes if (a) the hole is made larger and (b) the box length is doubled.Hard
Show Answer

(a) Larger hole: multiple overlapping rays from different angles → blurred image. (b) Longer box: rays diverge over greater distance → larger (magnified) image. Image size ∝ box length; image size/object size = image distance/object distance.

MYP Inquiry: If the pin-hole camera forms an inverted image, what would happen if two pin-holes were used? Predict and explain.Hard
Show Answer

Two pin-holes would produce two slightly offset inverted images of the same object, overlapping on the screen. Each hole independently forms its own image at a slightly different position (parallax). The result is a blurred double-image. This further confirms that each ray travels independently in a straight line through its respective hole — there is no interaction between the two light paths.

SET A Easy – Recall & Identify

What is a shadow?Easy
Show Answer

A shadow is a dark region formed behind an opaque object when it blocks light from reaching that area.

Name the three types of materials based on how they interact with light.Easy
Show Answer

Transparent (e.g., clear glass), translucent (e.g., frosted glass), and opaque (e.g., wood).

Which type of object forms the darkest shadow — transparent, translucent, or opaque?Easy
Show Answer

An opaque object forms the darkest shadow because it blocks all light.

What is the umbra of a shadow?Easy
Show Answer

The umbra is the region of total shadow where no direct light from the source reaches.

What is the penumbra of a shadow?Easy
Show Answer

The penumbra is the region of partial shadow surrounding the umbra, where some but not all light from the source is blocked.

What three things are needed to form a shadow?Easy
Show Answer

A source of light, an opaque (or translucent) object, and a screen or surface to receive the shadow.

What happens to the size of a shadow as the object moves closer to the light source?Easy
Show Answer

The shadow becomes larger as the object moves closer to the light source.

Why does a point (small) light source produce a sharper shadow than a large light source?Easy
Show Answer

A point source produces only an umbra with sharp edges. A large source also creates a penumbra (blurry partial shadow) around the umbra.

Why do shadows outdoors at noon tend to be shorter than in early morning or late evening?Easy
Show Answer

At noon the Sun is nearly overhead — light strikes at a steep angle making shadows short. In morning/evening the Sun is near the horizon, creating long oblique shadows.

Give one real-life example of umbra and one of penumbra in everyday life.Easy
Show Answer

Umbra: the very dark centre of your shadow on a bright sunny day. Penumbra: the soft blurry edge of your shadow on a cloudy day (diffuse light source).

SET B Medium – Understand & Apply

Explain why a larger light source produces both umbra and penumbra while a point source produces only umbra.Medium
Show Answer

With a large source, some parts of the source are blocked and others are not — regions where all light is blocked = umbra; regions where only partial light is blocked = penumbra. A point source either completely blocked or not — no partial zone exists.

A ball moves from close to a torch toward a wall. Describe how the shadow changes in size and sharpness.Medium
Show Answer

Close to torch: very large, blurry shadow (large penumbra). As ball moves toward wall: shadow shrinks progressively and edges sharpen; near the wall the shadow is nearly the same size as the ball with crisp edges.

Classify each material and predict shadow quality: (a) clear glass, (b) thick wood, (c) frosted shower glass.Medium
Show Answer

(a) Transparent → very faint or no visible shadow. (b) Opaque → sharp, dark shadow. (c) Translucent → pale, blurry shadow.

How would you investigate how shadow length changes with the angle of light? List the variables.Medium
Show Answer

Independent variable: Angle of light source above horizontal. Dependent variable: Length of shadow. Controlled variables: Same object height, same surface, same distance from object to screen, same light intensity.

A tree 6 m tall casts a 4 m shadow. At the same time a building casts a 20 m shadow. Find the building's height using similar triangles.Medium
Show Answer

Height/shadow ratio = 6/4 = 1.5. Building height = 1.5 × 20 = 30 m.

Explain why a shadow has the same shape as the silhouette (cross-section) of an object, not its full 3D shape.Medium
Show Answer

A shadow is a 2D projection — it shows only the outline of the object that faces the light source. The depth of the 3D object does not contribute to the shadow's outline since light travels in straight lines past the edges.

If you stand inside the umbra of an eclipse, what do you observe? What if you stand in the penumbra?Medium
Show Answer

Umbra: complete darkness — total solar eclipse (Sun fully hidden). Penumbra: Sun partially covered — partial solar eclipse; you still receive some sunlight.

Why does a shadow of a round ball always appear circular regardless of orientation?Medium
Show Answer

A sphere has a circular cross-section in every direction — rotating it does not change its silhouette. The shadow is always a circle because the profile facing the light is always a circle.

How do professional photographers use large "soft boxes" instead of a single flash? Explain the shadow science.Medium
Show Answer

A small flash (point source) creates sharp harsh shadows (clear umbra). A large softbox (extended source) creates broad penumbra with soft gradual shadow edges, making skin look smoother. The large source simultaneously illuminates from many angles, reducing deep shadows.

Explain using rectilinear propagation how sundials work and give one limitation.Medium
Show Answer

The gnomon (vertical rod) casts a shadow on a calibrated dial as the Sun moves across the sky. Because light travels in straight lines, the shadow moves predictably with the Sun's position, indicating solar time. Limitation: Cannot work at night or on cloudy days; shows local solar time, not standard clock time.

Explain, using the ray model, why a shadow's edges become sharper when (a) the light source gets smaller, and (b) the object moves closer to the screen.Hard
Show Answer

(a) Smaller source → less divergence from different source points → penumbra zone shrinks → sharper edge. Perfect point source eliminates penumbra entirely. (b) Object closer to screen → less space for shadow rays to diverge → narrower penumbra → sharper edge. When object touches the screen the shadow exactly matches the object's profile.

Forensic scientists use shadow analysis in photos to determine when a photo was taken. Explain the scientific principles involved.Hard
Show Answer

The Sun moves at ~15°/hour. Shadow length depends on the Sun's altitude (angle above horizon) and shadow direction gives the Sun's azimuth. Combining these with the location's latitude and longitude, scientists use solar position algorithms to calculate the precise time and date when the photo was taken.

A point source 30 cm from an opaque disc of diameter 5 cm projects a shadow on a screen 90 cm beyond the disc. Calculate the shadow diameter. Show working.Hard
Show Answer

Distance from source to screen = 30 + 90 = 120 cm. Scale factor = 120/30 = 4. Shadow diameter = 5 × 4 = 20 cm.

Explain why the shadow of a cylinder varies with orientation but the shadow of a sphere is always circular. Link to cross-section geometry.Hard
Show Answer

A sphere has a circular cross-section in every direction — rotating it does not change its silhouette. A cylinder has a circular cross-section along its axis but a rectangular silhouette from the side. The shadow is the 2D projection of the silhouette facing the light, so it changes for the cylinder but not for the sphere.

Critically analyse: "A translucent material is always better than opaque for architectural windows because it provides privacy and light." Evaluate scientifically.Hard
Show Answer

Partly valid. Translucent materials scatter light, reducing glare and providing privacy while admitting diffuse light. However, they reduce visible light transmission by up to 60%, can cause eye strain, and block outdoor views. Opaque walls prevent all light — unsuitable for windows. Best practice combines transparent glass (views/maximum light) with translucent panels (privacy partitions). The claim oversimplifies the design trade-off.

A student argues: "If light is a wave, it should diffract around objects and form no shadow." Evaluate this using wave physics.Hard
Show Answer

Diffraction is significant only when the wavelength is comparable to the obstacle size. Visible light wavelength ≈ 400–700 nm. Everyday objects (cm to m scale) are millions of times larger, making diffraction effects negligible. Light behaves as if it travels in straight rays (geometric optics limit). Only at extremely small slit widths (comparable to wavelength) does visible diffraction occur. Sharp shadows are the result of this geometric optics regime.

Analyse why shadows in space (like the Moon's shadow on Earth) have very large penumbra zones compared to everyday shadows.Hard
Show Answer

The Sun is a very large extended source (~1.4 million km diameter). The Moon, though much smaller, is far from Earth. The Moon's umbra (total shadow) is only ~100–270 km wide at Earth's surface. The penumbra, however, fans out over thousands of km because light from different parts of the massive Sun creates a huge partial-shadow zone. The scale is dominated by the Sun's enormous size acting as an extended source.

MYP Reflection: Eratosthenes (276–194 BCE) calculated Earth's circumference using shadows. Explain his method and the scientific principle it demonstrates.Hard
Show Answer

On the summer solstice, Eratosthenes observed that sunlight fell directly down a well in Syene (no shadow), but in Alexandria (800 km north) a vertical stick cast a shadow at ~7.2° from vertical. Since 7.2° ≈ 1/50 of 360°, he deduced Earth's circumference = 50 × 800 km = 40,000 km. The principle: parallel sunrays (light in straight lines) hit a curved Earth at different angles, and shadow angles reflect Earth's curvature — a brilliant application of rectilinear propagation and geometry.

What is a solar eclipse?Easy
Show Answer

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon comes between the Sun and Earth, blocking sunlight and casting a shadow on Earth.

What is a lunar eclipse?Easy
Show Answer

A lunar eclipse occurs when Earth comes between the Sun and the Moon, and Earth's shadow falls on the Moon.

State the correct alignment for a solar eclipse (Sun, Moon, Earth).Easy
Show Answer

Sun → Moon → Earth (Moon is between Sun and Earth).

State the correct alignment for a lunar eclipse.Easy
Show Answer

Sun → Earth → Moon (Earth is between Sun and Moon).

During which Moon phase can a solar eclipse occur?Easy
Show Answer

New moon phase (Moon between Earth and Sun).

During which Moon phase can a lunar eclipse occur?Easy
Show Answer

Full moon phase (Earth between Sun and Moon).

What is a total solar eclipse?Easy
Show Answer

A total solar eclipse occurs when the Moon completely blocks the Sun's disc as seen from within the Moon's umbra on Earth.

What is a partial solar eclipse?Easy
Show Answer

A partial solar eclipse occurs when only part of the Sun is covered by the Moon, seen from within the Moon's penumbra.

Why does the Moon appear reddish during a total lunar eclipse?Easy
Show Answer

Earth's atmosphere refracts red wavelengths of sunlight around Earth's curve onto the Moon, giving it a dark reddish-orange "blood moon" colour.

Why don't solar eclipses happen every new moon?Easy
Show Answer

The Moon's orbital plane is tilted ~5° relative to Earth's orbital plane. Most new moons, the Moon passes slightly above or below the Sun's position — perfect alignment occurs only occasionally.

SET B Medium – Understand & Apply

Compare a total and an annular solar eclipse. What causes the difference?Medium
Show Answer

Total eclipse: Moon near perigee (closest to Earth) — apparent size ≥ Sun's; completely covers Sun. Annular eclipse: Moon near apogee (farthest) — apparent size smaller than Sun's; a ring ("annulus") of sunlight remains visible around the Moon.

Explain why a total solar eclipse is visible from only a narrow strip on Earth while partial eclipse is seen over a much wider area.Medium
Show Answer

The Moon's umbra tip is only ~100–270 km wide where it touches Earth. Totality is experienced only within this narrow path. The much wider penumbra (thousands of km) allows a larger region to see a partial eclipse.

During a lunar eclipse, the Moon turns dark red rather than going completely black. Explain why.Medium
Show Answer

Earth's atmosphere acts like a lens, bending (refracting) red wavelengths of sunlight around the curve of Earth into the umbra. Blue and violet light is scattered away by the atmosphere (as in sunsets), so only red light reaches the Moon.

Why can a lunar eclipse be seen from anywhere on Earth's night side, but a total solar eclipse only from a narrow strip?Medium
Show Answer

Earth's shadow engulfs the entire Moon during a lunar eclipse — the darkened Moon is visible to everyone on the night side. During a solar eclipse, the Moon's tiny umbra falls on only a narrow strip of Earth; only people in that path see totality.

Describe what observers on the Moon would see during a total solar eclipse on Earth.Medium
Show Answer

From the Moon, observers would see Earth move in front of the Sun. Earth's dark disc would be surrounded by a brilliant ring of the lit atmosphere, and a small dark spot (the Moon's umbra) would move across Earth's surface.

Predict: If the Moon were twice as far from Earth as it is now, would total solar eclipses still occur? Explain scientifically.Medium
Show Answer

No. At twice the distance, the Moon's apparent angular diameter would be halved (~0.25°), much smaller than the Sun's apparent diameter (~0.5°). The Moon could never fully cover the Sun — only annular eclipses would occur.

Why were ancient civilisations frightened by solar eclipses while modern scientists value them as research opportunities?Medium
Show Answer

Ancient civilisations lacked scientific understanding and interpreted sudden daytime darkness as a supernatural omen. Modern scientists use totality to study the Sun's corona (normally invisible), test general relativity, and measure solar wind — opportunities unavailable at other times.

A total solar eclipse lasts at most about 7–8 minutes. Why is it so brief?Medium
Show Answer

The Moon's umbra moves at ~1700 km/h across Earth. The narrow umbra (~100–270 km wide) sweeps past any location in minutes. Both the Moon and Sun discs are nearly identical in apparent size, so the overlap window is brief.

Explain the difference between a solar eclipse and a transit of Venus. Why can't Venus cause a total eclipse?Medium
Show Answer

A transit occurs when Venus (or Mercury) passes in front of the Sun as seen from Earth. Venus is much farther from Earth than the Moon, so its apparent disc is tiny — it appears as a small dark dot, never covering the Sun fully. Only the Moon has the right apparent size to cause total eclipses.

Explain using geometry why the path of totality during a solar eclipse moves from west to east across Earth even though Earth rotates west to east.Medium
Show Answer

The Moon orbits west to east, same direction as Earth's rotation, but at a higher orbital speed equivalent at Earth's surface (~3,683 km/h) than Earth's rotation (~1,675 km/h at equator). The Moon's shadow moves faster eastward than Earth's surface, sweeping the path of totality from west to east.

SET C Hard – MYP Criterion B/D Analysis

The Sun's diameter is ~400× the Moon's diameter, yet they appear the same size from Earth. Use this to calculate the ratio of Sun-Earth distance to Moon-Earth distance.Hard
Show Answer

Apparent angular size ∝ actual diameter / distance. Equal apparent size means: D_Sun/d_Sun = D_Moon/d_Moon → d_Sun/d_Moon = D_Sun/D_Moon = 400. (Actual: Moon ~384,000 km; Sun ~150,000,000 km → ratio ~390 ≈ 400.)

Explain using the shadow-cone model why the Moon's umbra may not reach Earth, causing an annular eclipse.Hard
Show Answer

The Sun is larger than the Moon, so the Moon's umbra converges to a point ~379,000 km behind the Moon. When Moon is near apogee (farther than ~379,000 km from Earth), the umbra's tip doesn't reach Earth's surface → annular eclipse. At perigee (closer), the tip reaches Earth → total eclipse.

In 1919, Eddington used a total solar eclipse to test Einstein's General Theory of Relativity. Explain what he measured and why an eclipse was essential.Hard
Show Answer

Einstein predicted massive objects (like the Sun) bend light paths. Eddington photographed stars near the Sun during totality and compared positions to night photographs taken six months earlier. Stars appeared shifted by ~1.75 arcseconds — matching Einstein's prediction exactly. The eclipse was essential because normally the Sun's glare makes nearby stars invisible; only during totality can stars near the Sun be photographed.

Critically evaluate: "Solar eclipses prove that the Moon and Sun are the same size." Is this scientifically accurate?Hard
Show Answer

Scientifically inaccurate. Total solar eclipses result from a coincidence: the Sun is ~400× wider but also ~400× farther away, making apparent angular diameters similar. Actual sizes: Sun = 1,390,000 km diameter; Moon = 3,474 km — the Sun is ~400× larger in diameter and ~64 million times larger in volume. The eclipse is an optical coincidence of apparent sizes, not evidence of equal actual sizes.

Astronomers predict total solar eclipses will no longer occur in ~600 million years. Explain why using the Moon's orbital dynamics.Hard
Show Answer

The Moon recedes from Earth at ~3.8 cm/year due to tidal energy transfer. As the Moon moves farther away, its apparent angular diameter decreases. Eventually its apparent size will always be smaller than the Sun's apparent size — making total solar eclipses impossible. Only annular eclipses would occur. At ~600 million years, the recession (~22,800 km added) pushes the Moon's umbra consistently beyond Earth's surface.

Design a scale model of a solar eclipse. What ratio of sizes and distances would you need? Show calculations using real data.Hard
Show Answer

Scale: 1 cm = 1000 km. Moon = 3.47 cm diameter; Earth = 12.7 cm; Sun = 1390 cm (13.9 m!) diameter. Moon-Earth distance = 384 cm (3.84 m); Sun-Earth distance = 150,000 cm (1500 m). A practical table-top model is nearly impossible at correct relative distances — the Sun-equivalent would need to be 1.5 km away. Demonstrates how vast the true scale is compared to everyday intuition.

Compare the scientific value of studying a total solar eclipse corona versus normal solar observation. What is the corona and why is it normally invisible?Hard
Show Answer

The corona is the Sun's outermost atmosphere — superheated plasma at ~1–3 million °C extending millions of km. The photosphere (~5500°C) is ~1 million× brighter, completely outshining the corona. During totality, the photosphere is blocked, revealing the corona's pearly white streamers. Scientists study coronal structure, temperature anomalies (corona is paradoxically hotter than surface), and solar wind origins — unresolved questions in solar physics.

How have total solar eclipses influenced human culture, religion and scientific progress? Give three specific historical examples.Hard
Show Answer

1. Babylon (600s BCE): Astronomers recorded eclipses on clay tablets and discovered the 18-year Saros cycle, enabling eclipse prediction centuries early. 2. Thales of Greece (~585 BCE): Predicted a solar eclipse, reportedly halting a battle between Lydians and Medes — showing how scientific knowledge held political power. 3. Eddington (1919): Used eclipse to confirm Einstein's relativity, revolutionising physics. Eclipses drove calendar development, astronomical knowledge, and shaped religious worldviews across every civilisation.

Using the umbra/penumbra model, explain the difference between an observer experiencing a total eclipse and one experiencing a penumbral lunar eclipse.Hard
Show Answer

Total solar eclipse (umbra): observer is in the Moon's complete shadow — zero direct sunlight reaches them; totality is dramatic darkness. Penumbral lunar eclipse: the Moon passes through Earth's penumbra (partial shadow) — only part of sunlight is blocked from the Moon's surface. The Moon dims slightly but does not go dark; the effect is subtle and often barely noticeable to the naked eye. The penumbra is a region of partial blockage, not total shadow.

Scientific value: Extended viewing time (aircraft can follow the Moon's shadow, extending totality); valuable data collection platforms. SET A Easy – Recall & Identify

State the first law of reflection.Easy
Show Answer

The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r).

State the second law of reflection.Easy
Show Answer

The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

What is the normal in the context of reflection?Easy
Show Answer

The normal is an imaginary line drawn perpendicular (90°) to the reflecting surface at the point where the incident ray strikes.

From which line are the angles of incidence and reflection measured?Easy
Show Answer

Both angles are measured from the normal, not from the mirror surface.

If the angle of incidence is 35°, what is the angle of reflection?Easy
Show Answer

35° — angle of incidence always equals angle of reflection.

What is regular (specular) reflection?Easy
Show Answer

Regular reflection occurs on smooth surfaces where parallel rays all reflect at the same angle, producing a clear, sharp image (e.g., a plane mirror).

What is diffuse reflection?Easy
Show Answer

Diffuse reflection occurs on rough surfaces where parallel rays reflect in many different directions. No clear image forms, but the surface is still visible (e.g., a white wall).

A ray hits a mirror along the normal (at 0° angle of incidence). What happens?Easy
Show Answer

The ray reflects straight back along the same path (angle of reflection = 0°).

What angle does the incident ray make with the mirror surface if the angle of incidence is 40°?Easy
Show Answer

Angle with surface = 90° − 40° = 50° (angles with the surface and with the normal are complementary).

Name one instrument that uses the laws of reflection.Easy
Show Answer

SET A Easy – Recall & Identify

What is refraction of light?Easy
Show Answer

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another medium of different optical density.

In which direction does light bend when entering a denser medium (e.g., air to glass)?Easy
Show Answer

Light bends toward the normal when entering a denser (optically more dense) medium.

In which direction does light bend when entering a less dense medium (e.g., glass to air)?Easy
Show Answer

Light bends away from the normal when entering a less dense medium.

What is the refractive index of a medium?Easy
Show Answer

Refractive index (n) = speed of light in vacuum / speed of light in the medium. It indicates how much slower light travels in that medium compared to vacuum.

What is Snell's Law?Easy
Show Answer

Snell's Law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂, where n₁ and n₂ are refractive indices and θ₁ and θ₂ are the angles of incidence and refraction (measured from the normal).

What happens to the speed of light when it enters glass from air?Easy
Show Answer

The speed decreases — light travels more slowly in glass (denser medium) than in air.

Give two everyday examples of refraction.Easy
Show Answer

A pencil appears bent/broken when placed in a glass of water; a swimming pool appears shallower than it is (apparent depth).

What is apparent depth? How does it relate to refraction?Easy
Show Answer

Apparent depth is the depth at which an object in a denser medium (like water) appears to be when viewed from a less dense medium (like air). Because light refracts away from the normal when leaving water, objects appear shallower (closer to the surface) than they actually are.

If a ray of light hits a glass surface at exactly 0° angle of incidence (perpendicular to surface), does it refract?Easy
Show Answer

No — the ray passes straight through without bending because it is already travelling along the normal; there is no component of the wave encountering the medium at an angle.

What is the critical angle?Easy
Show Answer

The critical angle is the angle of incidence (from the normal) in the denser medium at which the refracted ray travels at 90° to the normal (along the surface). Beyond this angle, total internal reflection occurs.

1. White sunlight enters the front of a spherical raindrop and refracts at the air-water boundary — dispersion separates colours. 2. The dispersed light reflects off the back interior surface of the raindrop. 3. The reflected light refracts again as it exits the front of the drop — further separating colours. 4. Red exits at ~42°, violet at ~40° from the anti-solar direction. An observer sees the circular bow with red outside and violet inside.

Newton passed the spectrum through a second inverted prism and obtained white light again. What does this experiment prove?Medium
Show Answer

It proves: (1) White light is a mixture of all colours of the visible spectrum. (2) The prism does not add or create colours — it merely separates them. (3) The process is fully reversible — colours can be recombined into white light. (4) Each colour has a fixed wavelength that bends by a fixed amount.

Explain why a glass chandelier or prism creates rainbow spots on walls and ceilings when white light shines through it.Medium
Show Answer

The glass prism faces and droplet-shaped pieces disperse white light into its spectrum through refraction. Each angle of glass refracts different wavelengths by different amounts, projecting separate colour spots to different locations on surrounding walls. The overall pattern creates rainbow patches scattered around the room.

Why can you only see a rainbow when standing with the Sun behind you?Medium
Show Answer

A rainbow is seen at a specific angle (~42° for red) from the anti-solar direction (the point directly opposite the Sun from your perspective). To see light refracted and internally reflected from raindrops at this angle, the Sun must be behind the observer. The bow is always centred on the anti-solar point.

Explain what a secondary rainbow is and why the colour order is reversed compared to the primary rainbow.Medium
Show Answer

A secondary rainbow forms at ~51° angle from the anti-solar point, from raindrops where light undergoes two internal reflections instead of one. Each additional reflection reverses the colour order. The secondary bow has violet on the outside and red on the inside — opposite to the primary. It is also dimmer due to energy lost at each extra reflection.

How is dispersion used in spectroscopy to identify elements?Medium
Show Answer

When elements are heated and emit light, that light is passed through a prism or diffraction grating to disperse it into a spectrum. Each element emits a unique set of wavelengths (emission spectrum) — appearing as specific coloured lines. Scientists compare these "fingerprint" spectra to identify elements in stars, gases, and materials without physical contact. This is how hydrogen, helium, and over 60 elements were identified in the Sun before being found on Earth.

Explain what chromatic aberration is in a lens and how it relates to dispersion.Medium
Show Answer

Chromatic aberration is a lens defect where different colours focus at slightly different distances from the lens (because the lens refracts each wavelength by a different amount — dispersion). Red light focuses farther from the lens; violet closer. The result is blurred, colour-fringed images. Achromatic doublets (two lens elements of different glass types combined) cancel out the dispersion to produce a single focal point for all colours.

Why does the sky appear red and orange at sunrise and sunset but blue at midday?Medium
Show Answer

At midday, sunlight travels a short path through the atmosphere. Blue wavelengths scatter strongly (Rayleigh scattering ∝ 1/λ⁴) in all directions, making the sky appear blue. At sunrise/sunset, sunlight travels a much longer path through atmosphere. Most blue light is scattered away before reaching the observer; mainly longer-wavelength red and orange light remains in the direct beam — making the Sun and sky appear red/orange.

How does a CD or DVD produce rainbow colours without a prism?Medium
Show Answer

A CD's surface has microscopic tracks spaced ~1.6 µm apart — comparable to the wavelength of visible light. This acts as a diffraction grating, diffracting different wavelengths at different angles. When white light hits the CD, each colour reflects at a different angle to the observer's eye, producing a rainbow effect. This is diffraction-based separation, not refraction-based dispersion, but the visible spectrum result is similar.

Explain why Newton's demonstration that prisms split white light was initially controversial, and what conclusion it overturned about the nature of colour.Hard
Show Answer

Before Newton (1666), the prevailing view (championed by Descartes and others) was that white light was "pure" and prisms added colour to it through some modification. Newton showed by recombining the spectrum with a second prism that colours were intrinsic to white light — it was a mixture. He also showed each colour was associated with a fixed refrangibility (specific bending angle). This overturned the idea of white as "pure" light and established that colour is a property of specific light wavelengths, not a modification of a pure substance.

Explain why astronomers use spectroscopy to determine the composition, temperature, and motion of distant stars — and how dispersion is central to each.Hard
Show Answer

Composition: Starlight passed through a spectrograph (dispersive element) reveals absorption lines at specific wavelengths where atoms in the star's atmosphere absorb photons — each element has a unique "barcode." Temperature: The peak wavelength of the continuous spectrum (Wien's law: λ_max = b/T) reveals surface temperature — blue-hot stars have peak in UV; red-cool stars peak in infrared. Motion: Doppler shift — if a star moves toward Earth, all spectral lines shift to shorter wavelengths (blueshift); moving away → redshift. The shift amount gives the velocity. All three methods require accurate dispersion of starlight into its component wavelengths.

Explain how Isaac Newton used a second prism to prove that the prism does not add colours to light. What would have happened if the prevailing view (colour added by prism) was correct?Hard
Show Answer

Newton placed a second inverted prism in the path of the dispersed spectrum. The reversed prism recombined all colours back into a white beam. If the prevailing view were correct (that the prism "stains" or modifies light), the inverted prism would have added more colours or failed to produce white light. The clean white recombination falsified the "modification" hypothesis. Newton also isolated a single colour from the spectrum and showed it could not be split further by a second prism — confirming each colour is a pure, fundamental component of white light.

Explain how the human eye perceives colour in terms of wavelength detection by cone cells, and how this relates to the spectrum produced by dispersion.Hard
Show Answer

The human eye has three types of cone cells: S-cones (sensitive to short wavelengths ~420 nm, "blue"), M-cones (~530 nm, "green"), R-cones (~560 nm, "red"). Colour perception is based on the relative stimulation of these three cones. When a prism disperses white light, each wavelength band stimulates the cones in specific proportions. Pure spectral colours (monochromatic) stimulate cones according to their wavelength. The brain interprets the cone responses as colour. This is why the spectrum appears as a continuous gradation from red to violet — there is no "gap" because intermediate wavelengths stimulate combinations of cones producing intermediate perceived colours.

Critically evaluate: "A rainbow proves that sunlight contains only seven colours." Is this scientifically accurate?Hard
Show Answer

Scientifically inaccurate. The visible spectrum is a continuous gradation from ~380 nm (violet) to ~700 nm (red) — it contains infinitely many wavelengths, not seven discrete colours. The "seven colour" classification (ROYGBIV) is a human perceptual and cultural convention (Newton originally identified seven to match musical notes). In reality, the rainbow shows a continuous band of wavelengths. The boundaries between named colours are arbitrary and vary across cultures and languages. The rainbow proves sunlight contains all wavelengths of visible light — not seven specific ones.

Explain the phenomenon of "supernumerary rainbows" — extra faint arcs just inside the primary rainbow. Why do these form, and what do they tell us about the wave nature of light?Hard
Show Answer

Supernumerary bows form just inside the primary rainbow as alternating light-and-dark fringes. They arise from wave interference — light rays passing through slightly different paths within raindrops of similar size interfere constructively (bright fringe) or destructively (dark fringe) depending on their path length difference. This is a diffraction/interference effect that cannot be explained by geometric ray optics (reflection/refraction alone). Their existence is direct evidence of the wave nature of light. The fringe spacing depends on raindrop size — smaller drops produce more widely spaced supernumeraries.

How does dispersion of light through a prism relate to the concept of photons in quantum physics? Explain the dual perspective (wave and particle).Hard
Show Answer

Wave perspective: Different wavelengths (colours) have different frequencies. When entering glass, each frequency causes glass electrons to oscillate at different rates, slowing each wavelength differently (wavelength-dependent refractive index) → different bending angles → dispersion. Particle (photon) perspective: Each photon has energy E = hf. Higher-frequency (violet) photons interact more strongly with glass atoms (closer to resonance frequencies of electron transitions), experiencing greater energy exchange → larger refractive index → more bending. Both perspectives predict the same experimental result (violet bends most) but describe the mechanism at different levels of physical theory.

MYP Global Context: The discovery of helium in the Sun's spectrum (1868) before it was found on Earth was a landmark in science. Explain how this was possible using spectroscopy and dispersion, and discuss what this tells us about the universality of physical laws.Hard
Show Answer

In 1868, Janssen and Lockyer observed a bright yellow spectral line (587.56 nm) in sunlight during a solar eclipse that matched no known element on Earth. It was named helium (from Helios = Sun). How: Sunlight was dispersed by a spectrograph; the unique spectral "fingerprint" of helium — specific absorption/emission lines at fixed wavelengths — was identified. Helium was not found on Earth until 1895 (Ramsay in radioactive minerals). Significance for physical laws: The same atomic spectral line wavelengths occurring in a star 150 million km away confirms that atomic physics (quantum energy levels governing emission wavelengths) operates identically throughout the universe — powerful evidence for the universality of physical laws across space and time.

Explain why printing inks (CMYK) use a subtractive colour model rather than the additive RGB model used in screens.Medium
Show Answer

Screens emit light — adding more light gives brighter/lighter colours (additive: no light = black). Inks absorb light — each ink absorbs certain wavelengths from the white paper's reflected light. Adding more ink absorbs more wavelengths, making colours darker. Cyan absorbs red; Magenta absorbs green; Yellow absorbs blue. CMY combined absorbs everything = black (subtractive: all ink = black). The two models are complementary: additive for emitting displays, subtractive for absorbing pigments.

Explain the colour appearance of a red traffic light at night versus in daylight, using the principle of reflection and emission.Medium
Show Answer

At night: the traffic light emits red light from a coloured bulb/LED — the red wavelengths physically come from the light source itself. In both cases the observer receives red wavelengths, but the mechanism differs — emission at night, selective transmission (filtering) in day.

White paper reflects all wavelengths. Under red+blue (magenta) light, the paper reflects both red and blue — appearing magenta (the colour of the incident light, since it reflects everything that hits it).

Explain how a colour television screen produces millions of colours using only three types of phosphor/LED: red, green and blue.Medium
