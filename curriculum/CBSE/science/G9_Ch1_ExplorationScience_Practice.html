Scientific Inquiry Method

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

List the six main steps of the scientific inquiry method in order.Easy
Show Answer

Research question → Hypothesis → Experiment (method/materials) → Data collection → Analysis → Conclusion & Evaluation.

What is a hypothesis?Easy
Show Answer

A testable, falsifiable prediction that states the expected relationship between the independent and dependent variables, often written as an "If…then…because…" statement.

What does the term "iterative" mean in the context of scientific inquiry?Easy
Show Answer

The process is repeated; findings from one experiment lead to new questions, revised hypotheses, and further experiments in a cycle of continuous improvement.

What step comes immediately after data collection in the scientific method?Easy
Show Answer

Analysis – processing and interpreting the data using calculations, graphs, and patterns.

What is a research question?Easy
Show Answer

A focused, testable question that drives the investigation; it usually asks about the effect of one variable on another.

What does it mean if a hypothesis is "falsifiable"?Easy
Show Answer

It means the hypothesis can potentially be proven wrong by experimental evidence; it makes a specific, testable prediction.

What is the purpose of a conclusion in a scientific report?Easy
Show Answer

To state whether the hypothesis was supported or refuted by the data, summarising key findings with reference to specific data values.

Name the step in the scientific method where you identify sources of error and suggest improvements.Easy
Show Answer

Evaluation.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Write a hypothesis for the following research question: "How does the concentration of salt solution affect the mass of a potato chip?"Medium
Show Answer

If the salt concentration increases, then the mass of the potato chip will decrease because osmosis will cause water to move out of the chip into the more concentrated solution.

Explain why the scientific method is described as iterative rather than linear.Medium
Show Answer

After evaluating results, scientists often generate new questions or refine their hypothesis; the cycle repeats with new experiments, so knowledge builds progressively rather than in a single straight path.

Distinguish between a prediction and a hypothesis.Medium
Show Answer

A prediction is a specific outcome expected in a particular experiment; a hypothesis is a broader, reasoned statement that explains why the prediction is expected and can be tested across multiple experiments.

A student finds results that do not support their hypothesis. What should they do next?Medium
Show Answer

They should not discard the results; instead, they should evaluate the methodology for errors, consider alternative explanations, revise the hypothesis, and design a new experiment to re-test.

Why must a good research question involve measurable variables?Medium
Show Answer

Measurable variables allow data to be collected and compared objectively; without measurability, the investigation cannot produce evidence to support or refute a hypothesis.

Explain the difference between an observation and an inference in science.Medium
Show Answer

An observation is a direct measurement or sensory description of what is detected (e.g., "the solution turned blue"). An inference is a conclusion or explanation drawn from the observation (e.g., "starch is present").

Why is peer review an important part of the scientific method?Medium
Show Answer

Other scientists check the methodology, data, and conclusions for errors or bias; this process validates findings and ensures results are reliable before they are accepted by the scientific community.

How does the conclusion of an experiment feed back into the scientific inquiry cycle?Medium
Show Answer

A conclusion identifies what was found and what remains unexplained, generating new research questions that restart the inquiry cycle with refined hypotheses and improved methods.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate (MYP Criteria B & D)

A student claims: "My experiment proved my hypothesis." Critically evaluate this statement using scientific reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

Science cannot "prove" a hypothesis; it can only support or refute it. A single experiment provides limited evidence. The hypothesis is supported if results are consistent and repeatable, but future experiments could still falsify it. The correct statement is: "the results support the hypothesis."

Compare deductive and inductive reasoning in the context of the scientific method, giving an example of each.Hard
Show Answer

Deductive: starts from a general principle to predict a specific outcome (e.g., "All metals conduct electricity, therefore copper will conduct electricity"). Inductive: draws a general conclusion from specific observations (e.g., "Every metal tested conducted electricity, so all metals probably conduct electricity"). Science uses both: induction to form hypotheses, deduction to test predictions.

Explain why replication of experiments by independent scientists is essential for scientific knowledge to be accepted.Hard
Show Answer

Independent replication eliminates investigator bias and checks that results are not due to chance, faulty equipment, or errors specific to one lab. Consistent results across multiple independent studies establish reliability and increase confidence that findings reflect a true natural phenomenon.

Evaluate the limitations of the scientific method as a way of understanding the natural world.Hard
Show Answer

Limitations: (1) Some phenomena cannot be ethically or practically tested in controlled experiments. (2) Results can be influenced by researcher bias. (3) Science cannot address value judgements or ethical questions directly. (4) Findings are provisional and subject to revision. Despite these limitations, the method remains the most reliable tool because it demands evidence-based reasoning and self-correction.

A scientist repeats an experiment five times and gets wildly different results each time. What does this indicate, and how should they respond?Hard
Show Answer

High variability indicates large random error – uncontrolled factors are affecting results. The scientist should identify uncontrolled variables, standardise the procedure more rigorously, use more precise instruments, and increase sample size. Before drawing conclusions, the source of variability must be understood.

Discuss why a well-formulated research question is considered the foundation of a successful investigation in MYP Science Criterion B.Hard
Show Answer

Criterion B assesses the ability to design an investigation. A precise research question specifies both variables and the relationship being tested, making it possible to design a controlled, measurable experiment. A vague question leads to poorly controlled variables, uninterpretable data, and an untestable hypothesis – compromising all subsequent steps.

How does the concept of "falsifiability" (Popper) distinguish science from non-science? Give one example from biology.Hard
Show Answer

Karl Popper argued that for a claim to be scientific it must be possible to design an experiment that could prove it false. Non-scientific claims (e.g., astrology) cannot be falsified. Biology example: "Plants grow taller with more light" is falsifiable – you can grow plants in darkness to test it. Claims like "plants have souls" cannot be tested and are thus non-scientific.

A published study on a new drug is funded by the pharmaceutical company that makes the drug. Analyse the potential impact of this on the validity of the scientific inquiry.Hard
Show Answer

Funding bias can compromise objectivity: researchers may (consciously or not) design studies favouring positive outcomes, selectively report favourable data, or use statistical methods that inflate significance. This threatens internal validity. Independent replication, pre-registration of study designs, and conflict-of-interest declarations are safeguards. MYP Criterion D asks students to reflect on such real-world impacts of science.

Variables and Fair Testing

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

What is an independent variable (IV)?Easy
Show Answer

The variable that the scientist deliberately changes or manipulates in an experiment.

What is a dependent variable (DV)?Easy
Show Answer

The variable that is measured or observed; it responds to changes in the independent variable.

What is a controlled variable (CV)?Easy
Show Answer

A variable that is kept constant throughout the experiment so that it does not affect the dependent variable.

What is a control experiment?Easy
Show Answer

A baseline trial in which the independent variable is absent or set to zero, allowing comparison with experimental groups to show the true effect of the IV.

In an experiment testing how light intensity affects plant growth, identify the IV, DV, and one CV.Easy
Show Answer

IV: light intensity; DV: plant height (or mass); CV: temperature / volume of water / type of plant.

What does "fair test" mean?Easy
Show Answer

An experiment in which only the independent variable is changed while all other variables are kept constant, ensuring any change in the DV is caused only by the IV.

Why is it important to change only one variable at a time in an experiment?Easy
Show Answer

If more than one variable changes at the same time, it is impossible to determine which variable caused the change in the dependent variable.

What type of variable is the temperature of water when testing how temperature affects enzyme activity?Easy
Show Answer

Independent variable (IV).

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why a control group is necessary in a drug trial.Medium
Show Answer

The control group receives a placebo or no treatment; this provides a baseline to compare against the treatment group, showing that any improvement is due to the drug and not other factors (such as the placebo effect or natural recovery).

A student investigates how the amount of fertiliser affects plant height. They use different pots, different soil types, and different amounts of water. Identify the problem and explain how to fix it.Medium
Show Answer

The experiment is not a fair test because multiple variables (soil type, water volume) are not controlled. Fix: use identical pots, same soil type, and the same volume of water for all plants; only change the fertiliser amount.

Distinguish between a qualitative and a quantitative variable with examples.Medium
Show Answer

Qualitative: described in words/categories (e.g., colour, presence/absence). Quantitative: measured numerically with units (e.g., temperature in °C, mass in grams).

Why should an experiment use a range of values for the independent variable rather than just two?Medium
Show Answer

A range of values reveals the pattern or trend (e.g., linear, bell-shaped) of the relationship between IV and DV; only two data points cannot show a trend or identify an optimum.

What is the difference between a discrete variable and a continuous variable? Give one example of each.Medium
Show Answer

Discrete: can only take specific values, usually whole numbers (e.g., number of seeds germinated). Continuous: can take any value within a range (e.g., temperature, mass).

In a biology experiment, a student measures the effect of pH on enzyme activity. List four controlled variables they must keep constant.Medium
Show Answer

Temperature, concentration of enzyme, concentration of substrate, volume of solution, time of reaction (any four).

Why is it important to define variables precisely before starting an experiment?Medium
Show Answer

Precise operational definitions ensure that measurements are consistent across all trials and that different observers measure the same thing, improving reliability and allowing comparisons.

Explain what a "confounding variable" is and give one example.Medium
Show Answer

A confounding variable is an uncontrolled variable that influences the dependent variable, making it unclear whether the IV or the confound caused the change. Example: if testing fertiliser on plant growth outdoors, varying sunlight is a confounding variable.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate (MYP Criteria B & D)

Design a fair test to investigate how temperature affects the rate of yeast fermentation. State the IV, DV, three CVs, and describe how you would measure the DV.Hard
Show Answer

IV: temperature (e.g., 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 °C). DV: volume of CO₂ produced per minute (measured by collecting gas in an inverted measuring cylinder). CVs: yeast concentration, glucose concentration, volume of solution, pH. Set up water baths at each temperature, add identical yeast-glucose mixtures, and record CO₂ volume every minute for 5 minutes.

A student claims their experiment was fair because they only changed the IV. Critically evaluate this claim.Hard
Show Answer

Changing only the IV is necessary but not sufficient for a fair test. The student must also ensure that all other variables (CVs) were actually kept constant throughout – not just intended to be. They should verify this with measurements. Additionally, repeated trials are needed to check reliability, and the method must be consistent across all trials.

Explain the difference between a control experiment and a controlled variable. Why is this distinction important?Hard
Show Answer

A controlled variable is any variable kept constant throughout all trials. A control experiment is a separate, baseline trial in which the IV is absent. The control experiment allows scientists to attribute the observed effect specifically to the IV rather than background variation. A student can control variables but still lack a control experiment, weakening the ability to demonstrate causation.

In a clinical drug trial, a double-blind design is used. Explain what this means and why it improves the validity of results compared to a single-blind design.Hard
Show Answer

In a double-blind trial, neither the patient nor the doctor/researcher knows who receives the drug or placebo during the study. This eliminates both the placebo effect (patient bias) and observer bias (researcher unconsciously treating groups differently or interpreting results favourably). Single-blind only removes patient bias. Double-blind produces more valid comparisons between treatment and control groups.

Analyse why increasing the number of values of the independent variable and increasing the number of repeats both improve an investigation, but in different ways.Hard
Show Answer

More IV values improve the ability to identify the shape and nature of the trend (e.g., determining if growth has an optimum temperature). More repeats of each IV value reduce the impact of random error on each data point and allow calculation of a more reliable mean. Together they improve both the validity of conclusions and the reliability of the data.

A researcher tests a new fertiliser on crop yield but finds that plots receiving the fertiliser also happened to receive more rainfall. How does this confound the results, and how could the study be redesigned?Hard
Show Answer

The confound means the higher yield could be due to more rainfall rather than the fertiliser – causation cannot be established. Redesign: use a greenhouse where rainfall is controlled; randomly assign plots to fertiliser and no-fertiliser conditions; use the same irrigation for all plots; or statistically control for rainfall variation if field conditions cannot be controlled.

Evaluate the impact of sample size on the validity of experimental conclusions in biology.Hard
Show Answer

Small samples are more susceptible to random variation and outliers, which can produce misleading means and unreliable trends. Large samples reduce the effect of individual variability, making results more representative of the true population. In biology, organisms naturally vary, so large samples are especially important. However, increasing sample size also increases cost and time – a balance must be reached. Statistical tests (e.g., t-test) can quantify whether a sample is large enough to draw valid conclusions.

MYP Criterion B requires students to "explain how to manipulate the variables." Discuss what a full explanation should include and why vague descriptions lose marks.Hard
Show Answer

A full explanation must: name the IV and specify the exact range and number of values; name the DV and specify the measuring instrument and unit; list all CVs and state exactly how each will be kept constant (e.g., "temperature maintained at 25 °C using a water bath with thermometer"). Vague descriptions (e.g., "keep temperature the same") do not show understanding of how variables will be operationally controlled and do not allow another scientist to replicate the experiment – both required by Criterion B.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative data?Easy
Show Answer

Qualitative data is descriptive and non-numerical (e.g., colour, texture). Quantitative data is numerical and measured with units (e.g., 5.2 g, 37 °C).

Why should units always be placed in the column header of a data table rather than in each cell?Easy
Show Answer

Placing units in the header (e.g., "Mass / g") keeps the data cells clean and unambiguous, follows scientific convention, and makes the table easier to read.

Why should a minimum of three repeats be performed for each experimental condition?Easy
Show Answer

Three repeats allow a mean to be calculated, help identify outliers, and show whether results are reproducible – increasing reliability.

How do you calculate the mean of a set of values?Easy
Show Answer

Mean = sum of all values ÷ number of values.

What is an outlier in a data set?Easy
Show Answer

A data value that is significantly different from the other values in the same set, likely due to experimental error.

Name two measuring instruments used to collect quantitative data in a biology lab.Easy
Show Answer

Any two of: ruler (cm/mm), thermometer (°C), balance/scale (g), measuring cylinder (mL), stopwatch (s).

What is the purpose of recording raw data in a table during an experiment?Easy
Show Answer

To organise data clearly so it can be easily reviewed, processed, and shared; raw data provides a permanent record before any calculations are made.

What is the difference between accuracy and precision?Easy
Show Answer

Accuracy is how close a measurement is to the true value. Precision is how consistent repeated measurements are with each other (they may be consistently wrong, i.e., precise but inaccurate).

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

A student records the following masses of potato chips after osmosis (g): 3.2, 3.1, 3.4, 3.1. Calculate the mean and identify any outlier.Medium
Show Answer

Mean of all four: (3.2 + 3.1 + 3.4 + 3.1) / 4 = 3.2 g. The value 3.4 g may be an outlier as it deviates notably from the other three (3.1–3.2 g); it should be queried but not automatically excluded without investigation of a specific error.

Construct a suitable raw data table for an experiment measuring the effect of light intensity (20, 40, 60, 80, 100 lux) on the rate of photosynthesis (oxygen bubbles per minute), with 3 repeats.Medium
Show Answer

Table headers: Light intensity / lux | Repeat 1 (bubbles/min) | Repeat 2 (bubbles/min) | Repeat 3 (bubbles/min) | Mean (bubbles/min). Five rows for each light intensity value. Units in header, not in cells.

Why is it better to calculate the mean from repeated trials rather than just using one result?Medium
Show Answer

A single result may be anomalous due to random error. The mean of multiple trials reduces the influence of any single outlier and gives a more reliable estimate of the true value.

Explain the difference between primary data and secondary data in science.Medium
Show Answer

Primary data is collected first-hand by the researcher in their own investigation. Secondary data is obtained from other sources (e.g., textbooks, published papers, databases) and was collected by someone else.

A student uses a ruler with 1 mm divisions to measure plant height. What is the uncertainty of this instrument and how should measurements be recorded?Medium
Show Answer

Uncertainty = ±0.5 mm (half the smallest division). Measurements should be recorded to the nearest mm (e.g., 45 mm ± 0.5 mm).

Explain why qualitative observations are still valuable even when quantitative data is being collected.Medium
Show Answer

Qualitative observations (e.g., colour changes, cloudiness, movement) can indicate unexpected phenomena or support interpretation of quantitative trends; they provide context and can help identify errors or confounding factors.

Why should the same instrument be used throughout an experiment rather than switching between different instruments of the same type?Medium
Show Answer

Different instruments may be calibrated slightly differently, introducing systematic error. Using the same instrument ensures any inherent error is consistent, so comparisons between trials are valid.

Describe how to correctly use a thermometer to get a reliable reading when measuring the temperature of a reaction mixture.Medium
Show Answer

Ensure the thermometer bulb is fully submerged in the liquid; wait for the reading to stabilise; read at eye level to avoid parallax error; record to the nearest 0.5 °C (or the instrument's precision); repeat and average if possible.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate (MYP Criteria B & D)

Evaluate the claim: "More data is always better data." Is this always true in scientific investigations?Hard
Show Answer

More data generally improves reliability and reduces random error, making it a useful principle. However, it is not always better if: data quality is poor (systematic errors affect all readings equally); data is irrelevant to the research question; resources are limited and more trials prevent rigour elsewhere. Quality and relevance of data matter as much as quantity.

Distinguish between systematic error and random error in the context of data collection. Give one example of each and explain how each affects the mean.Hard
Show Answer

Systematic error: consistent offset in one direction due to a faulty instrument or flawed method (e.g., a scale that always reads 0.5 g too high). The mean is shifted by the same amount – not reduced by repeats. Random error: unpredictable variation due to uncontrolled factors (e.g., parallax when reading a meniscus). The mean approaches the true value as repeats increase because errors tend to cancel out.

A student collects data showing enzyme activity increases from 10 °C to 37 °C then drops steeply to zero at 50 °C. Explain how they should process and present this data to show the trend clearly.Hard
Show Answer

Calculate the mean of repeats at each temperature; present raw data and means in a table with appropriate headings and units; plot a line graph (temperature on x-axis, activity on y-axis) with a smooth best-fit curve (not dot-to-dot); label axes with quantity and unit; include error bars if uncertainty is calculated. The curve should show the optimum at 37 °C and sharp decline thereafter.

Explain why digital instruments are generally considered more precise than analogue instruments, but why this does not automatically mean data collected is more accurate.Hard
Show Answer

Digital instruments display more decimal places, reducing reading uncertainty (no parallax). But accuracy depends on calibration: a digital balance that was not zeroed or is poorly calibrated will give precise but inaccurate readings. Accuracy requires both a precisely reading instrument AND correct calibration and use.

Design a data table for an experiment investigating how exercise duration affects heart rate in five students, with measurements taken at rest, 5, 10, and 15 minutes of exercise. Explain the design choices you made.Hard
Show Answer

Table: rows for each student (S1–S5) plus a mean row; columns for each time point (0, 5, 10, 15 min); header row includes quantity and unit (Heart rate / bpm). Design choices: students as replicates; mean row to average out individual variation; IV (time) as column headers so trend can be graphed across time; unit in header, not in cells.

When is it appropriate to exclude an outlier from a data set? What risk is involved?Hard
Show Answer

Appropriate exclusion: when there is a confirmed reason for the anomaly (e.g., recorded "dropped sample," equipment malfunction noted at the time). Risk: if outliers are excluded without justification, the scientist is selectively reporting data (cherry-picking), which is a form of scientific fraud. Outliers should always be reported and investigated; exclusion should be documented and justified with evidence of error.

Compare the reliability and validity of an experiment. Can an experiment be reliable but not valid? Explain with an example.Hard
Show Answer

Reliability: consistency of results across repeated trials. Validity: whether the experiment actually measures what it claims to measure and controls confounds. Yes, an experiment can be reliable but not valid. Example: measuring plant growth by counting leaves instead of height – consistent leaf counts give reliable data, but leaf count is not a valid measure of height. Validity requires both the correct measurement of the DV and proper control of variables.

Discuss the ethical considerations in data collection when human subjects are involved in biology experiments at school level.Hard
Show Answer

Ethical considerations include: informed consent (participants must agree and understand the experiment); right to withdraw at any time; privacy and anonymity of data; no harm or discomfort (e.g., exercise should be appropriate for fitness level); honest reporting of all data. MYP Criterion D requires students to reflect on ethical implications, and human subjects research must follow school/institutional ethics guidelines. Data must not be used to identify or embarrass individuals.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Which variable is plotted on the x-axis of a graph?Easy
Show Answer

The independent variable (IV).

Which variable is plotted on the y-axis of a graph?Easy
Show Answer

The dependent variable (DV).

Why should a line of best fit be drawn rather than connecting data points dot-to-dot?Easy
Show Answer

A line of best fit shows the overall trend in the data and smooths out random errors; dot-to-dot exaggerates every small fluctuation and implies false precision.

What information must always be included on both axes of a graph?Easy
Show Answer

The quantity (variable name) and the unit (e.g., "Temperature / °C").

When would you use a bar chart instead of a line graph?Easy
Show Answer

When the independent variable is categorical (discrete named categories, e.g., different plant species) rather than continuous.

What does a positive correlation between two variables mean?Easy
Show Answer

As the IV increases, the DV also increases.

What does a "plateau" in a graph indicate?Easy
Show Answer

The DV stops changing even though the IV continues to increase; a limiting factor is present.

Give one reason why the scale on an axis should be even and start at zero (or at a clearly marked break).Easy
Show Answer

Starting at zero (or marking a break) prevents a misleading exaggeration of differences between data points; even intervals ensure the visual shape of the trend is not distorted.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Describe a trend in the following data: at 10 °C enzyme activity was 5 units/min; at 20 °C it was 11 units/min; at 30 °C it was 18 units/min; at 40 °C it was 20 units/min; at 50 °C it was 3 units/min.Medium
Show Answer

Enzyme activity increased from 5 units/min at 10 °C to a peak of 20 units/min at 40 °C, then sharply decreased to 3 units/min at 50 °C. This indicates an optimum temperature of 40 °C, beyond which denaturation reduces activity rapidly.

Explain how to draw a best-fit line for a scatter plot that shows an approximately linear trend with one outlier.Medium
Show Answer

Draw a straight line that passes as close as possible to the majority of points, with roughly equal numbers of points above and below the line; do not force the line through the origin unless theoretically justified; do not include the outlier in the line – mark it separately and investigate its cause.

A student plots a line graph of plant height (y) against days (x) and draws it dot-to-dot with sharp peaks and troughs. What is wrong with this and how should it be corrected?Medium
Show Answer

Dot-to-dot implies that height fluctuated sharply up and down daily, which is biologically unlikely – it exaggerates random error. The student should draw a smooth curve of best fit through the data points to show the general growth trend.

What type of graph would you use to display the percentage of students who prefer each of four sports? Justify your choice.Medium
Show Answer

A bar chart or pie chart; the IV (sport type) is categorical/discrete, so a line graph is inappropriate. A bar chart shows individual category frequencies clearly; a pie chart shows proportions of a whole.

Describe in words what a graph showing a negative correlation looks like.Medium
Show Answer

The data points fall from upper left to lower right; as the IV increases, the DV decreases. The line of best fit slopes downward.

Explain why describing a graph trend "using data values" is better than describing it in vague terms (e.g., "it goes up").Medium
Show Answer

Using specific values (e.g., "oxygen production increased from 5 to 20 mL/min as light intensity increased from 10 to 50 lux") demonstrates understanding of the actual data, allows quantitative comparison, and satisfies MYP assessment criteria for analysis.

Why is it important for a graph scale to be even (equal intervals) along each axis?Medium
Show Answer

Uneven intervals distort the visual shape of the trend; the slope and curvature of the line appear different depending on the scale, leading to incorrect interpretation of the relationship between variables.

How can error bars on a graph help evaluate data reliability?Medium
Show Answer

Error bars show the range or standard deviation of repeated measurements at each data point; large bars indicate high variability (low reliability) and that differences between groups may not be meaningful; small bars suggest consistent, reliable results.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate (MYP Criteria B & D)

Evaluate the difference between correlation and causation, using a graph as context. Why is this distinction scientifically important?Hard
Show Answer

A graph showing a positive correlation between two variables only indicates they change together – not that one causes the other. Both variables might be caused by a third (confounding) variable. Example: ice cream sales and drowning rates correlate positively in summer – the confound is hot weather, not causation. Scientifically, claiming causation from correlation alone can lead to false conclusions; controlled experiments are needed to establish causation.

A graph of photosynthesis rate vs. CO₂ concentration shows a linear increase that levels off into a plateau. Analyse what the graph tells us about limiting factors in photosynthesis.Hard
Show Answer

The linear increase shows CO₂ is the limiting factor in the lower range – adding more CO₂ directly increases the photosynthesis rate. The plateau indicates another factor has become limiting (e.g., light intensity or enzyme concentration), so adding more CO₂ no longer increases the rate. To increase the rate further, the new limiting factor must be addressed.

Compare the use of scatter plots and line graphs. When is each most appropriate for biological data?Hard
Show Answer

Scatter plots: best when there is uncertainty about whether a linear relationship exists; useful for identifying correlation with many data points (e.g., height vs. foot size in a population). Line graphs: best when the IV is continuous and data points come from the same subject/sample over time or at set intervals (e.g., plant growth over days, enzyme rate at different temperatures). Both use IV on x-axis and DV on y-axis.

A student plots a graph of heart rate against exercise intensity. The graph shows three distinct phases: flat, steep increase, then levelling again. Interpret each phase biologically.Hard
Show Answer

Phase 1 (flat): low exercise intensity; heart rate stays near resting level as muscles are adequately supplied by existing cardiac output. Phase 2 (steep increase): muscles demand more oxygen; the nervous system and adrenaline increase heart rate to boost oxygen delivery. Phase 3 (plateau): maximum heart rate reached; cardiac muscle cannot beat faster; other mechanisms (stroke volume, oxygen extraction) compensate.

Explain how to use a graph of two experimental groups (control vs. treatment) to assess whether the treatment had a significant effect.Hard
Show Answer

Plot both lines/bars on the same axes for direct comparison. Assess: (1) whether the lines diverge consistently or cross; (2) whether error bars of the two groups overlap (overlapping bars suggest no significant difference); (3) the magnitude of difference relative to the scale and variability. A statistical test (e.g., t-test) would confirm whether the difference is statistically significant, but visual analysis of error bar overlap is a useful first step.

Critically assess the following graph description: "The graph shows the plant grew more in the light." Rewrite it as a full MYP-standard graph description.Hard
Show Answer

The original is too vague – no data values, no trend description, no axis reference. MYP standard: "The line graph shows that plant height increased from 3.2 cm on day 0 to 18.6 cm on day 14 in the light condition, compared to only 7.1 cm in the dark condition. Both lines show a positive trend; however, the light plant grew 2.5× taller than the dark plant by day 14. The rate of growth was steepest between days 4 and 8 in the light group."

Discuss the limitations of using a line of best fit when only 3 data points are available.Hard
Show Answer

With only 3 data points, any line can be made to fit closely, so the line may not represent the true underlying trend. Extrapolation beyond the data range is highly unreliable. The shape of the curve (linear vs. non-linear) cannot be reliably determined. Any single anomalous point will significantly distort the line. More data points across a wider range are needed before drawing confident conclusions about the trend.

A media article claims a graph "proves" that social media use causes depression in teenagers. Critically evaluate this claim from a scientific graphing perspective.Hard
Show Answer

A graph can show correlation but not prove causation. The graph might show that higher social media use correlates with higher depression scores, but: (1) confounding variables (sleep deprivation, pre-existing mental health issues, academic stress) may explain both; (2) causality could be reversed (depressed teens use more social media); (3) self-reported data may be unreliable. A controlled longitudinal experiment is required to establish causation. MYP Criterion D requires students to question the media's interpretation of scientific data.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

What is the difference between a systematic error and a random error?Easy
Show Answer

Systematic error: consistent, in one direction, caused by a faulty instrument or flawed method. Random error: unpredictable variation in either direction, caused by uncontrolled factors.

Give one example of a systematic error in a biology experiment.Easy
Show Answer

A balance that is not zeroed, consistently reading 0.5 g too high for every measurement.

Give one example of a random error in a biology experiment.Easy
Show Answer

Slight variations in the time a student takes to read a measurement each time, due to reaction time differences.

Can repeating an experiment reduce systematic error? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

No. Repeating does not reduce systematic error because the same flaw affects every trial in the same way. Systematic error is only eliminated by fixing the faulty instrument or method.

What does the MYP Science Criterion D assess?Easy
Show Answer

Reflecting on the impacts of science – evaluating the experimental process, identifying sources of error, suggesting improvements, and considering the broader real-world implications of scientific work.

What is meant by "improving" an experiment in an evaluation?Easy
Show Answer

Suggesting specific, realistic changes to the method, equipment, or sample size that would reduce identified sources of error and increase the reliability or validity of the results.

Name two sources of error that could affect a calorimetry experiment measuring heat released by burning food.Easy
Show Answer

Any two of: heat loss to surroundings; incomplete combustion of food; evaporation of water; draught affecting the flame; variation in distance between flame and can.

What is the difference between a "weakness" and an "improvement" in an evaluation?Easy
Show Answer

A weakness identifies a specific flaw in the method or results. An improvement proposes a concrete, feasible change that would address that weakness.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

A student notices their results for enzyme activity at 37 °C vary between 18, 19, 17, and 25 units/min across four repeats. Analyse these results and suggest what to do.Medium
Show Answer

The value of 25 units/min is an outlier; the other three values are close. The student should investigate whether an error occurred in that trial (e.g., incorrect substrate concentration, pipetting error). If a specific error is found, exclude with justification; otherwise report all four and flag the anomaly. Perform additional repeats to confirm the trend.

Explain why "human error" is not an acceptable source of error in an evaluation.Medium
Show Answer

"Human error" is too vague to be useful. A good evaluation names the specific action that caused the error (e.g., "inconsistent timing of the stopwatch due to slow reaction time") and links it to the effect on the data, so that a specific improvement can be proposed.

A student measures the effect of temperature on enzyme activity but accidentally uses a different enzyme concentration in one trial. Classify and explain the type of error this introduces.Medium
Show Answer

This is a random error if it occurs unpredictably, or a systematic error if it consistently affects all trials at one temperature. Either way, it is a control variable failure that reduces validity because the change in DV cannot be attributed solely to the IV (temperature).

Suggest two realistic improvements for an experiment measuring the effect of light colour on plant growth, conducted over only one week with a single plant per condition.Medium
Show Answer

1) Increase sample size to at least 5 plants per light colour condition to account for individual variation and allow statistical analysis. 2) Extend the experiment duration to 4–6 weeks to allow meaningful growth differences to develop and be detected.

Explain why an evaluation must address both reliability and validity, not just one of them.Medium
Show Answer

Reliability (consistent results) and validity (measuring what is intended with proper controls) are both necessary for trustworthy conclusions. A reliable but invalid experiment gives consistent but meaningless results. A valid but unreliable experiment gives correct results that cannot be trusted due to high variability. Both aspects must be evaluated for a complete assessment.

How does increasing the number of data points (more IV values) improve the evaluation of a trend?Medium
Show Answer

More IV values reveal whether the relationship is truly linear or curved, identify an optimum or threshold, and make the best-fit line more reliable. With only a few data points, misleading trends or false peaks may appear.

A student concludes that "the experiment was successful." Explain why this is not a good evaluation statement and rewrite it more appropriately.Medium
Show Answer

"The experiment was successful" gives no information about the quality of the data or the method. Better: "The experiment produced results consistent with the hypothesis, showing a positive trend between temperature and enzyme activity up to 40 °C. However, the large variation in the 37 °C repeat suggests random error from inconsistent substrate pipetting; this could be improved by using a calibrated micropipette."

Why is it important to evaluate whether the experimental method was sufficient to answer the research question?Medium
Show Answer

The method may have produced data that is insufficient in range, precision, or quantity to answer the question definitively. Evaluating method sufficiency ensures the conclusion is justified by the evidence collected, rather than extrapolating beyond what the data can support.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate (MYP Criteria B & D)

Write a full MYP Criterion D–standard evaluation paragraph for an experiment testing the effect of pH on catalase activity, identifying one systematic error, one random error, and one specific improvement for each.Hard
Show Answer

Systematic: pH buffers used may not have been prepared accurately, introducing consistent offsets in pH values. Improvement: use commercially prepared certified buffer solutions of known pH. Random: timing the oxygen collection by eye introduced variable reaction times. Improvement: use an oxygen sensor connected to a data logger for automated, consistent timing. Both errors affect validity and reliability respectively; addressing them would increase confidence in the results.

Explain the concept of "percentage error" and discuss when it is useful to calculate it in an experimental evaluation.Hard
Show Answer

Percentage error = (|experimental value − accepted value| / accepted value) × 100%. It is useful when a known or theoretical value exists (e.g., specific heat capacity), allowing the magnitude of systematic error to be quantified. It helps judge whether the method was sufficiently accurate. It is not meaningful when no accepted value exists (e.g., a novel biology investigation).

Analyse why improvements in an evaluation must be "realistic" and "specific." Give an example of an unrealistic improvement and rewrite it as a realistic one.Hard
Show Answer

Improvements must be achievable with available resources and technology; vague or impossible suggestions show no real understanding. Unrealistic: "Use a perfectly controlled environment with no variables." Realistic: "Conduct the experiment in a temperature-controlled water bath (±0.5 °C) rather than room temperature to reduce thermal fluctuation as a random error."

Distinguish between an evaluation of the method and an evaluation of the conclusion. Why must both be included in a complete MYP lab report?Hard
Show Answer

Evaluation of the method: assesses the quality of the data (reliability, validity, sources of error, improvements). Evaluation of the conclusion: assesses whether the conclusion is fully supported by the data, considers alternative explanations, and discusses the degree of confidence. Both are required for Criterion D: method evaluation shows awareness of limitations; conclusion evaluation shows critical reasoning about what the data actually demonstrates.

A student finds results that partially support their hypothesis. How should they address this in the evaluation?Hard
Show Answer

The student should: (1) identify which specific results support the hypothesis and which do not, with reference to data values; (2) consider whether unsupported results are due to experimental error or a genuinely incomplete hypothesis; (3) propose further experiments to test the exceptions; (4) revise the hypothesis if necessary; (5) avoid cherry-picking only supportive data.

Evaluate the role of peer review in reducing experimental errors at the professional level. How does this relate to the evaluation step in MYP science?Hard
Show Answer

Professional peer review involves independent scientists checking methodology, data analysis, and conclusions before publication; it catches systematic flaws, statistical errors, and unsupported claims. In MYP science, the evaluation step mirrors this process at a student level: students critically examine their own methodology and data for weaknesses. Both processes ensure science is self-correcting and conclusions are supported by robust evidence.

Discuss how confirmation bias can affect the evaluation step of a student's lab report.Hard
Show Answer

Confirmation bias causes students to unconsciously focus on data that supports their hypothesis and downplay contradictory results. In evaluation, this leads to selective reporting of errors (only mentioning errors that explain away contradictory data), failure to consider alternative explanations, and overconfident conclusions. Awareness of this bias requires students to actively look for evidence against their hypothesis and evaluate all data equally.

Explain why a conclusion that merely states "I found X" without comparing to the hypothesis and using data values would receive low marks in MYP Criterion D.Hard
Show Answer

MYP Criterion D requires students to reflect on and evaluate the process and results. A conclusion without hypothesis comparison does not show whether the prediction was supported. Without specific data values, the conclusion is unsubstantiated and cannot be evaluated for accuracy. A complete conclusion must: restate the hypothesis, state whether it was supported/refuted, cite specific data values as evidence, explain the scientific reasoning, and acknowledge limitations that may affect the conclusion's validity.

List the main sections of a standard science lab report in order.Easy

What is the purpose of the Introduction section of a lab report?Easy
Show Answer

To provide background scientific context, define key terms, explain why the investigation is being done, and introduce the research question.

Why should a lab report be written in the third person and past tense?Easy
Show Answer

Third person (e.g., "the solution was heated") makes the report objective and impersonal, removing personal bias. Past tense describes what was done and observed, not what will be done.

What is peer review in a scientific context?Easy
Show Answer

The process by which independent experts in the same field critically evaluate a scientist's research before it is published, checking for errors, bias, and unsupported conclusions.

What does MYP Criterion B assess in a lab report?Easy
Show Answer

Inquiring and designing: the ability to write a focused research question, a clear and testable hypothesis, and a detailed, controllable method.

What does MYP Criterion C assess in a lab report?Easy
Show Answer

Processing and evaluating: the ability to collect, organise, and present data; perform calculations; describe trends; draw conclusions; and evaluate the investigation.

Give two examples of scientific language that should be used in a biology lab report.Easy
Show Answer

Any two of: "osmosis," "denaturation," "independent variable," "mean," "anomalous result," "enzyme-substrate complex," "hypothesis."

What is a citation and why is it included in a lab report?Easy
Show Answer

A citation gives credit to the original source of information (book, journal, website) and allows the reader to find and verify the reference. It is required to avoid plagiarism and to show the scientific basis for background information.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why the Method section of a lab report must be written in sufficient detail for another scientist to replicate the experiment exactly.Medium
Show Answer

Replicability is a core principle of science; if another scientist cannot reproduce the experiment with the same method, the results cannot be verified. A detailed method allows independent testing, which either confirms or challenges the original findings, building scientific knowledge reliably.

Compare the analysis and conclusion sections of a lab report. What is the key difference?Medium
Show Answer

Analysis describes and interprets the data: identifies trends, performs calculations, and explains what the results show. Conclusion states whether the hypothesis was supported or refuted based on the analysis, summarising the overall finding and linking it back to the research question.

A student writes: "We heated the solution and it went bubbly." Rewrite this sentence using appropriate scientific language and format.Medium
Show Answer

"The solution was heated to 37 °C; gas bubbles (CO₂) were observed as the reaction rate increased."

Why is it important to include both a table of raw data AND a processed data table (with means) in a lab report?Medium
Show Answer

Raw data shows all original measurements including variability and outliers; it provides transparency and allows the reader to verify calculations. The processed table shows calculated means used for graphing and analysis. Including both demonstrates honest data handling and allows assessment of reliability.

Explain the importance of referencing in a scientific report and describe one citation style used in IB MYP science.Medium
Show Answer

Referencing credits original authors, avoids plagiarism, and allows readers to verify background information. MYP commonly uses MLA or APA style. APA example: Last, A. A. (Year). Title of source. Publisher.

Why does scientific communication benefit from the use of standardised units (SI units)?Medium
Show Answer

Standardised SI units ensure that scientists worldwide understand the same measurements; they prevent confusion and conversion errors, allow direct comparison of results across studies, and are required for universal scientific communication.

Describe what makes a hypothesis "well-written" for a MYP Criterion B assessment.Medium
Show Answer

A well-written MYP hypothesis: uses "If…then…because" format; clearly states the IV and DV; makes a specific, testable, and falsifiable prediction; is supported by scientific reasoning in the "because" clause; uses precise scientific language.

Explain why a scientist must acknowledge when their conclusion contradicts an established scientific theory.Medium
Show Answer

Contradicting established theory requires careful justification; the scientist must rule out experimental error first, acknowledge the contradiction openly, consider whether their sample size/method is sufficient, and propose further investigation. Ignoring contradictions or suppressing them is scientific misconduct; openly reporting them advances scientific understanding.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate (MYP Criteria B & D)

Evaluate the role of scientific language in making a lab report credible. What happens when informal language is used?Hard
Show Answer

Scientific language (precise terminology, passive voice, quantitative descriptions) conveys findings unambiguously and demonstrates subject knowledge. Informal language is imprecise and subjective (e.g., "it got really hot") – it creates ambiguity, reduces credibility, makes replication difficult, and suggests a lack of scientific understanding. In MYP Criterion C, precise language is required to describe data and draw conclusions effectively.

Discuss how a lab report written according to MYP Criteria B and C differs from a general school essay.Hard
Show Answer

A lab report is structured (fixed sections), objective (third person, passive voice, no opinions), evidence-based (data tables, graphs, calculations), and replicable (detailed method). A school essay is flexible in structure, uses first person, argues a point of view, and does not require measurable evidence. Lab reports demand scientific precision; essays require argumentation. Both require clear written communication, but the purpose, audience, and content standards differ fundamentally.

A student uses Wikipedia as their only reference. Critically evaluate this choice and suggest better alternatives.Hard
Show Answer

Wikipedia can be edited by anyone and is not peer-reviewed, so accuracy is not guaranteed; it may contain errors, bias, or outdated information. It is not citable as a primary source in academic work. Better alternatives: peer-reviewed journal articles (Google Scholar, PubMed), textbooks, government science websites (NIH, WHO), or reputable educational resources (Khan Academy, IB Biology textbooks). Wikipedia is acceptable as a starting point to find original sources via its references.

Analyse the role of scientific journals and the publication process in maintaining the integrity of scientific knowledge.Hard
Show Answer

Journals collect and disseminate new research; the peer-review process ensures that before publication, experts in the field check the methodology, data, and conclusions. This filters out poorly designed studies, errors, and fraudulent claims. Published results can be challenged by subsequent studies. The process is not perfect (publication bias favours positive results), but it remains the most rigorous mechanism for validating and building scientific knowledge cumulatively.

Explain how MYP Criterion C strand "processes and interprets data" differs from "draws conclusions." Give an example of what each would look like in a lab report on enzyme activity.Hard
Show Answer

Processes and interprets: "The mean enzyme activity increased from 5 units/min at 10 °C to a peak of 20 units/min at 40 °C, as shown in Graph 1. The rate of increase was steepest between 20 and 30 °C." Draws conclusions: "The hypothesis was supported: as temperature increased to 40 °C, enzyme activity increased, consistent with increased molecular kinetic energy and more enzyme-substrate collisions. Above 40 °C, activity decreased sharply due to denaturation of the active site." The former describes the data; the latter explains it in terms of the hypothesis and science theory.

Discuss the ethical responsibility of scientists to communicate findings honestly, even when results are inconvenient or commercially sensitive.Hard
Show Answer

Scientists have a professional obligation to report all findings honestly, including negative or inconclusive results. Suppressing data that contradicts a preferred outcome (e.g., to protect a commercial product) is scientific misconduct; it misleads the scientific community and, in medical/environmental contexts, can harm the public. The MYP IB Learner Profile value of being principled applies directly: scientists must act with integrity even under pressure. Publication bias (favouring positive results) is a systemic problem in science that honest communication helps address.

Explain how communicating science to a non-specialist audience (e.g., a newspaper article) differs from communicating to a specialist audience (e.g., a scientific paper), and what risks arise when scientific findings are simplified.Hard
Show Answer

For a non-specialist audience: avoid jargon, use analogies, focus on key conclusions and implications, omit statistical detail. For specialists: full methodology, statistical analysis, uncertainty, raw data, prior literature. Risks of oversimplification: context is lost (a correlation presented as causation); nuance disappears ("scientists say X causes cancer" vs. "a small study found a weak association"); public may make harmful decisions. Scientists must balance accessibility with accuracy, and journalists should consult original sources rather than press releases.

A student's lab report scores 4/8 on MYP Criterion C. Using the MYP rubric descriptors, explain what they likely did well and what they need to improve to reach 7–8.Hard
Show Answer

A score of 4 (band 3–4) suggests the student: correctly collected and organised data, described a trend, and wrote a basic conclusion. To reach 7–8 (band 7–8), they need to: process data with full calculations and correct units; produce a well-labelled graph with best-fit line; describe the trend using specific data values; draw a conclusion that explicitly references the hypothesis; evaluate the procedure with specific, named errors and realistic, detailed improvements; consider the impact of errors on the conclusion.
