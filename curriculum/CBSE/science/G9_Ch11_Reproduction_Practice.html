Asexual Reproduction

Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Asexual Reproduction Overview Binary Fission in Bacteria
CrashCourse Biology Reproduction: Asexual vs Sexual

Set A Recall & Identify

Define asexual reproduction and state how many parents are involved. Easy
Show Answer

Asexual reproduction involves only one parent and produces offspring that are genetically identical clones. No gametes or fertilisation are required.

Name four types of asexual reproduction and give one organism example for each. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Binary fission - bacteria/Amoeba. (2) Budding - Hydra/yeast. (3) Fragmentation - Planaria/starfish. (4) Vegetative propagation - potato (tubers)/strawberry (runners).

Define vegetative propagation and give two plant examples. Easy
Show Answer

Vegetative propagation is asexual reproduction using non-reproductive plant parts. Examples: runners (strawberry), tubers (potato), bulbs (onion), rhizomes (ginger).

What is a clone? Are identical twins clones of each other? Easy
Show Answer

A clone is an organism genetically identical to its parent. Identical (monozygotic) twins are clones of each other - they share 100% of their DNA, having developed from the same fertilised egg. Fraternal twins are not clones.

State two advantages of asexual reproduction for organisms in a stable environment. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Only one parent needed - no energy cost of finding a mate. (2) Rapid reproduction - can produce large numbers of offspring quickly. (3) All offspring are well-adapted if the parent is already suited to the stable environment.

Describe binary fission in bacteria. How many cells result? Easy
Show Answer

The cell replicates its circular DNA, then the cell membrane pinches inward, dividing it into two identical daughter cells. Each cycle doubles the population. E. coli divides every ~20 minutes under ideal conditions.

Describe budding in Hydra in two steps. Easy
Show Answer

(1) A small outgrowth (bud) forms on the parent body through mitosis. (2) The bud grows, develops tentacles, and eventually detaches as a genetically identical independent organism.

Name two artificial methods of vegetative propagation used in horticulture. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Stem/leaf cuttings - placed in soil or water to grow roots. (2) Grafting - desired shoot (scion) joined to a hardy rootstock. Also: layering, tissue culture (micropropagation).

Set B Apply & Explain

A bacterium divides every 30 minutes. Calculate the population after 3 hours starting from 1 cell. Medium
Show Answer

3 hours = 180 minutes / 30 min = 6 divisions. Population = 2⁶ = 64 bacteria.

Why would a farmer prefer vegetative propagation over seeds for producing a commercial strawberry crop? Medium
Show Answer

Vegetative propagation produces offspring genetically identical to the parent. If the parent has desirable traits (large sweet fruit, disease resistance), all offspring will too. Seeds from sexual reproduction create variation - some offspring may lack desired traits. Vegetative propagation is also faster and gives a predictable, uniform crop.

Explain why Planaria can regenerate a whole organism from a fragment, at the cellular level. Medium
Show Answer

Planaria have abundant pluripotent stem cells called neoblasts throughout their bodies. After fragmentation, neoblasts in each piece migrate to the cut sites, divide by mitosis, and differentiate into all needed cell types, rebuilding the complete body plan.

Compare the genotype of offspring from asexual vs sexual reproduction. Why does this matter for species survival? Medium
Show Answer

Asexual offspring are genetically identical to the parent. Sexual offspring have unique combinations of alleles from two parents. Genetic uniformity means if one individual is susceptible to a new pathogen, all are. Genetic variation means some offspring may resist new threats - better for long-term species survival and adaptation.

Explain tissue culture in plants and its commercial advantage. Medium
Show Answer

A few cells (explant) are grown on sterile nutrient agar with plant hormones. Cells divide (callus), then differentiate into shoots and roots. Thousands of genetically identical plantlets are produced rapidly in a small space - commercially valuable for rare or high-value varieties with consistent traits.

State one disadvantage of asexual reproduction in a changing environment and give an example. Medium
Show Answer

Lack of genetic variation means all individuals are equally vulnerable to new threats. Example: the Irish Potato Famine (1845) - a genetically uniform potato monoculture meant one fungal pathogen (Phytophthora infestans) destroyed virtually the entire crop, causing mass starvation.

Explain how grafting combines advantages of two plants in fruit farming. Medium
Show Answer

The scion (upper shoot, chosen for fruit quality) is joined to the rootstock (chosen for disease resistance or vigour). They fuse and grow as one plant - the farmer gets the desired fruit from the scion and the hardiness/root qualities from the rootstock.

Compare binary fission (Amoeba) and budding (Hydra) - two similarities and two differences. Medium
Show Answer

Similarities: (1) Both produce genetically identical offspring. (2) Both use mitosis and require only one parent. Differences: (1) Binary fission divides the whole parent into two equal halves; budding produces a small outgrowth while the parent survives intact. (2) Binary fission is typical in unicellular organisms; budding occurs in multicellular organisms.

Set C Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate the claim that asexual reproduction is "evolutionarily inferior" to sexual reproduction. Hard
Show Answer

Oversimplification. Asexual reproduction is highly advantageous in stable environments: energy efficient, rapid population growth, all offspring reproductively capable. Sexual reproduction provides genetic variation, better for changing environments. Many organisms (fungi, plants, aphids) use both strategies. Asexual reproduction has persisted for billions of years - it is not inferior, its advantage is context-dependent.

The banana industry relies on vegetative propagation of one cultivar (Cavendish). Analyse the risk and suggest a solution. Hard
Show Answer

All Cavendish bananas are genetically identical - equally susceptible to the same pathogens. The Cavendish replaced the Gros Michel (wiped out by Panama disease in the 1950s) and is now threatened by Tropical Race 4 (TR4) fungus. Solutions: diversify cultivars through sexual breeding programs; develop disease-resistant transgenic varieties; maintain seed banks of wild banana relatives as sources of resistance genes.

A coral reef is damaged by bleaching. Corals can reproduce both sexually and asexually. Evaluate which is more important for recovery - short term vs long term. Hard
Show Answer

Short term: asexual (fragmentation/budding) is faster - surviving fragments recolonise damaged areas rapidly without finding mates. Long term: sexual reproduction generates genetic variation essential for corals to adapt to rising sea temperatures (the cause of bleaching). Conservation programs use both: fragment transplants for rapid recovery + selective breeding of heat-tolerant corals for long-term resilience.

Aphids reproduce asexually in summer and sexually in autumn. Explain the adaptive advantage of this dual strategy. Hard
Show Answer

In stable summer conditions with abundant food, asexual parthenogenesis allows explosive population growth (no males needed, all offspring female and reproductive). In autumn, the approaching winter makes conditions unpredictable; sexual reproduction produces genetically diverse dormant eggs that can survive winter and cope with varied next-year conditions. The strategy maximises short-term exploitation and long-term adaptability.

Design an experiment to compare growth of strawberry plants from runners vs seeds over 8 weeks. Identify all variables. Hard
Show Answer

Independent variable: propagation method (runners vs seeds). Dependent variable: plant height, leaf count, and mass at 8 weeks. Controlled variables: soil type, pot size, water volume, light intensity, temperature, fertiliser amount. Method: 20 plants per group, measure weekly. Expected: runner plants grow faster initially (established root system, stored energy); seed plants show more variation in growth rate and size.

Explain why bacteria evolve antibiotic resistance despite reproducing asexually. Hard
Show Answer

Bacteria gain variation through: (1) Random mutations during DNA replication - a rare copying error may create a resistance gene. (2) Horizontal gene transfer - resistance genes on plasmids are shared between bacteria via conjugation, transformation, or transduction (not during reproduction). The enormous population size and rapid division rate mean rare mutations appear frequently. Natural selection then favours resistant individuals. Genetic variation in bacteria does not require sexual reproduction.

Assess the ethical considerations of mass tissue culture of genetically identical crop plants for global food production. Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: Rapid, cost-effective production of disease-free, uniform, high-yield crops; supports food security. Concerns: (1) Genetic uniformity creates vulnerability to pests and diseases. (2) Loss of agricultural biodiversity as wild varieties and traditional cultivars are abandoned. (3) Corporate ownership of elite plant lines raises equity issues - farmers cannot save seed. (4) Environmental impact if clonal monocultures displace diverse ecosystems. A balanced approach: use tissue culture alongside maintaining seed diversity banks and traditional varieties.

Calculate: 1 Planaria is cut into 5 pieces. Each regenerates in 2 weeks. At week 2 every organism is cut into 5 again. What is the total population at week 4? Hard
Show Answer

Week 0: 1 organism cut into 5 pieces. Week 2: 5 complete organisms, each cut into 5 = 25 pieces. Week 4: 25 fully regenerated organisms. Total = 25 organisms. (Assumes 100% regeneration success each time.)

Meiosis

Recommended Videos

Khan Academy Meiosis Overview Crossing Over and Genetic Variation
CrashCourse Biology Meiosis - CrashCourse Biology

Set A Recall & Identify

What type of cell division is meiosis and where does it occur in humans? Easy
Show Answer

Meiosis is reductive cell division producing gametes (sex cells). It occurs in the gonads - testes (producing sperm) and ovaries (producing eggs).

State the chromosome number in human body cells (2n) and gametes (n). Easy
Show Answer

Body cells: 2n = 46 chromosomes (diploid). Gametes (sperm and eggs): n = 23 chromosomes (haploid).

How many divisions occur in meiosis and how many daughter cells are produced? Easy
Show Answer

Meiosis involves two sequential divisions (Meiosis I and II), producing four haploid daughter cells.

Define crossing over and state when it occurs in meiosis. Easy
Show Answer

Crossing over is the exchange of DNA segments between homologous chromosomes. It occurs during Prophase I of Meiosis I when homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) forming bivalents.

Define independent assortment and explain what it contributes to. Easy
Show Answer

Independent assortment is the random orientation of homologous chromosome pairs at the metaphase plate in Meiosis I. Each pair aligns independently, producing many possible chromosome combinations in gametes, contributing greatly to genetic variation.

State two differences between mitosis and meiosis. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Mitosis: 1 division produces 2 diploid cells; Meiosis: 2 divisions produce 4 haploid cells. (2) Mitosis: no crossing over, genetically identical daughter cells; Meiosis: crossing over and independent assortment produce genetically unique cells.

What is a homologous pair of chromosomes? Easy
Show Answer

Two chromosomes of the same length and shape, carrying genes for the same traits at the same positions (loci) - one from the mother and one from the father. Humans have 23 homologous pairs.

List three ways meiosis generates genetic variation. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Crossing over during Prophase I. (2) Independent assortment during Metaphase I. (3) Random fertilisation - any sperm can fertilise any egg.

Set B Apply & Explain

Explain why meiosis must reduce chromosome number before fertilisation. What would happen without it? Medium
Show Answer

If gametes were diploid (46 chromosomes), fertilisation would produce a zygote with 92 chromosomes - doubling every generation. Cells would quickly have an unmanageable number, causing developmental failure. Meiosis halves the number to 23 so fertilisation restores the normal 46.

Calculate the number of chromosome combinations possible from independent assortment alone in humans. Medium
Show Answer

With 23 homologous pairs, each assorts independently: 223 = 8,388,608 (over 8 million) possible combinations per gamete - before crossing over is even considered.

Explain what happens during Meiosis I that does not happen in Meiosis II or in mitosis. Medium
Show Answer

During Meiosis I: (1) Homologous chromosomes pair (synapsis). (2) Crossing over between non-sister chromatids. (3) Homologous chromosomes (not sister chromatids) are separated - the "reductive division" that halves chromosome number from 2n to n. This is unique to Meiosis I.

A cell with 2n=8 undergoes meiosis. State the chromosome number at the end of Meiosis I and Meiosis II. Medium
Show Answer

End of Meiosis I: 2 cells, each with 4 chromosomes (n=4) - each chromosome still has 2 sister chromatids joined. End of Meiosis II: 4 cells, each with 4 single chromosomes (n=4 single chromatids).

Explain why a mutation during meiosis can be passed to offspring, but a mutation in a skin cell cannot. Medium
Show Answer

Mutations in gametes (formed by meiosis) are incorporated into the fertilised egg and copied into every cell of the new organism - heritable. Mutations in somatic cells (skin) are confined to that individual's body and cannot be passed to offspring as they are not in the germline.

Explain what non-disjunction is and how it leads to Down Syndrome (trisomy 21). Medium
Show Answer

Non-disjunction is the failure of homologous chromosomes (Meiosis I) or sister chromatids (Meiosis II) to separate properly. In trisomy 21, chromosome pair 21 fails to separate in Meiosis I - both chromosomes move to one daughter cell. The resulting gamete has 2 copies of chromosome 21. When fertilised by a normal gamete (1 copy), the zygote has 3 copies of chromosome 21 = Down Syndrome.

How does crossing over increase variation beyond what independent assortment alone provides? Medium
Show Answer

Independent assortment shuffles whole chromosomes. But genes on the same chromosome would always stay linked without crossing over. Crossing over physically exchanges segments, creating recombinant chromosomes with new allele combinations. Genes on the same chromosome can now be separated - dramatically increasing variation beyond 223.

Compare the products of spermatogenesis vs oogenesis - how many functional cells result and why? Medium
Show Answer

Spermatogenesis: 1 primary spermatocyte produces 4 functional sperm. Oogenesis: 1 primary oocyte produces 1 functional egg + 3 polar bodies (non-functional). Unequal cytoplasm division in oogenesis ensures the egg has sufficient nutrients and organelles for early embryo development after fertilisation.

Set C Analyse & Evaluate

Critically compare meiosis and mitosis: purpose, location, genetic outcome, and number of divisions. Hard
Show Answer

Purpose: Mitosis - growth/repair/asexual reproduction; Meiosis - gamete production. Location: Mitosis - all body tissues; Meiosis - gonads only. Genetic outcome: Mitosis - 2 genetically identical diploid cells; Meiosis - 4 genetically unique haploid cells. Divisions: Mitosis - 1; Meiosis - 2 sequential. Unique to meiosis: synapsis, crossing over, independent assortment, and the reductive first division.

Justify the claim that the number of genetically distinct gametes humans can produce is effectively infinite. Hard
Show Answer

Independent assortment: 223 ≈ 8.4 million per individual. Two individuals: 8.4M x 8.4M ≈ 70 trillion possible offspring genotypes before crossing over. Crossing over can occur at ~45 sites in human meiosis, each generating unique recombinant chromosomes. The total number of combinations exceeds any astronomical figure, making no two humans (except identical twins) genetically identical.

Some organisms reproduce asexually most of the time but switch to sexual reproduction under stress. Explain the evolutionary advantage using meiosis. Hard
Show Answer

Under stable conditions, asexual reproduction is energetically efficient. Under stress (food shortage, disease, temperature change), sexual reproduction via meiosis (crossing over + independent assortment) generates diverse offspring. Some may carry allele combinations conferring survival in the new conditions. This flexibility maximises fitness across environments: exploit stability asexually, generate adaptive variation sexually when needed.

Evaluate the consequences of complete failure of crossing over in Meiosis I on genetic variation in offspring. Hard
Show Answer

Without crossing over, genes on the same chromosome are completely linked - always inherited together. Only independent assortment (223) and random fertilisation provide variation. While still substantial, variation would be far less. Linked disadvantageous alleles could not be separated from linked beneficial ones. Adaptive evolution would be slower, particularly for organisms where important genes happen to be on the same chromosome.

Analyse why trisomy 21 risk increases sharply with maternal age. Link to the biology of oogenesis. Hard
Show Answer

Human primary oocytes begin Meiosis I during fetal development but are arrested at Prophase I from birth until each ovulation - potentially for 40+ years. During this prolonged arrest, spindle checkpoint mechanisms and cohesin proteins holding chromosomes together can degrade. The probability of non-disjunction increases. Risk rises sharply after age 35 (from ~1/1000 at age 30 to ~1/30 at age 45).

If two genes are always inherited together in offspring experiments, what does this suggest and how could it be used to map chromosomes? Hard
Show Answer

Genes always inherited together are linked - on the same chromosome, very close together. Crossing over frequency is proportional to physical distance between loci. Very rare recombination between two genes means they are very close. By measuring recombination frequency for many gene pairs, geneticists build genetic linkage maps showing relative positions of genes on chromosomes (Morgan's method with Drosophila).

Compare the genetic consequences of non-disjunction in Meiosis I vs Meiosis II. Which produces more abnormal gametes? Hard
Show Answer

Meiosis I non-disjunction: both homologues go to one cell, and neither to the other. All 4 resulting gametes are abnormal (2 with n+1, 2 with n-1). Meiosis II non-disjunction: only one secondary cell is affected, producing 2 abnormal gametes (1 with n+1, 1 with n-1) while the other 2 gametes from the other cell are normal. Therefore Meiosis I non-disjunction produces all 4 gametes abnormal, vs only 2 of 4 in Meiosis II.

Design a model or analogy to explain both divisions of meiosis and genetic shuffling to a student who has never studied biology. Hard
Show Answer

Card deck analogy: Start with 2 decks of 23 different cards (one deck = maternal chromosomes, one = paternal; total 46). Crossing over: swap some cards at matching positions between decks (new combinations). Meiosis I: randomly split into 2 piles of 23 - one card from each pair randomly goes to each pile (independent assortment). Meiosis II: each pile is split into individual cards. Result: 4 hands of 23 unique cards = 4 unique haploid gametes. Justification: decks = homologous pairs; swaps = crossing over; random splitting = independent assortment; 4 final hands = 4 gametes.

Human Reproduction

Set A Recall & Identify

Where does fertilisation normally occur in the human female reproductive system? Easy
Show Answer

Fertilisation normally occurs in the fallopian tube (oviduct), usually in the outer third near the ovary, within 12-24 hours of ovulation.

State the chromosome number of human sperm, egg, and zygote. Easy
Show Answer

Sperm: 23 (haploid). Egg: 23 (haploid). Zygote: 46 (diploid).

Name four functions of the placenta during pregnancy. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Transfers oxygen from mother to fetus. (2) Transfers nutrients (glucose, amino acids) from mother to fetus. (3) Removes fetal waste (CO2, urea) to mother. (4) Produces hormones (hCG, progesterone) to maintain pregnancy. (5) Acts as partial barrier to pathogens.

What is the approximate human gestation period? Easy
Show Answer

Approximately 38-40 weeks (about 9 months) from fertilisation to birth.

Define zygote and describe its first developmental steps. Easy
Show Answer

A zygote is the single diploid cell formed when sperm and egg nuclei fuse. It undergoes rapid mitotic cleavage to form a morula (solid ball), then a blastocyst (hollow ball), which travels down the fallopian tube and implants in the uterine wall.

Name the main male and female sex hormones and where each is produced. Easy
Show Answer

Testosterone - produced in the testes (Leydig cells). Oestrogen and progesterone - produced in the ovaries (and placenta during pregnancy).

Name three structural adaptations of sperm cells for their function. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Flagellum (tail) - for swimming. (2) Many mitochondria in midpiece - produce ATP for flagellum movement. (3) Acrosome on head - contains enzymes to penetrate egg's zona pellucida. (4) Streamlined head - reduces drag. (5) Haploid nucleus - carries genetic material.

What is the function of amniotic fluid? Easy
Show Answer

(1) Cushions and protects the fetus from mechanical shocks. (2) Maintains a stable temperature around the fetus. (3) Allows fetal movement for muscle and lung development. (4) Prevents the fetus from drying out.

Set B Apply & Explain

Explain why fetal and maternal blood do not mix in the placenta, even though substances are exchanged. Medium
Show Answer

Fetal and maternal blood flow through separate vessels in the placenta separated by thin layers of cells. Exchange of gases, nutrients, and waste occurs by diffusion and active transport across the placental membrane - not by mixing. This prevents potentially incompatible blood types from coming into direct contact.

Describe the acrosome reaction and explain its importance in fertilisation. Medium
Show Answer

When sperm contacts the zona pellucida of the egg, the acrosome (cap on sperm head) ruptures, releasing hydrolytic enzymes. These digest a path through the zona pellucida, allowing the sperm to reach and fuse with the egg cell membrane. Without this, fertilisation cannot occur.

Explain the cortical reaction and why it prevents polyspermy. Medium
Show Answer

When one sperm fuses with the egg, cortical granules release enzymes that harden the zona pellucida into a fertilisation membrane, blocking all other sperm from entering. This prevents polyspermy (fertilisation by more than one sperm), which would produce an embryo with abnormal chromosome numbers.

Explain why both oestrogen and progesterone must remain elevated during pregnancy. Medium
Show Answer

Oestrogen maintains the uterine lining and prepares the body for birth. Progesterone maintains the thick endometrium, prevents uterine contractions (keeps the fetus in place), and partially suppresses the immune system to prevent rejection of the fetus. If levels fall, the uterine lining breaks down triggering miscarriage.

Trace the journey of a glucose molecule from the mother's blood to a fetal liver cell. Medium
Show Answer

Mother's blood vessels in placenta → diffusion/facilitated transport across placental membrane → fetal blood capillaries in placenta → umbilical vein → through umbilical cord → fetal liver. In the liver, glucose may be stored as glycogen or used for respiration.

Explain why alcohol consumed by a pregnant woman can harm the fetus despite the placenta acting as a "barrier." Medium
Show Answer

The placenta is not a complete barrier. Small, lipid-soluble molecules like alcohol cross freely by simple diffusion. The fetal liver is immature and cannot metabolise alcohol efficiently. Alcohol causes Fetal Alcohol Syndrome, including brain damage and developmental defects. The placenta protects against most bacteria but not against many small molecules and drugs.

Describe three structural adaptations of the placenta that make it efficient for gas exchange. Medium
Show Answer

(1) Large surface area - highly folded villi and microvilli. (2) Thin membrane - only 2-4 cell layers between maternal and fetal blood. (3) Rich blood supply - abundant maternal sinuses and fetal capillaries maintain a steep concentration gradient. (4) Fetal haemoglobin has higher oxygen affinity than adult - draws oxygen across the gradient.

Compare what happens to an egg that is fertilised vs one that is not fertilised after ovulation. Medium
Show Answer

Fertilised: sperm + egg → zygote in fallopian tube → cleavage → blastocyst → implants in uterine wall → pregnancy. Not fertilised: egg degenerates within 12-24 hours. Corpus luteum degenerates ~10-12 days after ovulation if no hCG signal → progesterone and oestrogen fall → uterine lining breaks down → menstruation.

Set C Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate why human gestation (9 months) is much longer than a mouse (3 weeks), considering brain development and bipedalism. Hard
Show Answer

Humans have a disproportionately large, complex brain. The neocortex requires extended development. However, the birth canal is constrained by bipedal pelvis shape - too large a skull cannot pass through. Humans are born relatively premature compared to other primates. The infant brain at birth is only ~25% of adult size and develops for years post-birth. Nine months is the evolutionary compromise between sufficient brain development and the physical limits of the birth canal.

Analyse the hormonal cascade that triggers childbirth (parturition). Why is birth timing important for neonatal survival? Hard
Show Answer

Near term: fetal cortisol rises, triggering the placenta to shift from progesterone to oestrogen production. Rising oestrogen increases uterine sensitivity to oxytocin and prostaglandin production. Oxytocin triggers contractions; a positive feedback loop amplifies them (more contractions → more oxytocin → stronger contractions → birth). Too early: immature lungs (no surfactant before ~24 weeks), immature gut and immune system. Too late: placental deterioration and oxygen deprivation risk.

Explain the immunological challenge of pregnancy - why the fetus is not rejected like a transplanted organ. Hard
Show Answer

The fetus carries paternal antigens not present in the mother - it should trigger immune rejection. Prevention mechanisms: (1) Placental trophoblast cells express HLA-G (non-classical MHC) that inhibits natural killer cells. (2) Local immunosuppression at the uterine-placental interface (regulatory T cells, progesterone suppresses T-cell activity). (3) The uterus is an immunologically privileged site with reduced immune surveillance. Failure of these mechanisms contributes to some recurrent miscarriages.

A mother is Rh-negative and the fetus is Rh-positive. Explain the risk in the second pregnancy and why the first is unaffected. Hard
Show Answer

During the first delivery, some fetal Rh+ blood enters the mother's circulation, causing her to produce anti-Rh antibodies (sensitisation). This usually occurs at/after birth so the first fetus is unaffected. In a second Rh+ pregnancy, maternal anti-Rh IgG antibodies cross the placenta and attack fetal red blood cells - haemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN). Prevented by giving the mother anti-D immunoglobulin after the first delivery to destroy fetal Rh+ cells before sensitisation occurs.

Evaluate the claim "natural childbirth is always preferable to Caesarean section." Consider evidence and ethics. Hard
Show Answer

Evidence for vaginal birth: faster maternal recovery, lower infection risk, baby colonised with beneficial vaginal microbiome, lower risk of childhood allergies. Evidence for C-section when indicated: saves lives in obstructed labour, fetal distress, placenta praevia, breech - maternal and infant mortality would be far higher without it. Concern: WHO recommends 10-15% C-section rate but many private hospitals exceed 50%, suggesting overuse beyond medical necessity. The "natural is always better" claim is false - context determines optimal care, and maternal autonomy must be respected.

Analyse the "Grandmother Hypothesis" as an evolutionary explanation for human menopause. Hard
Show Answer

Direct reproduction ends at menopause (~50 years), yet women live 30-40 years beyond this. The Grandmother Hypothesis (Hawkes): post-menopausal women increase their inclusive fitness by supporting grandchildren who share 25% of their genes. Data from Hadza hunter-gatherers shows grandmothers' foraging significantly boosts grandchild nutrition and survival. By ceasing own reproduction, they avoid competing with daughters for resources and can dedicate energy to grandchild survival. Supported by killer whale studies where post-reproductive females lead groups with crucial ecological knowledge.

Explain why pregnant women are advised to take folic acid in the first trimester. Link to neural development. Hard
Show Answer

Folic acid (vitamin B9) is essential for DNA synthesis and rapid cell division - both maximal during early fetal development. The neural tube (forming the brain and spinal cord) closes by week 4, often before the woman knows she is pregnant. Folic acid deficiency impairs closure, causing neural tube defects (NTDs) such as spina bifida or anencephaly. Supplementation before conception and in the first trimester reduces NTD risk by up to 70%.

Explain the physiological cardiovascular changes in the mother during pregnancy and why they are necessary. Hard
Show Answer

(1) Blood volume increases by ~45% - to supply the growing fetus and placenta. (2) Cardiac output rises ~40% - heart pumps more blood per minute. (3) Heart rate increases by ~10-15 bpm. (4) Blood pressure may fall slightly in second trimester (progesterone relaxes vessel walls). (5) Red blood cell production increases. These ensure adequate oxygen delivery to both mother and fetus. Failure to adapt (e.g., pre-eclampsia - dangerously high blood pressure) threatens both lives.

Set A Recall & Identify

Name the male and female reproductive parts of a flower. Easy
Show Answer

Male: Stamen (anther + filament) - anther produces pollen. Female: Pistil/Carpel (stigma + style + ovary) - ovary contains ovules.

Define pollination and distinguish it from fertilisation in plants. Easy
Show Answer

Pollination: transfer of pollen from anther to stigma. Fertilisation: fusion of male gamete (from pollen tube) with egg cell in ovule to form a zygote. Pollination must precede fertilisation - they are separate events.

State two agents of pollination and one flower adaptation for each. Easy
Show Answer

Insect pollination: brightly coloured petals, nectar, strong scent - attract insects. Wind pollination: feathery/branched stigmas (catch airborne pollen), light/smooth/abundant pollen, anthers hanging outside flower. Wind-pollinated flowers need no petals.

After fertilisation, what do the ovule and ovary develop into? Easy
Show Answer

Ovule → seed (contains embryo and food store). Ovary → fruit (protects the seed and aids dispersal).

List four methods of seed dispersal and give one example plant for each. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Wind - dandelion (parachute), maple (wings). (2) Water - coconut (buoyant husk). (3) Animals external - burdock (hooks on fur). (4) Animals eaten - apple, cherry (fleshy fruit). (5) Explosive - pea pods, squirting cucumber.

Name three conditions needed for seed germination. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Water - activates enzymes, softens seed coat. (2) Oxygen - for aerobic respiration to provide energy. (3) Suitable temperature - for optimal enzyme activity (most seeds: 10-30 degrees C). Light is not essential for most seeds' germination.

What is the function of the endosperm in a seed? Easy
Show Answer

The endosperm is a nutrient-rich tissue that provides food (starch, oils, proteins) for the developing embryo during germination, before the seedling can photosynthesize independently.

Define self-pollination and cross-pollination. State one advantage of cross-pollination. Easy
Show Answer

Self-pollination: pollen transferred to stigma of same flower or plant. Cross-pollination: pollen transferred between different plants of the same species. Advantage of cross-pollination: produces genetic variation in offspring, improving adaptability to changing environments.

Set B Apply & Explain

Explain double fertilisation in flowering plants. Why is it called "double"? Medium
Show Answer

A pollen grain contains two male gametes. After pollination, a pollen tube grows to the ovule. First fertilisation: gamete 1 + egg cell → zygote (2n) → embryo. Second fertilisation: gamete 2 + two polar nuclei → endosperm nucleus (3n) → food store. Called "double" because two separate fusion events occur simultaneously - unique to angiosperms (flowering plants).

Describe three structural differences between wind-pollinated and insect-pollinated flowers. Explain each. Medium
Show Answer

(1) Petals: insect = large, colourful (attract insects); wind = small/absent (not needed). (2) Pollen: insect = sticky/spiky (attaches to insect bodies); wind = light/smooth/abundant (airborne). (3) Stigma: insect = compact sticky knob; wind = large, feathery, protruding (maximises airborne pollen capture). (4) Nectar/scent: insect = present; wind = absent.

Why is seed dispersal important? Explain two consequences if seeds always fell directly under the parent. Medium
Show Answer

Consequences: (1) Overcrowding - parent and offspring compete for the same water, light, and minerals, reducing all plants' growth. (2) Limited gene flow - inability to colonise new habitats, reducing genetic diversity and adaptability. Dispersal moves seeds to new environments, reduces competition, enables colonisation, and promotes genetic variation through cross-pollination with other populations.

Explain how a fleshy fruit (e.g., cherry) is adapted for animal seed dispersal. What are the benefits to both plant and animal? Medium
Show Answer

Plant adaptations: bright colour (visible), sweet nutritious flesh (reward), hard seed coat (survives digestion). Animal eats the fruit, seed passes through gut undamaged and is deposited in droppings far from the parent. Plant benefit: seeds dispersed widely. Animal benefit: nutritious food. This is a mutualistic relationship.

Describe the structure of a seed, identifying the embryo, testa, and food store. Medium
Show Answer

Testa: tough seed coat protecting embryo from drying and damage. Embryo: miniature plant with radicle (root), plumule (shoot), and cotyledons (seed leaves). Food store: endosperm (in monocots) or the cotyledons themselves (in dicots like beans) - nutrients for early germination before photosynthesis begins.

Design an experiment to test the effect of temperature on seed germination. Medium
Show Answer

Independent variable: temperature (e.g., 5, 15, 25, 35, 45 degrees C). Dependent variable: % seeds germinated after 7 days. Controlled variables: seed variety, number of seeds (20 per dish), water amount, light. Method: seeds on damp cotton wool in Petri dishes at each temperature. Prediction: bell-shaped curve with optimal germination at ~20-25 degrees C; low germination at extremes (enzymes denatured above ~40 degrees C; metabolic rate too slow below ~10 degrees C).

Explain how monoecious plants promote cross-pollination despite having flowers on the same plant. Medium
Show Answer

(1) Protandry - anthers mature and release pollen before the same plant's stigmas become receptive. (2) Physical separation - male and female flowers on different branches, reducing direct transfer. (3) Chemical self-incompatibility - pollen from the same plant is chemically rejected by the stigma. All mechanisms ensure cross-pollination, producing genetically varied offspring.

Explain why global bee population decline threatens human food security. Medium
Show Answer

Bees pollinate approximately 75% of major food crops (fruits, vegetables, nuts). Without bee pollination, these crops produce little or no fruit or seeds. Global bee decline is driven by pesticides (neonicotinoids), habitat loss, Varroa mite parasites, and disease. Loss of pollination services would dramatically reduce global food production. Estimated economic value of bee pollination: ~$265 billion annually. One-third of the human food supply depends directly on insect pollination.

Set C Analyse & Evaluate

Compare wind vs insect pollination strategies in terms of energy investment and reliability. Which is more efficient? Hard
Show Answer

Wind: low energy per pollen grain but vast quantities produced as most never reach the right flower - high total pollen cost. No energy in petals/nectar/scent. Reliable in open habitats with consistent wind but imprecise. Insect: high energy per flower (petals, nectar, scent) but insects deliberately carry pollen to the correct species - highly precise per grain. No strategy is universally superior - wind suits grasslands and conifers; insects suit complex diverse ecosystems.

Some orchids mimic female insects, causing males to attempt mating with the flower. Analyse this as a reproductive strategy. Hard
Show Answer

Sexual deception - the orchid exploits mating behaviour without providing any reward. Male insects attempt to mate with the flower, pick up pollen, and deposit it on the next orchid of the same species. Advantage: very precise pollination, zero energy wasted on nectar. Risk: completely dependent on one pollinator species - if that species declines, the orchid cannot reproduce. An example of extreme evolutionary co-specialisation.

Analyse why cereals are wind-pollinated while many fruit trees are insect-pollinated. Discuss agricultural implications. Hard
Show Answer

Cereals grow in dense monocultures in open fields - wind is consistent and abundant; self-compatible varieties ensure grain set without insects. Fruit trees have complex flowers requiring precise pollination for fruit set - insects are far more efficient. Agricultural implications: cereal production is not threatened by pollinator decline but fruit/vegetable yields are. Fruit farmers must protect pollinator habitats, avoid pesticides during flowering, or rent beehives. Insecticide-treated cereal fields inadvertently harm pollinators needed by neighbouring orchards.

Evaluate the role of seed banks (e.g., Svalbard Global Seed Vault). What reproductive biology principles make seeds ideal for long-term storage? Hard
Show Answer

Role: Svalbard stores 1.3 million+ seed varieties from 98% of countries - safeguards against crop failure, conflict, climate change, or extinction. Biology of storage: seeds are adapted for dormancy - metabolic rate near zero when dry and cold. Reduced to ~5% moisture + stored at -18 degrees C, seeds remain viable for hundreds of years. The embryo contains the complete genetic information plus a food store in a compact, robust form. Challenge: recalcitrant seeds (e.g., oak, avocado) cannot be dried without dying - require cryopreservation of embryos or pollen instead.

GM crops with herbicide resistance can cross-pollinate with wild relatives. Analyse the ecological risks. Hard
Show Answer

Wind/insect pollination can transfer transgenes to related wild species (weeds). If herbicide resistance transfers, they become superweeds - uncontrollable by the herbicide, potentially dominating ecosystems. Ecological impacts: reduced plant biodiversity, altered food webs, displacement of native species. Risk is highest in centres of crop origin (e.g., Mexico for maize) where wild relatives are present. Mitigation: buffer zones, male-sterile GM varieties, regulatory containment protocols, and monitoring programs.

Explain the co-evolutionary relationship between flowering plants and pollinators. Give one example of a highly specialised pair. Hard
Show Answer

Co-evolution: plants and pollinators exert mutual selective pressures over millions of years - plants with traits attracting effective pollinators reproduce more; pollinators accessing more rewards survive better. Both evolve in tandem. Classic example: Darwin's orchid (Angraecum sesquipedale) has an 11-inch nectar spur. Darwin predicted a pollinator with an 11-inch tongue before one was found. The hawk moth (Xanthopan morganii praedicta) was discovered with exactly this proboscis length - neither can reproduce without the other, an extreme mutualistic dependency.

Critically evaluate whether human-assisted pollination (hand pollination or drones) can replace bee pollination globally. Hard
Show Answer

Arguments for: hand pollination is practised (vanilla, some greenhouse crops); pollinator drones demonstrated in Japan. Arguments against scale: global agricultural land covers ~4.9 billion hectares; one honey bee colony pollinates up to 300 million flowers per day. No technology can match this at comparable cost. Estimated cost of manual US pollination alone: over $100 billion/year. Conclusion: human-assisted pollination is a niche supplement at best. Protecting natural pollinator populations is vastly more cost-effective. Technology cannot substitute for ecosystem services at planetary scale.

Design a study to investigate the effect of habitat corridor width on pollination success of wildflowers in a fragmented agricultural landscape. Hard
Show Answer

Hypothesis: wider corridors support more diverse pollinator communities and higher seed set. Independent variable: corridor width (1m, 3m, 6m, 10m). Dependent variables: pollinator species richness and visit frequency (observation + camera traps), seed set rate (% flowers producing viable seeds). Controlled variables: plant species composition, corridor length, surrounding land use, season, observation time. Method: 20 replicate corridors per width across the landscape; pollinator surveys over 3 seasons; seed set analysis at season end. Ethical considerations: landowner consent, no wildlife disturbance, report findings to conservation agencies. Expected outcome: wider corridors produce greater pollinator diversity and higher seed set, with possible saturation above a threshold width.

Set A Recall & Identify

Define IVF in one sentence. Easy
Show Answer

IVF (in vitro fertilisation) is a reproductive technology where eggs are fertilised by sperm outside the body in a laboratory, and the resulting embryo is implanted into the uterus.

Approximately how many babies have been born worldwide through IVF? When was the first IVF baby born? Easy
Show Answer

More than 8 million babies worldwide. Louise Brown, the first IVF baby, was born in 1978 in the UK (Robert Edwards and Patrick Steptoe).

State three reasons a couple might seek IVF treatment. Easy
Show Answer

Any three: blocked/damaged fallopian tubes; low sperm count or poor motility; unexplained infertility; ovulation disorders; endometriosis; same-sex couples or single individuals wishing to have a child.

What was Dolly the sheep and why was she significant? When was she born? Easy
Show Answer

Dolly was the first mammal cloned from an adult somatic cell using SCNT (somatic cell nuclear transfer). Born in 1996 at the Roslin Institute, Scotland. She proved that differentiated adult cells retain complete genetic information to produce an entire organism - a landmark in biology.

Describe the technique of somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) used to clone Dolly. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Nucleus removed from an adult sheep udder cell. (2) Nucleus removed from a donor egg cell (enucleation). (3) Udder nucleus inserted into enucleated egg. (4) Electric pulse fuses them and stimulates development. (5) Embryo implanted into a surrogate mother. (6) Dolly born - genetically identical to the udder cell donor.

What is preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) and how does it relate to IVF? Easy
Show Answer

PGD involves removing one or two cells from an IVF embryo at the 8-cell stage and testing their DNA for genetic disorders or chromosomal abnormalities. Only embryos free of the tested condition are selected for transfer to the uterus.

What is surrogacy? Distinguish between traditional and gestational surrogacy. Easy
Show Answer

Surrogacy: a woman carries a pregnancy on behalf of another person/couple. Traditional: surrogate's own egg is used - she is genetically related to the child. Gestational: embryo created from intended parents' (or donors') gametes via IVF is implanted in the surrogate - no genetic connection to the child.

Name two genetic diseases that PGD can screen for before embryo implantation. Easy
Show Answer

Any two: cystic fibrosis, Huntington's disease, BRCA1/2 breast cancer predisposition, sickle cell anaemia, Tay-Sachs disease, Down Syndrome (chromosomal), Duchenne muscular dystrophy.

Set B Apply & Explain

Outline the step-by-step IVF process from hormone treatment to embryo transfer. Medium
Show Answer

(1) Ovarian stimulation: woman given FSH/LH injections to produce multiple eggs. (2) Egg retrieval: ultrasound-guided needle aspiration. (3) Sperm preparation: sample washed and concentrated. (4) Fertilisation: eggs and sperm mixed in lab dish, or ICSI (single sperm injected into egg). (5) Culture: embryos develop in incubator 3-5 days. (6) PGD (optional): genetic testing. (7) Embryo transfer: 1-2 embryos transferred via catheter. (8) Remaining viable embryos frozen. (9) Pregnancy test after 2 weeks.

Explain why IVF success rate decreases significantly with maternal age. Medium
Show Answer

Egg quality and quantity decline with age - older eggs have higher rates of chromosomal abnormalities and reduced mitochondrial function. The uterine lining also becomes less receptive. Success rate: ~45% per cycle under 35; ~5-10% per cycle over 42. This reflects biological decline in reproductive capacity, not a limitation of IVF technology.

Explain the process of embryo cryopreservation in IVF and state one advantage for patients. Medium
Show Answer

Vitrification (flash freezing) in liquid nitrogen at -196 degrees C prevents ice crystal formation that would damage cells. Embryos stored indefinitely. Advantage: patients can attempt further pregnancies without repeating the expensive/invasive egg collection process. If the first cycle fails, frozen embryos can be thawed and transferred in a subsequent cycle.

Explain why reproductive cloning of humans remains globally banned despite the technology theoretically being possible. Medium
Show Answer

Scientific concerns: very low success rate (Dolly required 277 attempts); cloned animals show premature aging and epigenetic abnormalities - applying this to humans would produce damaged children. Ethical concerns: violates human dignity (a copy of another person); removes the right to a unique identity; potential for exploitation (organ farming). The UN Declaration (2005) bans reproductive human cloning. No country legally permits it.

How does PGD select embryos free of a specific genetic disease? Describe the process and genetic basis. Medium
Show Answer

One cell biopsied from each 8-cell embryo. DNA amplified by PCR and analysed by probes (for point mutations) or FISH/array CGH (for chromosomal abnormalities). Embryos carrying the disease genotype identified and not implanted. Unaffected embryos (including carriers of recessive conditions) are transferred. Removal of one cell at the 8-cell stage does not harm subsequent development.

Explain mitochondrial replacement therapy (MRT). Why was it needed and why is it called "three-parent baby"? Medium
Show Answer

Mitochondrial DNA mutations cause severe diseases. MRT transfers nuclear DNA from a mother with faulty mitochondria into an enucleated donor egg (with healthy mitochondria) before fertilisation. The child has nuclear DNA from biological mother and father (~99.8% of all DNA) plus mitochondrial DNA from the donor (~0.2%). Three genetic contributors - hence "three-parent baby." Legalised in the UK in 2015. Needed because previously there was no way to prevent transmission of mitochondrial disease from mother to child.

Compare therapeutic cloning vs reproductive cloning: what is the same, what differs, and why does society treat them differently? Medium
Show Answer

Same: both use SCNT to create a genetically identical embryo. Different: reproductive cloning implants the embryo producing a live cloned individual; therapeutic cloning uses the embryo only to harvest stem cells for medical treatments - the embryo is destroyed. Social treatment: reproductive cloning widely banned (human dignity, safety risks). Therapeutic cloning permitted under strict regulation in some countries (UK, Singapore) due to potential medical benefits; contested by those who consider embryos to have the full moral status of persons.

Explain how IVF has enabled same-sex couples and single individuals to have genetically related children. Medium
Show Answer

Female same-sex couples: one partner provides eggs (fertilised with donor sperm via IVF), embryo transferred to either partner. Male same-sex couples: one or both partners provide sperm (with donor eggs) and a gestational surrogate carries the pregnancy. Single females: own eggs + donor sperm. Single males: own sperm + donor eggs + surrogate. Technologies used: sperm/egg donation, IVF, gestational surrogacy, PGD. Legal access varies widely by country.

Set C Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate ethical arguments for and against PGD to select embryos for non-medical traits ("designer babies"). Hard
Show Answer

For: reproductive autonomy; selecting traits could enhance wellbeing; already done for medical conditions - where is the line? Against: eugenics concerns; reduced genetic diversity; discarding embryos based on subjective value judgements; commodification of children; available only to wealthy families, entrenching genetic privilege (genetic inequality). Most jurisdictions permit PGD only for serious medical conditions. Non-medical trait selection is widely banned and requires ongoing public and regulatory debate.

Dolly died at age 6 (half normal sheep lifespan) with premature aging signs. Analyse what this suggests about cloned organisms biologically. Hard
Show Answer

Dolly was cloned from a 6-year-old sheep. Her cells' telomeres were already shorter than normal at birth - telomeres shorten with each cell division. Her epigenetic state reflected the donor cell's age rather than a fresh start. This suggests SCNT nuclear reprogramming is incomplete - the clone inherits the biological age of the donor cell. Implications: cloned humans would likely have abbreviated lifespans and accelerated aging - a serious safety objection beyond ethical ones.

Analyse the global equity issue around IVF access. Who can and cannot access this technology, and what are the implications? Hard
Show Answer

IVF cost: ~$12,000-15,000 per cycle in the USA; some countries (Israel, Denmark) fund multiple cycles nationally. In low-income countries, cost is prohibitive for most people. Implications: a "fertility divide" - reproductive technology becomes a privilege of the wealthy. Justice questions: is access to reproductive technology a right or a luxury? Should global health funding prioritise IVF or basic maternal healthcare (which saves more lives per dollar)? Cultural and religious contexts create further unequal access. An equitable global health framework must address whether reproductive technology is a human right.

Critically assess whether conservation cloning of endangered species is an effective conservation strategy. Hard
Show Answer

For: can preserve unique genetic lineages; 2003 Pyrenean ibex was briefly cloned; San Diego "Frozen Zoo" stores cells of 10,500+ species. Against: very low success rates; cloned populations have minimal genetic diversity - cannot adapt to disease or environmental change; does not address root causes of extinction (habitat loss, hunting); resources better spent on habitat protection and anti-poaching. Conclusion: conservation cloning is a last-resort tool to prevent genetic extinction, not a replacement for addressing extinction drivers. It may buy time but is not a long-term solution.

Evaluate the claim "IVF is unnatural and therefore morally wrong." Consider biological, ethical, and religious perspectives. Hard
Show Answer

Biological: "natural" does not mean "good" - natural diseases kill; vaccination is "unnatural" but saves millions. The biological outcome (a human life) is identical whether conceived naturally or via IVF. Ethical: IVF helps people who deeply desire children and cannot conceive - autonomy and wellbeing support its use. Concern: fate of unused embryos if they have full moral status. Religious: Catholic Church opposes IVF; many other traditions (Protestant, Jewish, Islamic with conditions) permit it. The "unnatural = wrong" argument is a naturalistic fallacy. The genuine ethical debate concerns the moral status of the embryo, not the process itself.

Analyse the potential and risks of CRISPR-Cas9 germline editing in human embryos, using the He Jiankui 2018 case. Hard
Show Answer

He Jiankui edited CCR5 gene in IVF embryos to confer HIV resistance - producing the first gene-edited babies (Lulu and Nana). Scientific concerns: off-target mutations (unintended genome edits); mosaic editing (not all cells edited); CCR5 deletion may increase susceptibility to West Nile virus and influenza; long-term effects unknown. Ethical violations: no independent ethics approval; parents not fully informed; germline editing affects all future descendants; done for a condition manageable by other means. He was sentenced to 3 years in prison. Scientific consensus: germline editing not permissible until safety is demonstrated and robust ethics frameworks are established.

Assess whether India's legal framework on surrogacy adequately protects surrogates and children. Hard
Show Answer

India was once the global surrogacy capital (~$400M/year), largely unregulated. Concerns: exploitation of poor women; children abandoned if born with disabilities; lack of legal parentage clarity. Response: Surrogacy (Regulation) Act 2021 banned commercial surrogacy; allows only altruistic surrogacy by a close relative for Indian citizens. Critique: the complete commercial ban may push surrogacy underground or overseas, reducing protection rather than increasing it. Altruistic-only model limits options for infertile couples. No protection for surrogates who change their minds. A regulated commercial framework with strong protections may serve all parties better than a blanket ban.

Propose an ethical framework with specific criteria for deciding which reproductive technologies should be permitted, restricted, or banned. Hard
Show Answer

Criterion 1 - Safety: technology must demonstrate acceptable safety for mother, child, and surrogate before proceeding (non-maleficence). Criterion 2 - Autonomy: fully informed consent from all adults; no coercion. Criterion 3 - Justice: technology must not systematically disadvantage vulnerable groups or create genetic inequality. Criterion 4 - Beneficence: clear therapeutic or social benefit required; enhancement without medical need needs stronger justification. Criterion 5 - Reversibility: germline changes (heritable) require stricter regulation than somatic changes because mistakes affect all descendants. Application: IVF for infertility - permit. PGD for serious disease - permit. PGD for sex preference - restrict/ban. Reproductive cloning - ban (fails criteria 1, 3, 5). Therapeutic cloning for medicine - permit with oversight.

Define reproductive health as described by the WHO. Easy
Show Answer

WHO: reproductive health is "a state of complete physical, mental, and social wellbeing in all matters relating to the reproductive system" - not merely absence of disease. Includes the right to a safe sex life, capability to reproduce, and freedom to decide if, when, and how often to do so.

Name three STIs and state whether each is caused by a bacterium or virus. Easy

HIV/AIDS - virus. Chlamydia - bacterium. Gonorrhoea - bacterium. Syphilis - bacterium. Herpes (HSV) - virus. HPV - virus.

State two methods of STI prevention. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Consistent and correct condom use - barrier preventing transmission. (2) Vaccination - HPV vaccine, Hepatitis B vaccine. (3) Mutual monogamy with tested partner. (4) Regular testing and early treatment.

What does "reproductive rights" mean? Name two rights included. Easy
Show Answer

Reproductive rights are legal rights and freedoms relating to reproduction. Include: (1) Right to decide whether and when to have children. (2) Right to access contraception and family planning. (3) Right to safe abortion where legal. (4) Right to reproductive healthcare free from discrimination or coercion.

State two consequences of untreated chlamydia in females. Easy
Show Answer

(1) Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - infection spreading to uterus and fallopian tubes. (2) Infertility - scarring of fallopian tubes. (3) Increased risk of ectopic pregnancy.

What is the HPV vaccine and who is it primarily recommended for? Easy
Show Answer

The HPV vaccine (Gardasil, Cervarix) protects against HPV strains causing cervical cancer and genital warts. Primarily recommended for boys and girls aged 9-14 (before sexual activity begins) for maximum effectiveness. Also recommended for young adults up to age 26 in many guidelines.

Name two methods of contraception and classify each as hormonal, barrier, or other. Easy
Show Answer

Condom - barrier. Combined oral contraceptive pill - hormonal. Copper IUD (coil) - other. Hormonal implant/injection - hormonal. Sterilisation (tubal ligation/vasectomy) - surgical.

State the global legal status of human reproductive cloning. Easy
Show Answer

Human reproductive cloning is banned or effectively prohibited in the vast majority of countries. The UN Declaration on Human Cloning (2005) calls on member states to ban all forms incompatible with human dignity. No country legally permits reproductive human cloning.

Set B Apply & Explain

Explain why HIV weakens the immune system over time, making patients vulnerable to opportunistic infections. Medium
Show Answer

HIV targets CD4+ T helper cells (critical immune coordinators). The virus replicates inside CD4 cells, eventually destroying them. As CD4 count falls over years, the immune system cannot coordinate responses against infections or cancers. When CD4 count falls below 200 cells per microlitre (normal: 500-1500), the patient has AIDS - vulnerable to opportunistic infections (Pneumocystis pneumonia, Toxoplasma, CMV) that a healthy immune system would easily defeat.

Evaluate two ethical considerations around mandatory STI testing in schools. Medium
Show Answer

For: early detection and treatment reduces transmission and complications; most students would not seek testing voluntarily; protects public health. Against: violates individual privacy and bodily autonomy (compulsory medical procedure without consent); stigmatisation risk; minors have rights over their own health information. Better approach: voluntary, confidential testing with supportive counselling rather than mandatory programs.

Explain how access to reproductive healthcare is unequal globally, referencing maternal mortality rates. Medium
Show Answer

Maternal mortality ratio (MMR): Sub-Saharan Africa ~545/100,000 live births; South Asia ~145/100,000; High-income countries ~12/100,000 (WHO 2020). Most maternal deaths are preventable with access to skilled birth attendants, emergency obstetric care, antenatal care, and contraception. Inequality is driven by poverty, geographic isolation, lack of trained healthcare workers, and gender discrimination. A girl in sub-Saharan Africa faces 1-in-37 lifetime risk of maternal death; in high-income countries, 1 in 5,900.

Analyse the biological and social arguments for comprehensive sex education in schools. Medium
Show Answer

Biological: knowledge of STI transmission, contraception, and reproductive anatomy reduces unintended pregnancies and STI rates. Studies show comprehensive sex ed delays first sexual activity and increases contraceptive use - not the reverse. Social: empowers young people (especially girls) to make informed decisions; reduces gender inequality; reduces shame and stigma; enables recognition of abusive relationships. Countries with comprehensive sex ed (Netherlands, Germany) have among the lowest teen pregnancy rates globally.

Explain "reproductive coercion" and give two examples of how it manifests. Medium
Show Answer

Reproductive coercion: behaviour by a person seeking to control a partner's reproductive choices without free consent. Examples: (1) Pressuring a partner to become pregnant or to have an abortion against their will. (2) Contraceptive sabotage - secretly tampering with or removing contraception. Others: forced marriage, denial of reproductive healthcare access, forced sterilisation. It is a form of intimate partner violence and violates reproductive rights.

India has had a problem with declining sex ratios. Link this to reproductive technology and suggest two policy responses. Medium
Show Answer

Sex-selective abortion following ultrasound sex determination contributed to a skewed Child Sex Ratio (India 2011 Census: 914 girls per 1000 boys aged 0-6 in some states). Policy responses: (1) PCPNDT Act 1994 - bans sex determination by ultrasound; penalties for doctors and clients. (2) Beti Bachao Beti Padhao - government campaign shifting cultural attitudes toward girls' value. Root cause: address socioeconomic conditions making sons more valued (dowry, patrilineal inheritance, male-dominated workforce).

What is "informed consent" in reproductive healthcare and why is it particularly important with reproductive technologies? Medium
Show Answer

Informed consent: a patient voluntarily agrees to a procedure after fully understanding what it involves, the risks, alternatives, and their right to refuse. In reproductive technology: critical because procedures are complex and emotionally charged; outcomes may affect future generations; vulnerable people (desperate for a child) may accept risks they do not fully understand; commercial interests of clinics can create pressure. Genuine consent requires clear language, time to decide, no coercion, right to withdraw, and independent counselling.

Discuss the role of paternal reproductive health in fertility. Why has research historically focused almost exclusively on female fertility? Medium
Show Answer

Paternal contributions: ~50% of infertility cases involve male factors (low sperm count, poor motility, DNA fragmentation). Paternal age increases sperm DNA mutation rates, linked to elevated risk of autism and schizophrenia in offspring. Lifestyle factors (obesity, smoking, heat exposure) impair sperm quality. Historical bias: women visibly carry pregnancy, so social and medical assumptions placed reproductive responsibility on women. Funding followed this assumption. This is changing - male reproductive health is increasingly researched as a co-equal factor in fertility and child health outcomes.

Evaluate the ethical arguments for and against legalising abortion, considering both fetal development and the rights of the pregnant person. Hard

Central debate: when does morally significant personhood begin? Analyse how antibiotic-resistant gonorrhoea is emerging and evaluate the public health strategies needed to address it. Hard
Show Answer

Neisseria gonorrhoeae has evolved resistance to penicillin, tetracycline, fluoroquinolones, and now emerging resistance to cephalosporins (last-line antibiotics). Mechanism: random mutations + antibiotic selection; resistance genes spread via horizontal gene transfer. WHO lists gonorrhoea among priority resistant pathogens. Evaluate the argument that access to contraception is a fundamental human right, using evidence from development studies. Hard
Show Answer

Rights-based argument: ICPD Programme of Action (1994) recognises reproductive rights as human rights. Without contraception, people cannot control family size, perpetuating poverty especially for women who bear the physical and economic burden of unintended pregnancies. Development evidence: countries that expanded contraceptive access (Bangladesh, Thailand, Colombia) saw rapid fertility declines and corresponding economic growth ("demographic dividend"). Each $1 spent on family planning saves ~$6 in maternal and child health costs (UNFPA). 218 million women in developing countries have unmet need for contraception. Counter-argument: some religious/cultural views oppose contraception. Evaluation: evidence strongly supports contraceptive access as both a rights issue and a development strategy; opposition is primarily normative, not empirical.

Discuss the long-term societal consequences of an aging population with a falling birth rate. What reproductive health policies could address this? Hard
Show Answer

Consequences: smaller working-age population; fewer workers per retiree; strain on pensions, healthcare, elder care. Japan: 29% over-65s; South Korea: fertility rate 0.78 (2023) - the lowest ever recorded. Economic growth slows; labour shortages; rural depopulation. Reproductive health policies: (1) Paid parental leave - Sweden's 480-day policy maintained relatively high birth rates. (2) State-subsidised childcare and IVF - Israel funds unlimited IVF cycles. (3) Child benefit payments. (4) Flexible working policies. (5) Immigration to add working-age population. (6) Address housing costs and job insecurity - key drivers of deferred childbearing. Policy must address both reproductive choices and the economic conditions shaping them.

Assess whether "reproductive autonomy" is a universal value across cultures or a culturally specific Western concept. Hard
Show Answer

Universalist argument: reproductive autonomy is grounded in universal principles of bodily integrity, recognised in international instruments (CEDAW, ICPD). Physical harm from forced pregnancy is documented globally regardless of culture. Cultural relativist argument: in many societies reproductive decisions are made collectively by families or governed by religious authority - imposing individual autonomy may ignore legitimate social norms. Critical evaluation: the relativist argument cannot justify practices that cause documented harm (forced marriage, FGM, denial of contraception). The IB MYP perspective: cultural practices violating human dignity cannot be defended purely by cultural relativity. Reproductive autonomy reflects a universal interest in bodily self-determination that transcends cultural context, though its expression varies.

Design a school campaign to improve STI prevention awareness among Grade 9-12 students. Include content, format, evidence basis, and ethical considerations. Hard
Show Answer

Campaign name: "Real Talk" - age-appropriate, inclusive STI awareness. Content: STI transmission and prevention (condoms, vaccination, testing); how to access confidential testing; myth-busting; healthy relationships and consent. Format: (1) Peer educator workshops - more effective than adult-led lectures. (2) Anonymous digital Q&A - removes embarrassment. (3) Student-designed posters/social media for authenticity. (4) Sexual health nurse Q&A session. Evidence basis: WHO synthesis shows comprehensive sex ed + peer education + access to services reduces STI rates; abstinence-only education does not. Ethical considerations: age-appropriate and gender-inclusive language; no shaming; confidentiality guaranteed; parental notification policies clearly stated; campaign developed with student input rather than imposed top-down.

Evaluate whether genetic engineering of human embryos to eliminate heritable diseases is ethically justified, using Beauchamp and Childress's four principles of biomedical ethics. Hard
Show Answer

Beneficence: eliminating heritable diseases (e.g., cystic fibrosis, Huntington's) prevents significant suffering - clear benefit to the future child. Non-maleficence: current CRISPR technology has off-target effects; the child and all descendants carry any unintended mutations - risks are not yet acceptable. Autonomy: the embryo cannot consent; the child's future autonomy and identity shaped by their genetics is affected without their input. Justice: technology available only to the wealthy creates genetic inequality; disability rights activists argue this devalues the lives of those currently living with these conditions. Conclusion: currently non-maleficence and justice concerns outweigh the beneficence argument. When technology matures to eliminate off-target effects and governance frameworks ensure equitable access, the ethical calculus may shift - particularly for severe, untreatable childhood diseases.
