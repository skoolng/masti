Set A — Recall & Identify
What is heredity?
Easy
Answer

The transmission of traits from parents to offspring through genes

Heredity explains why offspring resemble their parents but are not identical to them.
Why did Mendel choose pea plants for his experiments?
Easy
Answer

Short generation time, many clear contrasting traits, easy to control pollination (self or cross pollinate), large number of offspring

Mendel's choice of organism was crucial — pea plants are ideal for genetic experiments.
Define: gene, allele, genotype, phenotype.
Easy
Answer

Gene: a segment of DNA that codes for a trait; Allele: alternate form of a gene; Genotype: genetic makeup (e.g., TT, Tt, tt); Phenotype: observable trait (e.g., tall, short)

These four terms are the vocabulary of genetics.
What is the difference between dominant and recessive alleles?
Easy
Answer

Dominant (T): expressed even when one copy present; Recessive (t): only expressed when two copies present (homozygous recessive)

Dominant alleles 'mask' recessive alleles in heterozygotes.
What is a homozygous genotype? Give an example.
Easy
Answer

Genotype with identical alleles for a trait: TT (homozygous dominant) or tt (homozygous recessive)

Pure-breeding organisms are homozygous — they always pass the same allele to offspring.
What is a heterozygous genotype?
Easy
Answer

Genotype with two different alleles: Tt (one dominant, one recessive)

Heterozygotes express the dominant phenotype but carry the recessive allele.
List four traits Mendel studied in pea plants.
Medium
Answer

Seed shape (round/wrinkled), seed colour (yellow/green), pod colour (green/yellow), plant height (tall/short) — he studied 7 traits total

Mendel's success was partly due to choosing traits controlled by single genes with clear dominance.
What does 'true breeding' mean?
Medium
Answer

A plant that consistently produces offspring with the same trait when self-pollinated — it is homozygous for that trait

Mendel started with true-breeding lines to establish baseline genotypes.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
In Mendel's monohybrid cross: TT × tt. What are the F1 and F2 phenotypic ratios?
Medium
Answer

F1: all Tt (tall); F2: 3 tall : 1 short (3:1 phenotypic ratio)

F2 ratio of 3:1 was Mendel's key observation leading to the Law of Segregation.
Explain why Mendel's results were initially ignored for 35 years.
Hard
Answer

He presented his results as mathematical ratios — unusual for biologists of the time; work published in an obscure journal; not integrated with cell biology (chromosomes not yet linked to genes)

His work was rediscovered in 1900 simultaneously by de Vries, Correns, and Tschermak.
A true-breeding tall plant (TT) is crossed with a short plant (tt). Predict the genotype and phenotype of F1 offspring.
Medium
Answer

All Tt — all tall; the recessive 't' is present but masked by dominant 'T'

The F1 shows only the dominant phenotype, regardless of the recessive allele's presence.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: If you were Mendel in the 1860s with no knowledge of DNA or chromosomes, how would you design experiments to determine inheritance rules?
Hard
Answer

Cross pure-breeding lines; count thousands of offspring; identify ratios; cross F1 to produce F2; use reciprocal crosses; self-pollinate — exactly what Mendel did

MYP Criterion B: understanding how scientific laws are derived from experimental evidence.
Evaluate: Mendel worked with discontinuous traits (tall/short). What difficulties arise with continuous traits like height in humans?
Hard
Answer

Continuous traits (many genes + environment) don't show simple 3:1 ratios — polygenic inheritance; harder to track individual gene effects; environmental overlay

MYP Criterion D: limitations of Mendel's model when applied to complex human traits.

Set A — Recall & Identify
State Mendel's Law of Segregation.
Easy
Answer

During gamete formation, the two alleles for each gene separate (segregate) so each gamete carries only one allele for each gene

This law is the foundation of all Mendelian genetics.
What is a Punnett square?
Easy
Answer

A grid used to predict the genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross

Invented by Reginald Punnett; each box shows a possible offspring genotype.
In a cross Tt × Tt, what is the expected genotypic ratio?
Easy
Answer

1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt

The genotypic ratio (1:2:1) differs from the phenotypic ratio (3:1) because TT and Tt both appear tall.
What is a test cross?
Easy
Answer

A cross between an individual of unknown genotype (showing dominant phenotype) with a homozygous recessive individual (tt) to determine the unknown genotype

If any offspring show recessive phenotype, the parent must be heterozygous (Tt).
What are gametes?
Easy
Answer

Reproductive cells (sperm and egg in animals; pollen and ovule in plants) that carry one allele for each gene

Gametes are haploid (n); they fuse at fertilisation to produce diploid (2n) zygotes.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A tall plant of unknown genotype is test-crossed. 50% offspring are tall, 50% short. What is the parent's genotype?
Medium
Answer

Tt (heterozygous) — Tt × tt gives 1 Tt (tall) : 1 tt (short)

If all offspring were tall, the parent would be TT; 50% short proves it is Tt.
In guinea pigs, black coat (B) is dominant over white (b). Two black guinea pigs produce a white offspring. What are the parents' genotypes?
Medium
Answer

Both parents are Bb; white offspring = bb; only Bb × Bb can produce bb offspring

The white offspring 'reveals' that both parents carried the recessive allele.
A cross produces 75% round seeds and 25% wrinkled seeds. What were the parental genotypes?
Medium
Answer

Both parents Rr (heterozygous); Rr × Rr → 3 round : 1 wrinkled

The 3:1 ratio in F2 is the hallmark of a monohybrid cross between two heterozygotes.
Explain why the observed ratio in a small sample may differ from the expected 3:1 ratio.
Hard
Answer

Statistical fluctuation (chance) — the 3:1 ratio is a probability, not a guarantee; small samples show larger deviations; large samples closely match theoretical ratios

Mendel counted thousands of plants, which is why his ratios matched theory so well.
Draw a Punnett square for a cross between Tt × tt. List offspring genotypes and phenotypes.
Medium
Answer

Tt × tt → 1/2 Tt (tall), 1/2 tt (short); phenotypic ratio 1 tall : 1 short

This 1:1 ratio is the result of a cross between a heterozygote and a homozygous recessive.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: In a population, 1 in 4 plants is short (recessive). What does this tell you about the parents and about allele frequencies in the population?
Hard
Answer

Parents were both Tt (heterozygous); 25% recessive offspring confirms 1:2:1 genotype ratio; allele frequency t = 50% in parental population

MYP Criterion C: using phenotypic data to infer genotypic information and population genetics.
Evaluate: Farmers use selective breeding based on the Law of Segregation. What are the benefits and risks of eliminating recessive alleles?
Hard
Answer

Benefit: eliminate genetic diseases in livestock; Risk: loss of genetic diversity (recessive alleles may be beneficial in heterozygotes or future environments)

MYP Criterion D: ethical and ecological implications of genetic management.

Set A — Recall & Identify
State Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment.
Easy
Answer

The alleles of different genes assort independently of each other during gamete formation — provided the genes are on different chromosomes

This law explains why the inheritance of one trait does not affect the inheritance of another (usually).
What is a dihybrid cross?
Easy
Answer

A cross between parents differing in two traits — e.g., RRYY × rryy

Dihybrid crosses test whether two traits are inherited independently.
What is the expected phenotypic ratio from a dihybrid cross (RrYy × RrYy)?
Easy
Answer

9:3:3:1 (9 round yellow : 3 round green : 3 wrinkled yellow : 1 wrinkled green)

This 9:3:3:1 ratio is the hallmark of independent assortment of two heterozygous traits.
What are the four gamete types produced by a RrYy individual?
Easy
Answer

RY, Ry, rY, ry — in equal proportions (25% each)

Independent assortment creates all combinations of alleles in gametes.
When does independent assortment NOT apply?
Easy
Answer

When two genes are on the same chromosome (linked genes) — they tend to be inherited together unless crossing over separates them

Gene linkage is the exception to Mendel's second law — discovered after Mendel's death.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
In a dihybrid cross, what fraction of offspring will be homozygous recessive for both traits?
Medium
Answer

1/16 (rryy)

In the 16-square Punnett grid, only 1 square gives rryy.
Two plants, both RrYy, are crossed. How many of 160 offspring would you expect to be round yellow?
Medium
Answer

9/16 × 160 = 90 plants

9:3:3:1 ratio means 9/16 are round yellow in a dihybrid cross.
Explain why linked genes don't follow the 9:3:3:1 ratio.
Hard
Answer

Linked genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together; only crossing over during meiosis separates them, reducing frequency of recombinant types

Morgan's work on Drosophila showed linkage and provided evidence for chromosomal theory of inheritance.
A plant heterozygous for both height (Tt) and colour (Gg) is test-crossed (ttgg). What is the expected phenotypic ratio?
Hard
Answer

1 TtGg : 1 Ttgg : 1 ttGg : 1 ttgg = 1:1:1:1 phenotypic ratio (all four types equally)

Test cross with dihybrid parent gives 1:1:1:1 — confirms independent assortment.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment only works for genes on different chromosomes. How does this limit its applicability to humans (46 chromosomes, ~20,000 genes)?
Hard
Answer

Humans have far more genes than chromosomes — most genes are linked in groups; true independent assortment is the exception, not the rule in humans

MYP Criterion D: understanding the scope and limitations of a scientific law.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is incomplete dominance?
Easy
Answer

When neither allele is completely dominant — the heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype blending both

Example: red flower (RR) × white flower (WW) → pink flower (RW) in snapdragons.
What is codominance?
Easy
Answer

Both alleles are fully expressed simultaneously in the heterozygote — neither is dominant over the other

Example: AB blood type — both A and B antigens are expressed on red blood cells.
Give an example of incomplete dominance in plants.
Easy
Answer

Snapdragon flower colour: RR = red, WW = white, RW = pink (intermediate)

In incomplete dominance, the heterozygote is a 'blend', not a dominant phenotype.
Explain the ABO blood group system in terms of codominance.
Easy
Answer

I^A and I^B are codominant (both expressed); i is recessive; AB type = I^A I^B (both antigens); O type = ii (no antigen)

Multiple allele system: three alleles (I^A, I^B, i) control four blood types.
What are the possible blood types of children born to an AB parent and an O parent?
Easy
Answer

AB × ii: I^A i (blood type A) or I^B i (blood type B) — 50% type A, 50% type B

Neither A nor B allele is lost — each child inherits one from the AB parent.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
In snapdragons, RW × RW. What are the offspring phenotype ratios?
Medium
Answer

1 RR (red) : 2 RW (pink) : 1 WW (white) = 1:2:1

Incomplete dominance gives 1:2:1 phenotypic ratio (same as genotypic ratio) because het shows intermediate.
Parents are blood types A (I^A i) and B (I^B i). List all possible offspring blood types and probabilities.
Hard
Answer

I^A I^B (AB, 25%), I^A i (A, 25%), I^B i (B, 25%), ii (O, 25%) — all four types possible

This cross can produce all four ABO blood types — a common exam and forensics question.
Explain why an O-type mother cannot have an AB-type child, regardless of the father's blood type.
Hard
Answer

O-type mother = ii; she can only donate i allele; AB type requires I^A I^B — impossible to get both from a father when mother contributes only i

Paternity testing can use blood type to exclude (but not confirm) parentage.
Why does incomplete dominance appear to 'blend' traits but the alleles themselves don't blend?
Hard
Answer

The alleles remain separate (Mendel's segregation law holds); each R allele codes for less pigment than two R alleles — intermediate colour from intermediate protein amount, not blended DNA

The genotype is discrete; the phenotype appears to blend because of protein quantity.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Blood type matching is critical for transfusions. Explain why O-negative is the universal donor and AB-positive is the universal recipient.
Hard
Answer

O-neg: no ABO antigens (no antibody reaction) + no Rh antigen; AB-pos: has all antibodies tolerated + can accept Rh+ blood

MYP Criterion D: medical application of genetics — understanding immune basis of blood compatibility.

Set A — Recall & Identify
How is biological sex determined in humans?
Easy
Answer

By sex chromosomes: females are XX, males are XY; the sperm (carrying X or Y) determines the sex of the offspring

The father determines the biological sex of a child — X-bearing sperm → girl; Y-bearing sperm → boy.
What are sex chromosomes?
Easy
Answer

The 23rd pair of chromosomes that determine biological sex: XX (female) or XY (male)

The other 22 pairs are autosomes — identical in males and females.
What is a sex-linked trait?
Easy
Answer

A trait controlled by a gene located on the sex chromosomes (usually the X chromosome)

X-linked traits are more commonly expressed in males because they have only one X chromosome.
Why are males more likely to be colour-blind than females?
Easy
Answer

Colour blindness gene is on the X chromosome; males (XY) only need one recessive allele; females need two (one on each X) to show the trait

Males have no second X to mask a recessive X-linked allele.
What is a carrier female?
Easy
Answer

A female who has one normal and one recessive X-linked allele (X^N X^c) — does not show the trait but can pass it to sons

Carrier mothers pass the affected X to 50% of their sons, who then show the condition.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A colour-blind father and a carrier mother have children. What are the probabilities for sons and daughters?
Hard
Answer

Sons: 50% colour-blind (X^c Y), 50% normal (X^N Y); Daughters: 50% carrier (X^N X^c), 50% colour-blind (X^c X^c)

The father's X^c goes to all daughters; mother's alleles split equally.
A normal-vision mother (carrier) has a colour-blind son. Can the son's father be normal-visioned? Explain.
Medium
Answer

Yes — the son's colour blindness comes from his mother's X^c; his father's Y chromosome carries no colour-vision gene to counteract it

X-linked traits in males come entirely from the mother's X chromosome.
Explain why biological sex is determined by the father, not the mother, in humans.
Medium
Answer

Mother always contributes X; father contributes either X (daughter) or Y (son); so only the father's gamete type determines sex

All human eggs carry X; it is the sperm that is variable.
Haemophilia A is X-linked recessive. A normal-vision male marries a carrier female. What fraction of their daughters will be carriers?
Hard
Answer

50% of daughters will be carriers (X^N X^h); 50% will be normal (X^N X^N)

The father contributes X^N to all daughters; mother contributes X^N or X^h with equal probability.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Some cultures selectively value male children. How does genetics refute the idea that mothers determine the child's sex?
Hard
Answer

Males have XY; females XX; mother only contributes X (same allele always); father contributes X or Y randomly — biologically, sex is determined by chance at fertilisation, not maternal choice

MYP Criterion D: science addressing cultural misconceptions — a socially important application of genetics.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is DNA?
Easy
Answer

Deoxyribonucleic acid — the molecule that carries genetic information in living organisms; a double helix of two complementary nucleotide strands

DNA is found in the nucleus (chromosomes) and in mitochondria/chloroplasts.
Name the four DNA bases and their pairing rules.
Easy
Answer

Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T); Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C)

Complementary base pairing holds the two strands together and enables replication/transcription.
What is a nucleotide?
Easy
Answer

The monomer of DNA: a phosphate group + deoxyribose sugar + one of four nitrogenous bases

DNA is a polymer of nucleotides; the sequence of bases encodes genetic information.
What is a gene?
Easy
Answer

A specific sequence of DNA bases that codes for a functional product (usually a protein)

Humans have approximately 20,000 protein-coding genes distributed across 46 chromosomes.
Describe the structure of the DNA double helix.
Easy
Answer

Two antiparallel strands twisted into a helix; sugar-phosphate backbone on outside; complementary bases paired inside; held by hydrogen bonds between base pairs

Watson and Crick proposed this structure in 1953 based on Franklin's X-ray data.
What does 'antiparallel' mean in the context of DNA?
Easy
Answer

The two strands run in opposite directions: one 5'→3', the other 3'→5'

Antiparallel orientation is required for complementary base pairing across the double helix.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
If one DNA strand is 5'-AATGCCT-3', what is the complementary strand?
Medium
Answer

3'-TTACGGA-5' (or 5'-AGGCATT-3')

Apply base pairing rules: A-T, T-A, G-C, C-G and reverse the direction.
Explain how Chargaff's rules supported the Watson-Crick model.
Hard
Answer

Chargaff found: %A = %T and %G = %C in any organism's DNA → implies A pairs with T, G pairs with C → double-stranded complementary structure

Chargaff's rules provided critical biochemical evidence for the double helix model.
Why is the base sequence in DNA significant?
Medium
Answer

The sequence of bases (codons in sets of 3) encodes the sequence of amino acids in proteins — genetic information is stored as base sequence

Just as letters form words and sentences, base sequences form codons and genes.
What is a mutation? How can a single base change affect a protein?
Hard
Answer

A mutation is a change in the DNA base sequence; a single base change can alter a codon, causing a wrong amino acid to be incorporated — potentially changing protein shape and function

Sickle cell anaemia: one base change (A→T) in haemoglobin gene → one amino acid change → misfolded protein → disease.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography was crucial for discovering DNA structure, yet Watson and Crick received the Nobel Prize. Discuss the ethics.
Hard
Answer

Franklin's Photo 51 was used without explicit permission; her contribution was undervalued in publications; Nobel not awarded posthumously and Watson later acknowledged the oversight

MYP Criterion D: ethics in science — intellectual credit, gender bias in 1950s research community.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is the central dogma of molecular biology?
Easy
Answer

DNA → (transcription) → mRNA → (translation) → Protein

This describes the one-way flow of genetic information in living systems (with some exceptions in retroviruses).
What is DNA replication?
Easy
Answer

The process by which a DNA double helix is copied to produce two identical double helix molecules — each containing one original and one new strand (semiconservative)

Replication ensures each daughter cell receives a complete copy of the genome during cell division.
What is transcription?
Easy
Answer

The process of copying a gene's DNA sequence into a complementary mRNA molecule in the nucleus

mRNA (messenger RNA) carries the genetic information from nucleus to ribosome.
What is translation?
Easy
Answer

The process of reading the mRNA codons at the ribosome and assembling amino acids into a protein in the correct sequence

tRNA brings specific amino acids matching each codon; ribosome joins them into a polypeptide chain.
What is a codon?
Easy
Answer

A sequence of three consecutive mRNA bases that codes for a specific amino acid or a stop signal

There are 64 codons (4³) coding for 20 amino acids — the genetic code is redundant (multiple codons per amino acid).
Set B — Apply & Analyse
If a DNA template strand is 3'-TACGGA-5', what is the mRNA produced and the first two amino acids?
Hard
Answer

mRNA: 5'-AUGCCU-3'; AUG = methionine (start codon); CCU = proline; first two amino acids: Met-Pro

Transcription uses complementary base pairing (T→A becomes A→U in RNA); translation reads codons.
Explain why DNA replication is described as 'semiconservative'.
Medium
Answer

Each new DNA molecule contains one original (conserved) strand and one newly synthesised strand — proven by Meselson-Stahl experiment using heavy nitrogen

Semiconservative replication preserves genetic information accurately across generations.
What is the role of tRNA in translation?
Medium
Answer

tRNA has an anticodon complementary to the mRNA codon AND carries the matching amino acid — acts as the 'adapter' between codon language and amino acid language

Without tRNA, the ribosome cannot convert the base sequence into an amino acid sequence.
A mutation changes AUG to UUG in the start codon of an mRNA. What is the likely effect?
Hard
Answer

Translation cannot begin (no start codon) — no protein is made from that mRNA → likely loss of function

The AUG start codon is universal; changing it prevents ribosome recognition of the start site.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing can change specific DNA sequences. What are the potential benefits and ethical concerns?
Hard
Answer

Benefits: cure genetic diseases (sickle cell, beta-thalassaemia), improve crops, study gene function; Concerns: germline editing, off-target effects, 'designer babies', equity of access

MYP Criterion D: biotechnology ethics — transformative potential balanced against risks and equity.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is a mutation?
Easy
Answer

A change in the DNA base sequence of an organism

Mutations can occur spontaneously or be caused by mutagens (radiation, chemicals, viruses).
Name three types of gene mutations.
Easy
Answer

Substitution (one base changed), Insertion (base added), Deletion (base removed)

Insertions and deletions cause frameshift mutations — all subsequent codons are misread.
What is a frameshift mutation?
Easy
Answer

A mutation caused by insertion or deletion of a base — it shifts the reading frame, changing all subsequent codons downstream

Frameshift mutations are usually more severe than substitutions because they alter many amino acids.
What is a mutagen? Give two examples.
Easy
Answer

An agent that increases the rate of mutation: UV radiation (sunlight), X-rays, tobacco smoke chemicals, certain dyes

Mutagens cause DNA damage or errors in replication — many are carcinogens.
What is the difference between somatic and germline mutations?
Easy
Answer

Somatic: in body cells — not inherited, may cause cancer; Germline: in sex cells (sperm/egg) — inherited by offspring

Only germline mutations are passed to the next generation.
What is Down syndrome, and what is its chromosomal cause?
Easy
Answer

Trisomy 21 — three copies of chromosome 21 instead of two; caused by non-disjunction during meiosis

Non-disjunction: chromosomes fail to separate properly during meiosis I or II.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
A silent mutation changes GCU to GCC. Both code for alanine. Why might it still have effects?
Hard
Answer

Silent mutations don't change amino acid — usually no effect; however, may affect mRNA folding, splicing signals, or translation speed — can alter gene expression subtly

'Silent' is a simplification — gene expression involves more than just amino acid sequence.
Explain how UV radiation causes skin cancer.
Hard
Answer

UV causes thymine dimers (adjacent T bases bond to each other) — distorts DNA structure → incorrect replication → mutations in tumour suppressor genes → uncontrolled cell division

This is why sunscreen (blocking UV) reduces skin cancer risk.
Non-disjunction at meiosis I vs meiosis II — which produces more abnormal gametes?
Hard
Answer

Non-disjunction at meiosis I: ALL gametes are abnormal (none has the right chromosome number); Meiosis II: only 2 of 4 gametes are abnormal

Meiosis I non-disjunction is more severe because the chromosome number error affects the entire cell.
What is the difference between a chromosomal mutation and a gene mutation?
Medium
Answer

Gene mutation: change in base sequence of one gene; Chromosomal mutation: change in number or structure of whole chromosomes (deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation, non-disjunction)

Chromosomal mutations affect large numbers of genes simultaneously.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Evaluate: Radiation therapy for cancer deliberately causes mutations in cancer cells. Explain why this works and what side effects arise.
Hard
Answer

High-energy radiation damages DNA in rapidly dividing cells → cancer cells die; side effects: also damages normal rapidly dividing cells (hair follicles, gut lining, bone marrow) → hair loss, nausea, immunosuppression

MYP Criterion D: using mutation knowledge to understand cancer treatment trade-offs.
Investigate: Design a study to test whether a new chemical is mutagenic.
Hard
Answer

Ames test: expose bacteria (histidine-requiring mutant) to chemical; count colonies that revert to histidine independence — reversion rate indicates mutagenicity; use controls (no chemical, known mutagen)

MYP Criterion B: real scientific method for mutagenicity testing used in safety assessment of chemicals.

Set A — Recall & Identify
What is evolution?
Easy
Answer

The change in heritable characteristics of a population over successive generations

Evolution is driven by natural selection acting on genetic variation in a population.
What do fossils tell us about evolution?
Easy
Answer

Fossils record the sequence of past life forms, show intermediate forms (transitional fossils), and provide timeline of when organisms lived

The fossil record shows that life has changed over billions of years — older rocks have simpler organisms.
What are homologous organs?
Easy
Answer

Structures with the same basic anatomy (same evolutionary origin) but adapted for different functions — evidence for common ancestry

Example: human arm, bat wing, whale flipper, horse leg — all modified pentadactyl limbs.
What are analogous organs?
Easy
Answer

Structures with similar function but different evolutionary origin — evidence of convergent evolution, not common ancestry

Example: wings of birds and insects — both for flying but completely different in structure.
What does molecular evidence for evolution include?
Easy
Answer

DNA sequence comparisons, protein sequence similarities, same genetic code across all life, conserved genes (e.g., HOX genes)

More similar DNA sequences → more recent common ancestor; molecular clock measures evolutionary distance.
What is a vestigial organ?
Easy
Answer

A reduced, non-functional (or minimally functional) structure that was useful in an ancestor

Human examples: coccyx (tailbone), wisdom teeth, goosebumps (arrector pili), appendix.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Explain why the pentadactyl limb is evidence for evolution.
Medium
Answer

The same five-digit bone structure appears in humans, bats, whales, and horses — modified for different functions but same underlying anatomy → indicates common vertebrate ancestor

Structural homology is powerful evidence because the same blueprint is reused with modifications.
Compare fossil evidence and molecular evidence for evolution. Which is more reliable?
Hard
Answer

Fossil: direct but incomplete (only hard parts preserved, gaps exist); Molecular: complete genome available, more precise, but requires interpretation — both complement each other

Neither is perfect alone; convergent molecular evidence strengthens evolutionary conclusions.
Explain how vestigial organs support evolution.
Medium
Answer

Vestigial organs are functional in ancestral species but reduced in descendants — they are 'evolutionary leftovers' showing descent with modification

The coccyx shows humans descended from tailed ancestors; wisdom teeth from species with larger jaw.
Why do scientists consider humans and chimpanzees to have a common ancestor, despite obvious differences?
Hard
Answer

DNA similarity ~98-99%; shared genes, proteins, chromosomal structure, developmental genes; human chromosome 2 = fusion of two chimpanzee chromosomes — genetic evidence of divergence from common ancestor 5-7 MYA

Multiple independent lines of evidence (molecular, anatomical, fossil) all point to the same conclusion.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: Compare the amino acid sequence of cytochrome c protein in humans, chimps, and yeast. What would differences tell you about evolutionary distance?
Hard
Answer

More amino acid differences = more evolutionary distance; human-chimp share nearly identical cytochrome c; human-yeast differ at ~40 positions — reflects divergence time from common ancestor

Molecular clocks use mutation rate to estimate when species diverged.
Evaluate: Creationism and evolution are sometimes presented as alternatives. How should scientific evidence be used to evaluate claims about origins?
Hard
Answer

Science evaluates claims using testable, falsifiable evidence from multiple independent lines (fossils, DNA, comparative anatomy, biogeography); evolution is supported by overwhelming convergent evidence — not a matter of opinion

Set A — Recall & Identify
State Darwin's four postulates of natural selection.
Easy
Answer

1. Variation exists in populations; 2. Some variation is heritable; 3. More offspring produced than survive; 4. Individuals with favourable traits survive and reproduce more (differential reproduction)

These four statements form the logical basis of natural selection.
What is natural selection?
Easy
Answer

The process by which individuals with favourable heritable traits survive and reproduce more successfully, passing those traits to offspring

Over generations, favourable traits increase in frequency; the population becomes better adapted.
What is meant by 'survival of the fittest'?
Easy
Answer

'Fittest' means best adapted to the current environment — not strongest; the fittest survive to reproduce, passing on their advantageous traits

Coined by Herbert Spencer; Darwin used 'natural selection'; fitness is defined by reproductive success.
What is an adaptation?
Easy
Answer

A heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness (survival and reproductive success) in its environment

Adaptations can be structural (webbed feet), physiological (high-altitude haemoglobin), or behavioural (migration).
What is speciation?
Easy
Answer

The process by which new species arise from existing species through accumulated genetic changes over time

Geographic isolation often starts speciation — populations separated by barriers evolve independently.
What is artificial selection?
Easy
Answer

Selective breeding by humans — choosing individuals with desired traits to reproduce — the same principle as natural selection but directed by humans

Dog breeds, crop plants, and livestock are products of thousands of years of artificial selection.
Set B — Apply & Analyse
Explain antibiotic resistance in bacteria using the theory of natural selection.
Hard
Answer

Random mutations create resistant bacteria; antibiotic kills susceptible bacteria; resistant bacteria survive, reproduce → population becomes increasingly resistant over generations

Classic example of evolution in real time — a major public health crisis driven by natural selection.
Darwin observed finches on different Galapagos islands with different beak shapes. Explain this in terms of natural selection.
Hard
Answer

Ancestral finches colonised islands; each island had different food sources; different beak shapes gave feeding advantages; selection favoured different traits on different islands → divergent evolution

Darwin's finches are a textbook example of adaptive radiation driven by natural selection.
Explain why evolution by natural selection can only act on heritable variations, not acquired traits.
Hard
Answer

Acquired characteristics (muscles built by exercise) are not encoded in DNA and cannot be passed to offspring; only DNA mutations in gametes are heritable — Lamarck was wrong, Darwin correct

Lamarck's theory of inheritance of acquired characteristics was disproven by genetics.
What is the difference between convergent and divergent evolution?
Medium
Answer

Convergent: unrelated species develop similar traits due to similar environments (e.g., dolphin and shark shape); Divergent: related species develop different traits due to different environments (Darwin's finches)

Both are driven by natural selection but act in opposite directions — toward similarity or difference.
If a population of mice lives in a field that turns white in winter, what trait would be selected for and why?
Medium
Answer

White coat colouring — white mice are less visible to predators on snow; brown mice are eaten more → white mice survive and reproduce → white fur frequency increases

Camouflage is a classic example of natural selection acting on prey organisms.
Set C — Investigate & Reflect
Investigate: Peppered moths (dark and light forms) changed in proportion during the industrial revolution. Explain using natural selection.
Hard
Answer

Pre-industrialisation: light moths match light tree bark, camouflaged; dark moths visible to birds, selected against. Post-industrialisation: soot darkens trees; dark moths now camouflaged; light moths eaten more → dark moths increase in frequency

Biston betularia is a real-time documented example of industrial melanism — evolution by natural selection.
Evaluate: Human activities are accelerating the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria and pesticide-resistant insects. What does this tell us about the power of natural selection?
Hard
Answer

Intense selection pressure (antibiotics/pesticides) rapidly drives resistant genotypes to fixation; demonstrates natural selection acts measurably within years, not just millions of years; shows why antibiotic stewardship and resistance monitoring matter

MYP Criterion D: natural selection as a real-world process with urgent public health consequences.
Reflect: Darwin's theory required courage to publish — it challenged religious and cultural norms. What does this tell us about the relationship between science and society?
Hard
Answer

Scientific truth may conflict with prevailing beliefs; scientists face social pressure; empirical evidence ultimately prevails over cultural resistance; science progresses through evidence, peer review, and willingness to challenge assumptions

MYP Criterion D: the nature of science in society — how paradigm-shifting ideas develop and gain acceptance.
