Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

Define electric current and state its SI unit.
Show Answer

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor: I = Q/t. Its SI unit is the ampere (A), where 1 A = 1 coulomb per second.

Easy
Define electric potential difference (voltage) between two points.
Show Answer

Potential difference (V) between two points is the work done per unit charge to move a positive charge from one point to the other: V = W/Q. Its SI unit is the volt (V), where 1 V = 1 joule per coulomb.

Easy
State Ohm's Law.
Show Answer

Ohm's Law: At constant temperature and physical conditions, the current (I) through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) across it: V = IR, where R is the resistance of the conductor.

Easy
What is the SI unit of resistance? Define it.
Show Answer

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm (Ω). 1 Ω is defined as the resistance of a conductor through which a current of 1 A flows when a potential difference of 1 V is applied across it.

Easy
If a charge of 60 C passes through a wire in 2 minutes, calculate the current.
Show Answer

I = Q/t = 60 C / 120 s = 0.5 A.

Easy
Name the instrument used to measure (a) current and (b) potential difference in a circuit.
Show Answer

(a) Ammeter — measures current; connected in series. (b) Voltmeter — measures potential difference; connected in parallel across the component.

Easy
What does a V–I graph for an ohmic conductor look like? What does the slope represent?
Show Answer

For an ohmic conductor, the V–I graph is a straight line through the origin. The slope (gradient) = V/I = resistance R in ohms. A steeper slope means higher resistance.

Easy
A resistor has a resistance of 20 Ω and a current of 0.5 A flows through it. What is the voltage across it?
Show Answer

V = IR = 0.5 × 20 = 10 V.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

Distinguish between conventional current direction and the actual direction of electron flow.
Show Answer

Conventional current is defined as flowing from the positive terminal to the negative terminal of a source (high potential to low potential) — the direction a positive charge would move. In reality, electrons flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal (opposite direction). Conventional current was established before the electron was discovered and remains standard in circuit analysis.

Medium
A torch bulb has a resistance of 12 Ω when operating. If connected to a 3 V battery, find the current and the charge flowing in 5 minutes.
Show Answer

I = V/R = 3/12 = 0.25 A. Q = It = 0.25 × 300 = 75 C.

Medium
Explain why an ammeter must have very low resistance and a voltmeter must have very high resistance.
Show Answer

An ammeter is connected in series — if its resistance were high, it would significantly reduce the current it is trying to measure (disturb the circuit). Very low resistance ensures minimal voltage drop across it. A voltmeter is connected in parallel — if its resistance were low, it would draw significant current and change the circuit's behaviour. Very high resistance ensures negligible current through it, so the measured voltage remains accurate.

Medium
The V–I graph of two resistors A and B shows that A has a steeper slope. Which has greater resistance and why?
Show Answer

In a V–I graph (V on y-axis, I on x-axis), slope = V/I = R. A steeper slope means a larger R/I ratio — greater resistance. So resistor A has greater resistance. For the same current, A requires a higher voltage, confirming higher R.

Medium
Why does the resistance of a tungsten filament lamp not obey Ohm's Law strictly? What shape is its V–I graph?
Show Answer

As current increases, the filament heats up significantly (to ~2500°C in operation). Resistance of metals increases with temperature. So the ratio V/I is not constant — it increases. The V–I graph is a non-linear curve (concave upward when V is plotted against I), not a straight line. The lamp is a non-ohmic device.

Medium
A student connects a 6 V battery to a circuit and measures a current of 0.3 A. Later the same battery shows only 5.4 V when connected. Explain this observation in terms of internal resistance.
Show Answer

A real battery has internal resistance (r). When current flows, there is a voltage drop V_internal = Ir across the internal resistance. Terminal voltage = EMF − Ir = 6 − (0.3 × r). If terminal voltage = 5.4 V: 5.4 = 6 − 0.3r → r = 0.6/0.3 = 2 Ω. The internal resistance reduces the available terminal voltage under load.

Medium
Calculate the work done in moving a charge of 5 C through a potential difference of 12 V.
Show Answer

W = QV = 5 × 12 = 60 J. This is the energy transferred to the charges by the electric field as they move through 12 V.

Medium
Explain why it is incorrect to say "current is used up" in a resistor. What actually happens?
Show Answer

Current is the flow of charge — charge is conserved (cannot be created or destroyed). The same number of electrons that enter a resistor must exit it (Kirchhoff's current law). What is "used up" is electrical energy — electrons lose potential energy as they drift through the resistor, which is converted to thermal energy (heat). The current magnitude is unchanged.

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

A student plots V–I graphs for three devices: a resistor, a filament lamp, and a diode. Describe and explain the shape of each graph.
Show Answer

Resistor: straight line through origin — constant R, ohmic. Filament lamp: curve bending toward V-axis (slope increases with I) — resistance increases as temperature rises with current; non-ohmic. Diode: almost zero current for reverse/low forward voltage, then exponential rise — conducts only in one direction above threshold voltage (~0.7 V for silicon); highly non-ohmic. Each shape reflects the physical mechanism of the device.

Hard
Using MYP Criterion B, design an experiment to verify Ohm's Law for a resistor, including circuit diagram description, variables, and expected results.
Show Answer

Aim: Verify V ∝ I at constant temperature. Circuit: Battery, rheostat (variable resistor), ammeter (series), resistor under test, voltmeter (parallel across resistor), switch. Method: Vary rheostat to change current; record V and I for at least 6 values. Variables: Current I (independent, varied by rheostat), voltage V (dependent, measured by voltmeter), temperature/resistor identity (controlled). Analysis: Plot V vs I — should be a straight line through origin. Slope = R. Expected: Linear graph confirms Ohm's Law.

Hard
Derive the expression V = IR from the definition of resistance and potential difference.
Show Answer

Resistance R is defined as the ratio of potential difference to current: R = V/I (for ohmic conductors at constant temperature). Rearranging: V = IR. Physically: V (in volts) is the energy per unit charge; I (in amperes) is charge per unit time; R quantifies how much voltage is needed to drive unit current. The product IR gives the voltage required to push current I through resistance R.

Hard
A circuit has a battery of EMF 9 V and internal resistance 1 Ω connected to an external resistance of 8 Ω. Find the current, terminal voltage, and power lost internally.
Show Answer

I = EMF/(R + r) = 9/(8 + 1) = 1 A. Terminal voltage = EMF − Ir = 9 − 1×1 = 8 V. Power lost internally = I²r = 1² × 1 = 1 W.

Hard
Explain microscopically (using the drift velocity model) why resistance increases with temperature in metals.
Show Answer

In metals, current is carried by free electrons drifting under an electric field. At higher temperature, the metal ions vibrate more vigorously with greater amplitude. This increases the frequency of collisions between drifting electrons and the lattice ions, reducing the drift velocity for the same applied field. Lower drift velocity means lower current for the same voltage — hence higher resistance. R ∝ (1/mean free path between collisions).

Hard
Two identical resistors can be connected in series or in parallel to a fixed battery. In which arrangement does more current flow from the battery? Justify using Ohm's Law.
Show Answer

Let each resistor = R. Series: R_total = 2R → I_series = V/2R. Parallel: R_total = R/2 → I_parallel = V/(R/2) = 2V/R. Ratio: I_parallel/I_series = (2V/R)/(V/2R) = 4 times more current in parallel. Parallel combination has lower total resistance, so by Ohm's Law (I = V/R), more current flows from the battery.

Hard
The charge on an electron is 1.6 × 10^-19 C. If a current of 2 A flows through a wire, calculate the number of electrons passing a cross-section per second.
Show Answer

Q per second = I = 2 C/s. Number of electrons = Q/e = 2 / (1.6 × 10^-19) = 1.25 × 10^19 electrons per second. This enormous number explains why macroscopic currents appear continuous despite being discrete electron flows.

Hard
Analyse: a thermistor (NTC type) has resistance that decreases with temperature. How would its V–I graph differ from an ohmic resistor? Give a practical application.
Show Answer

As current through the NTC thermistor increases, it heats up → resistance decreases → for the same voltage increment, current increases faster. The V–I graph curves toward the I-axis (slope V/I decreases as I increases) — opposite to the tungsten lamp. Application: NTC thermistors are used in temperature sensors (fever thermometers, engine temperature monitoring) — as temperature rises, resistance falls measurably, providing an electrical signal proportional to temperature.

Resistance & Resistivity

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

State the formula relating resistance to length and cross-sectional area of a conductor.
Show Answer

R = ρL/A, where R = resistance (Ω), ρ = resistivity of the material (Ω·m), L = length of conductor (m), A = cross-sectional area (m²).

Easy
Define resistivity (specific resistance) and state its SI unit.
Show Answer

Resistivity (ρ) is the resistance of a unit length conductor of unit cross-sectional area of a given material. It is a material property, independent of shape. SI unit: ohm-metre (Ω·m).

Easy
How does resistance change if the length of a wire is doubled (keeping area constant)?
Show Answer

R = ρL/A. If L is doubled, R doubles. Resistance is directly proportional to length. Doubling the length doubles the resistance.

Easy
How does resistance change if the cross-sectional area of a wire is doubled (keeping length constant)?
Show Answer

R = ρL/A. If A is doubled, R is halved. Resistance is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area. A thicker wire has lower resistance.

Easy
Which material has lower resistivity — copper or rubber? What does this mean in practice?
Show Answer

Copper has very low resistivity (~1.7 × 10^-8 Ω·m). Rubber has very high resistivity (~10^13 Ω·m). In practice, copper wires conduct electricity with minimal energy loss; rubber is used as insulation because almost no current flows through it.

Easy
What is the effect of temperature on the resistance of (a) metals and (b) semiconductors?
Show Answer

(a) Metals: resistance increases with temperature — more lattice vibrations impede electron flow. (b) Semiconductors (e.g., silicon, thermistors): resistance decreases with temperature — more charge carriers are freed at higher temperatures, increasing conductivity.

Easy
A wire of length 2 m and cross-sectional area 0.5 × 10^-6 m² has a resistivity of 1.6 × 10^-8 Ω·m. Calculate its resistance.
Show Answer

R = ρL/A = (1.6 × 10^-8 × 2) / (0.5 × 10^-6) = 3.2 × 10^-8 / 5 × 10^-7 = 0.064 Ω.

Easy
Name two materials used for making resistors (high resistance) and two for making connecting wires (low resistance).
Show Answer

High resistance (resistors): nichrome, manganin (also used for heating elements). Low resistance (connecting wires): copper, aluminium — chosen for their very low resistivity and good conductivity.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

A copper wire and a nichrome wire have the same length and cross-section. Which has higher resistance and why? Give approximate values of resistivity.
Show Answer

Nichrome has higher resistance. Resistivity of copper ≈ 1.7 × 10^-8 Ω·m; nichrome ≈ 1.1 × 10^-6 Ω·m — about 65 times greater. For the same geometry (same L and A), R = ρL/A, so nichrome's resistance is ~65× that of copper. Nichrome's high resistivity is why it is used in heating elements.

Medium
A wire is stretched to double its original length. Assuming its volume remains constant, how does its resistance change?
Show Answer

Volume = L × A = constant. If L doubles, A halves. R = ρL/A. New R = ρ(2L)/(A/2) = 4ρL/A = 4 times original resistance. Stretching a wire to double its length increases resistance by a factor of 4 (R ∝ L²/V for constant volume).

Medium
Explain why thick wires are used for high-current appliances like electric kettles and thin wires for low-current devices.
Show Answer

R = ρL/A. Thick wire has larger A → lower R. Lower R means less voltage drop and less heat dissipated (P = I²R) for high currents. For a kettle drawing 10 A, even a small resistance in the supply wire would waste significant power as heat and create a fire risk. Thin wires suffice for low-current devices (e.g., phone chargers at 0.5 A) where the power loss I²R is negligible.

Medium
Two wires A and B are made of the same material. Wire A has twice the length and twice the diameter of wire B. Compare their resistances.
Show Answer

R = ρL/A. Area = π(d/2)². Wire A: L_A = 2L, d_A = 2d → A_A = π(d)² = 4πd²/4. Wire B: L_B = L, A_B = πd²/4. R_A/R_B = (L_A/A_A)/(L_B/A_B) = (2L/4A_B)/(L/A_B) = 2/4 = 1/2. R_A = R_B/2 — Wire A has half the resistance of wire B.

Medium
Explain the concept of resistivity at the atomic level — why do different materials have different resistivities?
Show Answer

Resistivity depends on: (1) Number of free charge carriers — metals have a sea of free electrons (low ρ); insulators have very few (high ρ); semiconductors are intermediate. (2) Mean free path between collisions — determined by the atomic structure and lattice imperfections. Alloys have more lattice disorder (higher ρ) than pure metals. Resistivity is an intrinsic material property reflecting these atomic-scale interactions.

Medium
A student wants to make a 10 Ω resistor using nichrome wire (ρ = 1.1 × 10^-6 Ω·m) with cross-sectional area 0.1 mm². Calculate the required length.
Show Answer

A = 0.1 mm² = 0.1 × 10^-6 m² = 10^-7 m². L = RA/ρ = (10 × 10^-7) / (1.1 × 10^-6) = 10^-6 / 1.1 × 10^-6 = 0.91 m ≈ 91 cm.

Medium
Why is manganin (an alloy) preferred over copper for making standard resistors used in laboratories?
Show Answer

Manganin has a very low temperature coefficient of resistance — its resistance barely changes with temperature. Copper's resistance changes significantly with temperature, so a copper standard resistor would give different readings at different room temperatures. Manganin also has a higher resistivity than copper, making compact resistors feasible. These properties make manganin standard resistors stable and precise.

Medium
Using MYP Criterion B approach, describe how you would experimentally determine the resistivity of nichrome wire in the school lab.
Show Answer

Materials: Nichrome wire, metre rule, micrometer screw gauge, ammeter, voltmeter, rheostat, battery. Method: Measure wire diameter (d) using micrometer (3 positions, average). Connect wire in circuit; measure V and I for a fixed length. Calculate R = V/I. Vary length L from 20 cm to 100 cm; record R for each L. Analysis: Plot R vs L — straight line through origin. Slope = ρ/A. Calculate A = π(d/2)². ρ = slope × A. Variables: L (independent), R (dependent), material/temperature/cross-section (controlled).

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

Derive the formula R = ρL/A from first principles using the microscopic model of current flow.
Show Answer

Current I = nAev_d (n = free electrons per volume, A = area, e = electron charge, v_d = drift velocity). Drift velocity v_d = eEτ/m (τ = mean collision time, E = electric field, m = electron mass). E = V/L. So I = nA·e·(eVτ)/(mL) = nAe²τV/(mL). Therefore V/I = mL/(nAe²τ) = R. Comparing with R = ρL/A: ρ = m/(ne²τ). This shows resistivity depends on carrier density n and collision time τ — a material property, independent of shape.

Hard
A resistor has resistance R₀ at 0°C. At temperature T°C, R = R₀(1 + αT). For copper, α = 3.9 × 10^-3 /°C. Calculate the resistance of a 10 Ω copper resistor at 100°C.
Show Answer

R = R₀(1 + αT) = 10 × (1 + 3.9 × 10^-3 × 100) = 10 × (1 + 0.39) = 10 × 1.39 = 13.9 Ω. A 39% increase from 0°C to 100°C — significant for precision applications.

Hard
A wire is cut into three equal pieces and then connected in parallel. What is the combined resistance compared to the original wire?
Show Answer

Original resistance = R. Each piece: length = L/3 → resistance = R/3. Three pieces in parallel: 1/R_total = 3/(R/3) + ... wait — 1/R_total = 1/(R/3) + 1/(R/3) + 1/(R/3) = 3/(R/3) = 9/R. R_total = R/9. The combined resistance is R/9 — one ninth of the original.

Hard
Superconductors have zero resistivity below a critical temperature. Explain the implications for power transmission and why this has not yet been fully implemented worldwide.
Show Answer

Zero resistivity means no ohmic loss (P = I²R = 0) in power lines — all generated electricity reaches consumers. Today, transmission losses are 5–15% globally. Implications: massive energy savings, reduced carbon footprint, smaller cables. Barrier to implementation: current superconductors require cooling to near absolute zero (~−196°C for high-temperature superconductors with liquid nitrogen, or ~−269°C for conventional superconductors with liquid helium) — expensive and complex. Room-temperature superconductors are an active research frontier; once achieved, they would revolutionise power grids.

Hard
Wire A has length L and diameter d. Wire B (same material) has length 2L and diameter 2d. Compare their (a) resistance and (b) resistivity.
Show Answer

(a) R_A = ρL/A_A = ρL/(πd²/4). R_B = ρ(2L)/(π(2d)²/4) = ρ(2L)/(4πd²/4) = ρ(2L)/(πd²) = 2ρL/(πd²). R_B/R_A = [2ρL/(πd²)] / [4ρL/(πd²)] = 2/4 = 1/2. R_B = R_A/2. (b) Resistivity is identical for both — it is a material property, independent of dimensions. Same material → same ρ.

Hard
Evaluate why alloys (e.g., nichrome) have higher resistivity than their constituent pure metals (nickel and chromium separately).
Show Answer

In pure metals, the crystal lattice is regular and periodic — electrons flow with relatively few scatterings. In alloys, different atoms of different sizes create lattice distortions and imperfections. These irregularities increase the probability of electron-lattice collisions (reduce mean free path), effectively increasing resistivity. Additionally, alloying disrupts the band structure. This is the Matthiessen's rule effect: total resistivity = temperature-dependent term + impurity/alloy term.

Hard
A heating element must dissipate 1000 W at 230 V. Calculate the required resistance, and then find the length of nichrome wire (ρ = 1.1 × 10^-6 Ω·m, A = 0.2 mm²) needed.
Show Answer

R = V²/P = 230²/1000 = 52900/1000 = 52.9 Ω. A = 0.2 mm² = 0.2 × 10^-6 m². L = RA/ρ = (52.9 × 0.2 × 10^-6) / (1.1 × 10^-6) = (10.58 × 10^-6) / (1.1 × 10^-6) = 9.6 m. The nichrome wire is coiled tightly to fit in the appliance.

Hard
Using MYP Criterion D, discuss the environmental trade-offs in choosing aluminium vs. copper for overhead power transmission cables.
Show Answer

Copper: lower resistivity (less energy loss), smaller cable diameter for same current capacity, but heavier and more expensive. Aluminium: higher resistivity (~2.8 × 10^-8 vs ~1.7 × 10^-8 Ω·m for copper), but much lighter (density ~2700 vs ~8900 kg/m³), cheaper, and more abundant. Environmental trade-off: aluminium requires larger cross-section for same conductivity (more material, wider cables), but its lighter weight means less structural support needed (fewer/cheaper towers). Aluminium mining has high environmental impact; copper mining is also damaging. Most modern long-distance high-voltage lines use aluminium. MYP D: science-society trade-off between material efficiency, cost, and environmental damage.

Series Circuits

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

State three characteristics of a series circuit.
Show Answer

(1) The same current flows through all components. (2) The total voltage equals the sum of individual voltage drops: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃. (3) The total resistance equals the sum of individual resistances: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃.

Easy
Three resistors of 2 Ω, 3 Ω, and 5 Ω are connected in series. What is the total resistance?
Show Answer

R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ = 2 + 3 + 5 = 10 Ω.

Easy
In a series circuit with a 12 V battery and total resistance 6 Ω, find the current.
Show Answer

I = V/R = 12/6 = 2 A. This current is the same through every component in the series circuit.

Easy
What happens to the other bulbs in a series circuit if one bulb blows (breaks the circuit)?
Show Answer

All other bulbs go out (stop glowing). In a series circuit there is only one current path. A break anywhere in the circuit stops current flow everywhere — like old-style Christmas lights where one failed bulb darkened the whole string.

Easy
Does adding more resistors in series increase or decrease the total resistance?
Show Answer

Adding more resistors in series increases the total resistance. R_total = R₁ + R₂ + ... — each added resistor adds to the total, never reduces it. More resistance in series means less current flows (by Ohm's Law).

Easy
Two resistors of 4 Ω and 6 Ω are in series with a 20 V battery. Find the voltage drop across each.
Show Answer

R_total = 10 Ω. I = 20/10 = 2 A. V₁ = IR₁ = 2 × 4 = 8 V. V₂ = IR₂ = 2 × 6 = 12 V. Check: 8 + 12 = 20 V ✓

Easy
State one disadvantage of series circuits for household wiring.
Show Answer

If one device fails (open circuit), all devices in the circuit stop working. Also, each added device reduces the current available to all others (increases total resistance). Different appliances needing different voltages cannot be independently controlled. Household wiring uses parallel circuits to avoid these problems.

Easy
In which type of circuit is an ammeter connected — series or parallel? Why?
Show Answer

An ammeter is connected in series. This ensures the current being measured flows through the ammeter. It has very low resistance so it does not significantly affect the circuit current it is measuring.

Easy

Set B Apply & Explain (Medium)

Three resistors R₁ = 5 Ω, R₂ = 10 Ω, R₃ = 15 Ω are connected in series to a 30 V battery. Find (a) total resistance, (b) current, (c) voltage across each, (d) verify using Kirchhoff's voltage law.
Show Answer

(a) R_total = 5 + 10 + 15 = 30 Ω. (b) I = 30/30 = 1 A. (c) V₁ = 1×5 = 5 V; V₂ = 1×10 = 10 V; V₃ = 1×15 = 15 V. (d) 5 + 10 + 15 = 30 V = battery voltage ✓ (Kirchhoff's Voltage Law: sum of drops = source voltage).

Medium
Explain why the voltage drop across resistors in series is proportional to their resistance.
Show Answer

In series, the same current I flows through all resistors. Voltage drop V = IR. Since I is constant, V is directly proportional to R. A resistor with twice the resistance has twice the voltage drop. Mathematically: V₁/V₂ = R₁/R₂. This is the voltage divider principle — series resistors divide voltage in proportion to their resistances.

Medium
A student adds a fourth resistor in series to an existing circuit. Describe the effect on (a) total resistance, (b) current, (c) voltage across each original resistor.
Show Answer

(a) Total resistance increases (R_total = R₁+R₂+R₃+R₄). (b) Current decreases (I = V/R_total, same battery voltage). (c) Voltage across each original resistor decreases (V = IR; I is smaller, each R is the same). The added resistor "takes some voltage" from the others.

Medium
Why do the headlights and wipers of a car work independently of each other, suggesting they are not in series?
Show Answer

In a series circuit, all devices share the same current path and the same on/off state — one switch controls all, and a failure in one stops all. Car headlights and wipers work independently — you can have headlights on without wipers, and a blown headlight doesn't stop the wipers. This is only possible in a parallel circuit, where each device has its own branch and switch, independent of the others.

Medium
A circuit has two resistors in series: R₁ = 8 Ω and R₂ = unknown. The voltage across R₁ is 16 V and the battery is 24 V. Find R₂ and the current.
Show Answer

V_R2 = 24 − 16 = 8 V. I = V_R1/R₁ = 16/8 = 2 A (same current in series). R₂ = V_R2/I = 8/2 = 4 Ω.

Medium
State and apply Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) to a series circuit with a 9 V battery and three resistors showing drops of V₁, V₂, V₃.
Show Answer

KVL: The algebraic sum of all potential differences around a closed loop is zero (or: sum of voltage drops = sum of EMFs). For the series circuit: 9 = V₁ + V₂ + V₃. If R₁ = 1 Ω, R₂ = 2 Ω, R₃ = 6 Ω: I = 9/9 = 1 A; V₁ = 1 V, V₂ = 2 V, V₃ = 6 V; sum = 9 V = battery EMF ✓.

Medium
Explain the concept of the potential divider using two resistors in series. Give a practical application.
Show Answer

Two resistors R₁ and R₂ in series with a supply voltage V_s. Voltage across R₂: V_out = V_s × R₂/(R₁ + R₂). By choosing R₁ and R₂, any fraction of V_s can be obtained. Application: Volume control in audio circuits (potentiometer is a variable potential divider); sensor circuits where a thermistor or LDR forms one arm — as conditions change, V_out changes, providing a signal to a microcontroller.

Medium
Two bulbs rated 60 W/12 V and 30 W/12 V are connected in series with a 24 V supply. Which glows brighter? Why?
Show Answer

Resistance: R = V²/P. R₁ (60 W) = 144/60 = 2.4 Ω. R₂ (30 W) = 144/30 = 4.8 Ω. In series, same current flows; brighter bulb = more power = I²R = more voltage drop. R₂ is larger → more voltage across it → 30 W bulb (R₂) glows brighter in series, despite having lower power rating for its designed voltage. This is counterintuitive — in series, the higher-resistance (lower-rated) bulb dissipates more power.

Medium

Set C Analyse & Evaluate (Hard)

Prove that the total resistance of n identical resistors each of value R in series is nR, and show this is always greater than any individual resistance.
Show Answer

By definition: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + ... + Rₙ. For n identical resistors: R_total = R + R + ... (n times) = nR. Since n ≥ 1 and R > 0: nR ≥ R for all n ≥ 1. Equality holds only when n = 1 (single resistor). For n > 1, nR > R — the series combination is always larger than any individual resistance. Each added resistor provides additional opposition to current flow.

Hard
A circuit has a 12 V battery (internal resistance 0.5 Ω) connected to two external resistors in series: 3 Ω and 5.5 Ω. Find current, terminal voltage, power delivered to each external resistor, and efficiency of energy transfer.
Show Answer

Total R = 0.5 + 3 + 5.5 = 9 Ω. I = 12/9 = 1.33 A. Terminal voltage = 12 − 1.33 × 0.5 = 12 − 0.67 = 11.33 V. P₁ = I²×3 = 1.78 × 3 = 5.33 W. P₂ = I²×5.5 = 1.78 × 5.5 = 9.77 W. Total input power = 12 × 1.33 = 16 W. Useful power = 15.1 W. Efficiency = 15.1/16 = 94.4%.

Hard
In a series circuit, if R₁ is doubled while keeping the battery voltage constant, analyse the effect on (a) current, (b) voltage across R₂, (c) power dissipated by R₂.
Show Answer

Original: I = V/(R₁+R₂). New R₁' = 2R₁: I' = V/(2R₁+R₂) — current decreases. (b) V_R2 = I'R₂ = VR₂/(2R₁+R₂) — voltage across R₂ decreases (it was VR₂/(R₁+R₂)). (c) P_R2 = I'²R₂ = V²R₂/(2R₁+R₂)² — power dissipated by R₂ also decreases. Doubling R₁ reduces current everywhere and reduces energy delivered to R₂.

Hard
Old Christmas lights were wired in series. Explain why finding the one faulty bulb was difficult, and why modern lights use parallel wiring.
Show Answer

In series: one broken bulb opens the circuit — all bulbs go dark simultaneously. To find the faulty one, each bulb must be tested individually (swap-out method) — tedious with 20+ bulbs. Additionally, if one bulb is replaced with higher resistance, it takes more voltage at the expense of others. Modern parallel wiring: each bulb is on an independent branch. Parallel Circuits

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

State three characteristics of a parallel circuit.
Show Answer

(1) The same voltage is applied across all branches. (2) The total current equals the sum of branch currents: I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃. (3) The reciprocal of total resistance equals the sum of reciprocals: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃.

Easy
Two resistors of 6 Ω and 3 Ω are connected in parallel. Find the equivalent resistance.
Show Answer

1/R = 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/6 + 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2. R = 2 Ω. Note: parallel combination is always less than the smallest individual resistance (2 < 3).

Easy
Is the equivalent resistance of a parallel combination greater or less than the smallest individual resistance?
Show Answer

Less than the smallest individual resistance. Adding any parallel path provides an additional route for current, reducing overall opposition to flow. The equivalent resistance is always less than any single branch resistance.

Easy
Three resistors of 6 Ω, 6 Ω, and 6 Ω are connected in parallel. What is the equivalent resistance?
Show Answer

Easy
What happens to the other appliances in a parallel circuit when one is switched off?
Show Answer

The other appliances continue to work normally. In a parallel circuit, each branch has an independent path to the supply. Switching off one device opens its branch only — the voltage across other branches is unchanged, so they operate normally. This is why household wiring uses parallel circuits.

Easy
State Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) and explain how it applies to a parallel junction.
Show Answer

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL): The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum of currents leaving it (charge is conserved). At a parallel junction: I_total = I₁ + I₂ + I₃. Current "splits" into branches at the junction and "recombines" at the other junction — total is conserved.

Easy
Give two reasons why household electrical circuits are wired in parallel rather than series.
Show Answer

(1) Each appliance receives the full supply voltage (230 V) for which it is designed. In series, voltage would be shared, making appliances under-voltage. (2) Each appliance operates independently — switching off or failure of one does not affect others. A fault in one branch does not disrupt the rest of the circuit.

Electrical Power & Energy

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

State the formula for electrical power in three different forms.
Show Answer

P = VI (power = voltage × current). Since V = IR: P = I²R. Since I = V/R: P = V²/R. All three are equivalent forms of electrical power, measured in watts (W).

Easy
What is the SI unit of electrical energy? What is the commercial unit?
Show Answer

SI unit: joule (J). Commercial unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh). 1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J. The kWh is the unit used by electricity boards for billing.

Easy
State Joule's Law of heating.
Show Answer

Joule's Law: The heat produced in a conductor is directly proportional to (1) the square of the current (H ∝ I²), (2) the resistance (H ∝ R), and (3) the time of current flow (H ∝ t). Combined: H = I²Rt joules.

What does the power rating of an appliance (e.g., 1500 W) indicate?
Show Answer

What is the function of an MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker)?
Show Answer

Set A Recall & Identify (Easy)

What is the standard voltage and frequency of the domestic electricity supply in India?
Show Answer

India's domestic supply is 230 V AC at 50 Hz. The 50 Hz means the current alternates direction 50 times per second (100 half-cycles per second).

Easy
What is earthing (grounding) in electrical circuits? Why is it important?
Show Answer

Earthing connects the metal body of an appliance to the Earth via a low-resistance wire. If a fault causes the live wire to touch the metal casing, current flows safely to the Earth instead of through a person touching the appliance. It prevents electric shock and causes the fuse/MCB to blow quickly, isolating the fault.

Show Answer

Why is it dangerous to touch electrical appliances with wet hands?
Show Answer

Pure water is a poor conductor, but tap water contains dissolved salts and minerals, making it a reasonably good conductor. Wet hands provide a low-resistance path for current from a faulty appliance to the body. The resulting current can cause cardiac arrest or severe burns. Dry skin has much higher resistance (~100,000 Ω), protecting against mild voltages; wet skin drops to ~1000 Ω.

Show Answer

A fuse is a thin wire of low-melting-point alloy (e.g., tin-lead alloy) connected in series with a circuit. When current exceeds the rated value, the fuse wire heats up and melts, breaking the circuit and protecting appliances from overload. Key property: low melting point and appropriate resistivity.

Show Answer

A short circuit occurs when the live and neutral wires touch directly (or through very low resistance), causing an extremely large current to flow. Causes: (1) Damaged insulation on wires allowing direct contact. (2) Water/moisture bridging the live and neutral terminals. The fuse/MCB must blow to prevent fire and equipment damage.

The BEE (Bureau of Energy Efficiency) star rating (1–5 stars) indicates the energy efficiency of an appliance. More stars = more energy efficient for the same output. In normal operation, the appliance casing is not connected to the live circuit — no fault current flows in the earth wire. It remains at 0 V (Earth potential). This large current blows the fuse/MCB instantly, disconnecting the live supply. Without the earth wire, fault current would flow through a person touching the casing instead.

Explain what a Residual Current Device (RCD/ELCB) does and why it provides better protection than an ordinary fuse for electric shock prevention.
Show Answer

An RCD (Residual Current Device) compares the current in the live and neutral wires. In normal operation, these are equal (what goes out comes back). If a person receives a shock, some current flows through them to Earth — the live current exceeds the neutral current by a small amount (as little as 30 mA). The RCD detects this imbalance and disconnects the circuit in ~30 milliseconds. A fuse only blows at currents well above lethal levels (amperes); an RCD trips at 30 mA — a potentially life-saving difference.

Show Answer

A geyser or AC may draw 15–20 A. If shared with other appliances on a 15 A circuit, total current could easily exceed the circuit rating, tripping the MCB repeatedly or, worse, overheating the wiring if an over-rated MCB is fitted. Separate circuits allow: (1) individually rated wiring (thicker cables for high-current circuits); (2) dedicated MCBs correctly sized for each appliance; (3) independent isolation for maintenance/fault without disturbing other circuits.

Using Ohm's Law, explain quantitatively why the current in a short circuit can be thousands of amperes and why this causes a fire risk.
Show Answer

In normal operation, an appliance has resistance R (e.g., 50 Ω for a 1 kW kettle at 230 V). In a short circuit, live and neutral touch directly — the only resistance is the wire resistance (e.g., 0.01 Ω). I_fault = V/R_short = 230/0.01 = 23,000 A — thousands of times the normal current. Power loss = I²R_wire: even in the low-resistance short-circuit wire, at 23,000 A and 0.01 Ω: P = 23000² × 0.01 = 5.29 × 10⁶ W — instantly incinerating the wire. The fuse/MCB must blow in milliseconds to prevent fire.

Explain the concept of power factor in AC circuits. Why does a low power factor increase electricity costs for industries even if actual power consumed is the same?
Show Answer

Power factor (PF) = real power (W) / apparent power (VA). In a purely resistive circuit, PF = 1. Inductive loads (motors) cause current to lag voltage — PF < 1. Apparent power = V × I. If PF = 0.7, to deliver 700 W real power: apparent power = 700/0.7 = 1000 VA — the utility must supply 1000 VA of current-voltage capacity. This means thicker cables, larger transformers, and more line losses (I²R). Electricity boards charge industries for low power factor (kVAR charges) to recover infrastructure costs. Industries use capacitor banks to correct power factor toward 1, reducing these charges.
