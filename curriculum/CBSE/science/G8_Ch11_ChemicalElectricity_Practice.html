Set A Easy – Recall & Recognition

Name two solid materials that are good conductors of electricity.Easy
Show Answer

Copper and aluminium (also graphite/carbon). Metals in general conduct because they have free electrons that move when a potential difference is applied.

What is an electrolyte? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

An electrolyte is a substance that, when dissolved in water, produces ions and allows the solution to conduct electricity. Example: sodium chloride (common salt) solution.

Does a sugar solution conduct electricity? Why or why not?Easy
Show Answer

No. Sugar dissolves but does not form ions. Without free ions, there are no charge carriers, so the solution cannot conduct electricity. Sugar is a non-electrolyte.

State whether graphite is a conductor or an insulator.Easy
Show Answer

Graphite is a conductor. It has delocalised electrons within its layered structure that can carry charge, which is unusual among non-metals.

What happens to the LED/bulb in a tester circuit when the electrodes are dipped in a salt solution?Easy
Show Answer

The LED or bulb glows because the salt solution conducts electricity, completing the circuit.

Is distilled water a conductor or non-conductor of electricity?Easy
Show Answer

Distilled water is a very poor conductor (essentially a non-conductor) because it contains virtually no ions.

Name the process by which an electrolyte conducts electricity and gets chemically changed.Easy
Show Answer

Electrolysis — the process by which an electrolyte is decomposed into its constituent elements when electricity passes through it.

Give two examples of liquids that are non-electrolytes.Easy
Show Answer

Sugar solution and alcohol solution (ethanol in water). Neither produces ions, so neither conducts electricity.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why metals conduct electricity while most non-metals do not.Medium
Show Answer

Metals have a 'sea' of delocalised electrons that are free to move through the lattice when a voltage is applied, constituting an electric current. Most non-metals have all electrons tightly bound to atoms or covalent bonds, leaving no free charge carriers.

A student dissolves table salt in water and tests it with a circuit tester. The bulb glows brightly. What does this indicate about the solution?Medium
Show Answer

A brightly glowing bulb indicates that the solution is a strong electrolyte with a high concentration of ions (Na⁺ and Cl⁻), allowing a large current to flow.

Differentiate between a conductor and an electrolyte with an example of each.Medium
Show Answer

A conductor carries charge through free electrons (e.g., copper wire) without any chemical change. An electrolyte carries charge through ions in solution (e.g., HCl solution) and undergoes chemical decomposition during the process.

Why does lemon juice conduct electricity even though it is a liquid and not a metallic solution?Medium
Show Answer

Lemon juice contains citric acid, which partially ionises in water to produce H⁺ ions and citrate ions. These ions act as charge carriers, making it a weak electrolyte.

In a conductivity tester, what observable change tells you that a liquid is a weaker electrolyte compared to a strong one?Medium
Show Answer

A weaker electrolyte produces fewer ions, so the bulb glows dimly (or the LED is faint) compared to the bright glow seen with a strong electrolyte. The smaller number of ions means a lower current.

Explain how salt water (brine) becomes an electrolyte when NaCl is dissolved in water.Medium
Show Answer

NaCl is an ionic compound. When it dissolves in water, the polar water molecules pull Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions apart. These free ions become charge carriers in the solution, enabling it to conduct electricity.

A student tests tap water and finds the bulb glows faintly. What does this suggest about tap water?Medium
Show Answer

Tap water contains dissolved mineral salts and ions (e.g., calcium, magnesium bicarbonates), making it a weak electrolyte. The faint glow shows small but measurable conductivity.

Why is graphite used as an electrode in electrolysis experiments instead of wood or plastic?Medium
Show Answer

Graphite is a good conductor of electricity and is chemically inert (does not react with most electrolytes). Wood and plastic are insulators and would not allow current to pass through.

Set C Hard – Analysis, Evaluation & Extended Response

Compare the mechanism of electrical conduction in (a) a metallic copper wire and (b) a copper sulphate solution. How do they differ fundamentally?Hard
Show Answer

(a) In copper wire, conduction occurs via free delocalised electrons moving toward the positive terminal. No chemical change occurs. (b) In CuSO₄ solution, conduction occurs via movement of Cu²⁺ ions toward the cathode and SO₄²⁻ ions toward the anode. The process involves chemical change (decomposition) at electrodes.

Design a simple experiment to classify five household liquids as strong electrolytes, weak electrolytes, or non-electrolytes. Describe the method and expected observations.Hard
Show Answer

Set up a circuit: battery → LED/bulb → two carbon electrodes dipped into each liquid in turn. Strong electrolytes (salt water, HCl): bulb glows brightly. Weak electrolytes (lemon juice, vinegar, tap water): bulb glows dimly. Non-electrolytes (sugar water, distilled water): bulb does not glow. Classify each liquid based on brightness.

Explain why increasing the concentration of a salt solution increases its electrical conductivity. What limit exists for this relationship?Hard
Show Answer

Higher concentration means more ions per unit volume, so more charge carriers are available. This reduces electrical resistance and increases conductivity. The limit is the saturation point of the salt — beyond that, no more salt dissolves, so conductivity no longer increases. At very high concentrations, ion-ion interactions can actually slightly reduce mobility.

A student claims that any liquid that flows freely must be an electrolyte. Evaluate this claim with two counterexamples and explain the underlying error.Hard
Show Answer

The claim is false. Fluidity (viscosity) is a physical property unrelated to ionic content. Counterexample 1: Petrol (gasoline) is a free-flowing liquid but contains no ions — it is a non-electrolyte. Counterexample 2: Sugar solution is fluid but non-electrolyte. The student confuses physical state (liquid) with chemical ability to ionise. Electrolytic behaviour depends on the presence of free ions, not on flow.

How would you modify the conductivity tester to give a quantitative measure of conductivity rather than just a visual indicator?Hard
Show Answer

Replace the LED with an ammeter. The current reading (in milliamps) would be proportional to conductivity. Using Ohm's law (R = V/I), you could calculate resistance, and from electrode dimensions, the specific conductivity. Keeping electrode geometry constant ensures comparability across tests.

Explain why the same salt (NaCl) has near-zero conductivity as a solid crystal but high conductivity when dissolved or melted.Hard
Show Answer

In a solid crystal, Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are locked in fixed lattice positions and cannot move — no conduction. When dissolved, water molecules separate and surround the ions (solvation), freeing them to migrate. When melted, thermal energy breaks the lattice, again freeing ions. Mobile ions = conduction in both liquid and dissolved states.

Evaluate the statement: "Electrolytes always decompose completely when electricity passes through them." Is this accurate? Justify with evidence.Hard
Show Answer

Not completely accurate. The extent of decomposition depends on the quantity of charge passed (Faraday's law: mass ∝ charge). Small current for a short time decomposes only a small fraction of the electrolyte. Complete decomposition would require passing charge equal to the total moles of ions × Faraday's constant. Additionally, weak electrolytes only partially ionise, so not all molecules are available for decomposition.

A scientist dissolves compound X in water and finds the solution conducts electricity, but bubbles also appear at both electrodes. What can you infer about compound X? Explain your reasoning step by step.Hard
Show Answer

Compound X is an electrolyte because the solution conducts electricity (presence of ions). The bubbles at both electrodes indicate gaseous products are being released, meaning the compound undergoes electrolytic decomposition to produce gases. This is consistent with an acid (e.g., HCl → H₂ at cathode, Cl₂ at anode) or water (H₂ at cathode, O₂ at anode). Further testing of the gas identity (pop test, glowing splint) would confirm the specific compound.

Set A Easy – Recall & Recognition

What is electrolysis?Easy
Show Answer

Electrolysis is the process of decomposing an electrolyte into its component elements by passing an electric current through it in its molten or dissolved state.

Which electrode is the cathode and which is the anode?Easy
Show Answer

The cathode is the negative (–) electrode. The anode is the positive (+) electrode.

Towards which electrode do positive ions (cations) migrate during electrolysis?Easy
Show Answer

Positive ions (cations) migrate towards the cathode (negative electrode) because opposite charges attract.

What type of current is used in electrolysis — AC or DC?Easy
Show Answer

Direct current (DC) is used in electrolysis because it maintains a constant direction, ensuring ions migrate consistently to the correct electrode.

Name the device that converts electrical energy into chemical energy during electrolysis.Easy
Show Answer

An electrolytic cell (electrolysis cell) converts electrical energy into chemical energy to drive the non-spontaneous decomposition reaction.

What are anions? Which electrode do they migrate to?Easy
Show Answer

Anions are negatively charged ions. They migrate towards the anode (positive electrode) during electrolysis.

What is the electrolyte used when electrolyzing copper sulphate solution?Easy
Show Answer

Copper sulphate (CuSO₄) solution dissolved in water is the electrolyte.

Define the term 'electrode'.Easy
Show Answer

An electrode is a conductor (usually metal or graphite) through which electric current enters or leaves the electrolyte during electrolysis.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain what happens at each electrode during the electrolysis of copper sulphate solution using copper electrodes.Medium
Show Answer

Cathode (–): Cu²⁺ ions from solution are attracted, gain 2 electrons, and deposit as copper metal (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu). Anode (+): copper metal dissolves, releasing Cu²⁺ ions into solution (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻). The concentration of CuSO₄ remains roughly constant.

Why must the electrolyte be in a liquid or dissolved state for electrolysis to work?Medium
Show Answer

Ions must be free to move for electrolysis to occur. In a solid electrolyte, ions are fixed in a rigid lattice and cannot migrate to the electrodes. In the liquid/dissolved state, ions gain freedom of movement and can conduct charge.

What is the role of direct current (DC) in electrolysis? Why can't AC be used?Medium
Show Answer

DC maintains a fixed polarity so each electrode always acts as cathode or anode consistently — ions always migrate in one direction and deposit at the same electrode. AC reverses polarity many times per second, causing ions to oscillate without net deposition, making electrolysis ineffective.

Describe the colour change you would observe at the cathode during electrolysis of CuSO₄ solution. Explain the chemistry.Medium
Show Answer

The cathode develops a pink-orange coating as copper metal deposits on it. Cu²⁺ ions (responsible for blue colour) are removed from solution and gain electrons at the cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu(s). The solution gradually becomes paler blue as Cu²⁺ concentration falls (if using inert electrodes).

Compare what happens at the anode when (a) a copper anode and (b) a carbon (graphite) anode is used in CuSO₄ electrolysis.Medium
Show Answer

(a) Copper anode: copper dissolves (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻), replenishing Cu²⁺ ions in solution — solution concentration stays constant. (b) Carbon anode: inert; instead, SO₄²⁻ ions or water are oxidised, producing oxygen gas. The Cu²⁺ concentration in solution decreases over time.

In an electrolysis experiment, a student reverses the battery connections midway through. What would happen to the deposits on the electrodes?Medium
Show Answer

Reversing connections swaps the cathode and anode. The previously deposited copper on the old cathode would now act as an anode and begin to dissolve. The other electrode (now cathode) would start accumulating copper. Net result: copper is stripped from one and plated onto the other.

Why is electrolysis described as an energy-intensive process?Medium
Show Answer

Electrolysis forces non-spontaneous chemical reactions to proceed by supplying electrical energy (from a battery or power source). The reaction requires continuous energy input because the products are at a higher chemical energy state than the reactants. This contrasts with spontaneous redox reactions that release energy.

How do you know electrolysis is a chemical change and not merely a physical one?Medium
Show Answer

In electrolysis, new substances are formed at the electrodes (e.g., copper metal from copper sulphate, gases from water). The original compound is broken down into its elements or simpler compounds — an irreversible process. These are hallmarks of chemical change: new substances formed, not easily reversed.

Set C Hard – Analysis, Evaluation & Extended Response

Explain Faraday's first law of electrolysis in your own words and give a real-world example of how it is applied.Hard
Show Answer

Faraday's first law states that the mass of substance deposited or dissolved at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electric charge passed (mass ∝ Q = I × t). Example: In industrial copper refining, engineers calculate exactly how long to run current to deposit a precise mass of copper, minimising energy waste.

Distinguish between oxidation and reduction in electrolysis. Identify which occurs at the cathode and which at the anode, giving half-equations.Hard
Show Answer

Reduction (gain of electrons) occurs at the cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu. Oxidation (loss of electrons) occurs at the anode: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ (copper anode) or 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ (inert anode). Memory aid: OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

A student passes a current of 2 A through a CuSO₄ solution for 30 minutes using copper electrodes. Predict all the observable changes and explain each.Hard
Show Answer

Cathode: pink-orange copper deposits and increases in mass. Anode: copper dissolves, decreasing in mass. Solution: remains blue (Cu²⁺ concentration constant, since anode replenishes what cathode removes). Charge passed = 2 × 1800 = 3600 C. Mass of Cu deposited ≈ 3600/(96500) × 63.5 ≈ 1.18 g at cathode.

Evaluate: "Electrolysis is always a wasteful process." Do you agree? Provide arguments for and against this statement.Hard
Show Answer

Against (disagreement): Electrolysis is used productively — electroplating protects metals, copper refining purifies metals to 99.99%, aluminium extraction from bauxite would be impossible without it, and water electrolysis produces green hydrogen fuel. For (agreement): It does require continuous electrical energy input, so it is energy-intensive compared to spontaneous reactions. However, "wasteful" is inaccurate when electrolysis achieves transformations impossible by other means. The efficiency depends on design and application.

How does the use of inert vs. reactive electrodes change the products and observations in electrolysis? Give a specific example.Hard
Show Answer

Inert electrodes (platinum, graphite): do not participate in reaction; electrolyte decomposes, releasing products from the electrolyte itself. In CuSO₄ with graphite anode: O₂ is released at anode, Cu deposits at cathode; solution becomes acidic as SO₄²⁻ ions remain. Reactive electrodes (copper): anode dissolves into the electrolyte, maintaining ion concentration. This principle is used in electroplating and copper refining to transfer pure copper from impure anode to pure cathode.

Explain, using particle theory, how increasing temperature affects the rate of electrolysis of a given electrolyte solution.Hard
Show Answer

Higher temperature increases the kinetic energy of ions in solution. Faster-moving ions migrate more quickly to the electrodes, increasing the rate of charge transfer (current for a given voltage). Electrode reactions also speed up. However, very high temperatures could evaporate the solvent or degrade the electrolyte, reducing effectiveness.

Describe a scenario where electrolysis could go wrong due to incorrect setup, and explain the chemical consequences.Hard
Show Answer

If the polarity of the DC supply is accidentally reversed, the designed cathode becomes an anode. Any previously plated metal would dissolve back into solution, and the other electrode would be plated instead. If AC is used instead of DC, ions oscillate without net deposition — no plating occurs, and potentially harmful gases may be produced at both electrodes alternately. Using an electrolyte that reacts with the electrode material could dissolve the electrode completely, contaminating the product.

Compare electrolysis to a galvanic (voltaic) cell. How does the energy transformation differ? Why do both involve electrodes and electrolytes?Hard
Show Answer

Electrolysis: electrical energy → chemical energy; non-spontaneous reaction driven by external power. Galvanic cell: chemical energy → electrical energy; spontaneous reaction generates current. Both use electrodes (to transfer electrons) and electrolytes (to carry ions). In a galvanic cell the reaction proceeds naturally; in an electrolytic cell an external source must force the reaction.

Electrolysis of Water

Set A Easy – Recall & Recognition

What gases are produced at the cathode and anode when water is electrolysed?Easy
Show Answer

Cathode: hydrogen gas (H₂). Anode: oxygen gas (O₂).

What is the ratio of hydrogen to oxygen produced by volume during electrolysis of water?Easy
Show Answer

Hydrogen : Oxygen = 2 : 1 by volume. This matches the formula of water (H₂O): 2 atoms of hydrogen for every 1 atom of oxygen.

How do you test for hydrogen gas collected during electrolysis of water?Easy
Show Answer

Hold a lit splint near the mouth of the test tube. If hydrogen is present, you hear a squeaky 'pop' sound as it ignites.

How do you test for oxygen gas collected during electrolysis of water?Easy
Show Answer

Hold a glowing (not burning) splint near the gas. Oxygen relights the glowing splint — the splint bursts back into flame.

Write the chemical equation for the electrolysis of water.Easy
Show Answer

2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)

Why is a small amount of acid or salt added to water before electrolyzing it?Easy
Show Answer

Pure water has very few ions and barely conducts electricity. Adding acid (H₂SO₄) or salt increases ion concentration, making the solution a good conductor so electrolysis proceeds at a reasonable rate.

Which gas is collected in greater volume — hydrogen or oxygen?Easy
Show Answer

Hydrogen is collected in greater volume (twice as much as oxygen) because water contains two hydrogen atoms for every one oxygen atom.

What does the electrolysis of water prove about its composition?Easy
Show Answer

It proves that water is a compound made of hydrogen and oxygen in the atomic ratio 2:1 (H₂O), since these are the only gases produced and their volume ratio (H₂:O₂ = 2:1) confirms this composition.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why twice as much hydrogen as oxygen is produced during the electrolysis of water.Medium
Show Answer

Water is H₂O — 2 hydrogen atoms per molecule vs. 1 oxygen. The half-equations show: Cathode: 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂ (2 volumes H₂). Anode: 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ (1 volume O₂). The 2:1 ratio follows directly from the molecular formula.

In an electrolysis of water experiment, a student collects 20 mL of gas at the cathode. How much gas should be present at the anode? Justify your answer.Medium
Show Answer

10 mL. Since H₂:O₂ = 2:1 by volume, if 20 mL of H₂ is collected at the cathode, then 20/2 = 10 mL of O₂ should be collected at the anode.

A student notices that after several minutes of electrolysis, the water level inside both tubes has decreased. Explain this observation.Medium
Show Answer

As electrolysis proceeds, water molecules are decomposed into hydrogen and oxygen gases. These gases accumulate in the sealed tubes, displacing the liquid water downward. The overall volume of water decreases as it is consumed in the reaction 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂.

Why is hydrogen regarded as a clean fuel? How does the electrolysis of water support a hydrogen economy?Medium
Show Answer

Hydrogen burns cleanly: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, producing only water. No carbon dioxide or pollutants are emitted. Electrolysis of water (powered by renewable electricity such as solar or wind) produces "green hydrogen" — the only by-product being oxygen. This hydrogen can be stored and used as fuel, forming the basis of a hydrogen economy.

What safety precautions must be taken when collecting hydrogen gas during electrolysis? Why?Medium
Show Answer

Keep flames away — hydrogen is highly flammable and explosive in air (between 4–75% concentration). Work in a well-ventilated area. Do not test the gas near the electrolysis apparatus. Perform the pop test carefully away from the setup. Hydrogen leaks pose a fire risk since the gas is also colourless and odourless.

If the current is doubled in a water electrolysis experiment, how does this affect the volume of hydrogen produced in a given time?Medium
Show Answer

By Faraday's first law, mass (and volume) of gas produced is proportional to charge (Q = I × t). Doubling the current doubles the charge per unit time, so the volume of hydrogen produced per minute also doubles.

Describe the Hoffmann voltameter and its purpose in electrolysis of water experiments.Medium
Show Answer

A Hoffmann voltameter is a glass apparatus with two graduated vertical tubes (one above each electrode) connected by a central container for the electrolyte. Gases generated at each electrode rise and collect in the graduated tubes, allowing precise volume measurement. It clearly demonstrates the 2:1 ratio of H₂ to O₂ produced.

Explain why pure water undergoes electrolysis very slowly but water with dissolved sulphuric acid does so rapidly.Medium
Show Answer

Pure water self-ionises to a tiny extent (Kw = 10⁻¹⁴), yielding very few H⁺ and OH⁻ ions — very low conductivity, very slow electrolysis. H₂SO₄ is a strong acid that fully ionises: H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻, greatly increasing ion concentration and conductivity, so current flows readily and the reaction is fast. The sulphate ions are spectator ions and water is the species actually decomposed.

Set C Hard – Analysis, Evaluation & Extended Response

Critically evaluate green hydrogen as a future energy solution. What are its advantages and current limitations?Hard
Show Answer

Advantages: zero carbon emissions at point of use; water is the only by-product; hydrogen has very high energy density per kg; can be stored and transported. Limitations: electrolysis is energy-intensive — currently most electricity comes from fossil fuels, making most hydrogen "grey" not "green"; hydrogen is difficult to store safely (requires high pressure or cryogenic tanks); current electrolyser technology is expensive; the H₂ infrastructure (pipelines, fuelling stations) barely exists. Net assessment: promising long-term but requires renewable energy scale-up and cost reduction.

A student claims that during electrolysis of acidified water, the sulphuric acid is consumed. Evaluate this claim with half-equations and explain what actually happens.Hard
Show Answer

The claim is incorrect. At cathode: 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂↑. At anode: 2H₂O → O₂↑ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻. The H⁺ ions consumed at the cathode are replenished at the anode by water oxidation. SO₄²⁻ ions are spectator ions — they do not participate in electrode reactions. Net reaction: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂. The H₂SO₄ concentration actually increases slightly as water is consumed.

Explain how you would calculate the theoretical volume of hydrogen produced when 2 A is passed through acidified water for 10 minutes at STP.Hard
Show Answer

Charge Q = I × t = 2 × 600 = 1200 C. Moles of electrons = 1200/96500 ≈ 0.01244 mol e⁻. Cathode half-equation: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂, so moles H₂ = 0.01244/2 ≈ 0.00622 mol. At STP (22.4 L/mol): volume = 0.00622 × 22.4 ≈ 0.139 L ≈ 139 mL. The practical volume may be slightly less due to hydrogen dissolving in water.

How does the electrolysis of water serve as evidence that water is a compound and not an element? What historical significance does this experiment hold?Hard
Show Answer

Electrolysis splits water into two distinctly different gases (H₂ and O₂) — showing it is not a pure element but is composed of at least two different elements chemically combined. If water were an element, it could not be broken into simpler substances. Historically, Nicholson and Carlisle performed this in 1800, just days after Volta invented the battery. It confirmed Lavoisier's earlier synthesis of water and established the molecular formula, overturning the ancient Greek idea of water as a fundamental element.

Compare the energy efficiency of producing hydrogen via electrolysis vs. steam methane reforming. Which is more sustainable and why?Hard
Show Answer

Steam methane reforming (SMR): CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂, then CO + H₂O → CO₂ + H₂. High efficiency (~75%) but produces CO₂ ("grey hydrogen"). Electrolysis: ~70-80% efficient (energy from electricity → chemical energy in H₂) but if powered by renewables, produces no CO₂ ("green hydrogen"). Electrolysis is more sustainable because its carbon footprint approaches zero with renewable electricity, while SMR is inherently tied to fossil fuels and carbon emissions. The sustainability gap widens as renewable electricity costs fall.

Design an experiment to determine whether the gas collected at the cathode during water electrolysis is truly hydrogen. Include controls and describe all steps.Hard
Show Answer

Setup: Hoffmann voltameter with dilute H₂SO₄, platinum electrodes, DC supply. Step 1: Confirm greater volume at cathode (2:1 ratio). Step 2: Isolate cathode gas by closing stopcock. Step 3: Remove collection vessel carefully. Step 4: Apply glowing splint — no response (rules out O₂). Step 5: Apply lit splint — squeaky pop confirms H₂ ignition. Control: collect gas from anode (should give glowing splint result for O₂). Additional: mass spectrometry or gas chromatography for definitive identification. The combination of volume ratio, pop test, and absence of relighting a glowing splint collectively confirms H₂.

A student reports that the ratio of gases collected was 1.8:1 instead of 2:1. Suggest three possible sources of error and how each could be minimised.Hard
Show Answer

1) Hydrogen dissolves slightly in water (more soluble at higher pressures/concentration) — minimise by using dilute electrolyte and allowing saturation before measurement. 2) Hydrogen leaks through poorly sealed joints — check all connections, use fresh rubber seals. 3) Measurement error — gas not fully displaced before reading; allow equilibration and read at meniscus. Also: if oxygen partially oxidises the anode material (some metals), less O₂ is collected, but this wouldn't explain the H₂ deficit. Ensure inert electrodes.

Explain the environmental implications of large-scale electrolysis plants powered by coal-fired electricity. How does this compare to solar-powered electrolysis?Hard
Show Answer

Coal-fired electrolysis ("black hydrogen"): burning coal produces CO₂, SO₂, and particulates. The electricity used for electrolysis effectively carries a large carbon footprint — you might emit more CO₂ producing the hydrogen than you save by using it as fuel. Net effect: worse than burning natural gas directly. Solar-powered electrolysis: solar panels generate electricity with near-zero operational emissions. Hydrogen produced is truly green — carbon footprint approaches zero over the panel lifetime. Life-cycle analysis shows solar H₂ emits ~0.5–2 kg CO₂/kg H₂ vs. ~10–20 kg CO₂/kg H₂ for coal-powered electrolysis.

Set A Easy – Recall & Recognition

What is electroplating?Easy
Show Answer

Electroplating is the process of depositing a thin layer of a metal onto an object using electrolysis to improve its appearance, protect it from corrosion, or give it special properties.

What is the role of the object to be plated in the electrolysis circuit?Easy
Show Answer

The object to be plated is connected to the negative terminal (cathode). Metal ions from the solution are attracted to it and deposit on its surface.

What metal is used as the anode when copper-plating an iron key?Easy
Show Answer

A piece of pure copper is used as the anode. It dissolves during the process, replenishing the Cu²⁺ ions consumed at the cathode (the iron key).

Name the electrolyte used in copper electroplating.Easy
Show Answer

Copper sulphate (CuSO₄) solution is used as the electrolyte in copper electroplating.

What change in appearance do you observe on the cathode after copper electroplating?Easy
Show Answer

A pink-orange shiny layer of copper metal appears on the surface of the cathode (the object being plated).

What happens to the anode (copper plate) during electroplating?Easy
Show Answer

The copper anode gradually dissolves (decreases in mass) as copper atoms lose electrons and enter the solution as Cu²⁺ ions, replenishing what is deposited at the cathode.

Give one reason why iron objects are copper-plated before being chrome-plated.Easy
Show Answer

Copper provides a smooth, adherent base layer that improves adhesion of the subsequent chrome layer. Chrome adheres better to copper than directly to iron.

State one practical use of copper electroplating in everyday life.Easy
Show Answer

Copper plating is used to coat printed circuit board (PCB) tracks, to make zinc coins appear copper-coloured, and as a base layer before applying other metal coatings.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Describe the step-by-step setup for copper-plating an iron nail.Medium
Show Answer

1) Clean the iron nail (cathode) thoroughly with sandpaper and wash with soap. 2) Connect nail to negative terminal of battery. 3) Connect a copper strip (anode) to positive terminal. 4) Immerse both in CuSO₄ solution (ensure they don't touch). 5) Switch on current. 6) After 10–15 minutes, observe copper deposit on nail.

Why is the object to be plated cleaned and polished before electroplating begins?Medium
Show Answer

Grease, rust, or oxide layers on the surface prevent good adhesion — the copper would not bond uniformly to a dirty surface and may peel off. A clean, smooth surface ensures the plated layer is uniform, shiny, and strongly adherent.

Explain why the concentration of CuSO₄ solution remains approximately constant during copper electroplating with a copper anode.Medium
Show Answer

At the cathode, Cu²⁺ ions are removed from solution (deposited as Cu metal). At the copper anode, copper dissolves into the solution at the same rate (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻). The removal and replenishment of Cu²⁺ are balanced, keeping concentration roughly constant.

How does increasing current density affect the quality of the copper plating?Medium
Show Answer

Higher current increases the rate of deposition but at too-high current densities, copper deposits too quickly as coarse, powdery, or dendritic (tree-like) crystals — poor quality, rough, non-adherent. Lower current gives slow, smooth, shiny plating. Optimum current is specified in electroplating industry standards.

What would happen if a graphite (inert) anode were used instead of a copper anode during copper plating?Medium
Show Answer

With a graphite anode, no copper enters the solution. Instead, water or SO₄²⁻ ions are oxidised at the anode (producing O₂). The Cu²⁺ concentration in the electrolyte gradually decreases as copper deposits at the cathode. Plating slows and eventually stops when the electrolyte is depleted of Cu²⁺.

Why must the plated object not touch the anode during electroplating?Medium
Show Answer

If the cathode and anode touch, a short circuit occurs — current bypasses the electrolyte. No electroplating takes place, and the battery may be damaged. The two electrodes must remain separated by the electrolyte solution for the process to work.

Explain how copper electroplating is related to copper refining in industry.Medium
Show Answer

In copper refining, impure copper is cast as the anode, and a thin pure copper sheet is the cathode. In CuSO₄ solution, copper (and noble metal impurities) dissolves from the anode, but only pure Cu²⁺ deposits at the cathode (impurities fall as anode sludge). This produces 99.99% pure copper using the same electroplating principle.

Explain how you could determine the thickness of a copper plated layer using mass measurements before and after plating. Include necessary calculations.Hard
Show Answer

Weigh the object before (m₁) and after (m₂) plating. Mass of copper deposited = m₂ − m₁. Measure the surface area (A) of the plated object in cm². Density of copper = 8.96 g/cm³. Thickness = mass/(density × area) = (m₂ − m₁)/(8.96 × A) cm. Example: if 0.448 g Cu deposited on 5 cm² area: thickness = 0.448/(8.96 × 5) = 0.01 cm = 0.1 mm.

Graphite anode: cheap and inert but depletes the electrolyte of Cu²⁺, requiring frequent CuSO₄ replenishment and generating acidic by-products. Discuss how the concept of electroplating copper is applied in PCB (printed circuit board) manufacturing. Why is this critical for modern electronics?Hard
Show Answer

A technician reports that the plated copper layer is peeling off after just a few days. Analyse possible causes and propose corrective measures.Hard
Show Answer

Explain how anodising aluminium differs from electroplating. What are the advantages of anodising over traditional electroplating?Hard
Show Answer

Anodising: aluminium is the ANODE (not cathode). In dilute H₂SO₄, Al is oxidised to Al₂O₃ at the surface. This grows an oxide layer from the metal itself — it's not a foreign metal deposited from solution. Electroplating: foreign metal deposited at cathode from electrolyte solution. Anodising advantages: oxide layer is integrated into the metal (won't peel); much harder than aluminium; can be dyed various colours; provides excellent corrosion protection; environmentally uses no heavy metals. Used on aircraft, architectural panels, consumer electronics (iPhone casing).

How would you experimentally verify that the mass loss at the anode equals the mass gain at the cathode during copper electroplating? What does this confirm about Faraday's laws?Hard
Show Answer

Procedure: Dry and weigh copper anode (m_A1) and cathode object (m_C1). Run electrolysis at known current I for time t. Dry and reweigh: anode m_A2, cathode m_C2. Mass loss at anode = m_A1 − m_A2. Mass gain at cathode = m_C2 − m_C1. If equal: confirms conservation of mass and Faraday's first law (equal charge causes equal molar change at each electrode). Theoretical mass = (I × t)/(96500) × 63.5. If theoretical ≈ experimental: confirms Faraday's second law (moles proportional to equivalent weight). Minor differences are due to oxygen evolution at anode or hydrogen evolution at cathode instead of Cu.

In copper refining, explain why the anode sludge is economically valuable. What does this reveal about the chemistry of selective electrode deposition?Hard
Show Answer

Explain how heavy metal ions from electroplating can affect an entire river ecosystem.Medium

Describe the chemical process of treating copper-containing electroplating wastewater before discharge.Medium

Why is the electroplating industry particularly subject to environmental regulations compared to other manufacturing industries?Medium
Show Answer

How can electroplating industries recover valuable metals from their wastewater, turning a waste problem into an economic opportunity?Medium
