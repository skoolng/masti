What is Force

SET A Foundational – Easy

What is a force?Easy
Show Answer

A force is a push or a pull that can change the speed, direction, or shape of an object. Its SI unit is the newton (N).

Name the SI unit of force and what it equals in base units.Easy
Show Answer

The SI unit of force is the newton (N). 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².

Give two examples of a push force in everyday life.Easy
Show Answer

Pushing a door open; pushing a trolley at a supermarket. Both involve applying force in the direction of motion.

Give two examples of a pull force.Easy
Show Answer

Pulling a drawer open; pulling a bucket of water from a well using a rope.

What is a contact force? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

A contact force requires physical touch between the objects. Example: friction between a shoe and the floor.

What is a non-contact force? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

A non-contact force acts without physical touch. Example: gravitational force pulling a ball downward after it is thrown upward.

State three effects that a force can have on an object.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Change the speed (speed up or slow down); (2) Change the direction of motion; (3) Change the shape or size of the object.

Is gravity a contact or non-contact force? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

Gravity is a non-contact force. The Earth attracts objects toward its center without touching them directly.

A football is kicked and moves in a straight line, then slows down and stops. Name the forces involved.Easy
Show Answer

The kick provides a contact (muscular) force. The ball slows due to friction (contact) from the ground and air resistance (contact), plus gravity acting downward.

Can a force change the shape of an object without changing its motion? Give an example.Easy
Show Answer

Yes. Squeezing a rubber ball or pressing clay changes shape without significant motion. The deforming force alters shape while net motion may remain zero.

SET B Developing – Medium

A stationary book lies on a table. List all forces acting on it and state whether each is contact or non-contact.Medium
Show Answer

Gravity (non-contact) pulls the book downward; Normal force from the table (contact) pushes it upward. These two forces are balanced, so the book remains stationary.

Explain why magnetic force is classified as a non-contact force. Give a real-life application.Medium
Show Answer

Magnetic force acts over a distance without physical touch through a magnetic field. Application: maglev trains levitate above the track using magnetic repulsion.

Two forces of 10 N (east) and 6 N (west) act on a box. What is the net force and its direction?Medium
Show Answer

Net force = 10 − 6 = 4 N eastward. The box accelerates to the east.

Distinguish between balanced and unbalanced forces with an example each.Medium
Show Answer

Balanced forces: equal and opposite, no change in motion (book on table). Unbalanced forces: net force ≠ 0, causes acceleration or deceleration (a car speeding up when engine force > friction).

How does a force change the direction of a moving object? Use a cricket ball being hit as an example.Medium
Show Answer

The bat exerts a contact force on the ball. If the force is at an angle to the ball's original path, the resultant velocity has a new direction. The ball changes from one direction to another because force alters the velocity vector.

A spring stretched by 5 cm when a 10 N force is applied. What force would stretch it by 12 cm? (Assume Hooke's Law holds.)Medium
Show Answer

Spring constant k = F/x = 10/0.05 = 200 N/m. For 12 cm: F = 200 × 0.12 = 24 N.

Why does a rubber band regain its shape after stretching but a clay ball does not?Medium
Show Answer

A rubber band is elastic — it returns to original shape when force is removed. Clay is plastic/inelastic — it retains the deformed shape. This is because their molecular structures respond differently to applied force.

Three forces of 5 N north, 5 N south, and 8 N east act on an object. Find the net force.Medium
Show Answer

North and south forces cancel (5 − 5 = 0). Net force = 8 N east. The object accelerates eastward.

Explain why electrostatic force is a non-contact force. Give one daily-life example.Medium
Show Answer

Electrostatic force acts between charged objects through an electric field without direct touch. Example: a charged comb attracting small pieces of paper.

A child pulls a toy car with 3 N and friction acts with 1.5 N opposing. What is the net force? What happens to the car?Medium
Show Answer

Net force = 3 − 1.5 = 1.5 N in the direction of pull. The car accelerates in the direction the child is pulling.

SET C MYP Depth – Hard (Criteria B & D)

Evaluate: "All non-contact forces are weaker than contact forces." Is this statement scientifically accurate? Justify using gravitational and nuclear forces.Hard
Show Answer

The statement is false. The strong nuclear force (a non-contact force at sub-atomic scales) is the strongest known force. Gravity, while non-contact, holds galaxies together over enormous distances. Contact forces like friction are macroscopic manifestations of electromagnetic force. Force strength depends on context, not contact type.

A 2 kg ball is thrown horizontally. Sketch and describe all forces acting on it in mid-air. What would change if the air resistance doubles?Hard
Show Answer

Forces: (1) Weight = mg = 19.6 N downward; (2) Air resistance opposing motion (horizontal and upward components). If air resistance doubles, the horizontal deceleration increases (ball slows faster) and the vertical acceleration decreases below g, causing a shallower curved path and reduced range.

Design an investigation to determine whether a force changes the shape, speed, or direction of an object. Identify the independent, dependent, and controlled variables.Hard
Show Answer

Investigation: Roll a ball on a flat surface and apply forces at different angles. IV: direction and magnitude of applied force. DV: change in speed (timer/photogates) and change in direction (protractor). CV: mass of ball, surface type, initial speed. Record data and plot force angle vs. direction change.

Explain how multiple forces acting simultaneously can result in zero net force yet the object still deforms. Give an example.Hard
Show Answer

When equal and opposite forces act on different points of an object (a couple or compression/tension pair), net translational force is zero but internal stresses cause deformation. Example: Stretching a rubber band at both ends — net force = 0 but the band elongates due to internal tension forces.

Compare gravitational force, magnetic force, and electrostatic force in terms of: (a) range, (b) attractive/repulsive nature, (c) dependence on mass/charge.Hard
Show Answer

Gravity: infinite range, always attractive, depends on mass (F = Gm₁m₂/r²). Magnetic: short-to-medium range, both attractive and repulsive (poles), depends on magnetic moment. Electrostatic: long range, both attractive and repulsive (charges), depends on charge (F = kq₁q₂/r²). All are non-contact, inverse-square forces.

A box on a ramp has three forces: weight 50 N downward, normal 43.3 N perpendicular to ramp, friction 15 N up the ramp. The ramp angle is 30°. Is the box accelerating? Show calculations.Hard
Show Answer

Component of weight along ramp = 50 sin30° = 25 N (down ramp). Friction = 15 N (up ramp). Net force along ramp = 25 − 15 = 10 N down the ramp. Since net force ≠ 0, the box accelerates down the ramp.

MYP Criterion D: Real-world connection — explain how understanding forces helped engineers design safer cars. Name at least two specific safety features.Hard
Show Answer

Understanding force, inertia, and deceleration led to: (1) Crumple zones — increase stopping distance, reducing peak force (F = Δp/Δt); (2) Seatbelts and airbags — distribute force over larger area and time, preventing injury. Engineers use Newton's laws to model crash dynamics and minimise harm.

Critique this claim: "A force is always needed to keep an object moving." Use examples from space and Earth to support your argument.Hard
Show Answer

The claim is false — it contradicts Newton's First Law. In space (near-zero friction), spacecraft continue at constant velocity without any thrust. On Earth, friction and air resistance make it seem like force is needed, but these are opposing forces, not evidence that motion itself requires force. A hockey puck on ice slides much farther than on grass — less friction, less need for continuous force.

A 500 g ball is dropped from 10 m. (a) What is the gravitational force on it? (b) What is its acceleration? (c) If air resistance = 1 N, what is the net downward force? (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

(a) F = mg = 0.5 × 10 = 5 N downward. (b) a = g = 10 m/s² (in vacuum). (c) Net force = 5 − 1 = 4 N downward; net acceleration = 4/0.5 = 8 m/s².

How do forces explain the formation of stars? Identify which forces are involved and how they balance or overcome each other.Hard
Show Answer

Gas clouds collapse under gravity (non-contact, attractive). As density increases, gas pressure (contact, outward) builds. When gravity overcomes pressure, the cloud collapses further until nuclear fusion ignites. A stable star is in hydrostatic equilibrium: inward gravity = outward radiation and thermal pressure.

Newton's First Law – Inertia

SET A Foundational – Easy

State Newton's First Law of Motion in your own words.Easy
Show Answer

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at the same speed and direction, unless acted upon by a net external (unbalanced) force.

What is inertia?Easy
Show Answer

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist any change in its state of rest or uniform motion. It is directly related to the object's mass.

A passenger in a bus jerks forward when the bus suddenly brakes. Explain using Newton's First Law.Easy
Show Answer

When the bus brakes, an unbalanced force (friction) acts on the bus and slows it down. The passenger's body, due to inertia, tends to continue moving forward at the original speed — hence the jerk.

Why is Newton's First Law also called the Law of Inertia?Easy
Show Answer

Because the law describes inertia — the natural tendency of all objects to maintain their state (rest or motion) without requiring any force.

Which has more inertia: a truck or a bicycle? Why?Easy
Show Answer

The truck has more inertia because it has greater mass. Inertia is proportional to mass — a heavier object is harder to start moving or stop.

Why do we wear seatbelts in a car?Easy
Show Answer

In a collision, the car stops suddenly but our body tends to continue forward (inertia). The seatbelt provides the opposing force to stop our body safely, preventing injury.

A ball rolling on a smooth surface eventually stops. Does this violate Newton's First Law? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

No, it does not violate Newton's First Law. The ball stops because unbalanced forces (friction and air resistance) act on it. In the absence of these forces, it would continue rolling indefinitely.

What is a "net external force"?Easy
Show Answer

The net external force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object from outside. If it is zero, the object's velocity does not change.

Give one real-life example of inertia of rest.Easy
Show Answer

When a tablecloth is pulled quickly, the dishes on it remain nearly stationary due to inertia of rest.

Give one real-life example of inertia of motion.Easy
Show Answer

A person jumping off a moving bus tends to fall forward because their body continues moving forward (inertia of motion) even after their feet touch the ground.

SET B Developing – Medium

Explain how airbags in cars work using Newton's First Law.Medium
Show Answer

In a crash, the car decelerates rapidly. The driver's body, due to inertia, continues moving forward. The airbag inflates and provides a cushion, extending the stopping time and distributing the force, reducing injury risk.

A coin is placed on a card over a cup. When the card is flicked away quickly, the coin falls into the cup. Explain this using Newton's First Law.Medium
Show Answer

The coin is at rest. When the card is flicked quickly, friction acts for very little time — insufficient to move the coin significantly. Due to inertia of rest, the coin remains stationary and falls into the cup under gravity.

A hockey puck slides on ice for a long distance. How does this demonstrate Newton's First Law?Medium
Show Answer

Ice has very low friction, so the net force on the puck is nearly zero. According to Newton's First Law, with near-zero net force, the puck continues at nearly constant velocity for a long distance.

Why does a spacecraft continue moving in space without any engine thrust?Medium
Show Answer

In the vacuum of space, there is no air resistance or friction. With no net external force, the spacecraft obeys Newton's First Law and continues at constant velocity indefinitely.

Distinguish between inertia of rest and inertia of direction with one example each.Medium
Show Answer

Inertia of rest: a stationary object resists being set in motion (coin-on-card trick). Inertia of direction: a moving object resists change in direction (passengers lean outward on a turning bus).

A 5 kg box and a 20 kg box are both at rest. Which requires more force to start moving? Why?Medium
Show Answer

The 20 kg box requires more force because it has greater mass and therefore greater inertia. More force is needed to overcome its resistance to change in state of rest.

Why does shaking a tree make fruits fall off? Use Newton's First Law in your answer.Medium
Show Answer

When the tree is shaken, branches move rapidly. The fruits, due to inertia of rest, tend to remain stationary while the branch moves away, causing the stem to snap and the fruit to fall.

Explain why a fast-moving bullet gets embedded in a wooden block while a slow-moving one might not penetrate as deep.Medium
Show Answer

A fast bullet has greater momentum and kinetic energy. Its inertia of motion resists deceleration, allowing it to penetrate deeper before stopping. A slower bullet is stopped by the wood's resistance force more quickly.

A ball is swung in a circle on a string. When the string breaks, which direction does the ball move and why?Medium
Show Answer

The ball moves in a straight line tangent to the circle at the point where the string broke. According to Newton's First Law, without the inward force (string tension), the ball continues at constant velocity in the direction it was moving.

Why should heavy cargo in a vehicle be securely fastened? Use Newton's First Law.Medium
Show Answer

Heavy cargo has large inertia. If the vehicle brakes suddenly, the cargo tends to continue moving forward at the original speed. If unsecured, it can crash into the cab or fall off, causing accidents or injury.

SET C MYP Depth – Hard (Criteria B & D)

Critically evaluate: "Newton's First Law is just a special case of the Second Law." Is this mathematically valid? Explain.Hard
Show Answer

Mathematically valid: if F_net = 0, then from F = ma → 0 = ma → a = 0 (if m ≠ 0), meaning constant velocity. First Law is indeed the F=0 case of Second Law. However, the First Law independently establishes inertial reference frames — frames where Newton's laws are valid — which the Second Law alone does not define.

Design a controlled experiment to demonstrate inertia. Identify variables, describe procedure, and predict results with scientific reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

Aim: Show inertia depends on mass. IV: mass of object. DV: distance travelled before stopping. CV: surface, initial speed, shape. Procedure: Roll 3 balls of different masses at the same speed on the same surface; measure stopping distance. Prediction: Heavier ball travels farther (more inertia, harder to stop). Supported by F=ma: same friction force, larger mass → smaller deceleration → larger stopping distance.

A 60 kg astronaut floats in a space station. Is Newton's First Law applicable? What happens when she pushes off a wall?Hard
Show Answer

Yes — the space station is an inertial reference frame (approximately). She floats due to near-zero net force (microgravity). When she pushes off the wall, a contact force acts briefly; she then moves at constant velocity (First Law) until she hits another wall. Her 60 kg mass means significant inertia — she's hard to stop mid-flight.

Explain the concept of an "inertial reference frame." Is the surface of the Earth a perfect inertial frame? Justify.Hard
Show Answer

An inertial reference frame is one in which Newton's First Law holds — objects with no net force move at constant velocity. Earth is not a perfect inertial frame because it rotates (centripetal acceleration ~0.034 m/s² at equator) and orbits the Sun. However, for most classroom experiments, these effects are negligible and Earth is treated as approximately inertial.

A train moving at 30 m/s suddenly stops. A 70 kg passenger is not wearing a seatbelt. Estimate the force they would experience if they hit the seat in front (stopping distance ~0.3 m). Should this be a safety concern?Hard
Show Answer

v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 900 + 2a(0.3) → a = −1500 m/s². F = ma = 70 × 1500 = 105,000 N. This is ~150 times the person's weight — lethal. This confirms why seatbelts and head restraints are critical safety features.

How did Galileo's experiments on inclined planes contribute to the concept of inertia, before Newton formalized it?Hard
Show Answer

Galileo observed that a ball rolling down one incline rolled up another to nearly the same height. He reasoned that if the second incline were made flatter, the ball would roll farther. With zero incline (flat surface), the ball would roll forever — implying no force is needed to maintain motion. This directly challenged Aristotle's view and gave Newton the foundation for the First Law.

A car crash test shows that a mannequin (70 kg) hits the steering wheel at 15 m/s after a collision. If the airbag extends stopping time to 0.1 s (vs 0.01 s for rigid steering wheel), compare the forces. What does this tell engineers?Hard
Show Answer

Impulse = Δp = 70 × 15 = 1050 N·s. With airbag: F = 1050/0.1 = 10,500 N. Without: F = 1050/0.01 = 105,000 N. Airbag reduces force by 10×. This tells engineers: extending collision time dramatically reduces peak force, guiding crumple zone and airbag design to save lives.

MYP Criterion D: Analyse the real-world impact of inertia on road safety statistics. Suggest two engineering and two behavioural solutions.Hard
Show Answer

Impact: Inertia causes injuries when vehicles stop suddenly — bodies continue moving. Engineering solutions: (1) Crumple zones extend deceleration time; (2) Seatbelt pretensioners reduce forward movement. Behavioural solutions: (1) Wearing seatbelts always; (2) Maintaining safe following distances to allow gradual deceleration. Together, these reduce crash fatalities significantly.

Explain why objects in a rotating space station feel "artificial gravity." Which law of motion is being "mimicked" and how?Hard
Show Answer

In a rotating station, the wall pushes inward (centripetal force) on astronauts. The astronauts' inertia resists this change in direction, so they feel pushed outward against the floor — mimicking gravity. Newton's First Law (inertia) creates the sensation; Newton's Second Law governs the centripetal acceleration a = v²/r needed to maintain circular motion.

Why is Newton's First Law considered a philosophical shift from Aristotelian physics? What was Aristotle's view and what evidence disproves it?Hard
Show Answer

Aristotle believed a constant force was needed to maintain motion ("natural state is rest"). Newton showed the natural state is constant velocity — no force needed to maintain motion, only to change it. Evidence: spacecraft continue at constant velocity in space with no engine; pucks on ice slide far; pendulums swing many times. Galileo's ramp experiments were the key experimental evidence.

Newton's Second Law – F = ma

SET A Foundational – Easy

State Newton's Second Law of Motion.Easy
Show Answer

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass: F = ma.

A net force of 20 N acts on a 4 kg object. What is its acceleration?Easy
Show Answer

a = F/m = 20/4 = 5 m/s².

What force is needed to accelerate a 10 kg box at 3 m/s²?Easy
Show Answer

F = ma = 10 × 3 = 30 N.

A 15 N force produces an acceleration of 5 m/s². What is the mass of the object?Easy
Show Answer

m = F/a = 15/5 = 3 kg.

If the mass of an object doubles while the force stays the same, what happens to the acceleration?Easy
Show Answer

Acceleration halves. From a = F/m, if m doubles and F is constant, a becomes F/(2m) = half the original.

If the force doubles and mass stays the same, what happens to the acceleration?Easy
Show Answer

Acceleration doubles. a = F/m; if F doubles, a = 2F/m = twice the original.

What is the unit of force derived from F = ma?Easy
Show Answer

Force unit = kg × m/s² = newton (N). So 1 N = 1 kg·m/s².

A car of mass 1000 kg accelerates from rest to 20 m/s in 10 s. What is the net force?Easy
Show Answer

a = (20−0)/10 = 2 m/s². F = ma = 1000 × 2 = 2000 N.

What does it mean if the net force on an object is zero? What is its acceleration?Easy
Show Answer

If net force = 0, then a = F/m = 0/m = 0 m/s². The object is in equilibrium — either at rest or moving at constant velocity.

A 2 kg ball is dropped (g = 10 m/s²). What is the gravitational force on it?Easy
Show Answer

F = mg = 2 × 10 = 20 N downward. This is the weight of the ball.

SET B Developing – Medium

A 5 kg object experiences a 30 N push and 10 N friction. Find net force and acceleration.Medium
Show Answer

Net force = 30 − 10 = 20 N. a = F/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s².

A rocket of mass 5000 kg has a thrust of 100,000 N. Find its acceleration. (Ignore gravity.)Medium
Show Answer

a = F/m = 100,000/5000 = 20 m/s².

Explain why a heavier cricket ball requires more force than a tennis ball to achieve the same acceleration.Medium
Show Answer

From F = ma, for the same acceleration a, force is proportional to mass. A cricket ball (~0.16 kg) vs tennis ball (~0.057 kg): the cricket ball needs about 3× more force for the same acceleration.

A 1200 kg car brakes from 25 m/s to 0 in 5 s. Calculate the braking force.Medium
Show Answer

a = (0 − 25)/5 = −5 m/s². F = ma = 1200 × (−5) = −6000 N. The braking force is 6000 N opposing motion.

Two forces act on a 3 kg block: 12 N east and 6 N west. Find the acceleration.Medium
Show Answer

Net force = 12 − 6 = 6 N east. a = 6/3 = 2 m/s² east.

Why does a loaded truck take longer to stop than an empty one, even with the same braking force?Medium
Show Answer

a = F/m. A loaded truck has greater mass m. Same braking force F produces smaller deceleration (a). Smaller deceleration means more time and distance to stop.

Graph sketch: Draw a force-acceleration graph for a fixed mass. What does the slope represent?Medium
Show Answer

The graph is a straight line through the origin (F ∝ a for fixed m). The slope = F/a = mass (m). A steeper slope means greater mass.

A 50 kg student stands in a lift. The lift accelerates upward at 2 m/s². What is the normal force on the student? (g = 10 m/s²)Medium
Show Answer

Net force = ma = 50 × 2 = 100 N upward. Weight W = 500 N down. N − W = 100 → N = 600 N. The student feels heavier.

How does Newton's Second Law explain why kicking a stone hurts your foot but kicking a ball does not?Medium
Show Answer

By Newton's Third Law, the stone exerts an equal force back on your foot. The stone's high mass and rigidity mean very little acceleration; almost all force is transferred back to your foot. The ball is lighter and deformable — it accelerates easily, reducing the reaction force on your foot.

A ball of mass 0.5 kg is kicked with a force of 100 N for 0.05 s. What velocity does it gain from rest?Medium
Show Answer

a = F/m = 100/0.5 = 200 m/s². v = u + at = 0 + 200 × 0.05 = 10 m/s.

SET C MYP Depth – Hard (Criteria B & D)

A 2 kg block on a surface has a coefficient of kinetic friction μ = 0.3. A 15 N horizontal force is applied. Find the acceleration. (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

Normal force N = mg = 20 N. Friction f = μN = 0.3 × 20 = 6 N. Net force = 15 − 6 = 9 N. a = 9/2 = 4.5 m/s².

A 70 kg person is in a lift decelerating downward at 3 m/s². What is the normal force? Does the person feel lighter or heavier?Hard
Show Answer

Deceleration downward = acceleration upward = 3 m/s². Net F upward = ma = 70 × 3 = 210 N. W = 700 N. N = W − 210 = 490 N. The person feels lighter (apparent weight < true weight).

Two blocks 3 kg and 5 kg are connected by a string. A force of 32 N pulls both. Find the acceleration and tension in the string. (Frictionless surface)Hard
Show Answer

Total mass = 8 kg. a = 32/8 = 4 m/s². Tension T acts on 3 kg block only: T = 3 × 4 = 12 N. Verify: 32 − T = 5 × 4 → 32 − 12 = 20 ✓

Design an experiment to verify F = ma. Describe how you would change F while keeping m constant and measure a. What graph would confirm the law?Hard
Show Answer

Setup: Trolley on frictionless track with hanging masses providing force. Procedure: Vary hanging mass (force), keep trolley mass fixed, measure acceleration with motion sensor or ticker tape. Graph: Plot F vs a — a straight line through origin confirms F ∝ a. Slope = 1/m (mass of trolley).

A bullet of mass 10 g leaves a gun at 400 m/s. The barrel is 0.5 m long. Find the average force on the bullet inside the barrel.Hard
Show Answer

v² = u² + 2as → 160,000 = 0 + 2a(0.5) → a = 160,000 m/s². F = ma = 0.01 × 160,000 = 1600 N.

Explain why astronauts on the Moon can jump higher than on Earth, using F = ma and weight equations.Hard
Show Answer

On Moon, g_moon ≈ 1.6 m/s² vs g_earth = 9.8 m/s². Weight W = mg is ~6× less on Moon. The same leg muscle force (F) produces a much larger acceleration: a = F/m − g_moon >> F/m − g_earth. This greater net upward acceleration results in a much higher jump.

MYP Criterion B: Analyse how Newton's Second Law is applied in the design of safety helmets. What quantity do helmets maximize to minimize force?Hard
Show Answer

From impulse: F = Δp/Δt. Helmets are made of crushable foam that increases the stopping time Δt. For the same momentum change, more time → less force. Also, the helmet distributes force over a larger area (P = F/A). Both effects dramatically reduce the peak force on the skull, preventing brain injury.

An Atwood machine has 4 kg and 6 kg masses connected over a pulley. Find the acceleration and string tension. (g = 10 m/s²)Hard
Show Answer

Net force = (6−4)g = 20 N. Total mass = 10 kg. a = 20/10 = 2 m/s². T = 4(g + a) = 4(12) = 48 N. Check: T = 6(g − a) = 6(8) = 48 N ✓

Critically compare Newton's Second Law in classical mechanics vs. relativistic mechanics. When does F = ma break down?Hard
Show Answer

At velocities approaching the speed of light (v → c), relativistic mass increases: m_rel = m₀/√(1−v²/c²). The classical F = ma gives incorrect predictions. The correct relativistic form is F = dp/dt where p = γm₀v. For everyday velocities (v << c), the classical approximation is valid to <0.01% error.

A 1000 kg car engine produces 5000 N, and wind resistance is 500 N + 0.5v² (v in m/s). Find the acceleration at v = 20 m/s. At what speed does the car reach maximum velocity?Hard
Show Answer

At v = 20: resistance = 500 + 0.5(400) = 700 N. Net F = 5000 − 700 = 4300 N. a = 4300/1000 = 4.3 m/s². Max velocity when F_net = 0: 5000 = 500 + 0.5v² → v² = 9000 → v ≈ 94.9 m/s ≈ 95 m/s.

Newton's Third Law – Action & Reaction

SET A Foundational – Easy

State Newton's Third Law of Motion.Easy
Show Answer

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. The forces act on different objects and are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

When you push a wall with 10 N, how much force does the wall exert on you?Easy
Show Answer

The wall exerts 10 N back on you (equal in magnitude, opposite in direction). This is the reaction force.

How does a rocket move forward in space?Easy
Show Answer

The rocket expels hot gases backward (action). By Newton's Third Law, the gases push the rocket forward (reaction). No air is needed — the reaction works in vacuum.

Explain how a swimmer moves forward in water.Easy
Show Answer

The swimmer pushes water backward with their hands (action). The water pushes the swimmer forward (reaction). The net reaction force propels the swimmer.

Are action and reaction forces balanced? Why or why not?Easy
Show Answer

No. Although equal and opposite, they act on different objects. Balanced forces act on the same object. Action-reaction pairs can each cause acceleration of their respective objects.

When a gun is fired, the bullet moves forward. What happens to the gun?Easy
Show Answer

The gun recoils backward. The gun pushes the bullet forward (action); the bullet pushes the gun backward (reaction). This is called gun recoil.

Identify the action-reaction pair when you walk on the ground.Easy
Show Answer

Action: your foot pushes the ground backward. Reaction: the ground pushes your foot forward. This reaction force propels you forward.

A bird flaps its wings downward. How does it move upward?Easy
Show Answer

The wings push air downward (action). The air pushes the bird upward (reaction). The upward reaction force lifts the bird.

Why does a boat move backward when you jump off it toward a dock?Easy
Show Answer

When you jump forward (action), you push the boat backward (reaction). The boat moves backward due to the reaction force your legs exert on it.

If a horse pulls a cart and the cart pulls the horse backward with equal force, why does the cart-horse system move?Easy
Show Answer

The cart-horse system moves because the horse's hooves push the ground backward; the ground's forward reaction on the hooves exceeds road friction on the cart. Action-reaction pairs act on different objects — the ground's reaction on the horse is the net driving force of the system.

SET B Developing – Medium

How do jet engines use Newton's Third Law to propel aircraft?Medium

Jet engines pull in air, combust fuel, and expel hot gases backward at high speed (action). By Newton's Third Law, the expelled gases push the aircraft forward (reaction). Greater exhaust speed and mass flow rate increase thrust.

Show Answer

Walking requires pushing the ground backward to get a forward reaction force. On ice, friction is very low — when you push, your foot slips instead of gaining traction, so the reaction force is greatly reduced and propulsion is difficult.

Show Answer

Pair 1 (gravity): Earth pulls person down (action); person pulls Earth up (reaction). Pair 2 (contact): Person's weight pushes chair down (action); chair pushes person up — normal force (reaction). The normal force and person's weight both act on the person and are balanced (not an action-reaction pair but they balance).

Why do rockets work in space but propellers don't? Use Newton's Third Law.Medium
Show Answer

Rockets carry their own fuel/oxidiser and eject mass (exhaust gases) — the reaction to ejecting gases pushes the rocket forward. Propellers work by pushing air backward, but space has no air. Without a medium, propellers cannot generate a reaction force.

Show Answer

Reaction force = rate of momentum expelled = mass flow rate × velocity = 20 × 15 = 300 N backward. The firefighter must exert 300 N forward to remain stationary.

Can Newton's Third Law pairs ever cancel each other? Explain with an example.Medium
Show Answer

No. Third Law pairs act on different objects, so they cannot cancel. A book on a table: the book's weight (Earth on book) and the normal force (table on book) are NOT a Third Law pair — they both act on the book and happen to be equal. The Third Law pair to the book's weight is the book pulling the Earth upward.

A rocket in space has mass 2000 kg (including 500 kg fuel). It ejects fuel at 1000 m/s relative to the rocket. Using the rocket equation concept, explain why burn rate matters.Hard
Show Answer

Thrust = exhaust velocity × mass flow rate (F = v_e × dm/dt). Higher burn rate increases thrust, giving greater acceleration. But burning fuel reduces mass, changing F = ma dynamics continuously. F₁₂ = −F₂₁ (Third Law). F = dp/dt → dp₁/dt = −dp₂/dt → dp₁/dt + dp₂/dt = 0 → d(p₁ + p₂)/dt = 0 → total momentum is constant. Thus Newton's Third Law directly implies conservation of momentum for isolated systems. Q.E.D.

What is a free body diagram (FBD)?Easy
Show Answer

A free body diagram shows an isolated object with all external forces acting on it represented as arrows, labeled with force names and directions. It helps analyse the object's motion.

What does the length of an arrow in a FBD represent?Easy
Show Answer

The length of the arrow represents the magnitude (size) of the force. Longer arrow = larger force.

What is the formula for weight (W)?Easy
Show Answer

W = mg, where m is mass in kg and g is gravitational acceleration (≈10 m/s² on Earth). Weight acts downward.

What is the normal force and in which direction does it act?Easy
Show Answer

The normal force (N) is the contact force a surface exerts on an object, perpendicular to the surface. On a flat horizontal surface, it acts upward.

Draw a verbal FBD of a book resting on a table. List all forces and their directions.Easy
Show Answer

Forces: (1) Weight W = mg — downward; (2) Normal force N — upward. Since the book is in equilibrium, N = W. No horizontal forces.

For a 3 kg object on a flat surface, calculate its weight. (g = 10 m/s²)Easy
Show Answer

W = mg = 3 × 10 = 30 N downward.

A box is pushed horizontally on a flat surface. What four forces typically act on it?Easy
Show Answer

(1) Weight downward; (2) Normal force upward; (3) Applied force horizontal (forward); (4) Friction horizontal (backward/opposing motion).

What is tension? In which direction does it act on an object hung by a string?Easy
Show Answer

Tension (T) is the pulling force in a string, rope, or cable. For an object hung by a string, tension acts upward, opposing the object's weight.

A 2 kg ball hangs at rest from a string. What is the tension in the string? (g = 10 m/s²)Easy
Show Answer

At rest, T = W = mg = 2 × 10 = 20 N upward.

What does it mean for an FBD to show "equilibrium"?Easy
Show Answer

Equilibrium means the net force is zero — all force arrows balance each other. The object is either at rest or moving at constant velocity.

On an incline of angle θ, the normal force N = mg cosθ (perpendicular component of weight). Since cosθ < 1 for any angle θ > 0°, N < mg. The remaining component (mg sinθ) acts along the incline.

Friction is a contact force opposing relative motion (or tendency of motion) between surfaces. On an FBD, it always points opposite to the direction of motion (or applied force if stationary).

In all three phases, the only significant force is weight (mg) downward (ignoring air resistance). The FBD is the same in all three cases — only the velocity changes, not the forces. This illustrates that force determines acceleration, not velocity.

SET A Foundational – Easy

Define pressure and write its formula.Easy
Show Answer

Pressure is force per unit area: P = F/A, where F is force (N) and A is area (m²). It measures how concentrated a force is over a surface.

What is the SI unit of pressure? Write it in terms of base units.Easy
Show Answer

The SI unit is the pascal (Pa). 1 Pa = 1 N/m² = 1 kg/(m·s²).

A force of 50 N acts over an area of 2 m². Calculate the pressure.Easy
Show Answer

P = F/A = 50/2 = 25 Pa.

Why does a knife cut better when its edge is sharp?Easy
Show Answer

A sharp edge has a very small contact area. For the same applied force, P = F/A gives much higher pressure, making it easier to cut through materials.

Why do snowshoes help a person walk on snow without sinking?Easy
Show Answer

Snowshoes have a large area. For the same weight (force), P = F/A gives lower pressure — below the snow's breaking strength — so the person doesn't sink.

Show Answer

The flat top has large area — low pressure on your thumb (comfortable to push). The sharp point has tiny area — very high pressure, easily penetrating a board.

Show Answer

Pressure halves. P = F/A — if A doubles, P = F/(2A) = half the original pressure.

Broad feet have larger contact area, reducing pressure on the sand. This prevents camels from sinking into sand in the desert — same principle as snowshoes.

(1) Stone tools (~2 million years ago): Flint knapping created sharp edges (small area, high pressure) for cutting — earliest applied pressure engineering. (2) Plough (~3000 BCE): Iron share concentrates oxen's pull onto a narrow blade edge to cut soil; wide beam distributes load. (3) Modern hydraulic press (1795, Bramah): Pascal's principle multiplies pressure to shape metal, stamp car parts, crush cars — enabling industrial manufacturing. Each shows humans deliberately manipulating F/A to achieve goals.
