Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

List the seven levels of classification from broadest to most specific.Easy
Show Answer

Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. (Mnemonic: King Philip Came Over For Good Soup.)

What is binomial nomenclature?Easy
Show Answer

A two-part naming system using Genus (capitalised) and species (lowercase) in Latin/Greek, e.g., Homo sapiens.

Who developed the modern classification system?Easy
Show Answer

Carl Linnaeus (18th century).

Write the scientific name of humans correctly.Easy
Show Answer

Homo sapiens (italicised; Genus capitalised, species lowercase).

What does the biological species concept define as a "species"?Easy
Show Answer

A group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring.

At which taxonomic level are organisms most closely related — Order or Family?Easy
Show Answer

Family (more specific/narrower than Order, so more closely related).

Name the kingdom to which humans belong.Easy
Show Answer

Animalia.

Why is Latin used in scientific naming?Easy
Show Answer

Latin is a "dead" language that does not change, ensuring names are stable and universally understood by scientists of all languages.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why classification systems are useful in biology.Medium
Show Answer

They organise millions of species into manageable groups, aid communication between scientists, reveal evolutionary relationships, and help predict shared characteristics of newly discovered organisms.

Two organisms share the same genus. What does this tell us about their relationship?Medium
Show Answer

They are closely related, share a recent common ancestor, and have many similar structural and genetic features, but are different enough to be considered separate species.

Distinguish between a dichotomous key and a phylogenetic tree.Medium
Show Answer

A dichotomous key identifies unknown organisms through a series of yes/no questions. A phylogenetic tree shows evolutionary relationships and common ancestry based on genetic/morphological data.

Give the full classification (all 7 levels) for the domestic cat (Felis catus).Medium
Show Answer

Kingdom: Animalia; Phylum: Chordata; Class: Mammalia; Order: Carnivora; Family: Felidae; Genus: Felis; Species: catus.

How has DNA analysis changed traditional morphology-based classification?Medium
Show Answer

DNA sequencing reveals genetic similarity more accurately than appearance; many organisms that looked similar have been reclassified, and some that looked different have been found to be closely related. e.g., whales reclassified closer to hippos.

Why might two organisms in the same family but different genera not be able to breed?Medium
Show Answer

They are different species; although sharing common ancestors at the family level, they have accumulated enough genetic differences (different chromosome numbers, reproductive barriers) to prevent interbreeding.

Explain why viruses are not classified in any of the five kingdoms.Medium
Show Answer

Viruses are not considered living organisms: they have no cells, cannot independently metabolise or reproduce, and only replicate inside host cells. The kingdom system classifies living organisms, so viruses fall outside it.

Use an example to explain how common names cause confusion in science, and how binomial nomenclature solves this.Medium
Show Answer

"Robin" refers to different birds in Europe (Erithacus rubecula) and North America (Turdus migratorius). Binomial nomenclature gives each a unique, universally agreed scientific name, eliminating ambiguity.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate the limitations of the biological species concept, especially for asexual organisms.Hard
Show Answer

The biological species concept requires interbreeding, which is impossible to test in asexual organisms (bacteria, many plants), extinct species, or allopatric populations that have never met. Alternative concepts (morphological, phylogenetic, ecological) address these gaps but each has its own limitations.

Compare morphological, phylogenetic, and ecological species concepts, giving one advantage of each.Hard
Show Answer

Morphological: identifies species by physical traits — useful for fossils. Phylogenetic: groups by common ancestry using DNA — most scientifically rigorous. Ecological: groups by niche/habitat role — useful for conservation where genetic data is unavailable.

Modern taxonomy includes three domains (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya). Explain why this replaced the five-kingdom system.Hard
Show Answer

DNA sequencing revealed that Archaea are more closely related to eukaryotes than to bacteria, despite their prokaryotic appearance. The three-domain system, proposed by Woese (1977), reflects true evolutionary relationships (phylogeny) rather than superficial structural similarities.

Critically assess whether "species" is a human construct or a real biological entity.Hard
Show Answer

Species are partly a construct: life is a continuum of variation, and classification imposes artificial boundaries. Ring species (e.g., Ensatina salamanders) show neighbouring populations that interbreed but distant ones that cannot — the "line" is arbitrary. However, reproductive isolation is a real biological phenomenon, supporting species as genuine units of evolution and ecology.

Analyse how horizontal gene transfer complicates the construction of a neat "tree of life" for prokaryotes.Hard
Show Answer

Unlike vertical gene transfer (parent to offspring), horizontal gene transfer (HGT) passes genes between unrelated organisms (e.g., antibiotic resistance genes between bacteria). This creates a "web" rather than a tree of relationships, making it difficult to reconstruct prokaryotic ancestry from any single gene and challenging the concept of a universal common ancestor.

Discuss the impact of discovering new species at a rate of ~20,000 per year on our understanding of biodiversity.Hard
Show Answer

~8.7M species are estimated to exist; only ~1.5M are formally described. New discoveries revise phylogenetic trees and highlight unknown biodiversity, especially in microbes, invertebrates, and deep-sea habitats. This underscores how incomplete our understanding is and why conservation decisions based on known species may miss critical ecosystem contributors.

Explain how cladistics differs from traditional Linnaean classification and why it is now preferred.Hard
Show Answer

Linnaean classification groups by overall similarity (which can be convergent). Cladistics groups by shared derived characters (synapomorphies) from a common ancestor, producing clades. Cladistics is preferred because it reflects true evolutionary history; traditional taxonomy sometimes groups unrelated organisms (e.g., "reptiles" is not a valid clade because it excludes birds, which share a closer ancestor with crocodiles than with lizards).

Evaluate the role of taxonomy in conservation biology, with reference to cryptic species.Hard
Show Answer

Correct species identification is essential for conservation policy (many laws protect species by name). Cryptic species look identical but are genetically distinct; misidentifying them as one species means population counts are inflated and each true species gets insufficient protection. DNA barcoding now reveals cryptic species, improving conservation targeting. Example: what was one Bornean slow loris species is now known to be four — each requires separate protection.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Name the five kingdoms in Whittaker's classification system.Easy
Show Answer

Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia.

Which kingdom contains prokaryotes?Easy
Show Answer

Monera (bacteria and archaea).

What is the main mode of nutrition in the Fungi kingdom?Easy
Show Answer

Saprotrophic (absorptive heterotrophy) — secreting enzymes to break down and absorb organic matter externally.

Give two examples of organisms in Kingdom Protista.Easy
Show Answer

Amoeba and Paramecium (also: Euglena, Plasmodium).

What feature distinguishes Kingdom Plantae from Kingdom Animalia in terms of nutrition?Easy
Show Answer

Plants are autotrophs (make food via photosynthesis); animals are heterotrophs (must consume other organisms for food).

Are fungi's cell walls made of cellulose like plants? What are they made of?Easy
Show Answer

No. Fungal cell walls are made of chitin (the same material as insect exoskeletons).

Name one organism from Kingdom Monera.Easy
Show Answer

Escherichia coli / any bacterium (e.g., Lactobacillus).

Which kingdom includes organisms that are mostly multicellular, eukaryotic, and cannot move?Easy
Show Answer

Plantae.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain why Euglena is difficult to place in either Plantae or Animalia.Medium
Show Answer

Euglena has chloroplasts and can photosynthesise (plant-like) but can also ingest food, lacks a cell wall, and can move with a flagellum (animal-like). It is placed in Protista as a "catch-all" for such organisms.

Compare the cell structure of Monera with that of Plantae.Medium
Show Answer

Monera: prokaryotic — no membrane-bound nucleus, no organelles (mitochondria/chloroplasts absent), circular DNA, smaller (1–10 µm). Plantae: eukaryotic — membrane-bound nucleus, mitochondria, chloroplasts, large vacuole, cellulose cell wall, larger (10–100 µm).

Why are fungi classified separately from plants despite both being non-motile and having cell walls?Medium
Show Answer

Fungi are heterotrophs (absorptive), have chitin cell walls (not cellulose), lack chloroplasts, and are more closely related to animals than plants phylogenetically. Plants are autotrophs with cellulose cell walls and chloroplasts.

Give one organism from each of the five kingdoms and state one distinguishing feature.Medium
Show Answer

Monera: E. coli (prokaryote, no nucleus). Protista: Amoeba (unicellular eukaryote, pseudopods). Fungi: mushroom (chitin cell wall, saprotrophic). Plantae: oak tree (photosynthesis, cellulose wall). Animalia: eagle (multicellular, heterotrophic, no cell wall).

Explain the ecological role of decomposers (Fungi and some Monera).Medium
Show Answer

Decomposers break down dead organic matter into inorganic nutrients (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus), recycling them back into the soil for use by plants, essential for nutrient cycling in all ecosystems.

Why is Kingdom Protista considered "artificial" or polyphyletic?Medium
Show Answer

Protista groups diverse eukaryotic organisms that do not fit neatly into other kingdoms, but many protists are more closely related to animals, plants, or fungi than to each other. Modern taxonomy splits them into multiple supergroups.

Describe two ways bacteria in Monera are beneficial to humans.Medium
Show Answer

1. Gut bacteria (e.g., Lactobacillus) aid digestion and produce vitamins. 2. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium) enrich soil fertility for crops. (Also: yoghurt/cheese production, antibiotic production by Streptomyces.)

Compare the structure of a plant cell and an animal cell, naming three differences.Medium
Show Answer

Plant cell has: (1) cellulose cell wall; (2) large central vacuole; (3) chloroplasts. Animal cell lacks all three but has centrioles (plants lack centrioles in most species).

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate the five-kingdom system against the three-domain system. Which better reflects evolutionary history and why?Hard
Show Answer

The three-domain system (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya) is based on ribosomal RNA sequencing and reflects true evolutionary relationships: Archaea are more closely related to Eukarya than to Bacteria. The five-kingdom system grouped Bacteria and Archaea together as "Monera" based on superficial prokaryotic appearance — a polyphyletic grouping now known to be incorrect.

Analyse how endosymbiotic theory explains the origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts in eukaryotes.Hard
Show Answer

Lynn Margulis (1967) proposed that mitochondria descended from engulfed aerobic bacteria and chloroplasts from cyanobacteria. Evidence: both have double membranes, their own circular DNA, 70S ribosomes (like bacteria), and replicate by binary fission. This event separated eukaryotes (Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia) from prokaryotes (Monera).

Discuss the medical significance of studying Kingdom Monera (bacteria) in the context of antibiotic resistance.Hard
Show Answer

Bacteria evolve resistance through random mutation and HGT (plasmid exchange). Understanding bacterial genetics, cell wall structure, and metabolic pathways guides drug development. Misuse of antibiotics accelerates resistance — by 2050, antimicrobial resistance could kill 10M people/year (WHO). Classifying bacteria accurately identifies which antibiotics target their specific cell wall or ribosome types.

Compare the life cycle and ecological role of a pathogenic Protista (e.g., Plasmodium) with a beneficial Protista (e.g., phytoplankton).Hard
Show Answer

Plasmodium: parasitic; complex two-host life cycle (Anopheles mosquito + human); causes malaria; kills ~600,000 people/year. Phytoplankton (diatoms, dinoflagellates): autotrophic; produce ~50% of Earth's oxygen via photosynthesis; base of marine food chains. Both are single-celled eukaryotes in Protista, demonstrating the kingdom's enormous ecological diversity.

Explain how mycorrhizal fungi form mutualistic relationships with plants and why this matters for ecosystems.Hard
Show Answer

Mycorrhizal fungi colonise plant roots; hyphal networks extend the plant's effective root area enormously, absorbing water and minerals (especially phosphorus). The plant provides glucose (from photosynthesis) to the fungus. ~90% of terrestrial plant species depend on this mutualism; disrupting it (e.g., by fungicides or land clearing) reduces plant growth and ecosystem productivity.

Critically assess whether the five-kingdom classification should still be taught in schools given modern advances in molecular phylogenetics.Hard
Show AnswerThe five-kingdom system is outdated but useful pedagogically: it introduces the concept of grouping organisms by shared features and provides a manageable framework. Modern phylogenetics (three-domain, supergroups) is more accurate but complex for beginners. A balanced approach is best: teach five kingdoms as an introduction, then discuss their limitations and how DNA evidence has refined our understanding.

Analyse how lichens (fungi + algae/cyanobacteria symbiosis) challenge traditional kingdom boundaries.Hard
Show AnswerLichens are composite organisms from two kingdoms (Fungi + Plantae/Monera). They cannot be classified in a single kingdom; they are assigned to Fungi by convention (the fungal partner dominates structurally). They demonstrate that kingdom boundaries are not always discrete — symbiosis can create entities that span them, challenging the assumption that each organism belongs to exactly one kingdom.

Discuss the implications of the discovery of Archaea for our understanding of the origin of eukaryotic life.Hard
Show AnswerArchaea (discovered by Carl Woese, 1977) share features with eukaryotes (similar transcription factors, histone-like proteins, DNA replication machinery) and are found in extreme environments. The "Asgard archaea" (discovered 2015) are the closest prokaryotic relatives of eukaryotes. Evidence suggests eukaryotes arose from a merger between an Asgard archaeon (providing cellular machinery) and a bacterial endosymbiont (becoming mitochondria) — fundamentally reshaping our understanding of life's tree.

Darwin's Natural Selection

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

State the four key observations Darwin made that led to his theory of natural selection.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Overproduction: more offspring than can survive. (2) Variation: individuals differ. (3) Competition (struggle for survival). (4) Differential survival: those with favourable traits survive and reproduce more.

What does "survival of the fittest" mean in biological terms?Easy
Show AnswerIndividuals best adapted (most fit) to their environment survive long enough to reproduce and pass on their alleles — "fitness" means reproductive success, not physical strength.

What is a heritable variation?Easy
Show Answer

A variation in a trait that is encoded in genes and can be passed from parent to offspring.

Name the book Darwin published in 1859 outlining natural selection.Easy
Show Answer

On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection.

Which islands inspired Darwin's theory of evolution?Easy
Show Answer

The Galapagos Islands (off Ecuador in the Pacific Ocean).

What drives variation in a population at the genetic level?Easy
Show Answer

Mutations and sexual recombination (crossing over, independent assortment, fertilisation).

Define "adaptation" in evolutionary biology.Easy
Show Answer

A heritable trait that increases an organism's fitness (reproductive success) in its environment.

What is the role of the environment in natural selection?Easy
Show Answer

The environment acts as the selective pressure — it determines which traits are advantageous and which individuals survive and reproduce.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Describe the industrial melanism of the peppered moth as an example of natural selection in action.Medium
Show Answer

Before industrialisation, pale moths were camouflaged on lichen-covered bark; dark moths were eaten by birds (selected against). Industrial soot blackened tree bark; now dark moths were camouflaged and pale moths were more visible and eaten. Dark allele frequency rose — natural selection driven by an environmental change.

Explain why natural selection cannot act on genotype directly — only on phenotype.Medium
Show AnswerPredators, diseases, and environmental pressures interact with the observable organism (phenotype). A lethal recessive allele hidden in a heterozygote (phenotypically normal) is invisible to selection; only when expressed in a homozygous phenotype can selection act on it.

Explain how Darwin's finches illustrate adaptive radiation.Medium
Show AnswerA common ancestor arrived in the Galapagos; different islands had different food sources (seeds, insects, cacti). Natural selection favoured different beak shapes on each island. Over time, finches diversified into ~15 species with specialised beaks — all from one ancestral species.

Distinguish between directional, stabilising, and disruptive selection with one example of each.Medium
Show AnswerDirectional: selection favours one extreme — bacteria evolving resistance (one allele frequency increases). Stabilising: extreme traits selected against — human birth weight (very small/large babies have lower survival). Disruptive: both extremes favoured — African seedcracker finch bills (small or large, not medium).

Why do populations evolve but individuals do not?Medium
Show AnswerAn individual's genotype is fixed at conception. Evolution is a change in allele frequencies across a population over generations. Selection acts on individuals, but evolution (measured as allele frequency change) occurs at the population level.

Describe one limitation of Darwin's original theory that was later filled by Mendelian genetics.Medium
Show AnswerDarwin could not explain the mechanism of inheritance or why variation didn't "blend out" over generations. Mendelian genetics (rediscovered 1900) showed that alleles are discrete particles, inherited independently — explaining how variation is maintained. The Modern Synthesis (1940s) united Darwin and Mendel.

Explain why overproduction of offspring is necessary for natural selection to work.Medium
Show AnswerIf all offspring survived, there would be no competition and no differential survival. Overproduction creates a "struggle for existence" — only some survive, and those with advantageous traits are more likely to be among the survivors.

How does sexual selection differ from natural selection? Give an example.Medium
Show AnswerSexual selection is driven by mate choice or competition for mates, not environmental survival. It can favour traits that reduce survival (e.g., a peacock's heavy tail makes it more visible to predators) if they increase mating success. Natural selection and sexual selection can work in opposite directions.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate why natural selection is considered a non-random process despite depending on random mutations.Hard
Show AnswerMutations are random (undirected), but which mutations are beneficial depends on the environment — a non-random filter. Selection consistently favours traits that improve reproductive success in the current environment. Over generations, this non-random differential reproduction produces the appearance of design (adaptations), making evolution directional even though its raw material (mutation) is random.

Analyse the concept of "evolutionary arms races" using the example of predator–prey or host–parasite co-evolution.Hard
Show AnswerIn a host-parasite arms race: as the host evolves resistance, the parasite evolves counter-resistance; neither gains a permanent advantage ("Red Queen" dynamics). Example: Plasmodium vs human immune system — humans evolved sickle-cell allele for partial malaria resistance; Plasmodium evolved to partly circumvent it. This reciprocal natural selection drives rapid evolutionary change in both lineages.

Discuss Lamarck's theory of inheritance of acquired characteristics and explain why Darwin's mechanism superseded it.Hard
Show AnswerLamarck proposed that traits acquired during an organism's lifetime (e.g., a giraffe stretching its neck) are passed to offspring. Darwin proposed that variation already exists in a population; individuals with beneficial traits leave more offspring. Lamarck's theory was disproved: somatic cell changes don't alter germline DNA (Weismann barrier). Darwin's mechanism is supported by genetics and molecular biology.

Critically evaluate the role of genetic drift vs natural selection in small isolated populations.Hard
Show AnswerIn large populations, natural selection dominates allele frequency change. In small populations, random fluctuations (genetic drift) can fix or eliminate alleles regardless of fitness — a neutral allele can become universal by chance (founder effect, bottleneck). This can actually reduce fitness. Both forces act simultaneously; their relative importance depends on population size.

Explain how the concept of inclusive fitness (kin selection) extends Darwin's theory of individual selection.Hard
Show AnswerDarwin's theory focused on individual reproductive success. Hamilton (1964) showed that altruistic behaviour (reducing own fitness to help relatives) can be selected for if the benefit to the relative, weighted by genetic relatedness (r), exceeds the cost (rB > C, Hamilton's rule). This explains worker bees sacrificing reproduction for the queen — they share ~75% of genes with sisters.

Analyse how epigenetics challenges the strict neo-Darwinian view that only DNA sequence changes are heritable.Hard
Show AnswerNeo-Darwinism holds that only DNA mutations create heritable variation. However, epigenetic marks (DNA methylation, histone modification) can be inherited across generations (transgenerational epigenetics), e.g., Dutch Hunger Winter studies show grandchildren of starved women have altered metabolic gene expression. This suggests acquired environmental experiences can influence offspring phenotype — a partial vindication of Lamarck — though the mechanism is distinct from Lamarck's proposal.

Discuss the philosophical implications of natural selection for concepts of purpose and design in nature.Hard
Show AnswerNatural selection produces the appearance of design (complex adaptations like the eye) without a designer. Richard Dawkins' "blind watchmaker" metaphor: selection acts as a cumulative non-random filter, producing functional complexity over deep time. Philosophically, this challenges teleological (purpose-driven) views of nature. However, it does not disprove a broader metaphysical design — it only explains the mechanism without reference to intent.

Evaluate evidence that evolution by natural selection is still occurring in modern human populations.Hard
Show AnswerEvidence: (1) Lactase persistence — allele frequency for adult lactose digestion has risen dramatically in cattle-herding populations in the last 8,000 years. (2) Tibetans evolved EPAS1 allele for high-altitude hypoxia tolerance in ~3,000 years. (3) CCR5-Δ32 allele (HIV resistance) rising in frequency. Modern medicine reduces some selection pressures but new ones (disease, diet, reproductive technology) continue to drive allele frequency change.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Name four types of evidence used to support the theory of evolution.Easy
Show Answer

Fossils, homologous structures, DNA similarity, embryology, and vestigial structures (any four).

What is a fossil?Easy
Show AnswerThe preserved remains, impressions, or traces of organisms that lived in the past, found in sedimentary rock.

What are homologous structures? Give two examples.Easy
Show AnswerStructures in different species with the same basic anatomy due to shared ancestry, even if they have different functions. E.g., human arm, whale flipper, bat wing, horse leg — all are modified pentadactyl limbs.

What is a vestigial structure? Give one human example.Easy
Show AnswerA reduced structure that had a function in an ancestor but no longer serves a clear purpose. E.g., the human coccyx (tailbone), appendix, or arrector pili (goosebumps muscles).

How does DNA similarity provide evidence for evolution?Easy
Show AnswerClosely related species share more DNA sequence similarity than distantly related ones. E.g., humans and chimpanzees share ~98.7% DNA, reflecting a recent common ancestor.

What are analogous structures? Give an example.Easy
Show AnswerStructures with similar function but different origin, arising by convergent evolution. E.g., a bird's wing and an insect's wing — both used for flight but not inherited from a common winged ancestor.

How do embryos provide evidence for evolution?Easy
Show AnswerEarly embryos of vertebrates (fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals) look remarkably similar, showing gill slits and tails — suggesting descent from a common aquatic ancestor.

What is a transitional fossil? Give one example.Easy
Show AnswerA fossil that shows features intermediate between an ancestral group and its descendants. E.g., Archaeopteryx — shows both reptile features (teeth, bony tail) and bird features (feathers, wishbone).

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain how radiometric dating is used to determine the age of fossils.Medium
Show AnswerRadioactive isotopes (e.g., carbon-14 for recent fossils; potassium-40 for older ones) decay at known rates (half-lives). By measuring the ratio of parent to daughter isotope remaining in rock layers, scientists calculate how long ago the organism lived.

Explain why the fossil record is incomplete and what gaps tell us.Medium
Show AnswerMost organisms do not fossilise — they decay. Fossilisation requires specific conditions (rapid burial in sediment). Soft-bodied organisms rarely fossilise. Gaps in the record may reflect periods of rapid evolution or lack of preservation; transitional fossils continue to be discovered, progressively filling gaps.

How do homologous structures support common ancestry?Medium
Show AnswerThe pentadactyl (five-digit) limb in humans, whales, bats, and horses shows the same bones (humerus, radius/ulna, carpals, metacarpals, phalanges) despite different functions. This structural similarity is best explained by inheritance from a common ancestor — it would be extremely unlikely to arise independently so many times.

Compare the evidence from comparative embryology with the evidence from DNA analysis for evolution.Medium
Show AnswerEmbryology shows morphological similarity in early development, suggesting shared genetic developmental programmes. DNA analysis quantifies genetic similarity directly, providing a molecular "family tree." DNA evidence is more precise and measurable; embryology is more visual but less quantitative. Both independently support common ancestry.

Why is the human appendix considered a vestigial structure, and how does it support evolution?Medium
Show AnswerIn human ancestors (herbivores), a large caecum with appendix helped digest cellulose. Humans evolved a different diet; the appendix shrank but was not eliminated. Its presence (with no essential function) makes no sense unless humans descended from organisms that used it — supporting evolutionary continuity.

Explain how biogeography (distribution of species) provides evidence for evolution.Medium
Show AnswerSpecies are found where their ancestors were; related species cluster in geographic areas. E.g., marsupials are abundant in Australia (isolated from placental mammals after Gondwana split). Galapagos finches are related to South American finches — colonising ancestors evolved in isolation. Patterns only make sense if species evolved from local ancestors.

How do shared non-functional "junk" DNA sequences (e.g., endogenous retroviruses) support common ancestry?Medium
Show AnswerEndogenous retroviruses (ERVs) are ancient viral insertions in host DNA. Humans and chimpanzees share many of the same ERVs at identical genomic locations — the chance of independent viral insertions at the exact same location is astronomically small. Shared ERVs are best explained by a common ancestor who carried the original insertion.

Evaluate molecular clocks as a method for dating evolutionary divergence.Medium
Show AnswerMolecular clocks use the rate of neutral mutation accumulation over time to estimate when two lineages diverged. Advantage: can date divergence even without fossils; works for any species with sequenced DNA. Limitation: mutation rates vary between lineages and genes; must be calibrated with fossil dates; gives estimates with large error ranges.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Critically analyse why the convergent evolution of the eye in vertebrates and cephalopods strengthens rather than weakens evolutionary theory.Hard
Show AnswerSome creationists argue the eye's complexity refutes Darwin. However, the cephalopod and vertebrate eyes, though superficially similar, differ fundamentally (inverted vs non-inverted retina, different photoreceptor molecules). They evolved independently from simple light-sensitive patches — demonstrating that natural selection can repeatedly produce functional complexity from simple beginnings, strengthening the power of natural selection as a mechanism.

Analyse the whale fossil series (Pakicetus → Ambulocetus → Rodhocetus → modern whale) as evidence for macroevolution.Hard
Show AnswerPakicetus (55 Mya): land mammal, small, dog-sized. Ambulocetus (49 Mya): semi-aquatic, could walk and swim. Rodhocetus (47 Mya): short limbs, streamlined. Modern whale: no hind limbs externally, vestigial pelvic bones internally. DNA confirms whales are most closely related to hippos. This series provides a nearly complete transitional sequence, directly contradicting the claim of "no transitional fossils."

Discuss how the discovery of CRISPR-edited gene sequences in natural bacteria populations blurs the distinction between molecular evidence for evolution and genetic engineering.Hard
Show AnswerCRISPR-Cas9 in bacteria is a natural adaptive immune system that incorporates foreign viral DNA into the bacterial genome — a form of directed genomic modification by the organism itself. This shows that natural mechanisms exist for targeted DNA insertion, which evolutionary theory must accommodate. It also demonstrates that the boundary between "natural" genomic change and "engineered" change is less clear than assumed.

Evaluate the relative strength of different evidence types for evolution if you could only use two to convince a sceptic.Hard
Show AnswerDNA evidence is the most powerful: quantifiable, objective, and directly links molecular ancestry — the genetic code itself is the same in all life, implying common origin. Direct observation of evolution (antibiotic resistance, lab experiments with E. coli — Lenski's experiment) provides real-time evidence. These two combine molecular ancestry with observed mechanism, making the strongest possible case.

Explain how whole-genome duplication events in evolutionary history complicate simple DNA similarity comparisons.Hard
Show AnswerPolyploidy (genome duplication) produces extra gene copies that can diverge in function over time (paralogs). When comparing species, paralogs may be mistaken for orthologs (true evolutionary counterparts), inflating or obscuring true similarity. E.g., many plant genomes have undergone multiple duplications; comparing specific gene families requires distinguishing paralogs from orthologs using phylogenetic analysis.

Analyse the Lenski long-term evolution experiment (LTEE) as direct experimental evidence for evolution.Hard
Show AnswerSince 1988, Lenski has grown 12 E. coli populations in glucose medium. Over 70,000+ generations: fitness has increased ~70%; genome sequencing confirms specific mutations fixed by selection; crucially, one population evolved the ability to metabolise citrate (a novel metabolic trait) — demonstrating macroevolutionary-scale change in real time. Frozen "fossil" bacteria allow replay experiments, confirming which mutations were key.

Discuss how ancient DNA analysis (paleogenomics) has revised our understanding of human evolution since 2010.Hard
Show AnswerSvante Pääbo's team sequenced the Neanderthal genome (2010) and discovered ~2% Neanderthal DNA in non-African modern humans — proving interbreeding. Denisovans were discovered entirely from DNA in a finger bone (2010). Ancient DNA shows multiple hominin lineages coexisted and interbred. This overturned a simple "Out of Africa replacement" model and reveals human evolution as a complex network of introgression events.

Critically evaluate whether the evidence for evolution constitutes "proof" or "strong scientific consensus."Hard
Show AnswerIn science, "proof" is a mathematical concept; scientific theories are never proven but can be falsified (Popper). Evolution is the strongest scientific consensus in biology — supported by convergent evidence from palaeontology, genetics, embryology, biogeography, and direct observation. No credible evidence contradicts it; all attempts to falsify it have failed. A Theory in science denotes a well-substantiated, well-tested explanation — not a guess. Calling evolution "just a theory" misunderstands scientific terminology.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

What are antibiotics?Easy
Show AnswerMedicines that kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria; they do not work against viruses.

What does "antibiotic resistance" mean?Easy
Show AnswerThe ability of bacteria to survive and reproduce despite the presence of an antibiotic that would normally kill them.

How does a random mutation contribute to antibiotic resistance?Easy
Show AnswerA random mutation may alter the bacterial target site, produce an enzyme that breaks down the antibiotic, or pump it out — making the bacterium resistant by chance before ever encountering the antibiotic.

What is MRSA, and why is it dangerous?Easy
Show AnswerMethicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus — a bacterium resistant to many antibiotics, causing infections difficult to treat; common in hospitals.

Name one way humans have accelerated the development of antibiotic resistance.Easy
Show AnswerNot completing a full course of antibiotics (leaving partially resistant bacteria to survive and reproduce).

What is the natural "selective pressure" in antibiotic resistance evolution?Easy
Show AnswerThe antibiotic itself — it kills susceptible bacteria but not resistant ones, so resistant bacteria survive and reproduce.

Why do viruses not develop antibiotic resistance?Easy
Show AnswerAntibiotics target bacterial cell structures (cell walls, ribosomes). Viruses lack these structures, so antibiotics have no target in viruses.

State one way antibiotic use in agriculture contributes to resistance in humans.Easy
Show AnswerAntibiotics fed to livestock select for resistant bacteria; resistant strains can transfer to humans via food, contact, or shared environment.

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Explain step-by-step how natural selection produces an antibiotic-resistant bacterial population.Medium
Show Answer(1) Random mutations in a few bacteria produce resistance. (2) Antibiotic introduced — kills susceptible bacteria. (3) Resistant bacteria survive, reproduce. (4) Resistance allele is passed to offspring. (5) Over many generations, the entire population becomes resistant.

Why does stopping antibiotic treatment early increase resistance risk?Medium
Show AnswerEarly in treatment, antibiotic levels kill most susceptible bacteria (symptoms ease). Stopping early leaves partially resistant bacteria alive; they reproduce and may pass on resistance genes. Completing the full course eliminates all bacteria including those with slight resistance.

Explain how horizontal gene transfer (plasmid exchange) spreads resistance faster than mutation alone.Medium
Show AnswerBacteria share plasmids (small circular DNA) containing resistance genes via conjugation — even between different species. One mutation event can rapidly spread resistance genes to millions of bacteria in a population, far faster than waiting for independent mutations to arise in each lineage.

Describe two strategies used by bacteria to resist antibiotics at the molecular level.Medium
Show Answer1. Enzyme production: e.g., beta-lactamase breaks down penicillin. 2. Efflux pumps: protein channels actively pump antibiotics out of the cell before they can act.

Why is tuberculosis (TB) particularly challenging to treat today?Medium
Show AnswerMycobacterium tuberculosis has evolved multi-drug resistant (MDR-TB) and extensively drug-resistant (XDR-TB) strains through mutation and improper treatment. It infects 10M+ people/year; XDR-TB strains require 18–24 months of toxic second-line drugs with lower success rates.

Explain why the development of new antibiotics has slowed down, from a commercial and scientific perspective.Medium
Show AnswerScientific: most easy antibiotic targets in bacteria have already been exploited; finding new targets is difficult. Commercial: antibiotics are taken short-term, providing lower return than long-term drugs (e.g., statins); pharmaceutical companies invest less. WHO has designated antibiotic resistance a global health emergency.

Compare the time scales of antibiotic resistance evolution in bacteria with evolution of large animals. Why the difference?Medium
Show AnswerBacteria: resistance can develop in days/weeks — E. coli divides every 20 minutes; millions of generations occur rapidly, producing many mutations. Large animals: generations take years; far fewer individuals. Evolution in bacteria is observable within human lifetimes because of extremely rapid reproduction rates.

Describe the "antibiotic ladder" approach to treatment and how it aims to slow resistance.Medium
Show AnswerFirst-line (common, inexpensive) antibiotics are used first; second-line (broader spectrum, more expensive) reserved for resistant infections; third-line (last resort) used only when others fail. This limits exposure to powerful antibiotics, reducing selection pressure for resistance to critical drugs.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Evaluate why antibiotic resistance is considered by WHO as "one of the biggest threats to global health, food security, and development."Hard
Show AnswerBy 2050, resistance could kill 10M/year (surpassing cancer). Modern medicine depends on antibiotics: surgeries, chemotherapy, and organ transplants all require antibiotics to prevent infection. Food security: resistant bacteria in livestock reduce treatment options and can spread to humans. Development: AMR disproportionately affects low-income countries with less healthcare capacity.

Analyse the ESKAPE pathogens and explain what makes each dangerous from an evolutionary perspective.Hard
Show AnswerESKAPE: Enterococcus faecium, Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), Klebsiella pneumoniae, Acinetobacter baumannii, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Enterobacter spp. Each has evolved multiple resistance mechanisms (enzyme production, efflux pumps, target modification, reduced membrane permeability) and spreads resistance genes via plasmids. Their rapid evolution makes them "priority pathogens" for new drug development.

Critically assess phage therapy as an alternative to antibiotics for resistant bacterial infections.Hard
Show AnswerBacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria) can target specific bacteria with high precision. Advantages: highly specific (doesn't disrupt microbiome); bacteria can evolve phage resistance, but this can sometimes re-sensitise them to antibiotics; phages co-evolve with bacteria. Challenges: narrower host range than antibiotics; regulatory hurdles; need patient-specific phage cocktails; limited clinical trial data. Several case studies (e.g., UC San Diego, 2016) show phage therapy as successful last resort.

Discuss the ethical and policy dimensions of antibiotic use in agriculture in the context of a global health commons.Hard
Show AnswerAgricultural antibiotic use (60% of global antibiotic use) accelerates resistance for human health benefit in livestock production. As a "tragedy of the commons," individual farmers benefit but the entire human population bears the cost of reduced antibiotic effectiveness. The EU has banned growth-promoter antibiotic use; other countries lag. Policy solutions: global monitoring, financial incentives for farmers to reduce use, development of alternatives (vaccines, probiotics).

Explain how bacteriophage–bacteria co-evolution is a real-world example of the Red Queen hypothesis.Hard
Show AnswerPhages evolve to infect bacteria; bacteria evolve resistance (e.g., modifying receptor sites, CRISPR immunity); phages evolve to overcome resistance; and so on indefinitely. Neither gains a permanent advantage — both must "run" (evolve) continuously just to maintain current fitness levels. This reciprocal co-evolution mirrors the Red Queen dynamic of host-parasite arms races.

How does the CRISPR-Cas system in bacteria function as an adaptive immune system, and how does this relate to the evolutionary biology of bacteria?Hard
Show AnswerCRISPR-Cas captures fragments of invading viral DNA into the bacterial genome as "memory" sequences. On re-infection, RNA guides direct Cas nucleases to cut the viral DNA. This is a form of acquired immunity — a remarkable mechanism evolved under strong selection from bacteriophages. It shows that even simple prokaryotes have evolved sophisticated adaptive responses, and gives insight into the evolutionary arms race between bacteria and phages.

Assess the "antibiotic stewardship" movement — what it is, why it matters, and whether it is sufficient to address the AMR crisis.Hard
Show AnswerAntibiotic stewardship: policies ensuring antibiotics are prescribed and used correctly — right drug, dose, duration, only when needed. It reduces selection pressure for resistance. Evidence: countries with strict stewardship (e.g., Scandinavia) have much lower resistance rates. However, stewardship alone is insufficient: the drug pipeline must be renewed; agricultural use must be curbed; global enforcement is needed. Low-income countries lack capacity for stewardship programmes — a global solution requires both supply-side (new drugs) and demand-side (stewardship) interventions.

Critically evaluate antibiotic resistance as "the best real-world proof of evolution by natural selection."Hard
Show AnswerAntibiotic resistance demonstrates all elements of natural selection: pre-existing heritable variation (random mutations); differential survival (antibiotic kills susceptible bacteria); reproduction of survivors; inheritance of resistance by offspring; observable change in population over short timescales. Unlike most macroevolution, it is observable in human lifetimes and has been replicated thousands of times in labs and hospitals. It is arguably the most direct, replicable, and medically significant demonstration of Darwinian evolution.

Set AEasy – Recall & Identify

Define speciation.Easy
Show AnswerThe evolutionary process by which new, distinct species arise from existing species through accumulated genetic differences leading to reproductive isolation.

What is allopatric speciation?Easy
Show AnswerSpeciation caused by geographic isolation — a physical barrier separates a population, which then evolves independently until the two groups can no longer interbreed.

What is sympatric speciation?Easy
Show AnswerSpeciation occurring within the same geographic area, driven by disruptive selection, polyploidy, or ecological differentiation — no physical barrier required.

Give one example of a geographic barrier that could cause allopatric speciation.Easy
Show AnswerA mountain range, ocean, river, or desert (e.g., the Andes separating populations on each side).

What does "reproductive isolation" mean?Easy
Show AnswerTwo populations cannot interbreed to produce fertile offspring — a key criterion for being classified as separate species.

How do the Galapagos finches illustrate allopatric speciation?Easy
Show AnswerFinches colonised different islands (geographic isolation); different food sources on each island drove different beak evolution; over time, they became so different they could no longer interbreed — forming ~15 separate species.

Name the type of selection most likely to lead to sympatric speciation.Easy
Show AnswerDisruptive selection (selects for two different extreme phenotypes within one population, splitting it).

State one pre-zygotic and one post-zygotic reproductive barrier.Easy
Show AnswerPre-zygotic: temporal isolation (breed at different times of year). Post-zygotic: hybrid infertility (e.g., mule — horse × donkey — is sterile).

Set BMedium – Apply & Explain

Outline the steps of allopatric speciation from one original population to two new species.Medium
Show Answer(1) One population exists with genetic variation. (2) Geographic barrier arises, splitting population into two. (3) Each subpopulation experiences different mutations, genetic drift, and selection pressures. (4) Genetic differences accumulate over generations. (5) If barrier removed, populations cannot interbreed → two species.

Explain why reproductive isolation is the key criterion for speciation rather than just physical difference.Medium
Show AnswerTwo organisms may look different (e.g., dog breeds) but if they can interbreed and produce fertile offspring, they are still the same species. Reproductive isolation ensures gene pools remain separate, allowing independent evolution — the defining feature of separate species.

Describe how polyploidy can cause rapid sympatric speciation in plants.Medium
Show AnswerIf a plant hybridises with a related species and the hybrid undergoes chromosome doubling (allopolyploidy), it becomes reproductively isolated from both parent species (cannot make compatible gametes with them) but can self-fertilise or mate with identical polyploids — instantly creating a new species. E.g., bread wheat is a hexaploid from three ancestral species.

Distinguish between prezygotic and postzygotic reproductive barriers with two examples of each.Medium
Show AnswerPrezygotic (prevent mating/fertilisation): temporal isolation (different mating seasons), mechanical isolation (incompatible genitalia or flower structures), behavioural isolation (different courtship displays). Postzygotic (after fertilisation): hybrid inviability (hybrid embryo dies), hybrid sterility (mule is sterile).

Explain the "founder effect" and how it can accelerate speciation.Medium
Show AnswerA small group ("founders") colonises a new area, carrying only a subset of the original gene pool's alleles. Genetic drift and new selection pressures in the new environment drive rapid differentiation from the source population. E.g., Pacific island colonisation by a few individuals led to rapid diversification of species.

How do ring species challenge the strict species boundary?Medium
Show AnswerRing species (e.g., Ensatina salamanders around California's Central Valley) form a chain: each neighbouring population interbreeds with adjacent ones, but the two "end" populations that overlap cannot interbreed. This shows speciation is a continuum, not a sudden event, and that species boundaries are sometimes arbitrary human constructs.

Explain how sexual selection can drive speciation within a population without geographic isolation.Medium
Show AnswerIf females in a population develop a preference for a specific male trait, and males with that trait are more reproductively successful, allele frequencies diverge between "preferred" and "non-preferred" groups. Over time, assortative mating reduces gene flow between subgroups, potentially leading to reproductive isolation — sympatric speciation via sexual selection.

Compare the rates of speciation proposed by gradualism vs punctuated equilibrium.Medium
Show AnswerGradualism: species change slowly and continuously; speciation takes millions of years. Punctuated equilibrium (Gould & Eldredge, 1972): long periods of stasis punctuated by rapid bursts of speciation (thousands of years). Fossil evidence (species appearing suddenly in the record with little change thereafter) supports punctuated equilibrium; molecular data supports some gradualism. Both likely occur in different lineages.

Set CHard – Analyse & Evaluate

Critically evaluate the role of genetic drift vs natural selection in driving speciation in small isolated populations.Hard
Show AnswerIn small isolated populations (e.g., island colonisers), genetic drift can fix neutral or even slightly deleterious alleles randomly — independent of selection. Over time, drift creates significant genetic divergence from the mainland. Combined with local selection pressures, this accelerates speciation. In very small populations, drift may dominate selection; in large populations, selection is the primary driver of change.

Analyse why the Galapagos Islands are considered a "natural laboratory for evolution."Hard
Show AnswerIslands of different sizes, ages, and distances from mainland provide replicated natural experiments. Each island has distinct environments; colonising species face different selective pressures. Geographic isolation between islands creates conditions for allopatric speciation. Young geological age (3–5 Mya) allows study of rapid speciation. Real-time data (Peter and Rosemary Grant's 40-year finch studies) captured natural selection occurring within decades.

Discuss how urban environments are driving rapid evolution and potential speciation of city-dwelling species.Hard
Show AnswerUrban heat islands, altered food sources, light/noise pollution, and reduced migration create new selection pressures. Evidence: urban white-footed mice in New York show distinct genetic variants from rural mice; urban blackbirds in Europe sing at higher frequencies to be heard over traffic noise. Over centuries, reproductive isolation between urban and rural populations could lead to speciation — "urban evolution" is an emerging research field.

Explain how hybridisation followed by polyploidy has produced many crop species, giving a specific example.Hard
Show AnswerBread wheat (Triticum aestivum, 6n=42) resulted from hybridisation of three grass species (T. urartu, Aegilops speltoides, Ae. tauschii) followed by two polyploidy events. Each hybridisation doubled the chromosome number; the hexaploid was reproductively isolated from its parents but could self-fertilise — a new species. This process (allopolyploidy) is common in crop plants (cotton, canola, tobacco).

Analyse the evidence from cichlid fish in African rift lakes for extremely rapid speciation.

Hard
Show AnswerLake Victoria's ~500 cichlid species diversified in ~15,000 years (after the lake refilled) — among the fastest known speciation events. Driven by: sexual selection (females choose males by colour, creating assortative mating rapidly); ecological opportunity (empty niches); and the lake's physical structure creating semi-isolated bays. DNA analysis confirms all descended from a small founding population, making cichlids a premier model for adaptive radiation.

Discuss how horizontal gene transfer in prokaryotes complicates the species concept and speciation models developed for sexually reproducing organisms.Hard
Show AnswerSexual speciation models rely on reproductive isolation reducing gene flow. Bacteria exchange genes across species boundaries (HGT) — including antibiotic resistance, metabolic genes, virulence factors. This means prokaryotic "species" share genes with unrelated organisms, creating a gene-sharing network rather than a diverging tree. Bacterial speciation is better described by ecological differentiation and niche adaptation; the biological species concept applies poorly to bacteria.

Evaluate the conservation implications of the speciation continuum — does it matter if two populations are "different species" for conservation purposes?Hard
Show AnswerLegal protection often depends on species status (e.g., the US Endangered Species Act). If two evolutionarily distinct populations are classified as one species, each subpopulation may receive insufficient protection. The Evolutionarily Significant Unit (ESU) concept addresses this: populations representing distinct evolutionary lineages warrant protection even if not fully separate species. Conservation biology increasingly focuses on preserving evolutionary potential, not just species counts.

Synthesise the mechanisms of speciation (allopatric, sympatric, polyploidy, genetic drift) into a unified framework explaining why Earth has ~8.7 million species.Hard
Show AnswerEarth's diversity arises from: (1) Allopatric speciation: geographic barriers (mountains, oceans, ice ages) repeatedly isolate populations → natural selection and drift produce divergence → reproductive isolation → new species. (2) Sympatric speciation: disruptive selection and polyploidy allow splitting without barriers, especially in plants. (3) Adaptive radiation: when lineages colonise new environments (islands, after mass extinctions), rapid diversification fills ecological niches. (4) Deep time: ~3.8 billion years of accumulation. Extinction removes species but speciation has outpaced it, producing the current diversity.
