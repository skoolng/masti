What are the three states of water?Easy

Solid (ice), liquid (water), and gas (water vapour / steam).

In which state does water have a fixed shape and fixed volume?Easy
Show Answer

Solid state (ice). Ice holds a definite shape and volume.

What is the scientific name for water in its gaseous state?Easy
Show Answer

Water vapour (steam is water vapour visible as white wisps just above boiling water).

Which state of water takes the shape of its container?Easy
Show Answer

Liquid water — it has a fixed volume but takes the shape of the container.

Is ice the same chemical substance as liquid water? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

Yes. All three states are H₂O — only the arrangement and energy of molecules differs, not the chemical composition.

Name one everyday example of water in solid form.Easy
Show Answer

Ice cubes, snow, hail, glaciers, frost — any one is correct.

What determines which state water exists in?Easy
Show Answer

Temperature (and pressure). At ordinary pressures, below 0 °C → solid; 0–100 °C → liquid; above 100 °C → gas.

True or False: Water vapour is invisible.Easy
Show Answer

True. Water vapour is a colourless, invisible gas. The white mist you see above a kettle is tiny liquid droplets formed when vapour cools.

Which state of water has molecules that are farthest apart?Easy
Show Answer

Gaseous state (water vapour) — molecules are widely spaced and move freely.

What physical property makes ice less dense than liquid water?Easy
Show Answer

Ice forms a hexagonal crystalline lattice that spaces molecules further apart than in liquid water, making it less dense — which is why ice floats.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Compare the arrangement of molecules in ice, liquid water, and water vapour.Medium
Show Answer

Ice: molecules held in a rigid, orderly lattice, vibrating in place. Liquid water: molecules close together but moving freely past each other. Water vapour: molecules far apart, moving rapidly and randomly with negligible attraction.

Why does a liquid take the shape of its container but a solid does not?Medium
Show Answer

In a liquid, intermolecular forces allow molecules to slide past each other, conforming to any shape. In a solid, molecules are locked in fixed positions by strong forces, maintaining a rigid shape.

A student fills a balloon with water vapour and another with ice-cold air. Which balloon will be heavier for the same volume? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

The one with ice-cold air — cold air is denser than warm, moist air. Surprisingly, humid air (water vapour mixed) is slightly lighter than dry air because water (M=18) is lighter than N₂ (M=28) or O₂ (M=32).

Explain why water is called a "substance with unusual properties."Medium
Show Answer

Unlike most substances, water's solid form (ice) is less dense than its liquid form. It also has high surface tension, high specific heat, and is a near-universal solvent — all due to hydrogen bonding between H₂O molecules.

A glacier in Antarctica is water in which state? What does this tell you about the temperature there?Medium
Show Answer

Solid state. It tells us temperatures in Antarctica are at or below 0 °C for prolonged periods, keeping water frozen as ice/snow.

Why does steam cause worse burns than boiling water at the same 100 °C?Medium
Show Answer

Steam carries extra energy as latent heat of vaporisation. When it condenses on skin, it releases this additional heat (≈2260 J/g) on top of the heat at 100 °C, causing more severe burns.

How do you convert liquid water to water vapour without heating it to 100 °C?Medium
Show Answer

By evaporation — surface molecules with enough kinetic energy escape into the air at temperatures below 100 °C. Spreading water over a larger area or blowing wind also speeds this up.

List one real-life application that depends on the fact that water expands when it freezes.Medium
Show Answer

Pipes bursting in winter — water expands ~9% on freezing, exerting enough force to crack metal pipes. Also: weathering of rocks (frost action), ice floats protecting aquatic life beneath.

A bottle of water is left in a freezer and it cracks. Why?Medium
Show Answer

Water expands when it freezes (becomes ~9% larger in volume). The expanding ice exerts outward pressure on the rigid bottle walls, causing them to crack.

In which state do water molecules have the most kinetic energy? In which do they have the least?Medium
Show Answer

Most kinetic energy: gaseous state (water vapour) — molecules move rapidly and freely. Least kinetic energy: solid state (ice) — molecules only vibrate about fixed positions.

Design an experiment to demonstrate that ice, liquid water, and water vapour are the same substance. Describe your method, expected observations, and conclusion.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Take an ice cube, heat gently until it melts (0 °C), then heat further until it boils (100 °C) and collects as condensed liquid in a cooled tube. Observation: mass before and after is identical; the condensed liquid still has properties of water (tasteless, neutral pH, dissolves salt). Conclusion: Only the physical state changed — the substance (H₂O) remains the same throughout, confirming all three states are the same material.

Compare and contrast the intermolecular forces in ice and liquid water. How do these differences explain that ice floats on water?Hard
Show Answer

In ice, hydrogen bonds lock molecules in a hexagonal open lattice — a highly ordered structure with gaps, making it less dense (≈917 kg/m³). In liquid water, hydrogen bonds break and reform constantly; molecules pack more closely (≈1000 kg/m³). Since ice is less dense, it floats. This has the crucial ecological consequence of insulating liquid water below, preserving aquatic life in winter.

If Earth's average temperature rose by 5 °C due to climate change, predict and justify the impact on the distribution of water in its three states.Hard
Show Answer

Solid decreases: glaciers and polar ice caps melt faster, shrinking the global solid-water store. Liquid increases: meltwater raises sea levels; more precipitation as liquid rather than snow. Gas increases: warmer temperatures increase evaporation, raising atmospheric water vapour. Net result: sea levels rise (threatening coastal cities), freshwater glaciers (drinking water for 2 billion) shrink, and the hydrological cycle intensifies — more extreme rainfall and droughts.

Explain how the three states of water are involved in the formation of a snowflake from ocean water vapour.Hard
Show Answer

1. Liquid → Gas: Ocean water evaporates (liquid to vapour). 2. Gas rises and cools in the atmosphere. 3. Gas → Liquid: Water vapour condenses into tiny droplets around dust particles (cloud). 4. Liquid → Solid: If temperature drops below 0 °C, droplets freeze into ice crystals. 5. Ice crystals accumulate symmetrically (hydrogen bonding drives hexagonal growth) → snowflake. All three states are involved in sequence.

A student says "boiling water is hotter than steam." Is this correct? Use the concept of latent heat to evaluate.Hard
Show Answer

Incorrect in terms of thermal energy. Both boiling water and steam at 100 °C have the same temperature, but steam contains significantly more energy per gram (~2260 J/g more) as latent heat of vaporisation. When steam contacts skin or food, it releases this extra energy as it condenses, making it thermally more dangerous despite the same temperature reading.

Show Answer

Natural ecosystems (+): Ice floats, insulating water below → fish survive winter under ice cover. Rock weathering via freeze-thaw creates soil and habitats. Challenges for infrastructure (−): Water pipes burst in sub-zero winters, costing billions in repairs. Road surfaces crack (frost heave). Building foundations shift. Engineers must use insulated pipes, antifreeze additives, and leave expansion joints to manage this property.

Using particle theory, explain why a gas fills its entire container but a solid does not.Hard
Show Answer

In a gas, particles have very high kinetic energy and negligible intermolecular attractions. They move freely in all directions at high speed, spreading to fill any available space. In a solid, strong intermolecular forces hold particles tightly in fixed lattice positions; they can only vibrate, so the solid retains its own shape and does not fill the container. The key variable is the balance between kinetic energy and intermolecular forces.

0 °C (32 °F). This is called the melting point of water.

What is the process called when liquid water turns into ice?Easy

Freezing (also called solidification). It occurs at 0 °C under normal pressure.

Does ice absorb heat or release heat when it melts?Easy

Ice absorbs heat (takes energy from surroundings) when it melts.

Does water release heat or absorb heat when it freezes?Easy
Show Answer

Water releases heat to its surroundings when it freezes.

What is the melting point of water the same as?Easy
Show Answer

The melting point (0 °C) is the same as the freezing point — both phase changes occur at the same temperature.

Name the change of state: solid → liquid.Easy
Show Answer

Melting (fusion).

Name the change of state: liquid → solid.Easy
Show Answer

Freezing (solidification).

Why does an ice cube feel cold when you hold it?Easy
Show Answer

Ice absorbs heat from your hand (and the surroundings) to melt. Your hand loses heat, so it feels cold.

Is melting a physical or chemical change?Easy
Show Answer

Physical change — only the state changes, not the chemical composition. The H₂O molecules remain unchanged.

What happens to the temperature of ice as it melts (at 0 °C)?Easy
Show Answer

The temperature stays constant at 0 °C during melting. The absorbed heat goes into breaking intermolecular bonds (latent heat), not raising temperature.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain why the temperature remains at 0 °C while ice is melting, even though heat is being supplied.Medium
Show Answer

The heat supplied during melting is used entirely to break the hydrogen bonds holding molecules in the ice lattice — this is called latent heat of fusion. No energy is available to raise kinetic energy (temperature) until all the ice has melted. Temperature only rises again once the phase change is complete.

Why does adding salt to icy roads in winter help prevent skidding?Medium
Show Answer

Salt lowers the freezing point of water (freezing point depression). The mixture of salt + water freezes at a temperature below 0 °C, so ice melts or fails to re-form at temperatures just below 0 °C, making roads less slippery.

A glass of water is placed in a freezer at −18 °C. Describe what happens to the water molecules as the water turns to ice.Medium
Show Answer

As temperature drops, molecules lose kinetic energy and slow down. At 0 °C, they release latent heat and begin arranging into the hexagonal hydrogen-bonded lattice of ice. Once all water is frozen, temperature continues to drop toward −18 °C as the ice cools further.

Distinguish between melting point and freezing point. Are they the same for water?Medium
Show Answer

Melting point: temperature at which solid → liquid. Freezing point: temperature at which liquid → solid. For water, both = 0 °C at standard pressure — they are the same temperature, approached from opposite directions.

Why does ice cream melt faster on a hot day than in an air-conditioned room?Medium
Show Answer

On a hot day, there is a larger temperature difference between the ice cream (solid, 0 °C or below) and the surroundings. Heat flows faster from hotter environments, supplying latent heat more quickly, so melting is faster.

What is "latent heat of fusion"? Give the value for water.Medium
Show Answer

Latent heat of fusion is the energy absorbed per gram (or mole) of substance to change from solid to liquid at constant temperature. For water: approximately 334 J/g (334 kJ/kg).

A student observes that a puddle of water freezes overnight even though the temperature was only −2 °C. Is this normal? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

Yes, perfectly normal. Water freezes at 0 °C. If the temperature is −2 °C, it is below the freezing point, so liquid water will freeze. The rate may be slow at just −2 °C, but overnight is sufficient time.

How does the concept of melting and freezing explain why lakes freeze from the top down, not from the bottom up?Medium
Show Answer

Surface water cools first, reaches 0 °C, and freezes into a less-dense ice layer that floats. This ice layer insulates the water below, slowing further freezing. Water at the bottom (especially near 4 °C where it is densest) remains liquid, allowing aquatic life to survive.

Why is rock salt (impure NaCl) scattered on icy pavements more effective than pure water at the same temperature?Medium
Show Answer

Rock salt dissolves in the thin film of surface water on ice, forming a salt solution with a lower freezing point than 0 °C. This melts the ice at temperatures below 0 °C (down to about −9 °C for typical concentrations) without needing external heat.

Compare what happens at the particle level during melting versus freezing.Medium
Show Answer

Melting: particles absorb energy → bonds weaken → rigid lattice breaks down → particles slide freely (liquid). Freezing: particles lose energy → bonds strengthen → disordered liquid particles arrange into an ordered lattice → solid forms. Both occur at 0 °C for water.

The latent heat of fusion of water (334 J/g) is much higher than most substances. Evaluate how this property benefits life on Earth.Hard
Show Answer

A high latent heat means water absorbs a large amount of heat when melting. This stabilises temperatures: glaciers melt slowly (buffering global temperature), the human body's internal ice packs absorb large heat, and frozen foods stay cold longer. It also means freezing releases large heat, warming surrounding air — coastal climates are moderated. These properties make Earth habitable.

Critique this statement: "Water always freezes at exactly 0 °C." Is it always true? Under what conditions might it differ?Hard
Show Answer

Not always true. 0 °C is the freezing point at standard atmospheric pressure (1 atm) for pure water. Dissolved substances (salt, sugar) lower the freezing point (freezing point depression). Higher pressure slightly lowers the freezing point too. Supercooled water can remain liquid below 0 °C in very clean conditions. Pure water at high altitude (lower pressure) also has a slightly different freezing point. So the statement is a simplification valid only for pure water at 1 atm.

Analyse the role of melting glaciers in the global water crisis. Use data and scientific reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

Glaciers store about 69% of the world's freshwater as solid ice. As global temperatures rise (currently +1.1 °C above pre-industrial levels), glaciers melt at accelerating rates. In the short term, this increases river flow (benefitting populations dependent on glacial melt). Long term: glaciers disappear, rivers shrink, and freshwater supply collapses for ~2 billion people in Asia and South America. Meltwater also raises sea levels (~3 mm/year), threatening coastal freshwater aquifers with saltwater intrusion.

A food scientist wants to preserve fish by freezing at −30 °C. Explain the science of why freezing preserves food, and identify one potential quality concern.Hard
Show Answer

Freezing converts water inside cells to ice, drastically slowing bacterial growth and enzymatic reactions (which require liquid water as a medium). At −30 °C, virtually all microbial activity stops. Quality concern: ice crystals (especially large ones from slow freezing) can rupture cell walls, damaging texture and causing a mushy product on thawing. Rapid ("flash") freezing produces smaller crystals that cause less damage — hence commercial fish are blast-frozen quickly.

Design a controlled experiment to determine the latent heat of fusion of ice using simple school-lab equipment. List your variables, method, and how you would calculate the value.Hard
Show Answer

Variables: Independent — mass of ice; Dependent — temperature change of known water mass; Controlled — initial water temperature, calorimeter type, time. Method: Place a known mass of water at ~30 °C in an insulated calorimeter. Add a known mass of 0 °C ice (dried). Stir and record final temperature. Calculation: Heat lost by water = mass_water × 4.18 × ΔT_water. Heat gained by ice to melt + warm = m_ice × L + m_ice × 4.18 × T_final. Solve for L (latent heat). Compare to 334 J/g and calculate % error.

Evaluate whether the freezing of water in rock cracks (frost weathering) is beneficial or harmful to the environment overall.Hard
Show Answer

Beneficial: Frost weathering breaks rocks into smaller particles, creating soil over geological time — essential for agriculture and terrestrial ecosystems. It creates habitats (crevices, scree slopes) for specialist species. Harmful: In human infrastructure, it cracks roads, foundations, and historical monuments. In mountains, it triggers rockfalls that endanger lives. Overall: on geological timescales it is essential for soil formation; on human timescales it poses infrastructure and safety challenges.

Compare the energy changes in melting and freezing using a particle-level model and an energy diagram.Hard
Show Answer

Melting (endothermic): Energy absorbed from surroundings → particles gain energy → hydrogen bonds break → ordered solid lattice → disordered liquid. Energy diagram: energy of system increases at 0 °C (plateau). Freezing (exothermic): Energy released to surroundings → particles lose energy → hydrogen bonds form → disordered liquid → ordered lattice. Energy diagram: energy of system decreases at 0 °C. Both are reversible and occur at the same temperature (0 °C), involving exactly the same energy quantity (334 J/g) in opposite directions.

A student notices that a pond near their school is not frozen even though the temperature has been −5 °C for three days. Give three possible scientific explanations.Hard
Show Answer

1. Dissolved salts or pollutants in pond water lower the freezing point below −5 °C. 2. Wind action keeps water surface turbulent — moving water is harder to freeze as ice crystals cannot form stable lattices. 3. The pond may be deep with a large thermal mass; surface may be just above 0 °C due to heat stored in the deeper water taking time to dissipate, even though air is −5 °C.

Using the concept of latent heat, explain why melting snow and ice on mountainsides in spring does not cause instantaneous flooding of rivers.Hard
Show Answer

To melt, snow and ice must absorb latent heat of fusion (334 J/g) before any temperature rise or runoff occurs. Even at temperatures above 0 °C, large snow/ice masses absorb solar energy slowly. This means melting is gradual and spread over weeks or months, releasing meltwater at a controlled rate. Rivers rise slowly rather than flooding instantly. This "natural buffer" is critical for river management — it is why glacial-fed rivers flow year-round rather than in sudden pulses.

What is evaporation?Easy

Evaporation is the process by which liquid water changes into water vapour (gas) from the surface of a liquid, at temperatures below the boiling point.

From which part of a liquid does evaporation occur?Easy

From the surface only. Only surface molecules with enough kinetic energy escape into the air.

Does evaporation cool or warm the surrounding area? Give an example.Easy

Evaporation cools the surrounding area. Example: sweat evaporating from skin cools the body.

Why do wet clothes dry on a clothesline even on a cool day?Easy

Water evaporates from the surface of the wet fabric even below 100 °C. Wind and exposure to air carry the water vapour away, drying the clothes.

Can evaporation occur at room temperature (25 °C)?Easy

Yes. Evaporation can occur at any temperature above 0 °C, as long as the air above is not already saturated with water vapour.

Name one cooking example that involves evaporation.Easy
Show Answer

Boiling a pot of water with the lid off — water evaporates into steam. Also: reducing a sauce by simmering removes water by evaporation.

Why does a puddle disappear after rain on a sunny day?Easy
Show Answer

Sunlight heats the puddle water, providing energy for surface molecules to evaporate into the air. Wind also carries the vapour away, allowing more evaporation.

What happens to the molecules that escape during evaporation?Easy
Show Answer

They become water vapour — individual gas molecules that mix with the air and move freely in the atmosphere.

True or False: Evaporation requires the liquid to reach 100 °C.Easy
Show Answer

False. Evaporation occurs at any temperature below 100 °C, from the surface of a liquid. Boiling (throughout the liquid) requires 100 °C at sea level.

Why does alcohol feel cold when applied to your skin?Easy
Show Answer

Alcohol evaporates very quickly (high evaporation rate). It absorbs latent heat from your skin to evaporate, making the skin feel cold.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain, using particle theory, why only the most energetic molecules escape during evaporation.Medium
Show Answer

At any temperature, molecules in a liquid have a range of kinetic energies. Only those at the surface with enough energy to overcome the intermolecular attractive forces can escape into the gas phase. The remaining liquid molecules have, on average, lower kinetic energy — so the liquid's temperature drops (evaporative cooling).

Why does sweating cool the human body?Medium
Show Answer

Sweat (water) is secreted onto the skin surface. To evaporate, it absorbs latent heat of vaporisation from the skin (and underlying blood vessels). This heat removal cools the skin and, in turn, the blood, reducing core body temperature.

A farmer leaves two identical buckets of water — one in full sun and one in shade. Which loses water faster to evaporation? Explain.Medium
Show Answer

The bucket in full sun. Sunlight heats the water, giving more molecules sufficient energy to escape. Higher temperature = faster evaporation. The shaded bucket has less thermal energy, so fewer molecules escape per unit time.

How does evaporation differ from boiling? (Mention temperature and location within liquid.)Medium
Show Answer

Evaporation: occurs at any temperature, only at the surface of the liquid, slowly. Boiling: occurs at a specific temperature (100 °C at sea level), throughout the entire liquid, rapidly with bubbles forming.

Why does an earthen pot (clay pot) keep water cool?Medium
Show Answer

Clay is porous — tiny pores allow water to seep through to the outer surface. This water evaporates, absorbing latent heat from the pot and its contents. The evaporative cooling keeps the water inside cooler than the surrounding air temperature.

Why do coastal areas near the sea experience more humidity than inland areas?Medium
Show Answer

Large ocean surfaces constantly evaporate water into the air. Coastal winds carry this moist air inland, but the water vapour content (humidity) is highest near the source (ocean). Inland areas are farther from the evaporation source and receive drier air.

How does evaporation contribute to the water cycle?Medium
Show Answer

Evaporation is the primary mechanism that transfers liquid water from oceans, lakes, and rivers into the atmosphere as water vapour. This vapour rises, cools, condenses into clouds, and eventually falls as precipitation — completing the cycle and distributing freshwater across the globe.

A student notices that a glass of water left uncovered in a room overnight loses some water. Explain this observation.Medium
Show Answer

Evaporation occurs at room temperature (~25 °C). Surface water molecules with sufficient kinetic energy escape into the air above the glass. Over hours, enough molecules leave to noticeably reduce the water level, especially if the room has low humidity or air movement.

Why do deserts have very low humidity despite receiving strong sunlight?Medium
Show Answer

Deserts have very little surface water to evaporate in the first place — limited rainfall, no lakes or rivers. Even though high temperatures create ideal evaporation conditions, there is insufficient liquid water available, so humidity remains low.

Explain the concept of "evaporative cooling" with a real-world engineering application.Medium
Show Answer

Evaporative cooling: when water evaporates, it absorbs latent heat from its surroundings, cooling them. Application: desert coolers (swamp coolers) — a fan blows hot dry air through water-soaked pads. Water evaporates, cooling the air by 10–15 °C before it enters the room. Power stations also use cooling towers where hot water is sprayed and evaporated to cool industrial processes.

Evaluate the role of evaporation in regulating Earth's climate. Consider both local and global scales.Hard

Local: Evaporation from moist soils and vegetation (transpiration) cools air temperatures, creating cooler microclimates. Coastal regions are moderated by sea-surface evaporation. Why do humans sweat but dogs pant to cool down? Compare the evaporative cooling mechanisms at work.Hard
Show Answer

Both use evaporative cooling. Humans: sweat glands secrete water onto the large body-surface area; evaporation cools the skin directly and removes heat via blood circulation. Dogs: have very few sweat glands (mainly paw pads). Panting pushes air rapidly over the wet mucous membranes of the tongue and respiratory tract — evaporation from these surfaces removes heat. Design an experiment to compare evaporation rates of water versus saltwater. Identify variables, predict results, and explain the science.Hard
Show Answer

Variables: Independent — type of liquid (pure water vs. saltwater of known concentration); Dependent — mass lost over time; Controlled — volume, dish surface area, temperature, air flow, time. Analyse how replacing forests with agricultural land affects evaporation and ultimately regional rainfall patterns.Hard
Show Answer

Forests have deep roots and large leaf-surface areas — they transpire enormous quantities of water (transpiration + direct evaporation = evapotranspiration). This moisture rises and condenses as regional rainfall, recycling water locally. Agricultural land has lower evapotranspiration (shallow-rooted crops, bare soil between crops). Deforestation therefore reduces atmospheric water vapour locally, weakens cloud formation, and can decrease regional rainfall — a positive feedback loop contributing to desertification, as seen in parts of the Amazon and West Africa.

A student claims "evaporation is wasteful in irrigation." Critically evaluate this claim and suggest how farmers can minimise evaporation losses.Hard
Show Answer

Partially valid: in flood irrigation, up to 60% of water can be lost to evaporation from wet soil surfaces in hot, dry conditions — genuinely wasteful where water is scarce. Strategies to minimise: 1. Drip irrigation — delivers water directly to roots, minimising wet surface area. 2. Night-time irrigation — cooler temperatures + no direct sunlight reduce evaporation rate. 3. Mulching — cover soil with straw/plastic to block surface evaporation. 4. Windbreaks — reduce wind speed over crops, slowing evaporation. 5. Grow drought-resistant crops needing less water. Estimated water savings: drip irrigation saves 30–50% vs. flood.

Using kinetic-molecular theory, explain why evaporation rate increases with temperature even though the substance hasn't reached its boiling point.Hard
Show Answer

At any temperature, molecules follow a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of kinetic energies — most are near the average, but a fraction have much higher energy. Only those with energy exceeding the intermolecular binding energy at the surface can escape. As temperature increases, the entire distribution shifts to higher energies — a larger fraction of molecules now exceed the escape threshold. Therefore, more molecules per unit time evaporate, increasing the rate. The boiling point is when even interior molecules have enough energy to form vapour bubbles — but surface escape begins at any temperature above 0 °C.

Compare evaporation from the Dead Sea versus a freshwater lake of equal surface area. Predict which evaporates faster and justify scientifically.Hard
Show Answer

The freshwater lake evaporates faster. The Dead Sea has extremely high salinity (~34% salt vs. ~3.5% ocean). Dissolved salts lower the vapour pressure of water (Raoult's Law) — fewer water molecules at the surface can escape because solute particles occupy surface sites. This reduces evaporation rate. Paradoxically, the Dead Sea is shrinking because more water enters as vapour than evaporates — but among equivalently heated lakes, higher salinity = slower evaporation. This principle is used in saltpans where seawater is evaporated slowly to harvest salt.

Evaluate the use of floating solar panels on reservoirs. How do they affect evaporation, and is this beneficial? Use scientific reasoning.Hard
Show Answer

Floating solar panels (floatovoltaics) shade the water surface, reducing solar radiation reaching the water and lowering water temperature. This significantly reduces evaporation — studies show 33–70% reduction. Benefits: 1. In water-scarce regions (India, Australia), preserving reservoir water is critical. 2. The cooler water improves solar panel efficiency (panels are more efficient at lower temperatures). 3. Dual land/water use. Drawbacks: reduced light penetration could affect aquatic photosynthesis and dissolved oxygen levels. Overall: highly beneficial in arid regions where water conservation outweighs ecological concerns, particularly for dedicated water-supply reservoirs.

Explain why evaporative cooling is less effective on a humid day compared to a dry day, using the concept of vapour pressure.Hard
Show Answer

Evaporation occurs when the vapour pressure above the liquid is less than the saturation vapour pressure at that temperature. On a dry day, the air holds little water vapour — the vapour pressure is low, creating a large gradient that drives rapid evaporation and effective cooling. On a humid day, the air is nearly saturated — the vapour pressure is close to saturation, greatly reducing the evaporation rate. Less evaporation = less latent heat removed = less cooling. This is why a 35 °C humid day (like Mumbai monsoon) feels more oppressive than a 40 °C dry day (like Rajasthan) — sweat cannot evaporate efficiently.

Explain, using vapour pressure, why boiling occurs at 100 °C at sea level.Medium

Compare the particle-level mechanism of evaporation and boiling. Why does boiling produce bubbles but evaporation does not?Hard

Dew point is the temperature to which air must be cooled (at constant pressure and water vapour content) for it to become saturated (RH = 100%) and begin to condense. High humidity = high dew point (air needs only slight cooling before condensation). Low humidity = low dew point (air must be cooled much more). How does a wet-and-dry bulb psychrometer measure relative humidity?Medium
Show Answer

Dehydration accelerates (the body keeps sweating even though it provides little cooling). Together these effects reduce endurance, reaction time, and performance, and increase risk of heat stroke.

Virus survival is also prolonged in dry conditions. At very high humidity, droplets remain large and fall quickly (less airborne spread), but mould and bacteria thrive. Moderate humidity (40–60% RH) is optimal for minimising respiratory disease spread — one reason flu season peaks in dry winter months.

What property of water molecules makes them good at dissolving ionic compounds like salt?Easy
Show Answer

Water is a polar molecule — the oxygen end is slightly negative (δ−) and the hydrogen end is slightly positive (δ+). These charged ends attract and surround the positive and negative ions in ionic compounds (like Na⁺ and Cl⁻ in salt), pulling them apart and dissolving the crystal.

Show Answer

Show Answer

Because water dissolves substances wherever it goes — from rocks, soil, and air. Rainwater picks up CO₂ (forming mild carbonic acid), groundwater dissolves minerals, and river water carries dissolved solids. Truly pure H₂O only exists in laboratory-prepared distilled or deionised water.

SET B Understanding & Application — Medium

Explain using polarity why water dissolves salt (NaCl) but not oil.Medium
Show Answer

Salt (NaCl) is ionic — Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions. Water's polar molecules surround these ions: δ− oxygen attracts Na⁺; δ+ hydrogen attracts Cl⁻. The ions are pulled from the crystal lattice and dispersed — dissolution. Oil is non-polar (no charge separation). Water molecules strongly attract each other (hydrogen bonds) and exclude non-polar oil molecules — "like dissolves like." Water-water bonds are stronger than any water-oil interaction, so oil does not dissolve. This is why oil floats on water in separate layers.

How does hard water form, and why is it a problem in households?Medium
Show Answer

Formation: Rainwater (slightly acidic) dissolves calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) from limestone/chalk rocks, forming soluble calcium bicarbonate [Ca(HCO₃)₂]. This Ca²⁺-rich water is called hard water. Problems: 1. Limescale builds up in kettles, pipes, and boilers — reducing efficiency and damaging appliances. 2. Soap does not lather well (Ca²⁺ reacts with soap to form insoluble grey scum). 3. Increased energy use for heating scaled pipes. Treatment: Water softeners (ion exchange resins replace Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ with Na⁺); boiling (temporary hardness only); adding washing soda (Na₂CO₃).

Why is seawater unsuitable for drinking, even when you're extremely thirsty?Medium
Show Answer

To excrete the excess salt, the kidneys must produce urine more concentrated than seawater, requiring more water than you drank. Drinking seawater causes net water loss from the body — accelerating dehydration. The high osmotic pressure of seawater also draws water out of cells by osmosis, causing cellular dehydration. Drinking seawater when thirsty makes you more thirsty and can lead to hypernatraemia (salt poisoning).

How does reverse osmosis work to desalinate water?Medium
Show Answer

In osmosis, water naturally moves through a semi-permeable membrane from low-solute to high-solute concentration. In reverse osmosis, high pressure (5–8 MPa) forces seawater through an extremely fine membrane that allows water molecules through but blocks larger salt ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻, Mg²⁺). Pure water passes through; concentrated brine is left behind. RO requires ~3–5 kWh/m³ of electricity — energy-intensive but far less than thermal distillation. Used in Chennai, Israel, Saudi Arabia for large-scale freshwater production.

Fish excrete urea which dissolves in water and disperses harmlessly. The entire chemistry of aquatic ecosystems depends on water being an excellent solvent — without it, nutrients would not distribute and organisms could not exchange gases.

Analyse the causes of the 2019 Chennai water crisis. What combination of factors led to the reservoirs running dry?Medium
Show Answer

How does rainwater harvesting improve water security? Give an example from India.Medium

Why is virtual water (embedded water) important in understanding the global water crisis?Medium
Show Answer

"Virtual water" or "water footprint" is the amount of water used to produce a product. Example: 1 kg of beef requires ~15,400 litres of water (for cattle drinking, fodder irrigation, processing). India exports water-intensive crops (cotton, sugarcane, rice) — effectively exporting its scarce groundwater embedded in food. Understanding virtual water reveals that trade can transfer water stress internationally — water-scarce countries exporting water-intensive goods are depleting their aquifers to serve consumers in water-rich countries. Managing virtual water flows is essential for national water security.

Why are wetlands important for water security, and how does their destruction worsen the water crisis?Medium
Show Answer

Wetlands act as natural water storage buffers — filling during monsoon and releasing water slowly during dry periods (like a sponge). They recharge groundwater through infiltration. They also filter water naturally (plants and microbes remove pollutants). Destroying wetlands (for construction, as happened with Chennai's Pallikaranai marsh) removes this buffer: monsoon water becomes flooding runoff; dry-season groundwater recharge drops; water quality deteriorates. India has lost ~30% of its wetlands since 1970, significantly contributing to both increased flooding and groundwater depletion.

Using the science of states of water and the water cycle, explain why melting glaciers represent a "double threat" for water security in South Asia.Hard
Show Answer

Threat 1 — Short-term flooding: Rising temperatures melt glacial ice → increases river flows in the short term → flooding downstream (Glacial Lake Outburst Floods, GLOFs). Pakistan, Bhutan, and India's Himalayan foothill communities face increasing GLOF risk. Himalayan glaciers have retreated significantly in recent decades. Threat 2 — Long-term water shortage: Glaciers store centuries of frozen water. They act as the "third pole" — feeding rivers like the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra that provide water for ~600 million people through the dry season when monsoon rains are absent. As glaciers shrink irreversibly, seasonal river flows will decrease dramatically — creating permanent dry-season water shortages for agriculture, drinking, and hydropower. The transition is: short-term water abundance (flood phase) → long-term water scarcity (glacier exhaustion) — making this a uniquely dangerous "boom then bust" dynamic that requires urgent adaptation planning now, while rivers still flow.

Critique the concept of "water as a human right" versus "water as an economic good." Which approach better addresses the global water crisis?Hard
Show Answer

Human right approach (UN Resolution 64/292, 2010): water is a basic human need — no one should be denied access due to inability to pay. Advocates: free lifeline allocation, public provision, equity focus. Limitation: without pricing signals, over-use is incentivised; public systems are often under-funded and poorly maintained; wealthy users consume far more than their share without penalty. Economic good approach (Dublin Principles, 1992): water has economic value in all its uses; pricing promotes efficiency, funds maintenance, and reduces waste. Advocates: full cost recovery, market allocation. Limitation: poor households cannot afford market rates; privatisation can exclude the poor; water corporations prioritise profit over access. Optimal approach: hybrid — rights-based lifeline allocation (free/subsidised minimum 50 L/person/day) combined with progressive pricing for additional consumption (pay for excess, fund infrastructure). This protects equity while creating conservation incentives. Evidence: Singapore, Israel, and Chile all use this hybrid approach with strong water security outcomes.

Analyse the specific role of each state of water (solid, liquid, gas) in the global water crisis — which state is most "at risk" and why?Hard
Show Answer

Solid water (glaciers, ice caps): Most at risk. Climate change is irreversibly melting ice at accelerating rates — glaciers in the Hindu Kush-Himalaya have lost ~40% volume since 1975. Once gone, this frozen freshwater store cannot be restored on human timescales. Impact: loss of dry-season river flow for ~2 billion people. Liquid water: Under stress from over-extraction (aquifers), pollution (industrial/agricultural), and reduced recharge (deforestation, urbanisation). However, liquid water flows continuously through the cycle and is partially renewable with good management. Gaseous water (vapour): Climate change intensifies the vapour cycle — more evaporation means more intense but erratic precipitation. This doesn't directly "threaten" water but redistributes it more unevenly. Conclusion: Solid water (glaciers) is most critically "at risk" — because it represents irreversible loss on human timescales, with catastrophic downstream consequences for billions. The crisis in liquid water, while severe, is more amenable to human intervention through policy and technology.

Systems narrative: Solar energy drives evaporation (Topic 3) of liquid water from oceans — the rate depends on temperature, surface area, humidity, and wind (Topic 4). Evaporated water exists as gas (Topic 1), rising into the atmosphere as part of the water cycle (Topic 7).
