Set A Easy – Knowledge & Recall

Define biodiversity.Easy
Show Answer

Biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth — including genetic diversity within species, the variety of species, and the variety of ecosystems.

Name the three levels of biodiversity.Easy
Show Answer

1. Genetic diversity 2. Species diversity 3. Ecosystem diversity.

What rank does India hold among the world's most biodiverse nations?Easy
Show Answer

India is the 12th most biodiverse nation and is classified as a mega-diverse country.

What is a mega-diverse country?Easy
Show Answer

A country that hosts an exceptionally large proportion of global species, especially endemic species. There are 17 such countries worldwide.

Give two examples of genetic diversity in plants.Easy
Show Answer

Different varieties of rice (e.g., basmati vs. ponni) and different varieties of mango (e.g., Alphonso vs. Dasheri).

Name two biodiversity hotspots found in India.Easy
Show Answer

The Western Ghats and the Eastern Himalayas (Indo-Burma hotspot).

What does the term 'endemic species' mean?Easy
Show Answer

Species found exclusively in a particular geographic region and nowhere else on Earth.

State one human activity that directly reduces species diversity.Easy
Show Answer

Deforestation — clearing forests destroys habitats, causing species to lose their homes and leading to their decline or extinction.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why genetic diversity is important for a species' survival.Medium
Show Answer

Genetic diversity means individuals differ slightly in their DNA. When disease or environmental change occurs, some individuals may carry genes that help them survive, preventing total extinction.

Distinguish between species diversity and ecosystem diversity.Medium
Show Answer

Species diversity = variety of different species in an area. Ecosystem diversity = variety of different habitats and ecological communities (e.g., forests, wetlands, grasslands) in a region.

Why is India considered mega-diverse despite its relatively small land area compared to continents?Medium
Show Answer

India has diverse climatic zones (tropical, temperate, alpine), varied geography (Himalayas, coasts, deserts), and ancient evolutionary history, supporting an extraordinary variety of habitats and species.

How does the loss of one species affect an entire food web?Medium
Show Answer

Each species occupies a role (producer, consumer, decomposer). Removing one species can cause overpopulation of its prey and starvation of its predators, causing a cascading collapse called a trophic cascade.

What is a biodiversity hotspot? State two criteria for a region to qualify.Medium
Show Answer

A biodiversity hotspot is a region with exceptional biodiversity under significant threat. Criteria: (1) must contain at least 1,500 endemic plant species; (2) must have lost at least 70% of its original habitat.

Explain how monoculture farming reduces genetic diversity.Medium
Show Answer

Monoculture grows a single crop variety over large areas, replacing many traditional varieties. If a pest or disease attacks, the genetically uniform crop has no resistant individuals and the entire crop can be wiped out.

How do keystone species maintain biodiversity?Medium
Show Answer

Keystone species have an outsized effect on their ecosystem relative to their abundance. For example, tigers control deer populations, preventing overgrazing and maintaining plant diversity.

Name two ecosystem services that high biodiversity provides to humans.Medium
Show Answer

1. Pollination of crops (food security). 2. Water purification by wetland plants and soil microorganisms.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Critically evaluate the statement: "Protecting biodiversity is ultimately about protecting human self-interest."Hard
Show Answer

For: humans depend on biodiversity for food, medicine, clean air/water, and climate stability — purely practical reasons. Against: biodiversity has intrinsic value independent of human use; species have a right to exist. Evaluation: both arguments are valid; policy is more effective when both intrinsic and instrumental values are recognized.

Design a method to measure species diversity in your school garden. Identify one limitation of your method.Hard
Show Answer

Method: Use quadrat sampling — place 0.5 m² quadrats randomly across the garden, count and identify all plant species in each. Calculate species richness and Shannon Diversity Index. Limitation: only captures visible plants; soil microorganisms, insects, and fungi are missed, underestimating true diversity.

Analyse why island ecosystems typically have lower biodiversity but higher rates of endemism than continental regions.Hard
Show Answer

Islands are isolated — fewer species colonise (lower diversity), but those that arrive evolve independently over time, adapting to local conditions and becoming endemic. Small population size also accelerates genetic divergence (founder effect and genetic drift).

Compare the roles of genetic and ecosystem diversity in resilience to climate change. Which is more critical?Hard
Show Answer

Genetic diversity allows species to adapt (individuals with advantageous traits survive changed conditions). Ecosystem diversity provides multiple habitats so species can migrate to suitable zones. Both are critical: genetic diversity acts at the individual/population level; ecosystem diversity acts at the landscape level. Neither alone is sufficient.

Evaluate the effectiveness of biodiversity hotspot designations as a conservation strategy.Hard
Show Answer

Strengths: focuses limited funds on areas with highest return (most species per unit area); draws international attention. Weaknesses: ignores species-poor but ecologically important areas (e.g., Arctic tundra); hotspot boundaries are political, not ecological; doesn't address root causes like poverty driving habitat destruction.

Using the concept of ecological niches, explain why tropical rainforests support far more biodiversity than temperate forests.Hard
Show Answer

Tropical rainforests have consistent year-round warmth, high rainfall, and multi-layered canopy structure, creating many distinct microclimates and niches. Greater niche specialisation allows more species to coexist without competing. Temperate forests have seasonal extremes reducing available niches and requiring generalist adaptations.

A river ecosystem loses its apex predator fish species due to pollution. Predict and explain the consequences at three trophic levels.Hard
Show Answer

Level 3 (apex predator): disappears → Level 2 (intermediate fish/invertebrates): population explodes unchecked → Level 1 (algae/plants/zooplankton): overgrazing leads to depletion → ecosystem may shift to algal bloom state with low oxygen (eutrophication), killing remaining species. This is a trophic cascade.

Argue for and against the view that economic development must be sacrificed to conserve biodiversity in developing countries like India.Hard
Show Answer

For sacrifice: habitat conversion for development is the leading driver of biodiversity loss; short-term gains create long-term ecological debt. Against: developing nations have legitimate rights to use resources for poverty reduction; sustainable development (ecotourism, agroforestry) can generate income while conserving biodiversity. Conclusion: the false dichotomy can be resolved through green economy policies and international funding for conservation.

Set A Easy – Knowledge & Recall

Define deforestation.Easy
Show Answer

Deforestation is the large-scale removal or clearing of forests, primarily for agriculture, timber, or urban development.

Name three main causes of deforestation.Easy
Show Answer

1. Agricultural expansion 2. Logging for timber 3. Urban and industrial development.

How does deforestation cause soil erosion?Easy
Show Answer

Tree roots bind the soil. Without roots, rain washes away the topsoil, making land infertile and causing erosion.

Why does deforestation reduce rainfall?Easy
Show Answer

Trees release water vapour through transpiration, contributing to cloud formation and local rainfall. Fewer trees mean less transpiration and reduced precipitation.

How does deforestation contribute to climate change?Easy
Show Answer

Trees absorb CO₂. When cut and burned, stored carbon is released as CO₂, increasing the greenhouse effect and warming the planet.

What is habitat loss and how does it cause species extinction?Easy
Show Answer

Habitat loss means destroying the natural environment where species live. Without food, shelter, and breeding space, populations decline and species may go extinct.

Define desertification.Easy
Show Answer

Desertification is the process by which fertile land becomes desert due to drought, deforestation, and inappropriate agriculture.

How does deforestation increase the risk of flooding?Easy
Show Answer

Tree canopies intercept rain and roots absorb water, slowing runoff. Without trees, rainwater flows rapidly over the surface, causing rivers to overflow and flooding nearby areas.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain the water cycle disruption caused by large-scale deforestation.Medium
Show Answer

Forests drive the water cycle by releasing water vapour (transpiration) which forms clouds and rainfall. Deforestation reduces evapotranspiration, breaks the moisture feedback loop, lowers regional rainfall, and increases surface runoff, degrading water availability and quality.

Why is topsoil loss particularly damaging for agriculture?Medium
Show Answer

Topsoil contains most of the nutrients, organic matter, and microorganisms that support plant growth. Its loss makes land infertile; topsoil takes hundreds of years to form, so damage is essentially permanent on human timescales.

Describe the link between deforestation in the Himalayan foothills and flooding in the Gangetic plains.Medium
Show Answer

Forests in the Himalayas regulate stream flow. Deforestation causes rapid runoff during monsoons, increasing river discharge, leading to severe flooding downstream in the Gangetic plains. Silt from eroded soil also raises riverbeds, worsening floods.

How does forest fragmentation differ from total deforestation in its ecological impact?Medium
Show Answer

Fragmentation divides forests into isolated patches, creating "edge effects" (invasive species, altered microclimate at borders) and preventing wildlife migration. Even small fragments lose interior species. Total deforestation removes all habitat, but fragmentation can be equally damaging over time due to isolation of populations.

Explain why deforestation in one country can have effects on climate in distant regions.Medium
Show Answer

Forests act as global carbon sinks. Releasing their stored CO₂ adds to the atmosphere globally, raising average temperatures worldwide. Reduced transpiration also alters atmospheric circulation patterns, potentially shifting monsoon and jet streams that affect distant regions.

What is the relationship between deforestation and increased disease transmission to humans?Medium
Show Answer

Deforestation brings humans into contact with wildlife reservoirs of disease (zoonotic spillover). As habitats shrink, animals carrying viruses (bats, rodents) move closer to human settlements, increasing chances of pathogen transfer, as seen with Ebola, Nipah, and COVID-19.

Compare the effects of deforestation on local indigenous communities versus urban populations.Medium
Show Answer

Indigenous communities: direct loss of livelihood, food, medicine, shelter, and cultural identity as forest is home. Urban populations: indirect effects — increased food prices, flood risk, reduced air quality. Indigenous communities suffer immediate and total disruption; urban populations face delayed economic and climate impacts.

How does deforestation affect groundwater levels?Medium
Show Answer

Tree roots facilitate rainwater infiltration into the ground, recharging aquifers. Without trees, water runs off the surface instead of seeping into groundwater, causing water table levels to drop and wells and springs to dry up.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Construct a systems diagram showing how deforestation creates a positive feedback loop accelerating climate change.Hard
Show Answer

Deforestation → ↑ CO₂ (burning) + ↓ carbon absorption → ↑ global temperature → ↑ drought/fire risk → more forest die-off → further ↑ CO₂ → amplified warming. This positive feedback loop means each increment of deforestation disproportionately worsens climate outcomes.

Critically assess whether economic compensation (REDD+ payments) is an effective solution to halt tropical deforestation.Hard
Show Answer

Strengths: provides developing countries financial incentive to preserve forests; quantifiable carbon credits. Weaknesses: leakage problem (deforestation shifts elsewhere); corruption in payment distribution; doesn't address poverty driving deforestation; carbon measurement is complex. Overall: REDD+ is a necessary but insufficient mechanism without governance reform and poverty alleviation.

Analyse how the destruction of mangrove forests specifically affects coastal communities differently from inland forest loss.Hard
Show Answer

Mangroves: protect coastlines from storm surge and tsunamis (wave energy reduction), act as fish nurseries (food security), trap coastal sediment (land stability), filter pollutants. Their loss exposes coasts to catastrophic flooding and erosion, crashes fisheries, and accelerates saltwater intrusion into farmland — impacts absent in inland deforestation.

Evaluate the relative contributions of subsistence farming versus commercial agriculture to global deforestation.Hard
Show Answer

Commercial agriculture (soy, palm oil, cattle ranching) drives ~70-80% of tropical deforestation in Latin America and Southeast Asia — large scale, profit-motivated, often backed by corporations. Subsistence farming drives ~20-30%, mainly in Africa — small-scale, survival-motivated. Policy implications differ: corporate deforestation needs supply-chain regulation and certification; subsistence requires land rights, alternative livelihoods, and agricultural intensification.

A scientist claims deforestation in Northeast India threatens the Bengal tiger more than direct poaching. Construct an argument to support or challenge this claim.Hard
Show Answer

Support: habitat loss is structural — it reduces prey density, fragments territory, increases human-tiger conflict, and ultimately sets a population ceiling that poaching cannot exceed. Without sufficient habitat, even zero poaching cannot sustain tigers. Challenge: poaching directly removes individuals, and at small population sizes even one tiger death can push a local population below minimum viable size. Both are interconnected — habitat loss brings tigers into human contact, increasing both conflict killings and poaching opportunity.

Propose and justify three forest management policies a state government should implement to balance timber revenue with ecological preservation.Hard
Show Answer

1. Selective logging with mandatory replanting (maintains canopy structure, carbon stock). 2. Community forest management rights — local communities earn income from sustainable harvest, aligning economic interest with conservation. 3. Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) — compensate forest communities for watershed protection and carbon sequestration, providing economic alternatives to logging. Together these align financial incentives with ecological goals.

Using evidence, argue whether current global rates of reforestation are sufficient to offset deforestation losses.Hard
Show Answer

Evidence against sufficiency: world loses ~10 million ha of forest/year (FAO); current reforestation adds ~5 million ha/year — a net loss. Planted forests have lower biodiversity than old-growth (often monocultures). New plantations take decades to reach carbon equivalence. Evidence for improvement: some nations (India, China) show net forest gain; Bonn Challenge pledges 350 million ha by 2030. Conclusion: reforestation efforts are insufficient in scale and ecological quality to offset losses; preventing deforestation in the first place is far more effective.

MYP D — Should forest-dwelling tribal communities be given legal rights to veto commercial logging in their ancestral territories? Argue both sides.Hard
Show Answer

For veto rights: indigenous peoples are proven effective forest stewards; their territories have lower deforestation rates; right to free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) is enshrined in UNDRIP; respects cultural rights and sovereignty. Against: could delay national development projects, reduce timber revenue affecting broader public; veto rights may be politically manipulated; state sovereignty concerns. Balanced view: FPIC with transparent, well-funded consultation processes and revenue-sharing agreements respects rights while enabling sustainable co-governance.

Set A Easy – Knowledge & Recall

What is a biosphere reserve?Easy
Show Answer

A biosphere reserve is a large protected area designated by UNESCO to conserve biodiversity while allowing sustainable human use. It has three zones: core, buffer, and transition.

Name and describe the three zones of a biosphere reserve.Easy
Show Answer

Core zone: strictly protected, no human activity allowed. Buffer zone: surrounds core, limited research and low-impact activities permitted. Transition (cooperation) zone: outermost, sustainable human use, settlements, and ecotourism allowed.

How is a national park different from a wildlife sanctuary?Easy
Show Answer

National park: strictly protected, no human habitation, grazing, or resource extraction allowed. Wildlife sanctuary: some human activities (grazing, limited resource collection) may be permitted; the focus is on protecting specific wildlife species.

Name India's first national park.Easy
Show Answer

Jim Corbett National Park (established 1936) in Uttarakhand.

What is the main purpose of a wildlife sanctuary?Easy
Show Answer

To protect specific wildlife species (especially animals) and their habitats while allowing limited human activities that do not threaten wildlife.

Name one UNESCO-designated biosphere reserve in India.Easy
Show Answer

Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve (Tamil Nadu/Kerala/Karnataka) — India's first biosphere reserve, established 1986.

What does UNESCO stand for?Easy
Show Answer

United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization.

Can people live inside a national park? Give a reason.Easy
Show Answer

No. Human settlements are not permitted inside national parks to ensure minimal disturbance to wildlife and habitats.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Why is the buffer zone in a biosphere reserve important?Medium
Show Answer

The buffer zone acts as a protective barrier around the core zone, reducing human pressure. It allows regulated research and education, filters out disturbances, and provides a gradual transition to human-inhabited areas.

Explain why a large national park is more effective at conserving biodiversity than several small reserves of the same total area.Medium
Show Answer

Large parks support viable populations of wide-ranging species (tigers, elephants), reduce edge effects, maintain complete ecosystems and food webs, and allow natural migration. Small fragmented reserves suffer isolation, inbreeding, and edge effects. Island Biogeography theory shows larger areas support exponentially more species.

What is the difference between in-situ and ex-situ conservation? Give one example of each.Medium
Show Answer

In-situ: conserving species in their natural habitat (e.g., national parks for tigers). Ex-situ: conserving species outside their natural habitat (e.g., zoo breeding programme for Indian rhinoceros).

How do wildlife corridors supplement the function of national parks?Medium
Show Answer

Wildlife corridors are strips of habitat connecting isolated parks, allowing animals to migrate, find mates, access seasonal resources, and maintain gene flow between populations. They effectively increase the functional size of protected areas.

Why might a biosphere reserve be more sustainable in the long term than a strictly protected national park?Medium
Show Answer

Biosphere reserves involve local communities in their transition zones, providing economic benefits (ecotourism, sustainable harvest). This reduces poaching pressure and increases local support for conservation, making it socially and economically sustainable. Purely excludionary national parks can generate local hostility.

Describe one challenge in enforcing protection regulations in Indian wildlife sanctuaries.Medium
Show Answer

Inadequate ranger staff and funding makes patrolling vast areas difficult. Poachers exploit remote, poorly monitored sectors. Local communities dependent on forest resources may resent restrictions and undermine enforcement. Corruption can also compromise protection.

Explain how national parks help preserve ecosystem services beyond just protecting animals.Medium
Show Answer

National parks protect entire ecosystems — preserving watersheds (clean water for downstream communities), carbon sequestration (forests absorb CO₂), soil retention (preventing erosion), pollination services, and local climate regulation.

What is a Ramsar site? How does it differ from a biosphere reserve?Medium
Show Answer

A Ramsar site is a wetland designated under the Ramsar Convention (1971) for international importance, especially for migratory birds and wetland biodiversity. Unlike biosphere reserves (which focus on overall biodiversity across all ecosystems), Ramsar sites specifically protect wetland habitats.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Evaluate the claim that India has enough protected areas and no new national parks are needed.Hard
Show Answer

Against claim: only ~5% of India's land is formally protected; many ecosystems (grasslands, wetlands) are under-represented; connectivity between parks is poor; climate change will shift species' ranges beyond current park boundaries. For claim: more parks without funding and governance improvements may be ineffective; existing parks face enforcement crises. Conclusion: more protected areas are needed but must be paired with better management of existing ones.

Design an ideal biosphere reserve for the Sundarbans. Justify the boundaries of each zone.Hard
Show Answer

Core zone: deepest mangrove forest with highest tiger and species density — strictly protected, no entry. Buffer zone: outer mangrove belt with ranger stations, permitted scientific research and eco-monitoring; no fishing. Transition zone: coastal villages, regulated sustainable fishing, ecotourism operations, honey collection (traditional Moli communities' livelihood). Justification: core protection maximises ecological integrity; buffer absorbs impacts; transition zone ensures local community support by providing legitimate livelihoods.

Analyse why many protected areas in developing countries exist only on paper ("paper parks"). What systemic changes are needed?Hard
Show Answer

Reasons for paper parks: insufficient funding for rangers and equipment; lack of political will; corruption; local communities not included as stakeholders; inadequate legal penalties for violations. Systemic changes: increased central government funding tied to measurable outcomes; community-based conservation with revenue sharing; international funding (REDD+, GEF); stronger penalties and anti-corruption mechanisms; integration of traditional ecological knowledge in management.

Compare the approaches of national parks (exclusionary conservation) and community forest management (inclusive conservation). Which better achieves long-term biodiversity goals?Hard
Show Answer

Exclusionary (national parks): maximises ecological integrity in small areas; effective where state capacity is high; but generates community resentment and requires perpetual enforcement. Inclusive (community management): lower cost, builds local stewardship, adaptable to local conditions; but may allow unsustainable exploitation if governance is weak. Evidence: community forests in Himachal Pradesh and Uttarakhand show recovery of biodiversity when communities have clear rights and benefits. Long-term: inclusive approaches are more sustainable in developing-country contexts where state capacity is limited.

Should marine protected areas (MPAs) be extended to cover 30% of India's Exclusive Economic Zone? Present evidence for and against.Hard
Show Answer

For: coral reefs, seagrass beds, and mangroves face severe degradation; MPAs allow fish stocks to recover (spillover effect benefits adjacent fisheries); protects sea turtles, dugongs, whale sharks. Against: severely impacts artisanal fishing communities relying on these waters for food security; enforcement at sea is extremely difficult and expensive; displacement of fishing effort to unprotected areas may worsen overall situation. Conclusion: 30% MPA target is scientifically justified but requires well-funded community consultation, enforcement technology, and alternative livelihood support.

Critique the use of GPS tracking collars on tigers in national parks. What are the scientific benefits and the ethical trade-offs?Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: reveals territory size, migration routes, habitat use, breeding behaviour, human-wildlife conflict hotspots; enables proactive anti-poaching deployment. Trade-offs: capture process stresses animals (risk of injury, anaesthesia mortality); collar can cause chafing or restrict movement; permanent human imprint on a wild animal; raises questions about where monitoring ends and control begins. Conclusion: benefits outweigh risks when protocols are rigorous and data is used to improve, not just study, tiger welfare.

Propose how technology (drones, AI, satellite imagery) could transform the effectiveness of protected area management in India. Identify one limitation.Hard
Show Answer

Drones: monitor vast areas cheaply, detect poachers and encroachers in real-time. AI: analyse satellite imagery to detect forest cover change, identify individual tigers by stripe patterns (camera traps), predict poaching events. Satellite imagery: track seasonal vegetation change and illegal clearings. Limitation: high initial cost limits adoption in low-budget reserves; technology requires trained personnel; power and connectivity issues in remote areas; data must translate into on-ground ranger action to be effective.

MYP D — Should governments have the right to forcibly relocate indigenous communities from newly declared national parks? Argue from multiple perspectives.Hard
Show Answer

Government perspective: national interest and wildlife protection may justify relocation with fair compensation; parks require strict protection for endangered species. Indigenous rights perspective: forced relocation violates human rights (UNDRIP); indigenous peoples often have lower ecological footprint than outsiders and maintain biodiversity; evictions have historically caused poverty and cultural destruction (e.g., Baiga tribe from Kanha). Scientific perspective: indigenous management practices can be ecologically beneficial — exclusion of communities often reduces forest health. Ethical conclusion: forced relocation is not justified; voluntary relocation with full informed consent, adequate compensation, land rights, and community participation in park governance is the only ethical path forward.

Set A Easy – Knowledge & Recall

What does IUCN stand for?Easy
Show Answer

International Union for Conservation of Nature.

List the IUCN threat categories from least to most threatened.Easy
Show Answer

Least Concern → Near Threatened → Vulnerable (VU) → Endangered (EN) → Critically Endangered (CR) → Extinct in the Wild (EW) → Extinct (EX).

What is the Red Data Book?Easy
Show Answer

A record maintained by IUCN that lists threatened species of plants, animals, and fungi, categorised by their extinction risk level.

When was Project Tiger launched and what was its goal?Easy
Show Answer

Project Tiger was launched in 1973 by the Government of India to protect the Bengal tiger from extinction and restore its population.

How many tigers were there in India in 1973 and approximately how many are there now?Easy
Show Answer

Approximately 1,800 tigers in 1973; the population had grown to approximately 3,700 by recent census counts.

What bird is the most famous example of an extinct species caused by human activity?Easy
Show Answer

The Dodo (Raphus cucullatus), a flightless bird from Mauritius that went extinct in the 17th century due to hunting and introduced predators.

What does 'Critically Endangered' mean?Easy
Show Answer

A species faces an extremely high risk of extinction in the wild in the immediate future (probability ≥50% within 10 years or 3 generations).

Name two animals on the IUCN Red List found in India.Easy
Show Answer

Bengal tiger (Endangered) and Indian one-horned rhinoceros (Vulnerable).

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain the difference between 'Extinct in the Wild' and 'Extinct'.Medium
Show Answer

Extinct in the Wild (EW): the species survives only in captivity or cultivation, with no wild populations remaining. Extinct (EX): no individuals of the species remain alive anywhere on Earth.

Why is it important to document species in the Red Data Book before they become extinct?Medium
Show Answer

Documentation helps governments prioritise conservation funding and legislation. It creates baselines for monitoring population trends, identifies threats, guides habitat protection, and enables public awareness campaigns. Early documentation allows preventive action before populations reach critically low levels.

Describe two causes of the dramatic decline in tiger populations in the 20th century.Medium
Show Answer

1. Poaching: tiger parts (bones, skin) were highly valued in traditional Chinese medicine and as trophies. 2. Habitat loss: deforestation for agriculture and human settlement destroyed the large territories tigers need to hunt and breed.

How has Project Tiger used the biosphere reserve concept to protect tigers?Medium
Show Answer

Project Tiger established tiger reserves that follow a core-buffer zone approach. Core zones are strict no-disturbance areas for breeding; buffer zones allow limited human activity. This reduces direct conflict, ensures prey abundance, and provides corridors between reserves.

Why are species with small, isolated populations particularly vulnerable to extinction even if they are legally protected?Medium
Show Answer

Small populations suffer inbreeding depression (reduced genetic diversity → weaker offspring, lower fertility, higher disease susceptibility). Random demographic events (one disease outbreak, one storm) can eliminate the entire population. This is known as the extinction vortex.

Why did the Dodo become extinct so rapidly after human arrival in Mauritius?Medium
Show Answer

Dodos had no fear of humans (no prior predators), making them easy to hunt. Sailors killed them for food. Introduced animals (rats, dogs, pigs) ate their eggs and chicks. Habitat destruction removed nesting sites. The combination of these factors caused extinction within less than a century of human contact.

Explain how international wildlife trade (CITES) regulations help protect endangered species.Medium
Show Answer

CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species) bans commercial trade in species listed in Appendix I (most endangered) and regulates trade in Appendix II species through permits. It closes international markets for poached wildlife products, reducing financial incentive for poaching.

What is minimum viable population (MVP) and why is it important in conservation?Medium
Show Answer

MVP is the smallest population size that can maintain itself without significant risk of extinction due to genetic or demographic factors. Conservation aims to maintain populations above MVP; below this threshold, inbreeding and random events rapidly drive the species to extinction.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Critically analyse the success of Project Tiger. Has it truly saved the tiger, or are there ongoing threats?Hard
Show Answer

Successes: population grew from 1,800 (1973) to ~3,700; 54 tiger reserves established; anti-poaching capacity improved; several populations are genetically viable. Ongoing threats: habitat fragmentation isolates sub-populations; human-tiger conflict rising as tigers move beyond reserve boundaries; climate change threatens prey base in semi-arid reserves; political interference in land use near reserves; poaching networks remain active. Conclusion: Project Tiger has been a landmark success but is not sufficient alone — landscape-level connectivity and conflict mitigation are still critical.

Evaluate whether the IUCN Red List categories are scientifically objective or influenced by economic and political pressures.Hard
Show Answer

Scientific basis: uses quantitative criteria (population size, decline rate, geographic range) based on evidence — relatively objective. Political pressure: economically important species (commercially fished tuna) have been historically under-listed despite clear population declines; countries sometimes resist listing their flagship species as threatened for reputational reasons. Data gaps: many species are assessed as Data Deficient because populations have never been studied — not listed as threatened even if genuinely at risk. Conclusion: Red List is the best available tool but not immune to political and economic bias; independent monitoring is necessary.

Should extinct species like the Dodo be brought back through de-extinction (cloning/genetic engineering)? Present a balanced argument.Hard
Show Answer

For de-extinction: restores lost ecological roles; symbolic value raises conservation awareness; scientific advancement in genetics; some cases (passenger pigeon, mammoth) could restore degraded ecosystems. Against: enormous cost diverts funds from saving currently endangered species; de-extinct animals face ecological mismatch (their habitat has changed); no intact DNA in most extinct species; raises false hope, reducing urgency to prevent current extinctions; welfare concerns for cloned animals. Conclusion: de-extinction is scientifically exciting but ethically and practically questionable; resources should primarily be directed at preventing ongoing extinctions.

Analyse how climate change will create new categories of extinction risk not adequately captured by current IUCN criteria.Hard
Show Answer

Current criteria focus on current population status and trends. Climate change creates: range shift extinctions (suitable habitat moves faster than species can migrate), phenological mismatch (breeding season misaligned with food availability — not visible in population numbers yet), compound stressors (drought + disease + habitat loss simultaneously), and coral bleaching extinction events that are periodic rather than linear. IUCN criteria would only capture these after significant population decline, too late for preventive action. Recommendation: climate vulnerability assessments should be integrated into the Red List methodology.

Compare the effectiveness of legal protection versus habitat restoration as conservation strategies for endangered species in India.Hard
Show Answer

Legal protection (e.g., Wildlife Protection Act 1972): creates deterrence against poaching; immediate protection of individuals; enforced through penalties. Limitation: ineffective without enforcement capacity; addresses symptom (killing), not root cause (habitat loss). Habitat restoration: increases carrying capacity, allowing natural population growth; provides long-term sustainable recovery; benefits multiple species simultaneously. Limitation: expensive, slow (decades), requires expertise. Conclusion: legal protection provides emergency protection but is insufficient long-term; habitat restoration is more costly but addresses the fundamental driver of decline. Both strategies must be implemented together.

Why might a species classified as Least Concern today face rapid extinction within decades? Give a specific ecological example.Hard
Show Answer

Least Concern species can face sudden collapses if: a single threatening process (disease, invasive species, climate shift) rapidly eliminates a large fraction of the population before monitoring detects the trend. Example: White-nose syndrome, a fungal disease, reduced North American bat populations (previously abundant) by 90% within a decade. Bats were Least Concern until the disease emerged; by the time they were uplisted, millions had already died. Sparse monitoring in remote habitats means declines often go undetected until they are catastrophic.

Design a science investigation to monitor the recovery of a critically endangered freshwater fish species in a river. Include variables, method, and how you would measure success.Hard
Show Answer

Species: Mahseer (Tor remadevii), Critically Endangered. Independent variable: conservation interventions (habitat restoration, fishing ban). Dependent variables: population count per 500 m stretch, age structure (juvenile:adult ratio), genetic diversity (microsatellite analysis). Method: electrofishing surveys every 6 months; mark-recapture for population estimate; water quality sampling (pH, dissolved oxygen, turbidity). Control sites: unprotected river stretches for comparison. Success metrics: 50% increase in population density over 5 years; juvenile recruitment confirmed; genetic diversity maintained at H>0.6. Limitation: electrofishing stresses fish; acknowledging welfare as a constraint.

MYP D — Should countries that caused the extinction of species through colonialism (e.g., Dodo in Mauritius) bear a special responsibility to fund global species recovery today?Hard
Show Answer

For responsibility: colonial powers exploited resources, destroyed habitats, and introduced invasive species that caused extinctions across colonised territories; benefited economically from overexploitation; have greater financial capacity today. Against: current citizens are not legally responsible for historical actions; extinction is a complex process — multiple factors including local over-hunting by pre-colonial peoples also played roles; enforcing historical responsibility creates complex precedents. Balanced view: a principle of ecological justice suggests wealthy nations that historically caused environmental harm should contribute disproportionately to global conservation funding (as reflected in CBD's financial mechanism). This is not punishment but equity in addressing historical ecological debt.

What is reforestation?Easy

Describe the Chipko movement. When and where did it begin?Easy
Show Answer

The Chipko movement began in 1973 in the Chamoli district of Uttarakhand, India. Villagers, primarily women, hugged trees ("chipko" = to hug/stick) to prevent contractors from cutting them. It was a nonviolent protest that became a landmark grassroots conservation movement.

What is a seed bank?Easy
Show Answer

A seed bank is a facility that stores seeds of plant species at very low temperatures to preserve their genetic diversity and ensure survival if plants become extinct in the wild.

What is captive breeding?Easy
Show Answer

Captive breeding is the process of breeding endangered animals in controlled environments (zoos, wildlife centres) with the aim of eventually reintroducing them into the wild.

What is a wildlife corridor?Easy
Show Answer

A wildlife corridor is a strip of natural habitat connecting fragmented wildlife areas, allowing animals to move safely between protected areas.

Name one famous international seed bank and where it is located.Easy
Show Answer

The Svalbard Global Seed Vault, located on the Norwegian island of Spitsbergen in the Arctic, stores over 1.3 million seed samples.

What is the difference between afforestation and reforestation?Easy
Show Answer

Reforestation: replanting trees in areas that were previously forested. Afforestation: planting trees in areas that have not historically been forested (creating new forest).

Name one animal that has been successfully reintroduced to its natural habitat through captive breeding.Easy
Show Answer

The Indian one-horned rhinoceros (Rhinoceros unicornis) was bred in captivity and some individuals reintroduced into Manas National Park.

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain how the Chipko movement's success influenced Indian forest policy.Medium
Show Answer

The Chipko movement led to a 15-year ban on commercial felling in the Himalayas. It influenced the Forest Conservation Act (1980), which requires government approval before forest land is used for non-forest purposes. It demonstrated the power of grassroots environmental activism and became a model for future movements globally.

Why is maintaining genetic diversity in seed banks important for future food security?Medium
Show Answer

Commercial agriculture uses few high-yield crop varieties. If disease, pests, or climate change attacks these varieties, seed banks provide genetic material from thousands of traditional varieties that may carry resistance genes, allowing breeders to develop new resilient varieties.

Compare the advantages and disadvantages of captive breeding as a conservation strategy.Medium
Show Answer

Advantages: prevents immediate extinction; allows population growth under safe conditions; enables scientific study. Disadvantages: expensive; captive animals lose wild survival skills (hunting, migration, predator avoidance); inbreeding in small captive populations; reintroduction success is variable; doesn't address habitat loss.

Why are native species preferred over exotic species in reforestation projects?Medium
Show Answer

Native species are adapted to local soil and climate, require less maintenance, provide food and shelter for local wildlife, support local insect and bird communities, and maintain natural ecosystem functions. Exotic species (e.g., eucalyptus) may use excessive water, reduce undergrowth, and fail to support local biodiversity.

Describe how ecotourism can fund and incentivise wildlife conservation.Medium
Show Answer

Ecotourism generates income for local communities from wildlife-watching (safari, bird tours), creating economic incentive to protect wildlife rather than poach or convert habitat. Revenue funds park management, ranger salaries, and habitat restoration. Communities that earn from living wildlife are motivated to report poachers and protect ecosystems.

How do wildlife corridors specifically help prevent inbreeding in tiger populations?Medium
Show Answer

Corridors allow tigers from different reserves to interbreed, introducing new genetic variation into isolated populations. This prevents inbreeding depression (reduced fertility, weaker immunity) that would otherwise occur in genetically isolated populations. Genetic mixing maintains healthy, viable populations.

What is the role of the Forest Rights Act (2006) in community-based conservation in India?Medium
Show Answer

The Forest Rights Act recognises the rights of forest-dwelling communities (Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers) to access, use, and manage forest land they have occupied for generations. When communities have legal rights over their forests, they have greater motivation to protect and sustainably manage them.

Explain how traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) contributes to modern conservation efforts.Medium
Show Answer

Indigenous communities have accumulated detailed knowledge of local species, seasonal cycles, medicinal plants, and ecological relationships over centuries. TEK helps scientists identify unknown species, understand historical ecological baselines, develop sustainable use practices, and implement culturally appropriate conservation strategies.

Set C Hard – MYP Criteria B & D Analysis

Evaluate whether planting 1 billion trees (as proposed by many governments) is a meaningful climate and biodiversity solution.Hard
Show Answer

For: trees sequester carbon; large-scale planting can restore degraded land; creates employment; if native species used, restores biodiversity. Against: monoculture plantations have low biodiversity; planted trees have high mortality rates; may replace biodiverse grasslands (misidentified as "degraded"); carbon sequestration takes decades; doesn't address fossil fuel use. Critical point: "tree planting" is not equal to "forest restoration." Effective planting requires native species, local community involvement, and long-term maintenance. Conclusion: valid when done correctly, but often used as a PR exercise rather than genuine ecological restoration.

Construct an argument that grassroots movements (like Chipko) are more effective than government-led conservation programmes.Hard
Show Answer

For grassroots: community members have direct knowledge of and stake in local ecosystems; movements are self-sustaining (not dependent on government budget cycles); generate political pressure that leads to lasting policy change; address root social causes of overexploitation; Chipko directly halted logging and led to the 1980 Forest Act. Against: government programmes have legal enforcement power, dedicated funding, scientific expertise, and cross-regional coordination that local movements lack. Conclusion: grassroots movements are vital for creating political will and ensuring local ownership, but need to be institutionalised through policy to achieve lasting systemic change.

Assess the trade-offs between using land for agricultural production versus ecological restoration in food-insecure regions.Hard
Show Answer

Food security argument: converting land to agriculture directly produces food; population growth increases demand; food sovereignty concerns. Ecological argument: degraded land produces declining yields over time; restored forests provide long-term watershed services that support agriculture; agroforestry systems can produce food while restoring ecological function. Resolution: land sparing (intensify agriculture on existing land to free marginal land for restoration) vs. land sharing (integrate trees and biodiversity within farming systems). Neither pure approach is universally optimal; context-specific analysis of land productivity, social needs, and ecological sensitivity is required.

Design a captive breeding and reintroduction programme for the Great Indian Bustard. Identify the three greatest challenges.Hard
Show Answer

Programme: establish breeding centres in Rajasthan and Gujarat with secure, large enclosures mimicking grassland habitat. Collect eggs from last wild nests (with extreme care), hatch and raise chicks avoiding human imprinting (hand puppets). Release into protected grassland reserves with prey base and no power lines. Challenges: 1. Power line collisions — bustards fly low and collide with overhead lines; underground cables needed near release sites. 2. Loss of wild behaviour in captive-bred birds — without role models, reintroduced birds may fail to find food or evade predators. 3. Critically small wild population — fewer than 150 birds; any egg collection reduces already tiny wild breeding output.

Analyse how climate change might undermine current reforestation and wildlife corridor efforts in India over the next 50 years.Hard
Show Answer

Reforestation: species planted based on current climate may not survive in 2070's conditions; monsoon shift may reduce survival rates; higher fire frequency may re-clear planted areas before maturity. Wildlife corridors: if species' climate ranges shift northward/uphill, corridors built for current distribution may become ecologically irrelevant; novel species assemblages in corridors may not support target species. Solutions: climate-smart reforestation using species with broad climate tolerance; design dynamic corridors that connect future as well as current habitats; model future species distributions to guide planting and corridor placement.

Critically evaluate the role of zoos in modern conservation. Are they conservation centres or entertainment businesses?Hard
Show Answer

Conservation role: housing captive breeding programmes (Indian rhino, Arabian oryx); funding field conservation; educating millions of visitors; maintaining insurance populations against wild extinction; genetic repositories. Entertainment business: most zoo visitors prioritise recreation over education; many zoo animals do not contribute to active conservation programmes; animal welfare compromised in small enclosures; divert conservation funding from habitat protection. Critical assessment: modern accredited zoos (AZA, WAZA) do genuine conservation work, but the sector is highly variable in quality and commitment. Zoos are justified only when actively contributing to species recovery and habitat conservation — not as standalone displays.

MYP D — Should wealthy nations be required to fund conservation programmes in biodiversity-rich developing nations? Argue using principles of justice and responsibility.Hard
Show Answer

Justice arguments for: biodiversity is a global public good that benefits all nations (ecosystem services, potential medicines); wealthy nations historically over-consumed global resources and emitted disproportionate CO₂, which drives extinction globally; developing nations with most biodiversity often have least capacity to protect it. Justice arguments against: sovereignty — no country should be compelled by others; conditional funding can undermine national self-determination; technology transfer may be more effective than cash. Principle of Common But Differentiated Responsibility (CBDR — embedded in CBD and Paris Agreement) provides a framework: all nations share responsibility for biodiversity, but capacity and historical contribution to the problem must inform burden-sharing. Conclusion: ethical and practical arguments strongly support mandatory, but not purely conditional, biodiversity funding from high-income nations.

Propose a school-level conservation project. Describe the scientific method, expected outcomes, and how you would evaluate its impact.Hard
Show Answer

Project: Native Pollinator Garden. Method: Survey school grounds to identify baseline butterfly/bee species (quadrat counts, photo ID). Remove invasive plants from a 50 m² plot; plant 10 native flowering species (marigold, lantana, basil, Indian coral tree seedlings). Monitor monthly over one academic year: count pollinator species and individual insects per 10-minute timed observation; compare to control area. Expected outcomes: measurable increase in pollinator species richness; phenological data (which species visit which plant). Impact evaluation: statistical comparison of pre- and post-planting species counts using a paired t-test; qualitative assessment of student awareness changes through pre/post survey. What is human-wildlife conflict?Easy

What is ecotourism?Easy

What does 'intrinsic value' of a species mean?Easy

Set B Medium – Understanding & Application

Explain why relocating tribal communities from national parks is considered ethically problematic.Medium
Show Answer

Forced relocation violates human rights (right to home, culture, livelihood); communities lose access to ancestral lands, traditional medicine, and food sources; relocation settlements often lack adequate compensation and services; destroys cultural identity; evidence shows relocated communities often end up in poverty. It is also ecologically questionable — many tribal communities are proven stewards of forest biodiversity.

Describe two strategies used in India to reduce human-elephant conflict.Medium
Show Answer

1. Beehive fences: rows of beehives hung around farm perimeters — elephants fear bees and avoid the boundary (low-cost, effective). 2. Chilli smoke barriers: burning dried chilli peppers along field borders repels elephants due to capsaicin irritation to sensitive trunks.

How can poorly managed ecotourism harm the very ecosystems it claims to protect?Medium
Show Answer

Over-visitation disturbs wildlife during breeding; vehicle tracks damage habitat; noise pollution alters animal behaviour; waste disposal pollutes water sources; tourist infrastructure (hotels, roads) fragments habitats; "wildlife selfie" tourism stresses animals. When local communities don't receive tourism revenue, they have no incentive to reduce poaching.

Explain the concept of intergenerational equity in environmental ethics.Medium
Show Answer

Intergenerational equity is the principle that the current generation has a responsibility to conserve natural resources and biodiversity so that future generations have equal opportunity to benefit from them. It is central to the concept of sustainable development.

How does the concept of 'tragedy of the commons' apply to wildlife conservation?Medium
Show Answer

The tragedy of the commons: when a resource is shared (no individual ownership), rational individual actors overexploit it because the benefit of use is individual but the cost of depletion is collective. Applied to conservation: open-access wildlife (ocean fish, shared forest) gets overexploited by multiple users each acting in self-interest. Solution: regulated commons management through community agreements, property rights, or state regulation.

Should the killing of a rogue tiger that attacks humans be permitted? Discuss from an ethical perspective.Medium
Show Answer

Arguments for: human safety is paramount; preventing retaliatory killing of entire tiger populations; removing a specific animal reduces broader conflict. Arguments against: the tiger is critically endangered; it was likely provoked by habitat encroachment; shooting is irreversible; non-lethal alternatives (translocation, better fencing) should be exhausted first. Ethical conclusion: lethal removal should be a last resort after all non-lethal alternatives have been genuinely attempted and failed.

How does the unequal distribution of conservation costs and benefits create social justice problems?Medium
Show Answer

Conservation benefits (biodiversity, ecosystem services, carbon storage) are often enjoyed globally or nationally, while costs (restricted land access, crop losses, no development) are borne by local, often poor, communities near protected areas. This inequity breeds resentment and undermines conservation compliance. Justice requires that those who bear conservation costs receive fair compensation and benefits.

Explain why hunting bans alone have often failed to reduce poaching in African national parks.Medium
Show Answer

Hunting bans remove a legal incentive to protect wildlife (no trophy revenue for communities) without addressing poverty driving poaching. Communities adjacent to parks who receive no benefit from wildlife have no motivation to report or deter poachers. In contrast, community-based conservation (e.g., CAMPFIRE in Zimbabwe) that shares wildlife revenue has shown more success in reducing poaching.

MYP D — Critically evaluate: "The right of an endangered species to survive outweighs the right of a local community to its traditional livelihood." Hard
Show Answer

For the claim: some species are at genuine point of extinction; once gone, cannot be recovered; communities have alternatives (relocation support, new livelihoods) while species have none. Against: human rights are not conditional on species status; framing it as a binary choice is a false dilemma; indigenous communities often caused minimal harm and should not bear the burden of conservation that benefits all of humanity. Balanced assessment: the real conflict is between conservation and poverty, not conservation and communities per se. Solutions exist that don't require choosing: community-based conservation, legal rights-based approaches, and genuine economic development can achieve both. The zero-sum framing should be rejected.

Analyse whether controlled trophy hunting can ethically fund conservation in developing countries.Hard
Show Answer

For: trophy hunting in Zimbabwe and Namibia generates revenue shared with communities — reducing poaching incentives; money funds anti-poaching; hunting quotas are below population impact thresholds; Cecil the lion incident notwithstanding, well-managed programmes may have net positive impact. Against: morally objectionable to most people; revenue rarely reaches local communities effectively (corruption, middlemen); creates normative acceptance of killing endangered animals; photographic ecotourism generates more revenue per animal; risk of quota mismanagement. Design a conflict resolution framework for a case where expanding a tiger reserve would displace 500 forest-dependent families in Maharashtra.Hard

Should corporations be held legally liable for biodiversity loss caused by their supply chains (e.g., palm oil deforestation)? Argue with examples.Hard
Show Answer

For liability: corporations directly profit from supply chains causing biodiversity loss; they have financial capacity to implement sustainability; legal liability creates strongest incentive; EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (2024) sets this precedent. Evaluate the ethical tensions in using AI surveillance (drones, camera traps with facial recognition of poachers) in protected areas.Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: dramatically increases anti-poaching effectiveness; deters would-be poachers; reduces human ranger risk. MYP D — Should India legalise trade in tiger bones for medicinal use (from zoo-bred tigers) to reduce pressure on wild populations? Present a rigorous argument.Hard
Show Answer

For legalisation: if legal supply from captive-bred animals floods the market, illegal wild-tiger poaching becomes less profitable; controlled trade could fund conservation; precedent from vicuña wool trade (legal trade in Peru/Bolivia reduced poaching). Against: legal trade normalises tiger product use, potentially increasing overall demand; impossible to distinguish legal from illegal products in practice (launder effect); all evidence from rhino horn and elephant ivory legalisation trials shows legal trade accelerates poaching; tiger farming infrastructure also generates animal welfare issues. Scientific consensus strongly against legalisation. Conclusion: overwhelming evidence shows legalisation would increase, not decrease, poaching pressure. The demand-reduction strategy (education campaigns, alternative medicines) is the evidence-based approach.

Assess the claim: "Climate justice and conservation justice are inseparable — you cannot solve one without the other."Hard
Show Answer

Supporting evidence: the same economic structures that drive fossil fuel use also drive deforestation (short-term profit over ecological cost); the same communities most vulnerable to climate change (small farmers, forest peoples) are most affected by both climate impacts and conservation restrictions; climate change is now a leading driver of biodiversity loss; funding for both issues competes for the same limited global environmental finance. Counter: different institutions, timelines, and strategies; conflating the two may dilute focused policy action on either. Assessment: the ecological and social linkages are deep and real. Integrated approaches (nature-based solutions, community-led conservation linked to carbon finance) that simultaneously address both are gaining scientific and policy traction and likely more effective than separated strategies.
