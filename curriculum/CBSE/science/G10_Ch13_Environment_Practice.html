Set A Easy — Ecosystem Components

Define an ecosystem.Easy
Show Answer

An ecosystem is a self-sustaining unit comprising all living organisms (biotic components) and non-living physical factors (abiotic components) in an area, interacting with each other and exchanging matter and energy.

Distinguish between biotic and abiotic components. Give two examples of each.Easy
Show Answer

Biotic: living components — e.g., plants (producers), animals (consumers), bacteria (decomposers). Abiotic: non-living physical/chemical factors — e.g., sunlight, temperature, water, soil, air, pH.

What are producers (autotrophs)? Give two examples.Easy
Show Answer

Producers are organisms that make their own food by photosynthesis using sunlight, CO₂, and water. Examples: green plants (grass, trees) and algae/phytoplankton in aquatic ecosystems.

What are consumers (heterotrophs)? Distinguish herbivores, carnivores, and omnivores.Easy
Show Answer

Consumers cannot make their own food and feed on other organisms. Herbivores: eat only plants (e.g., deer, rabbit). Carnivores: eat only animals (e.g., lion, eagle). Omnivores: eat both (e.g., humans, crow, bear).

What are decomposers? Why are they essential?Easy
Show Answer

Decomposers (saprotrophs) are organisms (bacteria, fungi) that break down dead organic matter into simple inorganic nutrients. They recycle nutrients back into the soil, making them available to producers again — essential for nutrient cycling.

Name two types of natural ecosystems and one artificial ecosystem.Easy
Show Answer

Natural: terrestrial (forest, grassland) and aquatic (pond, ocean). Artificial (man-made): crop field (agro-ecosystem), aquarium, garden.

What role does sunlight play in an ecosystem?Easy
Show Answer

Sunlight is the primary energy source for all ecosystems (except deep-sea hydrothermal vent ecosystems). Producers capture solar energy through photosynthesis and convert it to chemical energy (glucose), which then flows through the entire food chain.

Give one difference between a terrestrial and an aquatic ecosystem.Easy
Show Answer

Terrestrial: land-based; primary producers are rooted plants; abiotic factors include soil, humidity. Aquatic: water-based; primary producers are algae and phytoplankton; abiotic factors include water depth, salinity, dissolved oxygen.

Set B Medium — Ecosystem Components

Explain how a pond ecosystem is self-sustaining.Medium
Show Answer

A pond contains: producers (algae, water plants — photosynthesise), consumers (zooplankton, fish, frogs — feed on producers/each other), and decomposers (bacteria — break down dead matter). Nutrients cycle continuously: decomposers return minerals to water → producers use them → consumers eat producers. Energy input: sunlight. The system sustains itself without human intervention.

Distinguish between a natural and an artificial ecosystem in terms of stability and biodiversity.Medium
Show Answer

Natural: high biodiversity → complex food webs → greater resilience and stability (more redundancy). Artificial (e.g., wheat field): low biodiversity (monoculture) → simple food web → fragile — a single pest or disease can collapse the whole system. Needs human inputs (fertilisers, pesticides) to remain productive. Natural ecosystems self-regulate; artificial ones require constant management.

Why are decomposers sometimes called the "cleaning crew" of the ecosystem?Medium
Show Answer

Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead organisms and waste into simpler inorganic compounds (CO₂, water, mineral salts). Without them, dead matter would accumulate indefinitely, nutrients would be locked in unusable organic forms, and the ecosystem would collapse from nutrient depletion. They clean up biological waste and keep the nutrient cycle running.

How does the removal of decomposers affect an ecosystem?Medium
Show Answer

Without decomposers: dead organic matter piles up (no breakdown). Nutrients are not returned to the soil/water. Producers (plants) run out of minerals → photosynthesis declines → consumers starve → ecosystem collapses. Nutrient cycling stops completely. An ecosystem cannot sustain life without decomposers recycling nutrients back to the inorganic pool.

Explain why the amount of energy available decreases at each successive trophic level.Medium
Show Answer

Energy is lost at each trophic level as: (1) Heat from cellular respiration. (2) Energy used for growth, reproduction, movement. (3) Undigested material passed as waste (faeces). Only ~10% of the energy at one level passes to the next. This limits the number of trophic levels (typically 3-5) and explains why there are fewer top predators than herbivores.

Compare the roles of producers, consumers, and decomposers in nutrient cycling.Medium
Show Answer

Producers: absorb inorganic nutrients (CO₂, minerals) and convert to organic molecules via photosynthesis. Consumers: transfer organic nutrients up food chains, releasing some CO₂ via respiration. Decomposers: break dead organic matter back into inorganic forms (CO₂, NH₃, PO₄³⁻). Complete the cycle: inorganic → organic (producers) → organic up chain (consumers) → inorganic again (decomposers) → back to producers.

Why is a forest ecosystem considered more stable than a grassland ecosystem?Medium
Show Answer

Forests have: greater biodiversity (more species), multi-layered canopy structure, complex food webs with many alternative pathways, deeper root systems stabilising soil, and higher biomass. If one species is removed, others fill the role (redundancy). Grasslands have simpler food webs — removal of a key species can cause cascading collapse. Higher biodiversity = higher resilience.

What is the difference between a habitat and an ecosystem?Medium
Show Answer

Habitat: the specific physical place where an organism lives (e.g., a burrow, a tree, a rock pool) — defined from the organism's perspective. Ecosystem: the complete system of all organisms and their abiotic environment in an area, including all interactions and energy flows — defined at the community level. Many habitats exist within one ecosystem.

Set C Hard — Ecosystem Components

Explain the concept of ecological succession and describe how a bare rock becomes a forest (primary succession).Hard
Show Answer

Primary succession: bare rock → pioneer species (lichens, mosses — tolerate harsh conditions) → weathering creates thin soil → grasses/ferns colonise → shrubs follow → small trees → climax community (mature forest). Each stage (sere) modifies abiotic conditions (soil depth, moisture, shade) making it suitable for the next. Takes hundreds to thousands of years. Secondary succession (disturbed land with soil) is faster. Climax community is the stable endpoint.

Explain the concept of keystone species with an example. What happens when a keystone species is removed?Hard
Show Answer

A keystone species has a disproportionately large effect on ecosystem structure relative to its abundance. Example: sea otters — they eat sea urchins; without otters, urchins over-graze kelp forests → kelp disappears → entire ecosystem collapses (dozens of species dependent on kelp lose their habitat). Another example: wolves in Yellowstone — their reintroduction (1995) changed river courses by controlling deer behaviour (trophic cascade). Removing a keystone species causes cascading collapse far beyond what its biomass would suggest.

Analyse the "island biogeography" theory and what it predicts about species richness vs. island size and isolation.Hard
Show Answer

MacArthur-Wilson theory (1967): species richness on an island reflects balance between immigration rate (from mainland) and extinction rate (on island). Large islands: lower extinction (more habitats, larger populations). Close islands: higher immigration. Species-area relationship: S = cAᶻ (S = species, A = area, z ≈ 0.25). Predictions: larger island → more species; closer to mainland → more species. Applies to habitat fragments (forest patches = "islands" in farmland). Predicts 50% reduction in area → ~17% species loss (by S = cAᵒ·²⁵). Relevant to conservation biology — minimum viable habitat size.

Explain why artificial ecosystems (farms, fish ponds) require constant energy input while natural ecosystems are self-sustaining.Hard
Show Answer

Natural ecosystems: biodiversity creates multiple redundant pathways; nutrient cycling is complete (decomposers return all nutrients); natural pest control by predators; no harvesting removes biomass. Artificial ecosystems: monoculture removes natural competition checks → pest outbreaks; harvesting removes large biomass (nutrients leave the system in crops/fish) → must replace with fertilisers; no natural predators of pests → need pesticides; simplified food web → no self-regulation. Every input (fertiliser, pesticide, water, labour) represents external energy that natural systems provide for free through biodiversity.

How does eutrophication destroy aquatic ecosystems? Trace the full cascade from fertiliser runoff to fish kill.Hard
Show Answer

Fertiliser runoff (nitrogen, phosphorus) enters water → algal bloom (algae grow explosively → dense surface mat). Sunlight blocked → submerged plants die. Algae die → decomposers (bacteria) multiply enormously → consume dissolved O₂ in huge quantities (biological oxygen demand spikes). O₂ levels crash → aerobic organisms (fish, invertebrates) suffocate → mass fish kill → dead zone (hypoxic/anoxic). Dead fish → more decomposition → more O₂ depletion → self-reinforcing collapse. Examples: Gulf of Mexico dead zone (Mississippi fertiliser runoff), Lake Erie, Chilika lake threats.

What is the Gaia hypothesis? How does it relate to ecosystem stability at a planetary level?Hard
Show Answer

James Lovelock's Gaia hypothesis (1972): Earth functions as a single self-regulating system — the biosphere, atmosphere, hydrosphere, and geosphere interact to maintain conditions suitable for life. Example: phytoplankton produce DMS → forms cloud condensation nuclei → clouds reflect sunlight → cooling → feedback regulates temperature. O₂ is maintained at ~21% by photosynthesis/respiration balance. CO₂ levels regulated by carbon cycle. The hypothesis extends ecosystem homeostasis (individual ecosystems self-regulate) to the planetary scale. Controversial — not all aspects supported by evidence, but the concept that life actively shapes Earth's chemistry is now mainstream Earth system science.

Explain the carbon cycle and how deforestation disrupts it.Hard
Show Answer

Carbon cycle: CO₂ in atmosphere → photosynthesis → organic carbon in plants → eaten by consumers (respiration releases CO₂) → dead matter → decomposers release CO₂ → returns to atmosphere. Oceans absorb CO₂ (carbonate system). Fossil fuels = ancient carbon stored underground. Deforestation: (1) Trees (major carbon sinks) are cut → stored carbon released as CO₂ (burning or decomposition). (2) Fewer trees → less photosynthetic uptake of CO₂. Net effect: CO₂ rises faster → enhanced greenhouse effect → climate change. Deforestation accounts for ~10-15% of global CO₂ emissions. Reforestation and halting deforestation are key climate mitigation strategies.

Analyse why biodiversity loss is considered a greater long-term threat than many immediate environmental problems.Hard
Show Answer

Biodiversity provides: ecosystem services (pollination, water purification, soil formation, climate regulation) worth ~$125 trillion/year. Each extinction permanently removes unique genetic information. Functional redundancy (multiple species performing similar roles) provides resilience — as species disappear, resilience declines non-linearly. When critical thresholds are crossed, ecosystem collapse can be sudden and irreversible. Current extinction rate: 100-1000× background rate (6th mass extinction). Unlike pollution (can be cleaned), climate change (can be slowed) — extinction is irreversible. Loss of pollinators alone threatens 75% of food crops. Biodiversity loss undermines the ecological foundations of all life, including human civilisation.

Set A Easy — Food Chains and Webs

Define a food chain and give one example.Easy
Show Answer

A food chain is a linear sequence showing the transfer of energy from one organism to the next through feeding. Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle.

What is a food web?Easy
Show Answer

A food web is a network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem. It represents the complex feeding relationships where most organisms eat more than one type of food and are eaten by more than one predator.

Why does energy flow in only one direction in a food chain?Easy
Show Answer

Energy enters the food chain through photosynthesis (sun → producers) and flows from one trophic level to the next through feeding. At each level, energy is lost as heat (Second Law of Thermodynamics) — heat cannot be recaptured and reused in the food chain. Energy flow is unidirectional (sun → producers → consumers → decomposers → heat lost).

What is an apex predator? Give one example.Easy
Show Answer

An apex predator is at the top of a food chain — it has no natural predators. Examples: lion (land), great white shark (ocean), killer whale (marine), eagle (aerial). They regulate populations of organisms lower in the food web.

Name the trophic levels in the food chain: Grass → Deer → Tiger.Easy
Show Answer

Grass = 1st trophic level (producer). Deer = 2nd trophic level (primary consumer/herbivore). Tiger = 3rd trophic level (secondary consumer/apex carnivore).

Why is a food web more realistic than a single food chain?Easy
Show Answer

In nature, most animals eat a variety of organisms and are themselves eaten by several predators. A single food chain oversimplifies this. A food web shows multiple interconnected pathways, accurately representing the complex, flexible feeding relationships in real ecosystems.

Can a food chain begin with an animal? Explain.Easy
Show Answer

No. All food chains begin with producers (green plants or algae) that capture solar energy via photosynthesis. Animals are heterotrophs — they cannot make their own food. All energy ultimately traces back to photosynthesis (or chemosynthesis in deep-sea ecosystems).

What is the difference between a primary consumer and a secondary consumer?Easy
Show Answer

Primary consumer: eats producers directly (herbivore, 2nd trophic level). Example: rabbit eats grass. Secondary consumer: eats primary consumers (carnivore, 3rd trophic level). Example: fox eats rabbit.

Set B Medium — Food Chains and Webs

Explain how removing a middle predator from a food web can cause a "trophic cascade."Medium
Show Answer

Example: wolves removed from Yellowstone. Wolf population → elk/deer numbers increase → deer over-graze vegetation → plant community collapses → soil erodes → rivers destabilise → aquatic life suffers. This ripple effect through trophic levels is a trophic cascade. Removing wolves affected rivers, trees, fish, birds — far beyond the direct predator-prey relationship. Top-down control by predators is essential for ecosystem balance.

Why do food webs have greater stability than individual food chains?Medium
Show Answer

In a food web, if one prey species disappears, predators can switch to alternative prey (functional redundancy). Populations fluctuate less dramatically. In a simple food chain, if one link is broken, the entire chain collapses (no alternative pathway). Food webs provide resilience through multiple energy pathways — the ecosystem can absorb species losses without complete collapse.

Explain the detritus food chain. How does it differ from the grazing food chain?Medium
Show Answer

Grazing food chain (GFC): starts with living producers → herbivores → carnivores. Energy from live plant material. Detritus food chain (DFC): starts with dead organic matter (detritus) → detritivores (earthworms, millipedes, certain insects) → decomposers (bacteria, fungi). Transfers energy locked in dead material. In most terrestrial ecosystems, more energy flows through the DFC than the GFC. Both are interconnected — organisms from GFC die and enter DFC.

Construct a simple food web for a grassland with: grass, rabbit, deer, fox, wolf, eagle, insects, and sparrow. Show at least 5 relationships.Medium
Show Answer

Grass → Rabbit → Fox → Wolf. Grass → Deer → Wolf. Grass → Insects → Sparrow → Eagle. Insects → Frog → Snake → Eagle (if frog/snake added). Rabbit → Eagle. Deer → Fox. These show multiple pathways — the web is interconnected. Energy flows from grass to all higher levels through various routes.

Explain why there are usually no more than 4-5 trophic levels in a food chain.Medium
Show Answer

Only ~10% of energy passes from one trophic level to the next. After 4-5 levels, so little energy remains (0.01% of original) that it cannot support a viable population of organisms. Example: 1,000,000 J (grass) → 100,000 J (herbivore) → 10,000 J (primary carnivore) → 1,000 J (secondary) → 100 J (tertiary). Insufficient energy for a 6th level. Energy loss limits chain length.

Discuss the ecological consequences if all snakes in a wheat field were removed.Medium
Show Answer

Snakes control rodent (rat/mouse) populations. Without snakes: rats proliferate → consume large amounts of grain (wheat field pest). Crop damage → reduced yield → food loss. Also: frogs (snake prey) increase → insects they eat decrease. Trophic cascade through the farm ecosystem. Lesson: predators (even feared ones like snakes) provide essential pest-control "ecosystem services" — removing them has costly agricultural consequences.

Explain the concept of "trophic level" and why omnivores occupy multiple levels simultaneously.Medium
Show Answer

Trophic level: the position an organism occupies in a food chain based on what it eats. Omnivores (e.g., humans, bears, crows) eat both plants (2nd trophic level) and animals (3rd, 4th levels). Therefore they occupy multiple trophic levels simultaneously — their trophic position is a weighted average. This makes food webs more complex but also more stable (omnivores can switch food sources when one becomes scarce).

How does energy transfer efficiency in a food web affect human dietary choices from an environmental standpoint?Medium
Show Answer

10% efficiency rule: to produce 1 kg of beef protein, ~10 kg of grain must be fed to cattle (only 10% converted). That grain could directly feed 10 people. A plant-based diet uses land and water far more efficiently. A person eating at a lower trophic level (vegetarian) uses ~10× less agricultural land and produces ~5-10× less CO₂ than a meat-heavy diet. This is why reducing meat consumption is recommended for environmental sustainability — each step up the food chain multiplies resource use.

Set C Hard — Food Chains and Webs

Explain Lotka-Volterra predator-prey dynamics and what they predict about population cycles.Hard
Show Answer

Lotka-Volterra equations (1920s): dN_prey/dt = rN − aNP; dN_predator/dt = baNP − mP. Predictions: prey and predator populations oscillate out of phase in cycles. When prey is abundant → predators increase → prey declines → predators decline → prey recovers → cycle repeats. Classic example: Canada lynx and snowshoe hare (10-year cycles from Hudson Bay Company fur records). Assumptions: prey has unlimited food (exponential growth), predator feeds only on prey. Real-world modifications: multiple prey species, territorial limits, time lags dampen or stabilise oscillations.

Explain the concept of "connectance" in a food web and how it relates to ecosystem stability.Hard
Show Answer

Connectance C = actual links / possible links in a food web. Early ecological theory (May, 1972): mathematically, more complex (highly connected) webs are less stable. Later empirical work: real food webs are structured to be stable — weak interactions (many rare predator-prey pairs) with a few strong interactions provides stability. High connectance with mostly weak interactions dampens oscillations. "Complexity begets stability" only when interactions are structured appropriately — not random. Modern food web ecology integrates network theory with empirical data.

Explain inverted biomass pyramids — give an example and explain why they exist despite the 10% rule.Hard
Show Answer

Normal biomass pyramid: producers > herbivores > carnivores. Inverted: producers < consumers in biomass. Example: aquatic ecosystems — phytoplankton (producers) have very small biomass but reproduce extremely rapidly (high turnover rate). Zooplankton (consumers) have higher standing biomass at any instant even though they depend on phytoplankton. The 10% rule applies to energy flow (production rate), not standing biomass. Biomass pyramid is based on instantaneous stock; energy pyramid (always upright) is based on flux. The inversion occurs when producers are small but fast-reproducing, and consumers are large and slow-reproducing.

Compare top-down vs. bottom-up control of food webs with examples from real ecosystems.Hard
Show Answer

Top-down control: apex predators control populations below them. Example: wolves control deer → deer control vegetation. Remove wolves: deer increase, plants decrease. Evidence: Yellowstone wolf reintroduction (1995). Bottom-up control: nutrient/light availability controls primary production → limits herbivores → limits predators. Example: nutrient-poor deep ocean → sparse phytoplankton → few zooplankton → few fish. Add nutrients (iron fertilisation experiments): blooms cascade up food web. Most real ecosystems show both: bottom-up sets the ceiling; top-down structures within it. Human activities affect both: eutrophication (bottom-up), overfishing (top-down).

What is an "ecological network" and how does modern ecology use graph theory to analyse food webs?Hard
Show Answer

Ecological network = food web represented as a directed graph (nodes = species, edges = feeding links). Graph theory metrics: (1) Node degree (number of prey/predator links — highly connected species = "hubs"). (2) Clustering coefficient (how interconnected neighbours are). (3) Path length (average links between any two species). Real food webs show "small world" properties — short average path lengths, high clustering. Removing hub species (high degree) causes greater cascade than removing peripheral species. This informs conservation: protecting highly connected species (often keystone) provides most network resilience. Used to model disease spread, invasive species impacts, extinction cascades.

How did Paine's 1966 starfish removal experiment demonstrate the keystone species concept experimentally?Hard
Show Answer

Paine removed all Pisaster ochraceus (sea star) from an intertidal rocky shore in Washington. Result: mussels (the starfish's primary prey) took over, crowding out barnacles, limpets, chitons, whelks, and algae → biodiversity dropped from 15 to 8 species. The starfish, despite being a minor fraction of biomass, controlled the entire community composition. Control plot (starfish present): high diversity maintained. This first demonstrated experimentally that a single species can be disproportionately important — the "keystone species" concept (named after the central stone in an arch that holds everything together). Removed: arch collapses.

Explain how the reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone in 1995 altered the physical landscape — the "ecology of fear."Hard
Show Answer

Before wolves: elk grazed everywhere, including riverbanks → vegetation stripped → bank erosion → rivers meandered. After wolves: elk avoided valleys and riverbanks (fear of wolves, not just actual predation) — the "ecology of fear." Vegetation recovered on riverbanks → tree roots stabilised banks → rivers narrowed and deepened → new habitats for beavers, otters, fish. Beavers created ponds → more habitat diversity. The change in animal behaviour (driven by fear) caused geomorphic (landscape) changes — called a trophic cascade extending to the physical environment ("rivers changed their songs"). This demonstrated that predator effects extend through behaviour, not just direct killing.

Analyse how overfishing of large predatory fish (cod, tuna) transforms marine food webs and causes "fishing down the food web."Hard
Show Answer

Pauly et al. (1998) showed: as large fish (high trophic level) are depleted by overfishing, fisheries shift to smaller, lower trophic level species (sardines, anchovies, jellyfish). Mean trophic level of global catch declined from ~3.4 to ~3.1 between 1950-2000. Consequences: (1) Large predator removal → jellyfish blooms (no predation) → jellyfish eat fish larvae → fish stock collapse. (2) Lower trophic species (forage fish like anchovies) are themselves foundation of marine food webs → their depletion affects whales, seabirds, dolphins. (3) "Fishing down" eventually leads to fishing marine invertebrates and algae — an ecologically barren sea. Recovery requires multi-decade fishing bans and ecosystem-based management.

Set A Easy — Trophic Efficiency

State the 10% law (Lindeman's rule).Easy
Show Answer

Only 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is transferred to the next higher trophic level. The remaining 90% is lost as heat (respiration), movement, waste, etc. Proposed by Raymond Lindeman in 1942.

If grass stores 10,000 J, how much energy is available to the herbivore?Easy
Show Answer

10% of 10,000 = 1,000 J available to the herbivore. The remaining 9,000 J is lost as heat through the grass's own respiration and other metabolic processes.

What is an energy pyramid? Which direction does it always point?Easy
Show Answer

An energy pyramid shows the amount of energy available at each trophic level. It always has a wide base (producers, most energy) narrowing toward the apex (top predators, least energy). Unlike biomass pyramids, energy pyramids are always upright — energy decreases at every level.

Why are there fewer lions than zebras in a savanna?Easy
Show Answer

Due to the 10% rule. Zebras (herbivores) can only transfer 10% of the grass energy to lions (carnivores). Insufficient energy is available to support as large a lion population as the zebra population. Each successive trophic level has less available energy and thus supports fewer individuals.

Name the three types of ecological pyramids.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Pyramid of numbers — number of organisms at each level. (2) Pyramid of biomass — total mass of organisms at each level. (3) Pyramid of energy — energy content at each level. Only the pyramid of energy is always upright (never inverted).

Give a chain where 1,000,000 kJ of solar energy is captured by grass. Calculate energy at each level up to a tertiary consumer.Easy
Show Answer

Grass: 1,000,000 kJ. Primary consumer (herbivore): 100,000 kJ (10%). Secondary consumer: 10,000 kJ (1%). Tertiary consumer: 1,000 kJ (0.1%). Each step retains only 10% of the previous level's energy.

What happens to the 90% energy lost between trophic levels?Easy
Show Answer

Lost as heat through: (1) Cellular respiration — organisms use energy for maintenance, movement, growth, reproduction. (2) Undigested food excreted as waste (faeces). (3) Heat generated during metabolic reactions. This energy is dissipated and cannot be reused in the food chain (2nd Law of Thermodynamics).

Can a pyramid of numbers be inverted? Give an example.Easy
Show Answer

Yes. Example: a single large tree (1 producer) supports thousands of insects (herbivores), which support fewer birds (carnivores). The tree is one individual but supports many — so the pyramid of numbers is inverted at the base. Parasites also cause inverted pyramids (many parasites on one host).

Set B Medium — Trophic Efficiency

A food chain: Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Small fish → Large fish. If phytoplankton fix 800,000 kJ/year, how much energy is available to the large fish?Medium
Show Answer

Zooplankton: 80,000 kJ. Small fish: 8,000 kJ. Large fish: 800 kJ. The large fish (4th trophic level) receives only 800/800,000 = 0.1% of the original energy — illustrating why ocean fish are so expensive to produce and why overfishing is devastating.

Explain why a vegetarian diet is more sustainable than a meat-based diet using the 10% rule.Medium
Show Answer

Eating plant food (2nd trophic level): 10% of crop energy transferred to human. Eating meat (3rd trophic level): only 10% of herbivore energy (= 1% of crop) reaches human. To produce the same calorie for a meat-eater, ~10× more agricultural land, water, and energy are needed than for a vegetarian. A vegetarian diet feeds ~10× more people on the same agricultural resources — crucial for feeding a population of 8+ billion sustainably.

Explain why large apex predators (tigers, sharks) need vast territories or large ocean ranges.Medium
Show Answer

Apex predators are at the 3rd-4th trophic level — only 0.1-1% of original solar energy reaches them. To meet their large energy needs, they must hunt over enormous areas where sufficient prey is available. A tiger needs ~50-100 km² because this area contains sufficient deer (herbivores) which themselves need large areas for grazing. Energy scarcity at higher trophic levels directly translates to low population densities and large territory requirements.

Compare the pyramid of energy to the pyramid of biomass in an ocean ecosystem. Explain why the biomass pyramid may be inverted.Medium
Show Answer

Ocean biomass pyramid: phytoplankton biomass at any instant may be LESS than zooplankton biomass — inverted pyramid. Reason: phytoplankton reproduce extremely rapidly (high turnover/productivity), so their small standing stock produces large energy flow. Zooplankton are larger, longer-lived, accumulate more biomass despite depending on phytoplankton. Energy pyramid is always upright (energy flow always decreases up levels). Biomass ≠ energy flux — the inversion reflects rapid producer turnover, not a violation of energy rules.

If a tertiary consumer needs 500 kJ, how much primary producer energy was originally needed (assuming 10% efficiency at each level)?Medium
Show Answer

Work backward: Secondary consumer needed 500/0.1 = 5,000 kJ. Primary consumer needed 5,000/0.1 = 50,000 kJ. Producer needed 50,000/0.1 = 500,000 kJ. So 500,000 kJ of producer energy is needed to deliver 500 kJ to a tertiary consumer — a 0.1% overall efficiency.

Explain Lindeman's "trophic dynamic" concept and why it was a revolutionary idea in ecology.Medium
Show Answer

Raymond Lindeman (1942, based on Cedar Bog Lake study): quantified energy transfer between trophic levels — the first mathematical treatment of how energy flows through ecosystems. Before Lindeman, ecology was largely descriptive. His work showed ecosystems could be analysed as thermodynamic systems with measurable energy budgets. This transformed ecology from natural history into quantitative science, enabling ecosystem modelling, productivity measurement, and understanding of why food chains are limited in length. The "10% rule" is a simplification of his more nuanced efficiency measurements.

Why is gross primary productivity (GPP) different from net primary productivity (NPP)?Medium
Show Answer

GPP = total energy fixed by photosynthesis per unit time per unit area. NPP = GPP − plant respiration = energy actually stored in plant biomass (available to consumers). Plants use 20-50% of GPP for their own respiration (growth, maintenance). Example: tropical forest GPP ≈ 3000 g C/m²/year; plant respiration ≈ 1500 g C; NPP ≈ 1500 g C/m²/year. Only NPP is available to herbivores — GPP overestimates food web energy input. NPP is the correct measure for ecosystem productivity and is what the 10% rule applies to.

Why do warm-blooded (endothermic) animals transfer less energy per unit biomass than cold-blooded (ectothermic) animals?Medium
Show Answer

Endotherms (birds, mammals) maintain constant body temperature — require continuous heat production → large proportion of energy budget goes to thermogenesis (keeping warm). Only a small fraction remains for growth. Ectotherms (reptiles, fish, insects) rely on environment for body heat — minimal energy on thermogenesis → more energy available for growth and reproduction. A snake (ectotherm) can convert ~60-70% of food energy to biomass; a mouse (endotherm) only ~1-2%. Ectotherms are far more efficient energy converters in food webs.

Set C Hard — Trophic Efficiency

Distinguish between assimilation efficiency, net production efficiency, and ecological (trophic) efficiency. Give typical values.Hard
Show Answer

Assimilation efficiency = assimilated energy / ingested energy. Varies: herbivores ~60-90%; carnivores ~80-90%; detritivores ~10-40% (plant cell walls hard to digest). Net production efficiency (NPE) = production / assimilation. Endotherms: ~1-3% (burn most energy as heat). Ectotherms: ~10-40%. Ecological (trophic) efficiency = production at level n / production at level n-1. Typically 5-20% (commonly approximated as 10%). Trophic efficiency = assimilation efficiency × NPE × other factors. The 10% rule is a rough average across these components.

Using the concept of secondary productivity, explain why aquaculture (fish farming) of carnivorous fish (salmon) is less sustainable than farming herbivorous fish (tilapia, carp).Hard
Show Answer

Salmon (carnivore, 3rd-4th trophic level): must be fed fish meal (made from wild-caught forage fish — anchovies, herring). ~3-5 kg wild fish needed per kg salmon. Secondary productivity is low — 10% trophic efficiency amplified by feeding multiple levels. Depletes wild fish stocks just to farm salmon. Tilapia/carp (herbivore, 2nd trophic level): feed on algae/plant meal. Feed conversion ratio ~1.5-2 kg feed per kg fish — 5-10× more efficient. Growing herbivorous fish requires less land, water, and wild-fish input — far more sustainable. The same principle as vegetarian diet for humans.

Calculate the efficiency at each step and the overall ecological efficiency for the following: Phytoplankton produce 500 g C/m²/yr. Zooplankton produce 60 g C. Small fish produce 8 g C. Tuna produce 0.6 g C.Hard
Show Answer

Efficiency 1→2: 60/500 = 12%. Efficiency 2→3: 8/60 = 13.3%. Efficiency 3→4: 0.6/8 = 7.5%. Overall efficiency (1→4): 0.6/500 = 0.12%. Close to theoretical 10%×10%×10% = 0.1%. Real efficiencies vary (7.5-13%) around the ~10% mean. Tuna receives only 0.12% of phytoplankton production — explaining why large ocean predators are rare and why catching 1 kg of tuna "costs" the ecosystem >800 kg of phytoplankton production.

Explain how the HANPP (Human Appropriation of Net Primary Production) concept quantifies humanity's impact on the biosphere's energy budget.Hard
Show Answer

HANPP = fraction of Earth's terrestrial NPP appropriated by humans through harvest, land use change, and ecosystem alteration. Global terrestrial NPP ≈ 60 Pg C/yr. Human appropriation (food, fibre, wood + harvest losses + NPP reduction from land conversion): ~25-40% of potential terrestrial NPP. Implications: ~25-40% of what all land ecosystems could produce is captured for human use, leaving only 60-75% for all other species. This directly explains biodiversity loss — other species are squeezed out of energy budget. Reducing food waste and dietary shifts (lower meat) are the most effective ways to reduce HANPP.

How does the concept of "ecological footprint" relate to trophic efficiency? Calculate a simplified footprint for a meat-eater vs. vegetarian.Hard
Show Answer

Ecological footprint (EF) measures land area needed to support a person's diet. If 1 ha produces 1000 kg of grain: Direct vegetarian: 1 ha feeds ~10 people (1000 kg grain = 10 people × 100 kg grain/person). Via beef (10% efficiency): 1000 kg grain → 100 kg beef → feeds 1 person (1 ha/person). A meat-eater requires ~10× the land footprint. Global average: meat-heavy diet ≈ 2.5 ha; vegetarian ≈ 0.5 ha. If everyone ate like the average American (large meat portion), we would need ~4 Earths for food production alone. Trophic efficiency directly determines the ecological footprint of food choices.

Explain the "paradox of enrichment" — why does adding nutrients to an ecosystem sometimes destabilise predator-prey cycles?Hard
Show Answer

Rosenzweig (1971): In Lotka-Volterra models, increasing prey carrying capacity (by nutrient enrichment) doesn't always stabilise the system — it can actually cause larger amplitude oscillations → chaotic fluctuations → species extinctions. Mechanism: richer environment → prey population grows faster → predator overexploits prey → prey crashes → predator crashes → violent cycles. Simple enrichment intended to boost productivity can destabilise food webs. Observed in eutrophic lakes: algal blooms → zooplankton boom → zooplankton bust (algae gone) → unstable oscillations. Counter-intuitive: more nutrients → less stable ecosystem.

Explain how stable isotope analysis (δ¹³C, δ¹⁵N) is used to determine an organism's trophic position in a real food web.Hard
Show Answer

¹⁵N / ¹⁴N ratio (δ¹⁵N) increases by ~3-4‰ per trophic level. Reason: ¹⁴N is preferentially excreted in urine (lighter isotope lost) → ¹⁵N accumulates in tissues with each trophic step. Measuring δ¹⁵N of an organism and the local baseline (plants): trophic position = 1 + (δ¹⁵N_organism − δ¹⁵N_baseline)/3.4. δ¹³C indicates habitat/food source (terrestrial vs. aquatic). Together: map who eats what and where, without direct observation. Applications: revealed that dolphins eat fish from multiple trophic levels, that seabirds feed across ocean zones, that mercury accumulates predictably with δ¹⁵N — directly linking biomagnification to trophic position.

Why is the energy pyramid of an ecosystem always upright while the pyramid of numbers can be inverted? Explain thermodynamically.Hard
Show Answer

Energy pyramid: each trophic level can only use 10% of the level below (2nd Law of Thermodynamics — 90% becomes entropy/heat, irreversibly lost). Energy flows in one direction (thermodynamically downhill — from organised chemical energy to heat). Therefore energy at each level must be less than the one below. Always upright. Pyramid of numbers: counts individuals, not energy. One tree (producer) has far more energy than 1000 insects, but is counted as 1 organism → base has 1 vs apex has many → inverted numbers. Numbers reflect body size and reproductive strategy, not thermodynamics. Energy is the fundamental currency of ecology; numbers are an artefact of size distribution.

Set A Easy — Biomagnification

Define biomagnification.Easy
Show Answer

Biomagnification is the progressive increase in concentration of toxic substances (e.g., DDT, methylmercury) in organisms at successively higher trophic levels. Top predators accumulate the highest concentrations.

Why do toxins accumulate more at higher trophic levels?Easy
Show Answer

Fat-soluble, persistent toxins are not excreted. Each predator consumes many prey, accumulating all their toxin loads. Only 10% of energy (but ~100% of toxin) passes up each trophic level, so concentration increases at each step.

Name two chemicals that undergo biomagnification.Easy
Show Answer

(1) DDT (pesticide — now banned in most countries). (2) Methylmercury (from industrial waste). Also: PCBs, dioxins, organochlorine compounds.

Which organisms are most affected by biomagnification?Easy
Show Answer

Apex predators — large fish (swordfish, tuna), eagles, dolphins, polar bears, and humans. They accumulate the highest toxin concentrations by consuming many organisms from lower levels.

How is bioaccumulation different from biomagnification?Easy
Show Answer

Bioaccumulation: toxin buildup within a single organism over its lifetime. Biomagnification: increasing toxin concentration across multiple trophic levels in a food chain. Bioaccumulation is individual-level; biomagnification is ecosystem-level.

Why was DDT harmful to birds of prey like the bald eagle?Easy
Show Answer

High DDT concentrations (from biomagnification through fish) interfered with calcium metabolism, causing abnormally thin eggshells. Shells cracked under incubating parents, destroying eggs — causing population crashes in eagles and other raptors.

State two properties of a chemical that make it susceptible to biomagnification.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Lipophilic (fat-soluble) — stored in body fat, not excreted. (2) Persistent (non-biodegradable) — resists biological and chemical breakdown. Both ensure the chemical accumulates rather than being eliminated.

Name the book that made DDT's ecological dangers famous and its author.Easy
Show Answer

"Silent Spring" (1962) by Rachel Carson. It documented how DDT's biomagnification was killing songbirds and raptors and launched the modern environmental movement, leading eventually to DDT's ban in the USA (1972).

Set B Medium — Biomagnification

DDT: water = 0.003 ppm, phytoplankton = 0.04 ppm, zooplankton = 0.5 ppm, small fish = 2 ppm, large fish = 25 ppm. Calculate the magnification factor from water to large fish.Medium
Show Answer

25 / 0.003 ≈ 8,333×. DDT concentrates ~8000-fold from water to large fish — explaining why even "safe" environmental DDT levels reach toxic concentrations in apex predators.

Explain how mercury from an industrial outfall affects a coastal fishing community more than factory workers.Medium
Show Answer

Factory workers receive controlled doses. Discharged mercury → bacteria in sediments convert to methylmercury (more toxic, lipophilic) → biomagnifies: water → plankton → small fish → large fish (10,000-1,000,000× concentration). Fishermen eating contaminated fish daily receive far higher doses than workers. Minamata disease, Japan (1950s): thousands killed or disabled via methylmercury in fish.

How does DDT disrupt bird eggshell formation at the molecular level?Medium
Show Answer

DDT metabolite DDE inhibits carbonic anhydrase enzyme in the shell gland, reducing CaCO₃ deposition → thinner shells. DDT also mimics oestrogen (endocrine disruptor), disrupting hormonal regulation of calcium metabolism. Combined effect: shell thickness reduced up to 20% → shells crack under incubating parent → egg mortality → population collapse.

Explain how microplastics undergo biomagnification in marine food webs.Medium
Show Answer

Microplastics (<5 mm) ingested by zooplankton and filter feeders. They accumulate in tissues and are transferred to higher predators. Additionally, microplastics adsorb fat-soluble POPs (DDT, PCBs) on their surfaces — acting as "toxin delivery vehicles" that amplify chemical biomagnification when ingested. Found at highest concentrations in dolphins, seals, and large fish.

What health effects do biomagnified toxins cause in humans?Medium
Show Answer

Methylmercury: neurological damage especially to fetuses (Minamata disease — tremors, cognitive impairment). PCBs: immunosuppression, endocrine disruption, carcinogenesis. DDT/DDE: endocrine disruption, reproductive effects. Dioxins: carcinogenic, immune damage. All lipophilic — concentrate in human fat and breast milk. Dietary advisories: limit large predatory fish (swordfish, shark) for pregnant women.

Why was DDT initially considered a "miracle pesticide"? What lesson did its story teach?Medium
Show Answer

DDT was cheap, effective against malaria mosquitoes and agricultural pests, and long-lasting. Saved millions from malaria (Nobel Prize 1948). Lesson: immediate toxicity to target organisms tells nothing about long-term ecosystem effects. Its persistence and lipophilicity (valued as features) became catastrophic through biomagnification — establishing the precautionary principle: study ecological fate before widespread use.

Describe the events that led to the near-extinction of Peregrine falcons due to DDT.Medium
Show Answer

Peregrines eat birds (3rd-4th trophic level). DDT biomagnified to eggshell-thinning levels. USA population: ~7,000 pairs (1940s) → ~324 pairs (1970). DDT banned 1972. Captive breeding + reintroduction (Cornell Lab). By 1999: delisted from Endangered Species List; ~3,000+ pairs recovered. Conservation success enabled directly by the DDT ban.

Are humans affected by biomagnification? Justify with examples.Medium
Show Answer

Yes. (1) Minamata, Japan: 2,000+ deaths from methylmercury in fish. (2) Inuit communities: highest PCB body burdens of any human population from marine mammal diet. (3) WHO limits tuna/swordfish for pregnant women due to mercury. (4) Breast milk contamination — lipophilic pollutants transfer to infants. Biomagnification is a direct human health issue worldwide.

Set C Hard — Biomagnification

Derive mathematically how BMF > 1 implies magnification. Calculate final concentration after 4 levels with BMF = 10 and C₀ = 0.001 ppm.Hard
Show Answer

BMF = concentration in predator / concentration in prey. If BMF > 1 at each step: C_n = C₀ × BMF^(n-1). For C₀ = 0.001 ppm, BMF = 10, n = 4: C_4 = 0.001 × 10³ = 1 ppm. 1000× increase. Contrast: energy transfer factor = 0.1 (diminishes). Toxin: amplifies while energy decreases — the thermodynamic asymmetry that makes biomagnification possible.

Explain the mechanism of mercury methylation by bacteria in sediments and why inorganic mercury is more dangerous in aquatic sediments.Hard
Show Answer

Sulfate-reducing bacteria (Desulfovibrio) in anaerobic sediments methylate Hg²⁺ → methylmercury (CH₃Hg⁺) as a metabolic byproduct. CH₃Hg⁺ is 100× more toxic, lipophilic, crosses cell membranes/blood-brain barrier/placenta, and is highly bioavailable to phytoplankton. Eutrophication (more organic sediment, low O₂) accelerates methylation. Acid rain lowers pH → increases Hg mobility. Warming increases bacterial activity → more methylmercury → greater biomagnification. Inorganic Hg becomes dramatically more dangerous after conversion in sediments.

Critically evaluate the DDT ban: entirely beneficial or were there unintended consequences for human health?Hard
Show Answer

Benefits: raptor recovery, reduced human DDT body burdens, removed endocrine disruptor/carcinogen from ecosystems. Unintended consequence: malaria resurgence in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. Alternative insecticides more expensive; mosquitoes develop resistance faster. WHO reversed course in 2006: re-endorsed indoor residual spraying (IRS) with DDT for malaria control. Stockholm Convention (2001) allows DDT for malaria vector control while banning agricultural use. Balanced position: total DDT ban was simplistic — targeted indoor IRS is far less ecologically damaging than outdoor agricultural spraying. Balance required between human health (malaria kills ~600,000/year) and ecosystem protection.

How do "legacy pollutants" like PCBs persist in Arctic ecosystems despite being banned decades ago?Hard
Show Answer

(1) Long-range atmospheric transport (grasshopper effect): volatile PCBs evaporate in warm regions, travel in air, condense and deposit in cold polar regions. (2) Arctic food webs are long (phytoplankton→zooplankton→cod→seal→polar bear) — more trophic steps → more magnification. (3) Cold temperatures slow metabolism → PCBs degrade extremely slowly. (4) Environmental half-life: 10-100 years. 50 years of PCB use = 100+ years of Arctic contamination. Polar bears and Inuit people still have very high PCB body burdens — demonstrating that "banned" ≠ "gone."

Explain how endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) cause harm at sub-toxic concentrations. Give two examples with mechanisms.Hard
Show Answer

EDCs mimic or block hormones at concentrations far below conventional toxicity thresholds. (1) DDT/DDE: mimics oestrogen → binds oestrogen receptors → feminisation of male fish (intersex fish producing egg-yolk protein vitellogenin in UK rivers). (2) Atrazine (herbicide): activates aromatase enzyme → converts testosterone to oestrogen in frogs → male frogs develop female gonadal tissue → reproductive failure (Tyrone Hayes, Berkeley). Even parts per trillion can cause disruption — hormones naturally operate at 10⁻¹² mol/L. Biomagnification amplifies tiny environmental concentrations to hormonally active levels in apex predators.

Design a monitoring programme to detect mercury biomagnification in a river system using stable isotopes.Hard
Show Answer

(1) Baseline: collect water, sediment, filter-feeding invertebrates from reference and study sites; measure total Hg and methylmercury. (2) Trophic structure: measure δ¹⁵N across organisms; calculate trophic position = 1 + (δ¹⁵N_organism − δ¹⁵N_baseline)/3.4. (3) Tissue Hg: sample muscle tissue from organisms spanning all trophic positions. (4) Regression: plot log(MeHg) vs. δ¹⁵N; slope = trophic magnification factor (TMF). TMF > 1 confirms biomagnification. (5) Compare large fish MeHg to WHO guideline (0.5 mg/kg wet weight); calculate safe consumption rates for local fishermen. Stable isotopes allow food web mapping without direct observation.

Analyse the Stockholm Convention as an international response to biomagnification. What are the "dirty dozen" and have they been effectively controlled?Hard
Show Answer

Stockholm Convention (2001): global treaty targeting Persistent Organic Pollutants. Original 12 ("dirty dozen"): aldrin, dieldrin, endrin, chlordane, heptachlor, DDT, mirex, toxaphene, PCBs, dioxins, furans, HCB. Selection criteria: persistence, bioaccumulation (log Kow >5), long-range transport, adverse effects. Progress: production of most dirty dozen eliminated or restricted; Arctic contamination slowly declining (seal/polar bear blubber measurements). Challenges: new POPs (PFAS, PBDEs) added but chemical production growing faster than regulation; enforcement gaps in developing countries. The framework works but the chemical universe keeps expanding.

Explain what PFAS ("forever chemicals") are and why they pose unique waste management challenges.Hard
Show Answer

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances): ~12,000 compounds. The C-F bond is the strongest in organic chemistry — essentially indestructible by biological or chemical processes. Half-lives: decades to centuries in environment, ~5 years in human blood. Detected everywhere: Arctic ice, ocean sediments, penguins, human cord blood, breast milk. Health effects: cancer (kidney, testicular), immune suppression, thyroid disruption. Waste challenges: (1) No biodegradation. (2) Standard incineration does not destroy them — need plasma incineration (>1400°C). (3) Landfill leachate spreads PFAS to groundwater (1,000+ contaminated sites). (4) Sewage sludge concentrates PFAS → soil contamination when used as fertiliser. True solution requires phasing out all PFAS production — difficult with >200 manufacturers and 12,000 compounds.

Set A Easy — Ozone Depletion

Where is the ozone layer and what does it do?Easy
Show Answer

The ozone layer is in the stratosphere, 15–35 km above Earth's surface. It absorbs most harmful UV-B and UV-C radiation from the Sun, protecting life from DNA damage, skin cancer, and cataracts.

What is the chemical formula of ozone and how is it formed naturally?Easy
Show Answer

O₃ (triatomic oxygen). Formed when UV radiation splits O₂ → 2O; then O + O₂ → O₃. This natural formation-destruction equilibrium maintains a steady ozone layer under pre-industrial conditions.

What are CFCs and where were they used?Easy
Show Answer

Chlorofluorocarbons — man-made compounds containing C, F, Cl. Used as refrigerants (fridges, ACs), aerosol spray propellants, and foam-blowing agents. Trade names: Freons (CFC-11, CFC-12).

How do CFCs destroy ozone? Write a simplified equation.Easy
Show Answer

UV breaks CFCs → Cl atoms. Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂; ClO + O → Cl + O₂. Net: O₃ + O → 2O₂. Cl is regenerated (catalyst) — one Cl atom destroys ~100,000 O₃ molecules.

What is the ozone hole and where does it occur?Easy
Show Answer

Severe thinning of the ozone layer primarily over Antarctica each spring (September-November). Caused by CFCs reacting on polar stratospheric cloud (ice) surfaces at very low winter temperatures.

Name the international treaty that controls ozone-depleting substances.Easy
Show Answer

The Montreal Protocol (1987) — signed by 197 countries. Mandated phase-out of CFCs and other ozone-depleting substances. Considered the most successful international environmental treaty.

What health effects are caused by increased UV-B reaching Earth's surface?Easy
Show Answer

(1) Skin cancer (UV-B damages skin cell DNA). (2) Cataracts (lens protein damage). (3) Immune suppression. (4) Damage to phytoplankton → disrupted marine food webs. (5) Reduced yield of UV-sensitive crops (soybeans, wheat).

Is the ozone layer recovering? Give evidence.Easy
Show Answer

Yes, slowly. The ozone hole peaked in size in the late 1990s-early 2000s; it has been gradually shrinking since. Atmospheric CFC concentrations are declining. Scientists predict full recovery by ~2060-2075. The Montreal Protocol is credited with reversing the damage.

Set B Medium — Ozone Depletion

Explain the catalytic nature of chlorine in ozone destruction.Medium
Show Answer

Cl• is a catalyst — consumed in step (1): Cl• + O₃ → ClO + O₂, then regenerated in step (2): ClO + O → Cl• + O₂. Cl• cycles ~100,000 times before being deactivated (e.g., by forming HCl reservoir). One CFC molecule → one Cl• → destroys 100,000 O₃ molecules. The chain reaction is why even small CFC quantities cause catastrophic ozone loss.

Why does the ozone hole form specifically over Antarctica?Medium
Show Answer

Three unique factors: (1) Polar stratospheric clouds (PSCs) form at very low winter temperatures (<−78°C); PSC surfaces activate Cl₂ from reservoir compounds (HCl, ClONO₂). (2) Spring sunlight breaks Cl₂ → 2Cl• → explosive ozone destruction begins. (3) Antarctic polar vortex isolates and concentrates ozone-depleted air. These three factors combine uniquely at the South Pole.

What alternatives to CFCs were developed after the Montreal Protocol?Medium
Show Answer

HCFCs (transitional, less Cl, phased out by 2030-40). HFCs (no Cl, ozone-safe, but powerful greenhouse gases — addressed by 2016 Kigali Amendment). HFOs (low GWP, no Cl — newest generation). Natural refrigerants: CO₂, ammonia, propane — ozone-safe and low GWP (best long-term solution).

Distinguish between "good ozone" (stratospheric) and "bad ozone" (tropospheric).Medium
Show Answer

Good ozone: stratosphere (15-35 km); naturally occurring; absorbs UV-B; protects life. We want more. Bad ozone: ground level; formed from NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight; component of photochemical smog; toxic to human respiratory systems, animals, and plants. Same molecule, opposite effects depending on altitude.

Why is ozone depletion a decades-long problem even after CFC production has stopped?Medium
Show Answer

CFCs have atmospheric lifetimes of 50-100 years. CFCs released in the 1970s-80s are still rising to the stratosphere. Stratospheric mixing is slow (years to decades). Ozone recovery requires CFC concentrations to fall below pre-hole levels — predicted by 2060-2075. "Chemical inertia" of stable compounds means their environmental damage greatly outlasts their production.

How does increased UV-B affect marine food webs?Medium
Show Answer

UV-B damages phytoplankton/zooplankton at ocean surface: disrupts photosynthetic machinery, damages DNA, alters behaviour. Reduced phytoplankton productivity → less food for zooplankton → reduced fish/whale/seabird food. Antarctic ozone hole estimated to reduce Southern Ocean phytoplankton productivity by 6-12%, rippling up the entire Antarctic food web (krill → penguins → leopard seals → orcas).

What is the Dobson Unit and what value indicates a dangerously low ozone level?Medium
Show Answer

Dobson Unit (DU): thickness of ozone column (100 DU = 1 mm of pure ozone at standard conditions). Normal stratospheric ozone: ~300 DU. Ozone hole defined as <220 DU. Worst years: ~90-100 DU over parts of Antarctica — less than one-third of normal, causing significant UV-B increases at the surface.

Why are halons more potent ozone destroyers than CFCs?Medium
Show Answer

Halons (bromofluorocarbons, used in fire extinguishers) release Br radicals. Br is ~45× more effective per atom than Cl at destroying ozone — Br cycles faster and is harder to lock into stable reservoir compounds. Halon-1301 ODP = 10-16 vs CFC-11 ODP = 1. Despite smaller volumes used, halons contribute disproportionately to ozone depletion. Also phased out under Montreal Protocol.

Set C Hard — Ozone Depletion

Write the complete catalytic cycle for Cl-mediated ozone destruction including chain-terminating reactions.Hard
Show Answer

Initiation: CFC + UV → Cl• + fragments. Propagation: (1) Cl• + O₃ → ClO• + O₂. (2) ClO• + O → Cl• + O₂. Net: O₃ + O → 2O₂. ClO dimer: 2ClO → Cl₂O₂ + UV → 2Cl• + O₂ (dominant at low T). Chain termination (reservoir): Cl• + CH₄ → HCl + CH₃•; ClO• + NO₂ → ClONO₂. HCl and ClONO₂ are "reservoir species" reactivated on PSC surfaces. Each Cl destroys ~10⁵ O₃ before termination. BrO + ClO → Br• + Cl• + O₂ (cross-halogen catalysis amplifies depletion).

Why was the Kigali Amendment (2016) needed even though HFCs don't harm ozone?Hard
Show Answer

HFCs are powerful greenhouse gases (GWP: HFC-134a = 1,430; HFC-23 = 12,400). Production surged after CFC phaseout. Projected HFC emissions without control: +0.5°C by 2100. Kigali Amendment: phase down HFCs by 80-85% by 2040-2047. Estimated to prevent 0.4-0.5°C of warming. Demonstrates that fixing one environmental problem (ozone) without considering climate implications creates a new problem — emphasising the need for holistic solutions.

Analyse the Montreal Protocol: is it truly the world's most successful environmental treaty, or do problems remain?Hard
Show Answer

Successes: CFC production down >99%; ozone hole area peaked ~2000 and is shrinking; estimated 2 million fewer skin cancers/year; universal ratification. Problems: (1) 2019: illegal CFC-11 emissions from East Asia detected — enforcement gaps. (2) "Very short-lived substances" (VSLS, e.g., dichloromethane) exempt but rising. (3) HFCs addressed only in 2016 (29 years late). (4) Nitrous oxide (N₂O) — now the main ozone-depleting substance not covered. (5) Rocket emissions (growing commercial space launches). Still the gold standard, but ongoing vigilance required.

Explain the ozone-climate interaction: how does climate change delay ozone recovery?Hard
Show Answer

CO₂ greenhouse effect warms troposphere but cools stratosphere (heat trapped below). Colder stratosphere → more PSC formation → more Cl activation → faster ozone loss. Climate change delays full ozone recovery by 5-10 years beyond what CFC decline alone would achieve. Conversely, ozone depletion strengthened the Antarctic polar vortex → shifted Southern Hemisphere precipitation belts → altered Southern Ocean circulation (carbon uptake). The systems are deeply coupled — separate policy silos (ozone vs. climate treaties) are scientifically inadequate.

Could volcanic eruptions cause ozone depletion? Explain with reference to Mt. Pinatubo (1991).Hard
Show Answer

Yes, partially. Pinatubo injected ~20 Tg SO₂ into the stratosphere → aerosol cloud provided surfaces for heterogeneous reactions (similar to PSCs), activating Cl reservoirs → global ozone declined 3-6% in 1992-93. The depletion was NOT from volcanic HCl (mostly rained out in troposphere). It amplified destruction via anthropogenic Cl from CFCs. Without background CFC loading, Pinatubo would have had minimal ozone impact — demonstrating that CFCs made the ozone layer more vulnerable to natural disturbances.

Explain ODP (Ozone Depletion Potential) and compare values for CFC-11, HCFC-22, and halon-1301.Hard
Show Answer

ODP = ozone destruction relative to CFC-11 (reference = 1.0) per unit mass released. Depends on: number of Cl/Br atoms, atmospheric lifetime, and halogen effectiveness. CFC-11: ODP = 1.0. CFC-12: ODP = 1.0. HCFC-22: ODP = 0.055 (90% lower — short lifetime, breaks down before reaching stratosphere). Halon-1301: ODP = 10-12 (Br is ~45× more effective than Cl per atom). ODP determines phase-out priority under Montreal Protocol: higher ODP compounds restricted first and most strictly.

In 2019, scientists detected new CFC-11 emissions violating the Montreal Protocol. What does this reveal about international environmental governance?Hard
Show Answer

Montzka et al. (Science 2018): NOAA atmospheric monitoring detected anomalous CFC-11 increases since ~2013; source triangulated to East Asia (confirmed as Shandong/Hebei, China in 2019). Illegal production for cheap polyurethane foam blowing. Governance failures: no global emissions monitoring system (relies on voluntary reporting); no real-time satellite CFC-11 monitoring; economic incentives to cheat (CFC-11 cheaper than legal HFC alternatives). Response: China investigated and announced enforcement. Lessons: international agreements need independent monitoring, rapid detection systems, and economic incentives for compliance — self-reporting alone is insufficient. The incident delayed ozone recovery by ~1 year.

Explain the N₂O threat to stratospheric ozone and why it is not covered by the Montreal Protocol.Hard
Show Answer

Ravishankara et al. (Science 2009): N₂O is now the dominant ozone-depleting emission not controlled by Montreal Protocol. In stratosphere: N₂O → NO + NO radical; NO + O₃ → NO₂ + O₂; NO₂ + O → NO + O₂ (catalytic cycle, similar to Cl). N₂O sources: agricultural soils (synthetic fertilisers), livestock manure, industrial processes. Emissions are rising with global food production growth. N₂O is also a powerful greenhouse gas (GWP₁₀₀ = 273). Not covered by Montreal Protocol because: it was not among the original ODS when the Protocol was negotiated (1987); it was considered a natural background gas; its ODP is relatively low per molecule but large in total due to enormous emission volume. Addressing N₂O requires reducing agricultural fertiliser use — a far more complex socio-economic challenge than banning industrial CFCs.

Distinguish between biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste.Easy

State the 3Rs of waste management in order of priority.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Reduce — use less material/energy (most effective; prevents waste generation). (2) Reuse — use items multiple times. (3) Recycle — convert waste into new products (least effective; still requires energy). Reduce is best because it avoids waste entirely.

What is composting and why is it beneficial?Easy
Show Answer

Composting is aerobic decomposition of organic waste by microorganisms into humus-rich compost. Benefits: reduces landfill waste, produces natural fertiliser, improves soil structure, and sequesters carbon instead of releasing it as methane (as would happen in anaerobic landfills).

Name one Indian government initiative to manage waste and reduce plastic pollution.Easy

Swachh Bharat Mission (Clean India Mission, 2014) — promotes cleanliness and solid waste management. India also banned single-use plastics below 75 microns from July 2022 under the Plastic Waste Management Rules.

Give one biodegradable and one non-biodegradable waste from a school.Easy

Biodegradable: leftover canteen food, waste paper, fallen leaves. Non-biodegradable: plastic water bottles, ball-point pens, plastic wrappers, electronic waste (old calculators).

What is e-waste and why is it harmful in landfills?Easy
Show Answer

E-waste is discarded electronics (phones, computers, TVs, batteries). In landfills, heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium) leach into soil and groundwater, contaminate drinking water, and bioaccumulate in food chains — causing neurological damage and cancer.

State three disadvantages of landfills.Easy
Show Answer

(1) Produces methane (powerful greenhouse gas) from anaerobic organic decomposition. (2) Leachate (toxic liquid) contaminates groundwater. (3) Uses large land areas. (4) Resources buried = permanently lost. (5) Odour and pest problems.

Give three reasons to avoid plastic bags.Easy

Set B Medium — Waste Management

Explain integrated solid waste management (ISWM) and how it differs from landfilling.Medium
Show Answer

ISWM follows the waste hierarchy: (1) Reduce, (2) Reuse, (3) Recycle (materials recovery), (4) Recover energy (waste-to-energy), (5) Landfill only residuals. Landfilling alone wastes resources, generates methane, and contaminates soil/water. ISWM minimises landfill use, recovers materials and energy, and handles different waste streams (organic, dry recyclables, hazardous, e-waste) separately. Germany and Japan recycle >50% of waste using ISWM principles.

Why is source segregation of wet (biodegradable) and dry (non-biodegradable) waste important?Medium
Show Answer

Mixed waste: wet waste ruins dry recyclables (wet paper/cardboard unusable); composting impossible with plastic contamination; entire mixed load goes to landfill. Segregated: wet waste → composting/biogas; dry recyclables → materials recycling; hazardous → special treatment. Reduces landfill by 60-70%. Indian Solid Waste Management Rules (2016) mandate source segregation via green (wet) and blue (dry) bins.

Explain how ocean plastic breaks down into microplastics and enters the food chain.Medium
Show Answer

UV radiation and wave action fragment large plastic items into microplastics (<5 mm) and nanoplastics (<1 μm). These are ingested by phytoplankton, zooplankton, and filter feeders (mussels, oysters), then progress up the food chain. Microplastics also adsorb fat-soluble POPs on their surfaces, acting as toxin delivery vehicles. Microplastics are now found in human blood, placentas, and lung tissue.

What is Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) and how does it change plastic packaging incentives?Medium
Show Answer

EPR: manufacturers are legally and financially responsible for end-of-life management of their products. For plastic packaging: companies must collect and recycle a percentage of plastic they put on the market. Incentive: companies paying for recycling design lighter, more recyclable packaging → reduce plastic use. India's PWM Rules 2022 mandate EPR. Germany's deposit-return system (€0.25 per bottle) achieves 98% return rate — EPR that internalises disposal costs into product price.

Compare environmental costs of recycling plastic vs. producing virgin plastic.Medium
Show Answer

Recycling: saves ~70% energy (PET), reduces virgin petroleum use, lowers GHG emissions, reduces ocean/landfill pollution. Limitations: only ~9% of all plastic ever produced has been recycled; plastic degrades in quality (downcycling, 2-3 cycles max); contamination reduces recyclability; not all types recyclable (#1, #2 best; #3, #6, #7 rarely). Virgin plastic: cheaper, consistent quality. Conclusion: recycling better than virgin production but reduction is the only truly sustainable strategy.

Explain the circular economy concept and how it differs from the linear "take-make-dispose" model.Medium
Show Answer

Linear: extract → manufacture → use → dispose. Circular: (1) Eliminate waste/pollution by design. (2) Keep products and materials in use (repair, reuse, remanufacture, recycle). (3) Regenerate natural systems (compost organic materials). Example: phone "as a service" — manufacturer retains ownership and incentive to design durable/repairable phones. Requires redesigning entire production-consumption systems, not just end-of-pipe recycling.

Why is open burning of waste a serious health and environmental problem?Medium
Show Answer

Open burning of mixed waste releases: dioxins/furans (from PVC — highly toxic carcinogens and endocrine disruptors), heavy metals (Hg, Pb), black carbon (accelerates glacier melt), CO, PAHs (carcinogens), PM2.5 (deep lung penetration). WHO estimates waste burning causes 270,000 premature deaths/year. India is working to phase out open burning through Swachh Bharat and waste-to-energy plants.

Explain the impact of improper medical waste disposal on public health.Medium
Show Answer

Medical waste contains used needles/syringes (HIV, hepatitis B/C transmission if reused), body fluids (pathogen spread), pharmaceuticals (antibiotic resistance, endocrine disruptors in water), cytotoxic drugs (genotoxic), and radioactive materials. Bio-Medical Waste Rules, India (1998/2016): mandate colour-coded segregation, autoclaving/incineration in certified facilities. Informal waste pickers handling mixed medical waste face direct infection risk — the most vulnerable group affected by improper disposal.

Set C Hard — Waste Management

Explain the chemistry of why PVC is particularly hazardous to incinerate.Hard
Show Answer

PVC contains ~57% chlorine by mass. Incomplete combustion at <850°C: chlorinated aromatics react → dioxins (PCDDs) and furans (PCDFs). Dioxins cause carcinogenicity and endocrine disruption at ng/m³ concentrations. Complete destruction requires >850°C with adequate O₂ and residence time — impossible in open burning. PVC also contains phthalate plasticisers and historical heavy metal stabilisers (Pb, Cd). Alternatives (polypropylene, cross-linked polyethylene) have less hazardous combustion products. PVC is one reason why waste-to-energy plants require expensive emission controls.

Critically evaluate India's single-use plastic ban (2022): achievements and remaining challenges.Hard
Show Answer

Scope: 19 categories of single-use plastics <75 microns banned from July 2022. Achievements: raised awareness, some industry switch to alternatives (bamboo straws, paper bags). Challenges: (1) Weak enforcement — plastic still widely available. (2) Alternatives more expensive → consumer resistance. (3) No adequate formal waste collection infrastructure. (4) Informal economy (street vendors) hardest to regulate. (5) India generates ~3.4 million tonnes plastic waste/year — 19 categories is a small fraction. Needs complementary EPR, alternatives R&D, recycling infrastructure, and formal waste-picker integration to be truly effective.

Is waste-to-energy (WTE) incineration a sustainable solution for India's municipal solid waste? Evaluate both sides.Hard
Show Answer

For: reduces landfill volume by 90%, generates electricity (~600 kWh/tonne), destroys pathogens. Against: (1) India's MSW has high moisture (~50-60%) and food content → low calorific value → needs supplementary fuel. (2) Inadequate emission controls → dioxins, HCl, heavy metals. (3) Ash (25-30% of input) contains concentrated heavy metals — hazardous waste. (4) Guaranteed waste supply contracts discourage waste reduction and recycling (plant needs garbage to be profitable). Verdict: WTE should process only residuals after reduction/reuse/recycling — not compete with the waste hierarchy. Composting and biogas for organic fraction are more appropriate for India's waste composition.

How does biogas generation work? Write the overall reaction and explain environmental and energy benefits.Hard
Show Answer

Anaerobic digestion (4 stages): hydrolysis → acidogenesis → acetogenesis → methanogenesis. Overall: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 3CH₄ + 3CO₂. Biogas composition: ~60% CH₄, ~40% CO₂. Benefits: (1) Renewable energy replacing LPG/firewood. (2) Prevents methane emissions from open dumping/landfilling. (3) Digestate is nutrient-rich biofertiliser. (4) Reduces landfill use. India's NBMMP (National Biogas Programme) subsidises household plants. A 2 m³ plant processes ~50 kg wet waste/day → ~2 m³ biogas/day → 2 hours cooking fuel. Converts a waste problem into an energy and fertiliser resource.

Explain the role of informal waste pickers in India's recycling economy and why their formal integration matters.Hard
Show Answer

India has ~1.5-4 million waste pickers who collect, sort, and sell recyclables — recovering ~20-25% of recyclable material, more than formal systems in most cities. They save municipalities ~₹5,000-15,000/tonne in collection/disposal costs. Problems: social stigma, no occupational safety, no health insurance. Formal integration (Pune Swachh model, Chhattisgarh Recyclers' Association): cooperative status, official contracts, MRFs with equipment, better incomes. Outcomes: higher recycling rates, better worker health, reduced municipal costs. Integration is essential for Zero Waste Cities — formal systems alone cannot achieve 50%+ recycling rates.

What are PFAS "forever chemicals" and what unique waste management challenges do they pose?Hard
Show Answer

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, ~12,000 compounds): C-F bond is essentially indestructible. Environmental half-lives: decades to centuries; human blood half-life ~5 years. Detected everywhere including Arctic ice, penguins, human cord blood. Health: cancer, immune suppression, thyroid disruption. Waste challenges: (1) No biodegradation — cannot compost or biodegrade. (2) Standard incineration fails — need plasma incineration (>1400°C) or supercritical water oxidation. (3) Landfill leachate spreads PFAS to groundwater (>1,000 contaminated sites). (4) Sewage sludge used as fertiliser → soil contamination. EPA set maximum contaminant level at 4 ppt for PFOA/PFOS (2024). Solution requires phasing out all PFAS production — extremely difficult with >200 manufacturers globally.

Apply the "social cost of carbon" concept to compare waste reduction vs. landfilling of food waste.Hard
Show Answer

Social cost of carbon (SCC): monetised harm per tonne CO₂ equivalent (US EPA 2023: ~$190/tonne). Landfill food waste: 1 tonne → ~0.25 tonne CH₄ (GWP₁₀₀ = 29.8) → 7.45 tonne CO₂-eq → social cost = $190 × 7.45 = $1,415. Composting same 1 tonne: near-zero CH₄ (aerobic) → social cost ≈ $0-50. Source reduction (prevent food waste): avoids all downstream costs. SCC framework makes composting infrastructure economically justified — the "true cost" of landfilling includes climate, health, and ecological damages not captured by private market prices. Government intervention (taxes, bans, mandates) is justified when private costs understate social costs.

Evaluate India's Plastic Waste Management Rules 2022 EPR provisions and what systemic changes would make them truly effective.Hard
Show Answer

EPR provisions: PIBOs (producers/importers/brand owners) register on central portal, declare annual plastic footprint, meet annual collection targets (100% by 2024-25 for rigid plastic), can buy EPR certificates from recyclers. Strengths: first legally binding EPR in India; covers all packaging; creates recycling demand signal; funds infrastructure. Gaps: (1) No mandatory minimum recycled content → weak demand for recycled material. (2) Certificate trading allows paper compliance without real collection. (3) No deposit-return system (Germany: 98% return rate with €0.25 deposit). (4) Informal waste pickers not formally recognised as EPR collection agents. (5) Monitoring relies on self-reporting. For true effectiveness: mandatory recycled content laws, deposit-return systems for bottles, formal waste-picker integration in EPR certificates, independent third-party audits, modulated EPR fees rewarding eco-design, and government procurement of products with recycled content.
